# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook structural problem: the cap exists on paper, but the carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove it from every category of dispute that is likely to matter in a federal contractor engagement. Indemnification obligations (§9), confidentiality breaches (§5), and IP claims (§4) are all carved out of the cap entirely — and those three categories collectively cover the primary vectors of commercial exposure in a professional services agreement of this type. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive significant litigation value. The agreement as drafted does not provide the liability protection it appears to offer.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. The Cap Is Structurally Hollow

Section 8.1 establishes a 12-month trailing fees cap on total cumulative liability. In isolation, that is a defensible and commercially standard position. The problem is Section 8.2.

Read the carve-outs in sequence:

- **(a) Indemnification obligations (§9):** Vendor's indemnification scope under §9.1 is broad — it covers performance of Services, IP infringement by Deliverables, breach of the Agreement generally, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. That is not a narrow carve-out. That is the agreement's primary liability mechanism, and it is entirely outside the cap.

- **(b) Confidentiality breaches (§5):** In a national security and federal services context, confidentiality is not a peripheral obligation. A confidentiality breach here can implicate classified or controlled unclassified information, federal regulatory exposure, and reputational harm that dwarfs any fee structure. Removing the cap from confidentiality breaches is standard in many agreements — but in this engagement context, the absence of any sub-cap or carve-out floor makes this a potentially unlimited exposure with no ceiling defined anywhere in the document.

- **(c) IP infringement and misappropriation:** §4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but §4.3 preserves a license-back for Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into those Deliverables. The scope of what constitutes "Vendor Pre-Existing IP" is undefined beyond a circular reference to materials developed "prior to or independently of this Agreement." In practice, a vendor can characterize significant portions of a Deliverable as Pre-Existing IP. Any dispute over that characterization — or over unauthorized use of Vendor IP — is now outside the cap entirely under §8.2(c).

- **(d) Gross negligence or willful misconduct:** Standard carve-out, not objectionable in isolation.

- **(e) Fees, expenses, and taxes owed by Client:** This carve-out is asymmetric. It protects Vendor's right to collect payment outside the cap, but there is no corresponding carve-out protecting Client's right to recover overpayments or fee disputes. A Client claim for fee recovery is capped; a Vendor claim for fee collection is not.

The structural result: the cap governs ordinary, run-of-the-mill breach claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or payment. In a professional services agreement, that residual category is thin. The cap does not apply where the real disputes live.

---

### 2. Indemnification Scope Creates Circular Unlimited Exposure

The interaction between §8.2(a) and §9.1 deserves specific attention. Section 9.1(c) indemnifies Client against losses arising from "Vendor's breach of this Agreement" — broadly stated, without limiting to third-party claims in that subsection. Read literally, a direct breach by Vendor triggers indemnification obligations, which are then carved out of the cap under §8.2(a). This creates a pathway where nearly any material breach by Vendor could be characterized as triggering indemnification, bypassing the cap entirely.

This may or may not be the drafting intent, but ambiguity in a limitation of liability clause resolves against the party seeking to enforce the cap. In litigation, opposing counsel will find this argument. I already have.

---

### 3. No Sub-Cap on Uncapped Obligations

The agreement removes the cap from multiple categories but provides no alternative ceiling on those uncapped obligations. There is no aggregate limit on indemnification exposure, no per-incident ceiling on confidentiality breach damages, and no defined floor for IP claims. In a federal contractor context where the underlying contracts may involve significant government program value, "unlimited" is not a theoretical concern.

---

### 4. Asymmetric Fee Carve-Out (§8.2(e))

As noted above: Vendor's right to collect fees is outside the cap. Client's right to dispute or recover fees is inside the cap. This asymmetry is not commercially neutral. If Client overpays, disputes a fee, or seeks to recover amounts paid under a terminated SOW, that claim is subject to the 12-month cap. If Vendor claims unpaid fees, no cap applies. This should be either made mutual or removed.

---

### 5. Pre-Existing IP Definition Is Operationally Vague

Section 4.1 defines Pre-Existing IP as materials developed "prior to or independently of this Agreement." The word "independently" is doing significant work here and is undefined. A vendor who builds a Deliverable using internal frameworks, libraries, or methodologies developed outside this engagement can characterize substantial portions of the work product as Pre-Existing IP, retain ownership, and then assert IP rights against Client — outside the cap — if Client uses those components in ways Vendor disputes. In a federal services context where Deliverables may be integrated into government systems, this is not a hypothetical risk.

---

## Requested Changes

| Issue | Required Change |
|---|---|
| Cap carve-outs swallow the cap | Narrow §8.2 exceptions or establish sub-caps on indemnification and confidentiality obligations |
| Indemnification scope in §9.1(c) | Limit "breach of Agreement" indemnification to third-party claims only, or exclude from §8.2(a) carve-out |
| No ceiling on uncapped obligations | Add aggregate cap on indemnification exposure; consider per-incident cap on confidentiality breach damages |
| Asymmetric fee carve-out in §8.2(e) | Make mutual, or remove and address fee disputes through separate mechanism |
| Pre-Existing IP definition | Define "independently" with specificity; require SOW-level disclosure of Pre-Existing IP to be incorporated into Deliverables |
| Confidentiality survival (§5.3) | Five-year survival is below standard for federal contractor engagements involving sensitive government information — consider extending or making indefinite for classified/CUI categories |

---

*This agreement is not approved for execution in its current form. The liability structure as drafted does not provide the protection it represents. These issues are correctable — but they need to be corrected before signature, not litigated after.*
