# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this tier — the liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, IP assignment is conditioned on payment rather than automatic, and the indemnification procedure in Section 9.3 includes a consent-to-settlement requirement that actually protects the indemnified party. However, three issues create unacceptable exposure for a federal contractor engagement that must be resolved before execution: the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 is incompatible with federal contractor dispute obligations and forecloses judicial remedies in ways that can conflict with prime contract flow-down requirements; the confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is facially inadequate for a national security context and may conflict with statutory obligations that do not expire on a commercial timeline; and the IP assignment in Section 4.2 is conditioned on "full payment of all fees," creating a window in which Deliverables incorporated into federal work product may be legally owned by the Vendor during any payment dispute, with no interim license to bridge that gap. Each of these issues is a trap that will not surface until the worst possible moment.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 ISSUE 1 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause
**Risk Level: High | Category: Structural / Flow-Down Incompatibility**

**The Problem:**
The clause mandates binding AAA arbitration for *any* dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement, with no carve-outs. For a federal contractor, this is operationally dangerous on multiple fronts:

- **Prime contract flow-down:** Most federal prime contracts — particularly those under FAR, DFARS, or IC-specific vehicles — contain mandatory dispute resolution clauses, Boards of Contract Appeals jurisdiction provisions, or Contracting Officer final decision requirements. A downstream vendor agreement that locks disputes into private AAA arbitration creates a direct conflict. If Meridian is dragged into a government dispute that implicates DataStream's performance, Meridian cannot compel DataStream into the federal dispute forum, and cannot use arbitration findings in a federal proceeding.

- **No emergency injunctive relief carve-out:** The clause contains no exception for seeking emergency equitable relief in court. In an IP misappropriation scenario, a data breach, or a confidentiality violation involving classified or sensitive federal information, the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction is not a theoretical risk — it is a practical catastrophe. The arbitrator cannot issue a TRO with the speed or enforceability of a federal court.

- **Venue:** Wilmington, Delaware is appropriate for commercial disputes. It is not appropriate as the exclusive forum for disputes that may involve federal jurisdiction, national security equities, or government-sensitive information that cannot be disclosed in a private arbitration proceeding.

**Required Fix:**
Add a carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights. Add a second carve-out for disputes that are subject to mandatory federal dispute resolution mechanisms under applicable prime contract terms. Consider whether arbitration should be replaced entirely with litigation given the federal contracting context.

---

### 🔴 ISSUE 2 — Section 5.3: Confidentiality Survival Period
**Risk Level: High | Category: Regulatory / Statutory Conflict**

**The Problem:**
The five-year confidentiality survival period is a standard commercial term. It is not adequate — and may be affirmatively non-compliant — in a federal contractor context.

- **CUI and statutory obligations:** If any information exchanged under this agreement constitutes Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) under 32 C.F.R. Part 2002, or is subject to protections under FISMA, the Privacy Act, or agency-specific handling requirements, those obligations do not expire in five years. A contractual provision that purports to sunset confidentiality obligations at year five creates a false ceiling that could be used by DataStream to argue its obligations have lapsed, even where statutory obligations continue.

- **Trade secrets:** The five-year cap also applies to trade secrets by its plain terms. Trade secret protection under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) and applicable state law is not time-limited. A contractual provision that expires confidentiality obligations in five years could be read to limit remedies that would otherwise be available under statute.

- **Classified information:** If there is any possibility that Vendor personnel will be exposed to classified information — even inadvertently — the five-year survival period is legally meaningless. Classification-based obligations are indefinite and non-waivable by contract. This clause does not acknowledge that reality.

**Required Fix:**
Extend the survival period to the longer of: (a) ten years, or (b) the period required by applicable law, regulation, or prime contract terms. Add explicit language stating that nothing in Section 5.3 limits obligations that arise independently under applicable federal law, regulation, or agency directive. Add a specific carve-out for trade secrets, which shall remain protected for as long as they qualify as trade secrets under applicable law.

---

### 🔴 ISSUE 3 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment
**Risk Level: High | Category: Operational / Ownership Gap**

**The Problem:**
The assignment of Deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a common vendor-protective term in commercial agreements. In a federal contracting context, it creates a structural trap.

- **Ownership gap during disputes:** If a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy, scope creep, or a contested change order — Vendor retains legal title to all Deliverables until resolution. During that window, Meridian is using, delivering, or incorporating into federal work product materials it does not legally own. This creates exposure under the prime contract (which likely requires Meridian to warrant ownership or right to use all deliverables), and creates a leverage asymmetry that DataStream can exploit.

- **No interim license:** Section 4.3 grants a license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables. There is no corresponding interim license to the Deliverables themselves pending full payment. If payment is disputed and the assignment has not yet transferred, Meridian has no contractual right to continue using the Deliverables during the dispute period.

- **"Full payment of all fees" is undefined in scope:** The phrase "all fees" is not limited to fees for the specific SOW under which the Deliverable was created. A plausible reading is that Vendor retains title to all Deliverables under all SOWs until every outstanding invoice across the entire engagement is paid in full. This is an unreasonable and likely unintended result, but it is the result the plain language supports.

**Required Fix:**
Either (a) remove the payment condition and replace it with a payment obligation enforceable through breach and collection remedies rather than title retention, or (b) add an interim license provision granting Client a fully-paid, irrevocable license to use all Deliverables from the date of delivery, with the formal assignment to follow upon payment, and clarify that the payment condition applies only to fees for the specific SOW under which the Deliverable was created.

### 🟡 ISSUE 4 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Approval Rights
**Risk Level: Medium | Category: National Security / Personnel**

**The Problem:**
Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors without Client pre-approval, subject only to Vendor's retained responsibility for performance. In a federal contractor engagement, this is insufficient.

- **Personnel security:** Subcontractors may require security clearances, background investigations, or citizenship verification depending on the nature of the work. Vendor's unilateral right to engage subcontractors without Client approval creates a scenario where unvetted personnel are performing work on federal engagements without Meridian's knowledge or consent.

- **Flow-down obligations:** FAR and DFARS impose specific subcontracting requirements, including mandatory flow-down clauses, small business subcontracting plans, and in some cases prior approval requirements. This clause does not require DataStream to flow down any prime contract obligations to its subcontractors, and does not give Meridian visibility into or approval rights over subcontractor selection.

**Required Fix:**
Require Client's prior written approval for any subcontractor engagement. Require Vendor to flow down all applicable prime contract obligations to subcontractors. Require Vendor to represent that all subcontractor personnel meet applicable security and eligibility requirements before commencing work.

---

### 🟡 ISSUE 5 — Section 6.3: "As Is" Disclaimer vs. Section 6.2 Warranty
**Risk Level: Medium | Category: Internal Inconsistency / Warranty Scope**

**The Problem:**
Section 6.2 provides a warranty that Deliverables "will not *knowingly* infringe" third-party IP rights. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties not expressly set forth in Section 6.2, including non-infringement, and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is."

- **The "knowingly" qualifier is a material limitation:** The IP non-infringement warranty is limited to knowing infringement. Unknowing infringement — which is the more common scenario in software IP disputes — is expressly not warranted. This means the warranty provides substantially less protection than its placement in the agreement implies. A client reading Section 6.2 in isolation would believe they have an IP warranty. A client reading Section 6.2 and 6.3 together would understand the warranty has a significant scienter carve-out that renders it nearly unenforceable in practice.

- **Interaction with Section 9.1(b):** The indemnification in Section 9.1(b) covers IP infringement claims without a "knowingly" qualifier. This creates an internal inconsistency: Vendor warrants only against *knowing* infringement but indemnifies against *all* third-party IP infringement claims. The indemnification is broader than the warranty, which is favorable to Client, but the inconsistency creates interpretive risk and should be resolved expressly.

**Required Fix:**
Remove the "knowingly" qualifier from Section 6.2, or add explicit language in Section 9.1(b) confirming that the indemnification obligation is not limited by the warranty standard in Section 6.2. Clarify the interaction between the warranty disclaimer in Section 6.3 and the indemnification scope in Section 9.1(b).
