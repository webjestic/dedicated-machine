**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection the four categories — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — that account for virtually every dispute of consequence in a federal contractor engagement of this type, leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary breach claims where the exposure is already limited by the nature of the damages. Three additional provisions compound this problem in ways that create independent risk requiring resolution before execution.

---

## Analysis

### 1. The Liability Cap Does Not Function as a Cap

Section 8.1 establishes a twelve-month fee cap on total cumulative liability. Section 8.2 then carves out indemnification (8.2(a)), confidentiality breaches (8.2(b)), IP infringement (8.2(c)), and gross negligence or willful misconduct (8.2(d)).

In a government contracting engagement, the disputes that generate material financial exposure are IP ownership disputes, confidentiality breaches involving government or classified-adjacent data, indemnification obligations triggered by third-party claims, and conduct-based claims that meet the gross negligence threshold. The cap as written does not govern any of those categories. What remains inside the cap — ordinary breach claims that do not touch IP, confidentiality, or indemnification, and that do not rise to gross negligence — is a narrow residual that rarely produces significant litigation in practice.

This is not a balanced mutual cap. It is a cap that applies symmetrically on paper but asymmetrically in practice, because the categories of exposure that are most likely to materialize in this engagement are the categories that have been carved out. The CFO who reads "twelve-month fee cap" and believes exposure is bounded has not read Section 8.2.

**Required change:** Negotiate hard caps — either dollar-denominated or fee-multiple — on the carve-out categories, or accept that the agreement contains no effective limitation of liability and price the engagement accordingly. At minimum, confidentiality and IP carve-outs should carry their own defined ceilings. Leaving them uncapped is not a standard commercial position in this sector; it is a vendor-favorable drafting choice that transfers unlimited downside risk to the client.

---

### 2. Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Structurally Dangerous

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." That language is not limited to negligent or wrongful performance. It reaches any performance — including performance that was contractually compliant, directed by Client, or dependent on Client-provided specifications or access.

In a federal services context, where the work product often touches sensitive systems, government data, or regulated environments, this provision could expose Vendor to indemnification obligations for claims arising from Client's own direction of the work. That is not a risk Vendor should accept without modification, and it is not a risk Client should want Vendor to accept on terms that are likely to be contested or unenforceable when a claim actually arises.

**Required change:** Section 9.1(a) should be limited to claims arising from Vendor's *negligent or wrongful* performance, and should expressly exclude claims arising from Client-directed specifications, Client-provided materials, or Client's own acts or omissions. A clean carve-out for contributory client conduct is standard and should be non-negotiable here.

---

### 3. IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment — With No Cure Mechanism

Section 4.2 conditions the IP assignment on "full payment of all fees." There is no cure period, no dispute carve-out, and no mechanism to address good-faith payment disputes. If Client withholds payment pending a legitimate dispute over deliverable quality or scope, the IP assignment fails by its terms — meaning Client is using Deliverables it does not own, which creates immediate infringement exposure.

In a government contracting context, this is not a theoretical risk. Deliverables are often integrated into federal systems before payment disputes are resolved. A payment dispute that triggers an IP ownership question in that environment is a compliance and contractual crisis, not a billing disagreement.

**Required change:** The payment condition should be restructured. Either the assignment should be unconditional with a license-back to Vendor as security for unpaid fees, or the condition should include a defined cure period and a good-faith dispute carve-out that preserves Client's ownership rights during legitimate payment disputes. The current structure creates a trap that activates at the worst possible moment.

---

### 4. Arbitration Venue Creates Practical Risk

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules. For a government contracting firm, mandatory arbitration in a fixed venue creates two concerns worth flagging before execution.

First, certain federal contract disputes may implicate regulatory or statutory frameworks — including the False Claims Act, federal procurement regulations, or agency-specific requirements — that are not well-suited to private arbitration and may require judicial resolution. A blanket arbitration clause with no carve-out for regulatory or government-mandated proceedings creates a potential conflict.

Second, Wilmington venue is vendor-favorable and imposes cost and logistical burden on Client for any dispute. This is a negotiating point, not a fatal defect, but it should be addressed.

**Required change:** Add a carve-out from mandatory arbitration for claims involving regulatory compliance, government-mandated proceedings, or injunctive relief for IP or confidentiality breaches. Negotiate venue to a jurisdiction more favorable to Client's operations, or add a provision allowing either party to seek emergency injunctive relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights.

---

### Summary of Required Changes

| Issue | Section | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Carve-outs gut the liability cap | 8.1 / 8.2 | Critical |
| Vendor indemnity scope overbroad | 9.1(a) | High |
| IP assignment conditioned on full payment without cure | 4.2 | High |
| Arbitration clause lacks regulatory carve-out | 10.2 | Moderate |

Do not execute this agreement in its current form. The liability cap, as written, does not provide the protection it represents itself as providing, and the IP assignment structure creates a compliance risk that is unacceptable in a federal services engagement.
