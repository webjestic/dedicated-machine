**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately carved out, the IP assignment is conditioned on payment, and the indemnification procedure includes a consent-to-settlement requirement. However, several provisions present unacceptable or unresolved risk for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses contain internal tensions that will fail under adversarial conditions. The agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 Critical Issues — Must Be Resolved Before Execution

---

**1. Arbitration Clause Conflicts with Federal Contractor Dispute Obligations (§ 10.2)**

The mandatory binding arbitration provision is facially incompatible with the regulatory environment in which Meridian Federal Partners operates. Federal contractors are subject to the Contract Disputes Act (41 U.S.C. §§ 7101–7109), agency-specific dispute resolution requirements, and in many cases FAR flow-down clauses that govern how disputes affecting federal contract performance must be handled. A blanket arbitration clause administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware does not account for:

- Government audit rights or agency intervention in disputes touching federal program performance
- Situations where the dispute involves classified information, CUI, or matters that cannot be disclosed in a private arbitration proceeding
- Potential conflicts with prime contract dispute clauses that Meridian may be obligated to flow down to subcontractors and vendors

**Required change:** Carve out disputes that implicate federal contract performance, classified or controlled information, or regulatory obligations from mandatory arbitration. At minimum, add a provision preserving the right to seek injunctive or emergency relief in a court of competent jurisdiction, and confirm that arbitration does not waive any rights or obligations arising under federal law or the prime contract.

---

**2. Confidentiality Term Is Inadequate for a Federal Contractor Engagement (§ 5.3)**

The five-year survival period on confidentiality obligations is a standard commercial term. It is not appropriate here. In a national security or federal services context, Meridian will routinely handle information that carries protection obligations extending well beyond five years — including Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) under 32 C.F.R. Part 2002, information subject to NIST SP 800-171 or CMMC requirements, and potentially information subject to statutory nondisclosure obligations with no fixed expiration.

A vendor agreement that lets confidentiality obligations lapse at year five creates a gap that a counterparty could exploit and that regulators will not excuse. The five-year term may also conflict with Meridian's obligations under its prime contract or applicable federal regulations, which would survive regardless — but the contractual framework should not create a lower floor than the regulatory one.

**Required change:** Either extend the confidentiality survival period to match the longest applicable regulatory or prime contract requirement, or add a carve-out providing that information subject to federal law, regulation, or prime contract obligation remains protected for as long as required by such authority, notwithstanding the five-year term.

---

**3. Subcontractor Provision Lacks Security and Compliance Controls (§ 2.3)**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to engage subcontractors with no restrictions beyond a general statement that Vendor "remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement." In a federal contractor context, this is insufficient. The provision does not:

- Require Vendor to obtain Client's prior written approval before engaging subcontractors on work touching federal systems, data, or deliverables
- Require subcontractors to be subject to background check, security clearance, or citizenship requirements where applicable
- Require subcontractors to be bound by CUI handling requirements, CMMC obligations, or equivalent security controls
- Address foreign national restrictions or ITAR/EAR compliance obligations

The practical risk is that Vendor could route sensitive work through an unapproved subcontractor with no security vetting, and Meridian's only remedy would be a breach claim after the fact — which is inadequate when the harm is disclosure of federal program information.

**Required change:** Add a provision requiring prior written Client approval for any subcontractor performing work that involves access to federal systems, CUI, or program-sensitive information. Require that all such subcontractors be bound by security, compliance, and confidentiality obligations at least as stringent as those applicable to Vendor under this Agreement and under applicable federal regulations.

---

### 🟡 Significant Issues — Require Negotiation or Clarification

---

**4. IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment — Creates Operational Risk (§ 4.2)**

The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a standard vendor-protective term, but it creates a specific operational problem in a federal contractor context: if a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy or scope — Vendor could argue that IP ownership has not transferred, potentially leaving Meridian without clear title to deliverables already integrated into federal program work.

This risk is compounded by the fact that Vendor retains undefined Pre-Existing IP that is incorporated into Deliverables, licensed back on a non-exclusive basis. If the license is tied to the same payment condition (which the agreement does not clearly address), a payment dispute could theoretically impair Meridian's right to use work product already delivered and in use.

**Required change:** Clarify that the IP assignment and Pre-Existing IP license are not subject to suspension or rescission in the event of a good-faith payment dispute. Consider adding an escrow or conditional assignment mechanism that protects both parties without creating a binary ownership cliff tied to invoice payment.

---

**5. "As Is" Disclaimer Undermines the Practical Value of Section 6.2 Warranties (§ 6.3)**

Section 6.2 provides that Vendor warrants Services will be performed professionally and that Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties not expressly set forth in Section 6.2, including non-infringement, and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is."

The inclusion of "non-infringement" in the Section 6.3 disclaimer list is in direct tension with the Section 6.2 warranty. While Section 6.3 purports to preserve Section 6.2 warranties by reference, the explicit disclaimer of non-infringement in the same sentence creates an ambiguity that a counterparty will exploit in litigation. The "as is" language further undermines the professional services warranty by importing a product-sale disclaimer framework into a services engagement where it does not cleanly apply.

**Required change:** Remove "non-infringement" from the Section 6.3 disclaimer list, as it directly contradicts the Section 6.2 warranty. Clarify that the "as is" disclaimer applies only to implied warranties and does not limit or qualify the express warranties in Section 6.2.

---

**6. Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Broad — Confirm It Covers Data Breach and Security Incidents (§ 9.1)**

Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Client for third-party claims arising out of "Vendor's performance of the Services." This is broad language and likely covers a data breach or security incident caused by Vendor. However, given the sensitivity of the engagement, the agreement should explicitly address Vendor's obligations in the event of a security incident involving Client data or federal program information — including notification obligations, cooperation with federal incident reporting requirements, and indemnification for regulatory penalties or agency-imposed costs.

The current agreement is silent on data security obligations entirely. In a CMMC or CUI-adjacent engagement, this silence is a material gap.

**Required change:** Add a data security and incident response provision requiring Vendor to maintain security controls appropriate to the sensitivity of Client data, to notify Client promptly (and within any timeframe required by federal regulation) of any actual or suspected security incident, and to cooperate with Client's incident response and federal reporting obligations. Confirm that indemnification under Section 9.1 extends to costs arising from Vendor-caused security incidents, including regulatory response costs.

---

### ⚪ Observations — No Change Required, But Note for Record

---

**7. Delaware Governing Law Is Acceptable (§ 10.1)**

Delaware governing law is commercially standard and presents no specific concern for this engagement. Note, however, that federal law will govern any dispute touching federal contract performance, regulatory compliance, or government data obligations regardless of the contractual choice-of-law provision.

**8. Termination for Convenience Is Mutual — Acceptable but Confirm Prime Contract Alignment (§ 7.2)**

Sixty-day mutual termination for convenience is reasonable. Confirm that this term is consistent with Meridian's termination rights and obligations under its prime contract, particularly if Vendor's services are on the critical path for federal deliverables.

**9. Late Payment Interest Rate Is Enforceable but Should Be Reviewed Against State Law (§ 3.2)**

1.5% per month (18% annualized) is at or near the usury ceiling in several jurisdictions. The "whichever is lower" construct provides protection, but confirm applicability under Delaware law as the governing jurisdiction.
