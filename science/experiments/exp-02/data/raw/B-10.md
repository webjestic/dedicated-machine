**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct from the cap entirely — which means the cap governs only a narrow residual category of ordinary breach claims, the disputes least likely to produce catastrophic exposure in a federal contractor engagement. In practice, the scenarios most likely to generate serious liability in this context (a data breach implicating classified or sensitive federal information, an IP dispute over deliverables, a subcontractor incident triggering indemnification) all fall outside the cap and face unlimited exposure. Three additional provisions compound this: Section 9.1 indemnifies Vendor's *performance of the Services* broadly, without limiting that obligation to third-party claims arising from Vendor's fault — meaning Client could be defending Vendor for outcomes Client did not cause; Section 4.2 conditions IP assignment on "full payment of all fees," creating a leverage mechanism where a billing dispute strips Client of ownership over work product already delivered and potentially integrated into a federal engagement; and mandatory arbitration in Wilmington under AAA rules, while not unusual commercially, requires scrutiny in a government contracting context where dispute resolution posture may be constrained by agency requirements or FAR obligations that this agreement does not acknowledge. Before execution, we need: (1) a meaningful aggregate cap applied to indemnification obligations, or at minimum a mutual cap floor that prevents asymmetric unlimited exposure; (2) the IP assignment in 4.2 restructured so that ownership vests on delivery, with payment disputes handled as a separate contractual remedy rather than a condition that unwinds title; and (3) confirmation that the arbitration clause does not conflict with any applicable federal contract requirements governing this engagement.
