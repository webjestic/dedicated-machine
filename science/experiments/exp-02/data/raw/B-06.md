**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a fees-paid cap as meaningful bilateral protection, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct — which is to say, it carves out every category of dispute that is likely to generate serious exposure in a federal contractor engagement. What remains inside the cap after those exceptions is a residual category so narrow that the cap provides no practical protection in the disputes that actually matter. Three additional provisions compound the risk: the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1 is drafted asymmetrically in ways that disadvantage Meridian, the IP assignment in Section 4.2 is conditioned on full payment and contains an embedded Pre-Existing IP carve-out with no scope limitation, and the mandatory arbitration clause in Section 10.2 requires proceedings in Wilmington under AAA Commercial Rules with no carve-out for emergency injunctive relief — a structural problem in any engagement where IP misappropriation or confidentiality breach requires immediate judicial intervention.

**Specific issues requiring resolution before execution:**

**Section 8.2 — Carve-Out Scope Renders Cap Illusory**
The exceptions to the liability cap collectively cover indemnification (Section 9), confidentiality breach (Section 5), IP infringement or misappropriation (Section 4), gross negligence, willful misconduct, and all amounts owed under Section 3. In a government contracting context, the realistic dispute universe is: a vendor breaches confidentiality involving federal data, a deliverable infringes a third-party patent, a subcontractor performs negligently on a national security engagement, or the vendor misappropriates client IP. Every one of those scenarios falls outside the cap. The 12-month fees figure that Section 8.1 advertises as the exposure ceiling is operative only for a narrow band of ordinary commercial disputes — schedule slippage, minor deliverable defects, routine breach — where the damages are unlikely to be catastrophic in the first place. Request: Either negotiate a meaningful aggregate cap that applies to the carve-out categories with a higher ceiling (e.g., 3x or 5x fees, or a fixed dollar floor), or acknowledge in writing that the cap provides no protection in the categories of dispute most likely to arise and price the engagement accordingly.

**Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Uncontrolled**
Vendor's indemnification obligation under 9.1(a) covers "Vendor's performance of the Services" without qualification — meaning any third-party claim arising from how the vendor performed is an indemnifiable event, regardless of whether Meridian contributed to the conditions giving rise to the claim. Read alongside the Section 8.2(a) carve-out, this indemnification obligation is entirely uncapped. There is no aggregate limit, no per-claim ceiling, and no proportionate fault allocation. In a subcontractor-heavy engagement (permitted under Section 2.3 with no prior approval requirement), Meridian's exposure under this clause could extend to subcontractor conduct that Meridian had no visibility into and no ability to control. Request: Add a proportionate fault allocation provision, require prior written approval for subcontractors on sensitive SOWs, and negotiate either a cap on indemnification obligations or a mutual cap structure that applies symmetrically.

**Section 4.2 and 4.3 — IP Assignment Is Conditional and License Scope Is Undefined**
The assignment of Deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." In a multi-SOW engagement, a payment dispute on one SOW could theoretically suspend or defeat the assignment of deliverables across the entire relationship. More critically, Section 4.3 grants a license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables" with no definition of what constitutes Pre-Existing IP, no disclosure obligation requiring the vendor to identify Pre-Existing IP before incorporation, and no restriction on the vendor's ability to incorporate Pre-Existing IP that is encumbered by third-party licenses or open-source obligations. In a federal contractor context where deliverables may be subject to FAR/DFARS IP clauses or government purpose rights, an undefined Pre-Existing IP carve-out is a structural defect. Request: Require vendor to disclose and obtain approval for any Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables prior to incorporation; clarify that the assignment is not defeated by a good-faith payment dispute; and confirm that no Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables is subject to open-source licenses or third-party restrictions incompatible with government use.

**Section 10.2 — Arbitration Clause Forecloses Emergency Injunctive Relief**
The dispute resolution clause requires all disputes to proceed through AAA arbitration in Wilmington with no carve-out for emergency equitable relief. In a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation scenario — both of which are carved out of the liability cap and therefore represent the highest-stakes disputes under this agreement — the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief from a court of competent jurisdiction is a material operational risk. AAA emergency arbitrator procedures exist but are slower, less predictable, and not equivalent to a temporary restraining order from a federal district court. Request: Add a standard carve-out preserving each party's right to seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute.
