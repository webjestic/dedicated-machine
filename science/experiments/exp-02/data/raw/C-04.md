## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement contains several provisions that create asymmetric or unacceptable risk exposure for Meridian Federal Partners in the context of a federal contractor engagement, most critically: a broad "as-is" disclaimer that functionally contradicts the limited warranty in Section 6.2, a liability cap structure that could leave Meridian severely underprotected on short-duration or low-value contracts, and a mandatory arbitration clause in Delaware that may conflict with federal dispute resolution requirements applicable to government-adjacent work. These issues are not cosmetic — they represent structural failure points that will activate precisely when Meridian needs protection most.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. Section 6.3 — "As-Is" Disclaimer Undermines Section 6.2 Warranty**

**The Problem:**
Section 6.2 provides that Deliverables "will not *knowingly* infringe" third-party IP rights. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties "including… non-infringement" on an "as-is" basis. These provisions are in direct conflict. A court or arbitrator interpreting this agreement could reasonably conclude that the Section 6.3 disclaimer swallows the Section 6.2 warranty — particularly because Section 6.3 is written in all-caps (the standard mechanism for enforcing warranty disclaimers under the UCC) while Section 6.2 is not.

**Why It Matters Here:**
In a federal contractor context, Deliverables may be incorporated into government systems or submitted as contract performance. An IP infringement claim against a government deliverable can trigger agency-level consequences, cure demands under the prime contract, and reputational damage that cascades beyond this agreement. A warranty that can be read out of existence is not a warranty.

**The Trap:**
The checklist marks "Representations and warranties included" as satisfied. Technically true. Functionally misleading. The warranty exists on paper and is negated in the same document.

**Required Fix:**
Section 6.3 must be amended to expressly carve out the warranties stated in Section 6.2 from the "as-is" disclaimer, mirroring the structure used in Section 8.2 for the liability cap exceptions. Suggested language:

> *"EXCEPT AS EXPRESSLY SET FORTH IN SECTION 6.2, WHICH WARRANTIES ARE NOT DISCLAIMED HEREBY…"*

---

**2. Section 8.1 — Liability Cap Is Dangerously Thin on Short or Early-Stage Contracts**

**The Problem:**
The aggregate liability cap is set at fees paid or payable in the **twelve (12) months immediately preceding the event giving rise to the claim.** On a contract that is six months old, or on a contract where invoicing is back-loaded, this cap could be a fraction of the actual harm Meridian sustains. If a critical Deliverable fails in month three of a 24-month engagement, Meridian's recovery ceiling may be negligible relative to its downstream exposure to the government client.

**Why It Matters Here:**
Government contracts frequently involve milestone-based or deliverable-triggered payment structures. Meridian may have incurred substantial obligations to its government customer — including liquidated damages, cure costs, or re-performance obligations — well before significant fees have flowed to DataStream. The cap as written rewards vendor failure early in the engagement.

**Required Fix:**
The cap should be modified to reference the **greater of** (a) fees paid in the preceding 12 months, or (b) the **total fees payable under the applicable SOW**. This ensures the cap reflects the full economic value of the engagement, not an arbitrary trailing window.

---

**3. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration Clause Creates Federal Contractor Conflict Risk**

**The Problem:**
The agreement mandates binding AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware for all disputes. In a federal contractor context, this creates two distinct risks:

- **Government contract flow-down:** If Meridian's prime contract with a federal agency contains dispute resolution clauses (e.g., FAR 52.233-1, Disputes clause), those provisions govern disputes related to government contract performance. A mandatory arbitration clause in a subcontract or vendor agreement cannot override federal statutory dispute resolution mechanisms. If a dispute with DataStream is intertwined with a government contract dispute — which is likely given the nature of the services — Meridian could find itself simultaneously bound to AAA arbitration and subject to the Contracts Disputes Act process, with no clean path through either.

- **Classified or controlled information:** If the Services involve access to CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information) or classified systems, arbitration proceedings — even under AAA rules — may be procedurally incompatible with the handling requirements for such information. There is no provision in this agreement addressing how arbitration proceedings would be conducted if sensitive government information is implicated.

**Required Fix:**
At minimum, add a carve-out preserving Meridian's right to pursue claims in federal court where required by applicable federal law or the terms of any prime contract. Strongly recommend adding a provision addressing the handling of government-sensitive information in any dispute resolution proceeding.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — High Priority Negotiation Points

---

**4. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on "Full Payment" Creates Ownership Uncertainty**

**The Problem:**
The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This means that until every invoice under the SOW is paid in full — including any disputed amounts — legal title to the Deliverables remains with DataStream. In a government contractor context, Deliverables may already have been submitted to and accepted by the government agency before all fees are settled. Meridian could be in a position where it has delivered work product to the government that it does not legally own.

**Why It Matters Here:**
Federal agencies generally require that contractors own or have sufficient rights in all deliverables submitted under a contract. A title dispute with DataStream over a Deliverable already in government hands is a compliance and reputational crisis, not just a commercial dispute.

**Required Fix:**
Negotiate for a **license-back structure** that grants Client a fully paid-up license to use Deliverables immediately upon delivery, with assignment of title upon payment. Alternatively, require that the payment condition apply only to undisputed fees, with a dispute resolution mechanism that does not suspend Client's rights to use or submit Deliverables during a fee dispute.

---

**5. Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnity for "Performance of Services" Is Overbroad in One Direction and Underprotective in Another**

**The Problem:**
Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." This is broad language — but it contains no carve-out for claims arising from Client-provided materials, specifications, or direction. If Meridian provides DataStream with a flawed specification and DataStream builds to it, a resulting third-party claim could still be characterized as "arising out of performance of the Services."

Simultaneously, the indemnity is limited to **third-party claims**. There is no first-party indemnity for direct losses Meridian sustains as a result of DataStream's breach — those are governed solely by the liability cap in Section 8.1, which, as noted above, may be inadequate.

**Required Fix:**
- Add a carve-out to Section 9.1 excluding claims arising from Client-furnished materials or specifications.
- Consider negotiating a first-party indemnity or enhanced direct damages right for specific high-risk breach scenarios (e.g., data breach, regulatory violation, failure to deliver on a government deadline).

---

**6. Section 5.3 — Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Insufficient for Federal Contractor Context**

**The Problem:**
A five-year confidentiality survival period is standard in commercial agreements. It is not adequate for a federal contractor engagement. Government contract information — including pricing, technical approaches, teaming arrangements, and program details — may remain competitively and legally sensitive well beyond five years. Certain categories of information (e.g., information subject to ITAR, EAR, or classified program details) carry indefinite or regulatory-defined protection obligations that cannot be contracted away.

**Required Fix:**
Negotiate for an indefinite survival period for Confidential Information that constitutes trade secrets, government-sensitive information, or information subject to regulatory protection requirements. At minimum, extend the general survival period to ten (10) years for this engagement type.

---

### 🟢 ACCEPTABLE — With Notation

---

**7. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Use Permitted Without Prior Approval**

DataStream may use subcontractors without Client's prior written consent, subject only to DataStream remaining responsible for their performance. In a national security context, this is worth flagging. Meridian should consider whether any SOW should include a provision requiring advance written approval for subcontractors who will access Client systems, government data, or CUI. This is not a blocker, but it should be addressed at the SOW level if not here.

---

**8. Section 8.2(e) — Fees Carved Out of Liability Cap**

The carve-out preserving DataStream's right to collect unpaid fees outside the liability cap is asymmetric — it benefits only the Vendor. Meridian's damages for breach remain capped; DataStream's fee recovery does not. This is a common vendor-favorable construct and may be acceptable given the overall structure, but Meridian should be aware of the asymmetry.
