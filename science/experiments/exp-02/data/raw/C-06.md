# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it carries **five material issues** that create unacceptable or unquantified risk for a federal contractor engagement. The most critical defects are: (1) a confidentiality survival term that is almost certainly inadequate for a national security context; (2) an IP assignment conditioned on full payment, which creates a chain-of-title vulnerability on government-deliverable work product; (3) an indemnification trigger in Section 9.1(a) that is overbroad and untethered from fault; (4) a mandatory arbitration clause that may conflict with federal dispute resolution requirements or prime contract flow-downs; and (5) the complete absence of federal contracting compliance provisions. These are not cosmetic deficiencies — each one has the potential to fail at the exact moment it is needed most.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Issue 1: Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Context

**Clause:** Section 5.3
**Text:** *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**Problem:**
A five-year survival window is a standard commercial term. It is not appropriate here. Meridian Federal Partners operates in the **national security and federal services space**, where information exchanged under this agreement may include Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), Sensitive But Unclassified (SBU) data, or information subject to regulatory regimes including NIST SP 800-171, DFARS 252.204-7012, or ITAR. None of those frameworks permit a five-year sunset on protection obligations. If this agreement governs work touching any such information category, the five-year cap creates a specific, foreseeable moment at which Vendor is contractually released from confidentiality obligations on information that may remain sensitive indefinitely.

The checklist marks confidentiality as satisfied. It is not satisfied for this engagement type.

**Required Fix:**
Carve out any information that constitutes CUI, classified information, export-controlled data, or information subject to federal regulatory protection requirements from the five-year sunset. Those categories should survive **indefinitely**, or for the duration of any applicable regulatory obligation, whichever is longer. At minimum, add: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, confidentiality obligations with respect to information subject to applicable federal law, regulation, or government contract requirement shall survive for the duration required by such law, regulation, or requirement."*

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Issue 2: IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates Chain-of-Title Risk

**Clause:** Section 4.2
**Text:** *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**Problem:**
The payment-contingent assignment is a standard vendor-protective mechanism in commercial software agreements. In a federal contracting context, it is a structural defect. If Meridian is performing under a prime contract or subcontract that requires delivery of work product to the government, the government's rights in that work product — including rights under FAR 52.227-14 or DFARS equivalents — cannot be held hostage to a private payment dispute between Meridian and its vendor. If a payment dispute arises mid-performance, Vendor could assert that title has not transferred, leaving Meridian in breach of its upstream delivery obligations with no clean title to tender.

Additionally, the phrase "full payment of all fees" is undefined in scope. Does a disputed invoice constitute non-full payment? This ambiguity is a litigation vector.

**Required Fix:**
Assignment should be **unconditional upon delivery and acceptance**, with Vendor's remedy for non-payment being a breach of contract claim for fees owed — not retention of title to government-deliverable work product. Alternatively, if a payment condition is retained, it must be narrowly scoped, time-limited, and explicitly subordinated to any upstream government contract requirements. Add a provision stating that to the extent required by any applicable prime contract or government contract clause, assignment is effective upon delivery regardless of payment status.

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Issue 3: Vendor Indemnification in Section 9.1(a) Is Untethered from Fault

**Clause:** Section 9.1(a)
**Text:** *"Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Client...from and against any and all third-party claims...arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**Problem:**
Section 9.1(a) obligates Vendor to indemnify Client for **any third-party claim arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services** — with no fault threshold. "Arising out of or related to" is among the broadest causal nexus standards in contract drafting. Read literally, this provision would require Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims that arise from Vendor's performance even where Client's own conduct, instructions, or specifications were the proximate cause of the harm. This is not a balanced provision — it is a near-strict-liability indemnity obligation on Vendor for the entire scope of its engagement.

Compare this to Section 9.1(d), which correctly limits indemnity for negligence/misconduct claims to **gross negligence or willful misconduct**. Section 9.1(a) has no such qualifier and effectively swallows that limitation.

This provision, as written, would also be difficult to enforce in many jurisdictions and may invite a challenge to the entire indemnification structure if a court finds it unconscionable or contrary to public policy.

**Required Fix:**
Section 9.1(a) must be qualified. Suggested revision: *"arising out of or related to Vendor's negligent or wrongful performance of the Services, except to the extent such claims arise from Client's acts, omissions, instructions, or specifications."* This aligns the indemnity obligation with fault and preserves Client's protection without creating an unenforceable or legally unstable provision.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Issue 4: Mandatory Arbitration Clause May Conflict with Federal Contract Requirements

**Clause:** Section 10.2
**Text:** *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration...The arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding..."*

**Problem:**
Binding arbitration with a finality clause is standard in commercial agreements. In a federal contractor context, it presents two distinct risks:

First, if Meridian's prime contract or any applicable subcontract contains a **disputes clause** (e.g., FAR 52.233-1), mandatory arbitration of disputes that touch on government contract performance may be preempted or create a conflict with the government's rights to participate in or oversee dispute resolution affecting its contracts.

Second, **"any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement"** is drafted with maximum breadth. If a dispute implicates classified information, export-controlled data, or national security equities, private AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware may not be an appropriate or legally permissible forum. There is no carve-out for disputes that cannot be resolved in a private commercial forum.

Third, the clause grants the arbitrator final and binding authority with no safety valve for disputes involving federal law, regulatory compliance, or government contract interpretation — areas where federal courts, not private arbitrators, may have exclusive or preferred jurisdiction.

**Required Fix:**
Add an explicit carve-out: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, disputes that implicate the rights or obligations of the U.S. Government, classified or export-controlled information, or that are subject to mandatory dispute resolution procedures under applicable federal law or prime contract requirements shall not be subject to arbitration and shall be resolved in accordance with applicable law."* Additionally, confirm with Meridian's contracts team whether any applicable prime contract flow-down prohibits or restricts binding arbitration.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Issue 5: Complete Absence of Federal Contracting Compliance Provisions

**Clause:** N/A — Structural Omission
**Text:** None present.

**Problem:**
This agreement contains **zero federal contracting compliance provisions**. For a vendor agreement executed by a federal contractor in the national security space, this is not a minor gap — it is a foundational omission. The following are absent and, depending on the nature of the engagement, may be legally required or operationally essential:

| Missing Provision | Relevance |
|---|---|
| FAR/DFARS flow-down clause | Required if prime contract mandates subcontractor compliance |
| DFARS 252.204-7012 (Cyber Incident Reporting) | Required for covered defense information |
| NIST SP 800-171 compliance representation | Required for CUI handling |
| Export control compliance (ITAR/EAR) | Required if deliverables involve controlled technology |
| Security clearance and personnel vetting requirements | Required for national security engagements |
| Government audit rights / records retention | May be required by FAR 52.215-2 flow-down |
| Organizational Conflict of Interest (OCI) representation | Standard in federal services engagements |
| Anti-kickback and ethics compliance | Required under FAR 52.203 series |

The agreement as presented is a standard commercial MSA. It is not a federal contractor vendor agreement. Executing it in this form creates a structural mismatch between Meridian's upstream obligations and its downstream contractual framework with DataStream.

**Required Fix:**
Add a Federal Compliance Addendum or incorporate by reference a standard flow-down clause schedule. At minimum, require Vendor to represent compliance with all applicable federal laws and regulations relevant to the Services, and to flow down any prime contract requirements communicated by Meridian in writing.
