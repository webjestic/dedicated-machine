**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability carve-outs in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the indemnification obligations are reasonably balanced, and the IP assignment mechanics in Section 4 are workable. However, several provisions present unacceptable or unresolved risk for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses contain internal tensions that will create exposure at the moment of a dispute. The agreement cannot be executed as written.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 🔴 Critical Issues — Must Be Resolved Before Execution

---

**1. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration Is Incompatible With Federal Contractor Obligations**

This is the most significant structural problem in the agreement.

Binding arbitration administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware is a standard commercial dispute resolution mechanism. It is not appropriate here. Meridian Federal Partners operates in the national security and federal services space. Disputes arising from this engagement may involve:

- Classified or controlled unclassified information (CUI) that cannot be introduced in a private arbitral forum without specific security protocols that AAA proceedings do not provide;
- Federal statutory claims (False Claims Act exposure, FAR/DFARS compliance disputes, export control violations) that are not arbitrable or that carry mandatory federal jurisdiction;
- Government-owned data or deliverables subject to federal property interests that a private arbitrator has no authority to adjudicate.

The clause as written would force Meridian into a private forum for disputes that may require federal court jurisdiction, and would do so under a "final and binding" standard that forecloses appellate review. This is not a negotiating preference — it is a structural incompatibility with the operating environment.

**Required change:** Replace Section 10.2 with a federal court jurisdiction clause (District of Delaware or the relevant district for the primary place of performance), with a limited carve-out for injunctive relief. If the counterparty insists on arbitration, require explicit exclusions for any dispute involving federal law claims, government-furnished information, or classified/CUI material, and mandate that any arbitral proceeding comply with applicable security requirements.

---

**2. Section 5.3 — Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for This Engagement**

The five-year survival period on confidentiality obligations is standard in commercial software agreements. It is not adequate here.

Federal contractor engagements routinely involve information that carries protection obligations well beyond five years — including but not limited to: proprietary program data, source selection information, personally identifiable information (PII) subject to federal privacy statutes, and technical data with export control implications under EAR or ITAR. A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means that a vendor who receives sensitive federal program information in Year 1 has zero contractual confidentiality obligation by Year 6. That is an unacceptable gap.

**Required change:** Extend the survival period to a minimum of ten (10) years for standard confidential information. For any information that constitutes CUI, classified information, export-controlled technical data, or information subject to a federal statute or regulation (HIPAA, Privacy Act, ITAR, EAR), confidentiality obligations must survive indefinitely or for the period required by applicable law, whichever is longer. This should be stated explicitly, not left to implication.

---

**3. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization Is Unrestricted and Unvetted**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors to perform any portion of the Services with no prior notice to Client, no Client approval right, and no security or eligibility requirements. In a federal contractor context, this is a significant problem.

Subcontractors performing work on federal engagements may be required to hold security clearances, comply with FAR/DFARS flow-down clauses, satisfy foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) requirements, or meet specific cybersecurity standards (e.g., CMMC). The current language allows DataStream to subcontract sensitive work to an entity that has never been vetted by Meridian, the government customer, or any applicable federal authority. Vendor's promise to remain "responsible for subcontractor performance" does not cure this — it is a remedy after the fact, not a prevention mechanism.

**Required change:** Add a provision requiring Vendor to (a) obtain Client's prior written approval before engaging any subcontractor on work performed under this Agreement; (b) represent that all subcontractors meet applicable federal security, eligibility, and compliance requirements; and (c) flow down all material obligations of this Agreement — including confidentiality, IP assignment, and compliance requirements — to each subcontractor by written agreement. Client should retain the right to reject or remove a subcontractor for cause.

---

### 🟡 Significant Issues — Require Negotiation

---

**4. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment and Contains an Unresolved Carve-Out**

The IP assignment in Section 4.2 has two problems.

First, assignment is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This means that if there is any payment dispute — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — legal title to the Deliverables remains with Vendor until the dispute is resolved. In a federal engagement where the government customer may have an interest in or right to the Deliverables, this creates a title gap that could affect contract performance, option exercise, or government audit. A payment dispute should not cloud IP title.

Second, the carve-out for "Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein" is commercially standard but operationally problematic without a disclosure requirement. Client is receiving an assignment of Deliverables that may contain embedded Vendor IP of unknown scope, licensed back under Section 4.3 on a use-restricted basis. If Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is substantial, Client's actual ownership interest in the Deliverables could be narrow. There is no obligation on Vendor to identify what Pre-Existing IP is incorporated at the time of delivery.

**Required change:** (a) Remove the payment condition from the assignment, or replace it with a security interest mechanism that does not affect title; and (b) require Vendor to identify and disclose, in each SOW or at delivery, any Pre-Existing IP incorporated into the Deliverables, with sufficient specificity for Client to assess its actual ownership interest.

---

**5. Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification Carve-Out Creates a Circular Liability Structure**

Section 8.1 caps liability at twelve months of fees. Section 8.2(a) removes the cap for indemnification obligations under Section 9. Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Client for, among other things, "Vendor's breach of this Agreement" — which would include a breach of the liability cap itself.

This creates a structural loop: if Vendor's breach of the liability cap triggers indemnification, and indemnification is itself uncapped, then the liability cap is effectively unenforceable against Vendor for any breach that can be characterized as giving rise to a third-party claim. That may be the intended result, but it should be explicit. More importantly, the same logic applies to Client's indemnification obligations, meaning Client's exposure for its own breach is also potentially uncapped. In a federal contractor context where Client may be the deeper-pocketed party, this asymmetry should be examined carefully.

**Required change:** Clarify the relationship between the breach-of-contract indemnification trigger in Section 9.1(c) and the liability cap in Section 8.1. If the intent is that direct breach claims remain capped but third-party claims arising from breach are uncapped, say so explicitly. Consider whether "breach of this Agreement" in Section 9.1(c) should be limited to breaches that give rise to third-party claims, rather than all breaches.

---

**6. Section 6.3 — "As Is" Disclaimer Partially Conflicts With Section 6.2**

Section 6.2 provides an express warranty that Deliverables "will not knowingly infringe" third-party IP rights. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties including non-infringement. The modifier "knowingly" in Section 6.2 is already a significant limitation — it means Vendor has no warranty obligation for infringement it was unaware of. The "as is" disclaimer in Section 6.3 then purports to disclaim even that limited warranty.

Courts in most jurisdictions will read the express warranty in 6.2 as surviving the general disclaimer in 6.3 under the principle that specific provisions control over general ones. But this is not guaranteed, and the tension creates litigation risk at the moment of a claim. The agreement should not require a court to resolve an internal contradiction to determine whether a warranty exists.

**Required change:** Revise Section 6.3 to explicitly state that the disclaimer does not apply to the express warranties set forth in Section 6.2. This is a drafting correction, not a substantive concession.

### 🔵 Items Requiring Clarification Before Execution

---

**7. No Federal Compliance Representations**

This agreement contains no representations or warranties regarding compliance with federal law applicable to government contractor engagements — no FAR/DFARS flow-down acknowledgment, no CMMC or cybersecurity framework representation, no export control compliance representation, and no representation regarding foreign ownership or control of Vendor. For an engagement in the national security space, the absence of these representations is a gap that cannot be filled by the general "professional manner" warranty in Section 6.2.

**Action required:** Confirm whether a separate Vendor compliance certification or federal flow-down addendum is being executed alongside this MSA. If not, these representations must be added to this agreement before execution.

---

**8. Section 3.3 — Tax Responsibility Allocation Should Be Confirmed Against Applicable Exemptions**

Client is assigned responsibility for all sales, use, and similar taxes. Federal contractors frequently operate under tax exemption frameworks that affect whether such taxes are properly chargeable. This provision should be reviewed against the specific tax status of the engagement and any applicable government contract exemptions before execution.

**Action required:** Confirm with tax counsel whether Client holds applicable exemptions that should be reflected in this provision, and whether the SOW-level fee structures are consistent with this allocation.
