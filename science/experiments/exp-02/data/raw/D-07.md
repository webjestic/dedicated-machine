**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability carve-outs in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the IP assignment mechanics are workable, and the indemnification procedures are clean. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk for a federal contractor engagement that must be resolved before execution: the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 is incompatible with likely federal contract flow-down requirements and could conflict with agency dispute resolution obligations; the confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is facially inadequate for a national security context where CUI, export-controlled, or classified-adjacent information may be involved; and the subcontractor authorization in Section 2.3, while containing a performance responsibility carve-in, lacks any security vetting, clearance, or approval requirement that a federal prime contractor would be obligated to enforce. The agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 HIGH PRIORITY — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**Section 10.2 — Binding Arbitration**

*Issue:* Mandatory AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is structurally incompatible with federal contractor obligations. If Meridian Federal Partners holds prime contracts with flow-down dispute resolution clauses — particularly under FAR Part 33 (Disputes), the Contract Disputes Act, or agency-specific supplements — those clauses will govern disputes touching federal contract performance. A blanket arbitration clause that purports to be "final and binding" creates direct conflict with those obligations and could be read to waive statutory rights Meridian cannot legally waive.

*Additional concern:* Arbitration proceedings are private. In a national security engagement, that may seem advantageous, but AAA arbitrators are not cleared personnel. If a dispute implicates sensitive program details, the arbitration forum itself becomes a security and disclosure problem.

*Required change:* At minimum, carve out disputes arising from or related to federal prime contract performance, and add explicit language preserving any rights under the Contract Disputes Act or applicable agency dispute mechanisms. Consider replacing mandatory arbitration with tiered escalation (executive negotiation → mediation → litigation in Delaware courts) or making arbitration elective rather than mandatory.

---

**Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization**

*Issue:* The agreement permits Vendor to use subcontractors freely, subject only to a performance and compliance responsibility carve-in. For a federal contractor engagement, this is insufficient on its face. There is no requirement that:

- Subcontractors be pre-approved by Client;
- Subcontractors hold applicable security clearances or be eligible to access controlled environments;
- Subcontractors be U.S. persons or entities, or comply with export control requirements (EAR/ITAR);
- Subcontractors be subject to background investigation requirements consistent with the engagement.

If Vendor places an unvetted or foreign-national subcontractor on work touching federal systems or sensitive data, Meridian bears the downstream liability under its prime contract. This clause, as written, creates that exposure without any contractual mechanism to prevent or remedy it.

*Required change:* Add a Client pre-approval requirement for subcontractors. Require that subcontractors meet all applicable security, clearance, citizenship, and export control requirements. Require Vendor to flow down all material obligations of this Agreement — including confidentiality, IP assignment, and compliance requirements — to any approved subcontractor.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Strongly Recommend Resolution

---

**Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival: Five-Year Cap**

*Issue:* A five-year post-termination confidentiality period is commercially standard for ordinary commercial engagements. It is not appropriate here. Federal contractor engagements routinely involve Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), Sensitive But Unclassified (SBU) material, proprietary program data, source selection information, or information subject to agency-specific handling requirements. Many of these carry indefinite or programmatic protection obligations that survive the underlying contract by decades, not years.

A five-year cap means that beginning in year six, Vendor's personnel are contractually free to disclose information that may still be subject to federal protection requirements. The agreement's confidentiality clause would have expired; the federal obligation would not have.

*Required change:* For information that constitutes CUI, is subject to agency-specific marking or handling requirements, or is identified as sensitive at the time of disclosure, confidentiality obligations should survive indefinitely or for the duration of any applicable federal protection requirement, whichever is longer. At minimum, add a carve-out that extends survival for any information subject to government-imposed handling requirements beyond the five-year window.

---

**Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment**

*Issue:* The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a standard vendor-protective mechanism, but it creates a specific risk in the federal context: if a payment dispute arises — or if fees are withheld pending a cure notice under Section 7.3 — Vendor retains ownership of Deliverables that may already be embedded in federal systems, submitted to a government agency, or incorporated into program deliverables under a prime contract.

The government may have independent rights to those Deliverables under the prime contract (e.g., FAR 52.227-14 data rights clauses), but Meridian's contractual position vis-à-vis Vendor would be legally ambiguous during any payment dispute period.

*Required change:* Consider adding an escrow or conditional assignment mechanism that vests ownership upon substantial completion, with a security interest or license-back to Vendor as a payment remedy — rather than a full ownership retention. Alternatively, add explicit language confirming that any government-required data rights or license grants are not affected by a payment dispute between the parties.

---

**Section 6.3 — Broad "As Is" Disclaimer**

*Issue:* The disclaimer in Section 6.3 is sweeping. Read literally, it disclaims all implied warranties — including fitness for a particular purpose — for software and deliverables that will be used in federal operational environments. If a Deliverable fails in a manner that causes program disruption, mission impact, or agency-reportable incidents, Meridian's ability to pursue Vendor beyond the narrow warranty in Section 6.2 is contractually foreclosed.

Section 6.2's warranty is limited to: (1) professional manner consistent with industry standards, and (2) no *knowing* infringement. The word "knowingly" in the IP warranty is particularly problematic — it effectively requires Meridian to prove Vendor's subjective awareness of an infringement before the warranty is triggered.

*Required change:* Remove "knowingly" from the IP non-infringement warranty in Section 6.2. Consider adding a fitness warranty for Deliverables intended for use in specific federal operational contexts identified in the SOW. At minimum, ensure the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(b) is not inadvertently narrowed by the "knowingly" qualifier in 6.2.

---

### 🔵 LOWER PRIORITY — Flag for Negotiation

---

**Section 3.2 — Late Interest at 1.5% Per Month**

1.5% per month compounds to approximately 19.6% annually. This is above the judgment rate in most jurisdictions and may be characterized as a penalty rather than a liquidated damages provision in some courts. Not a blocking issue, but worth negotiating down to a flat annual rate (e.g., prime rate plus 2%) to reduce exposure if a payment dispute is litigated.

---

**Section 10.1 — Delaware Governing Law**

Delaware is a reasonable choice for a commercial agreement and is not objectionable on its face. However, confirm that no prime contract clause requires disputes to be governed by federal law or the law of a specific jurisdiction. If the prime contract contains a choice-of-law provision, this clause should be conformed to avoid conflict.

---

**Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure: Sole Control of Defense**

The requirement that the indemnifying party have "sole control" of defense and settlement is standard, but note that in a federal contractor context, Meridian may have independent obligations to notify its contracting officer, cooperate with agency investigations, or accept agency-directed resolution of certain claims. The indemnification procedure should include a carve-out preserving Meridian's ability to comply with those obligations without triggering a breach of the cooperation requirement or being deemed to have assumed control of the defense.
