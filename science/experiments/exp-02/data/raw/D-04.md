**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it presents material risks that are unacceptable for a federal contractor engagement before they are resolved. The issues identified below range from provisions that are merely suboptimal to provisions that could create serious operational, regulatory, and legal exposure for Meridian Federal Partners. None are fatal to the deal, but several require substantive negotiation before execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### ✅ Provisions That Are Acceptable

**Section 1 — Definitions**
Clean and workable. "Deliverables," "Losses," "Services," and "SOW" are defined with sufficient precision. No issues.

**Section 3 — Fees and Payment**
Standard commercial terms. The 1.5%/month interest rate is aggressive but not unusual, and the "lower of statutory maximum" carve-out is appropriate. The tax allocation in 3.3 is correctly structured.

**Section 6.1 — Authority Representation**
Mutual and adequate.

**Section 7.3 — Cure Period**
Thirty-day cure period for material breach is standard and acceptable.

**Section 8.1 — Consequential Damages Waiver**
Mutual, clearly drafted, and appropriately scoped. The carve-outs in 8.2 are well-structured and protect the interests that matter most.

**Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure**
The notice, control, and settlement consent requirements are properly drafted. The prohibition on adverse settlements without consent is essential and present.

**Section 10.4–10.8 — Boilerplate**
Severability, waiver, amendment, counterparts, and notices are all standard and acceptable.

### ⚠️ Issues Requiring Changes

---

#### ISSUE 1 — CRITICAL
**Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Use: No Security Vetting Requirement**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**Problem:** In a federal contractor context, this provision is dangerously permissive. It allows DataStream to route work to any subcontractor without restriction, subject only to a general performance and compliance backstop. There is no requirement that subcontractors be disclosed to Client, vetted for security clearance eligibility, screened against debarment or exclusion lists (SAM.gov), or subjected to any background check or personnel security requirements. If Meridian is operating under a government contract that imposes flow-down obligations — FAR, DFARS, or agency-specific clauses — those obligations are not automatically captured by "compliance with this Agreement" because this Agreement does not incorporate them.

**Risk:** A subcontractor with foreign national personnel, a debarment history, or inadequate security posture could compromise a federal program, expose Meridian to prime contract breach, or trigger national security concerns. Meridian could bear full contractual and regulatory liability for a subcontractor it never approved and never knew about.

**Required Change:** Add a provision requiring: (a) prior written Client approval for any subcontractor performing work under a federal engagement; (b) subcontractor compliance with all applicable government security requirements, including personnel vetting and clearance requirements where applicable; (c) verification that subcontractors are not debarred, suspended, or otherwise excluded from federal contracting (SAM.gov check); and (d) explicit flow-down of all applicable FAR/DFARS clauses to subcontractors.

#### ISSUE 2 — CRITICAL
**Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival: Five-Year Cap Is Inadequate for Federal Engagements**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**Problem:** A five-year survival period is standard in commercial software agreements. It is not adequate here. Federal contractor engagements routinely involve Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), Covered Defense Information (CDI), sensitive government data, or information subject to statutory protection regimes (e.g., FISMA, ITAR, EAR, Privacy Act). Many of these frameworks impose confidentiality obligations that are indefinite or that extend well beyond five years. A contractual five-year sunset does not override a statutory or regulatory obligation, but it creates ambiguity about the parties' intent and could be used by a breaching party to argue that post-sunset disclosures are permissible under the contract.

**Risk:** If Vendor retains sensitive federal program data and discloses it after year five, Meridian's contractual remedy is extinguished even if the harm is severe. This is an unacceptable gap in a national security context.

**Required Change:** Either (a) extend the survival period to a minimum of ten years for information related to federal engagements, or (b) add a carve-out providing that, notwithstanding the five-year period, obligations with respect to classified information, CUI, or information subject to applicable law or regulation survive indefinitely or for the period required by law, whichever is longer. Also add an explicit obligation on Vendor to return or destroy federal program data upon termination, with written certification of destruction.

#### ISSUE 3 — SIGNIFICANT
**Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**Problem:** The payment condition on IP assignment creates a scenario where, if a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy or scope — Vendor retains title to Deliverables that may already be integrated into a federal system or program. In a government contracting context, Meridian may have already delivered those Deliverables to its government customer under a prime contract. If title has not transferred because of a disputed invoice, Meridian could be in breach of its prime contract obligations regarding IP ownership and data rights.

**Risk:** A vendor-initiated payment dispute, even a pretextual one, could be used to cloud title to work product already delivered to the government. This is a leverage mechanism that should not exist in this structure.

**Required Change:** Either (a) remove the payment condition and replace it with a present-tense assignment with a license-back to Vendor until payment is made, or (b) add a provision clarifying that assignment is effective upon delivery of the Deliverable and that any payment dispute is resolved through the fee recovery mechanism in Section 3, not through IP title retention. At minimum, add a provision that Vendor may not assert IP ownership against the U.S. Government or Meridian's government customer regardless of any payment dispute.

#### ISSUE 4 — SIGNIFICANT
**Section 6.3 — Broad "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts with Section 9.1 Indemnification**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**Problem:** The "as is" disclaimer in Section 6.3 is facially inconsistent with the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(a), which indemnifies Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." The disclaimer could be invoked by Vendor in a dispute to argue that it made no representations about service quality and therefore the indemnification obligation should be narrowly construed. While Section 8.2 carves indemnification out of the liability cap, it does not resolve the interpretive tension between 6.3 and 9.1.

Additionally, the "as is" disclaimer is particularly problematic in a federal context where Deliverables may be required to meet specific security, compliance, or functional standards (e.g., FedRAMP authorization, NIST SP 800-171 compliance, Section 508 accessibility). If Vendor delivers non-compliant software and invokes the "as is" disclaimer, Meridian's ability to recover may be impaired.

**Required Change:** Add an explicit representation in Section 6.2 that Deliverables will conform to the specifications set forth in the applicable SOW, and that Vendor's performance will comply with all applicable laws and regulations, including those specifically identified in each SOW. Clarify that the "as is" disclaimer in 6.3 does not limit Vendor's indemnification obligations under Section 9 or Vendor's obligation to meet SOW specifications.

#### ISSUE 5 — SIGNIFICANT
**Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration Without Carve-Out for Injunctive Relief or Government Proceedings**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**Problem:** The arbitration clause is written without exception. There is no carve-out for: (a) emergency injunctive or equitable relief (e.g., to stop an ongoing IP misappropriation or confidentiality breach); (b) disputes involving the U.S. Government or arising under federal procurement law; or (c) enforcement of security obligations. Requiring arbitration for all disputes, including those requiring immediate judicial intervention, could leave Meridian without an effective remedy in a time-sensitive situation.

Additionally, Wilmington, Delaware as the mandatory arbitration seat may be inconvenient or impractical for federal program personnel and could create logistical issues in disputes with national security dimensions.

**Required Change:** Add an explicit carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute. Consider whether arbitration is appropriate at all for disputes involving classified or sensitive government information, and if so, whether AAA Commercial Rules are adequate or whether a specialized forum is required.

#### ISSUE 6 — MODERATE
**Section 5 — No Data Security or Breach Notification Obligation**

**Problem:** The confidentiality section imposes a duty to protect confidential information but contains no affirmative obligation on Vendor to: (a) maintain specific security controls (e.g., encryption, access controls, NIST 800-171 compliance); (b) notify Client of a security incident or data breach within a defined timeframe; or (c) cooperate with Client's incident response obligations to the government.
