# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook structural problem: the cap exists on paper, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — which collectively represent the primary categories of dispute that arise in professional services engagements of this type. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure in practice. For a government contracting firm operating in the national security space, where confidentiality breaches and IP disputes carry consequences that dwarf any 12-month fee baseline, this structure provides less protection than it appears to on its face. Several additional provisions require attention before this agreement is executable.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 Critical Issues

---

**1. Section 8.2 — Carve-Outs Hollow the Cap**

This is the central structural defect.

Section 8.1 establishes what appears to be a bilateral cap: total cumulative liability not to exceed fees paid in the preceding 12 months, with a consequential damages exclusion. That framing suggests meaningful mutual protection.

Section 8.2 then removes from that cap:

- All indemnification obligations (§9)
- All confidentiality breaches (§5)
- All IP infringement or misappropriation claims (§4)
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct
- All unpaid fees

In a federal services context, run through the actual dispute scenarios this agreement is likely to generate:

| Dispute Type | Governed by Cap? |
|---|---|
| Vendor discloses classified or sensitive program data | ❌ Carve-out (b) |
| Deliverables infringe third-party IP | ❌ Carve-out (c) |
| Vendor's subcontractor causes a security incident | ❌ Carve-out (a)/(d) |
| Vendor misappropriates Client's proprietary methods | ❌ Carve-out (c) |
| Client fails to pay invoices | ❌ Carve-out (e) |
| Vendor performs substandard work, no IP/confidentiality nexus | ✅ Capped |

The cap, in practice, governs run-of-the-mill service delivery disputes — the category least likely to produce catastrophic exposure. Every scenario that could produce existential liability for a national security contractor operates outside it. This is not a balanced bilateral cap. It is a cap that protects the vendor against ordinary breach claims while leaving the client's most serious exposures unconstrained.

**Required Change:** The carve-outs in §8.2 require renegotiation. At minimum:

- Carve-out (b) for confidentiality should be replaced with a separate, negotiated sublimit — a fixed dollar amount that reflects the actual sensitivity of the information being handled, not an uncapped exposure. In national security-adjacent work, an uncapped confidentiality carve-out is not a standard commercial term. It is a blank check.
- Carve-out (c) for IP should similarly carry a sublimit, or be narrowed to third-party infringement claims only, with first-party IP disputes remaining capped.
- Carve-out (a) removing indemnification from the cap means that §9's indemnification obligations — which are themselves broad — operate with no ceiling. The indemnification obligations and the cap must be read together. If indemnification is uncapped and covers "any and all Losses" from Vendor's performance of Services, breach, or subcontractor conduct, the cap in §8.1 is functionally inapplicable to the most common vendor-side failures.

---

**2. Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad in One Direction, Then Uncapped**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." That is an extraordinarily broad trigger. "Arising out of or related to" performance of services can sweep in claims that have only a tangential connection to Vendor's work — including claims where Client's own conduct contributed substantially to the loss.

This provision, combined with the §8.2(a) carve-out removing indemnification from the liability cap, means Vendor has accepted uncapped indemnification exposure for any third-party claim with a plausible nexus to its service delivery. In a government contracting context where the downstream consequences of vendor work can involve federal agencies, classified systems, or regulated data, "any and all Losses arising out of performance of Services" is not a standard commercial indemnification. It is a risk transfer that a sophisticated vendor will either reject or price into the engagement in ways that are not visible in the fee structure.

**Required Change:** Section 9.1(a) should be narrowed to claims arising from Vendor's negligence, willful misconduct, or material breach — not from performance of Services generally. The current language creates indemnification exposure for outcomes that are not attributable to Vendor fault.

---

**3. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates a Latent Title Defect**

The assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a standard vendor-protective mechanism, but in a government contracting context it creates a material operational risk that is not adequately addressed elsewhere in the agreement.

If Client is using Deliverables under an active SOW — potentially in a federal engagement — and a payment dispute arises, the IP in those Deliverables reverts to Vendor. There is no license bridge provision that would permit Client to continue using the Deliverables during a payment dispute. The §4.3 license applies only to Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables, not to the Deliverables themselves.

The practical consequence: a billing dispute could interrupt Client's ability to use work product that is already integrated into a federal deliverable or system. In a national security context, that is not a theoretical risk.

**Required Change:** Add an interim license provision granting Client a license to use Deliverables during any good-faith payment dispute, pending resolution. Alternatively, negotiate removal of the full-payment condition and replace it with a contractual payment obligation with defined remedies for non-payment that do not include IP reversion.

### 🟡 Significant Issues

---

**4. Section 5.3 — Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for This Engagement Type**

A five-year survival period for confidentiality obligations is standard in ordinary commercial agreements. It is not adequate for a federal contractor engagement where the information being handled may include:

- Federal agency operational data
- Personally identifiable information subject to federal privacy frameworks
- Controlled unclassified information (CUI) or other regulated categories
- Proprietary program methodologies or competitive intelligence

Five years is a negotiated commercial term. It is not a legal floor. In engagements of this type, confidentiality obligations should survive either indefinitely or for a period tied to the sensitivity classification of the information — not a fixed commercial default.

**Required Change:** Extend survival period to at least ten years, or negotiate indefinite survival for information that constitutes trade secrets or that is subject to federal regulatory requirements. At minimum, carve out federally regulated data categories from the five-year limit.

---

**5. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization Without Client Approval Right**

Vendor may use subcontractors without Client consent, subject only to Vendor remaining responsible for their performance. In a federal services context, this is a significant gap. Subcontractors may require security clearances, may be subject to federal contractor vetting requirements, or may be restricted under applicable agency rules or FAR/DFARS provisions.

A blanket subcontractor authorization with no Client approval right — and no requirement that subcontractors meet the same compliance standards applicable to the prime engagement — creates both operational and regulatory exposure.

**Required Change:** Add a Client approval right for subcontractors, or at minimum require that subcontractors: (a) meet the same security and compliance requirements applicable to Vendor under the relevant SOW; (b) be disclosed to Client prior to engagement; and (c) be subject to the same confidentiality and IP obligations as Vendor under this Agreement.

---

**6. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware**

Binding arbitration is not inherently problematic. The specific concern here is the combination of: (a) AAA Commercial Rules, which involve significant filing fees and arbitrator costs; (b) a fixed venue in Wilmington, Delaware; and (c) no carve-out for emergency injunctive relief.

In a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation scenario — the exact scenarios carved out of the liability cap and therefore most likely to produce high-stakes disputes — Client may need immediate injunctive relief. The agreement as written requires that all disputes go to arbitration, with no explicit preservation of the right to seek emergency relief in a court of competent jurisdiction.

**Required Change:** Add a provision expressly preserving each party's right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction, notwithstanding the arbitration obligation. This is a standard carve-out in arbitration clauses and its absence here is a gap, not a deliberate negotiating position.

---

### 🟢 Provisions That Are Acceptable

- **Section 3** — Payment terms, late fees, and tax allocation are standard and acceptable.
- **Section 6.1** — Authority representations are mutual and standard.
- **Section 6.2** — Vendor's warranty of professional performance and non-knowing infringement is acceptable as a baseline, though "knowingly infringe" is a lower standard than Client should accept in a federal context. Consider negotiating to remove the "knowingly" qualifier.
- **Section 7.2 / 7.3** — Termination for convenience (60 days) and for cause (30-day cure) are commercially reasonable.
- **Section 9.3** — Indemnification procedure is standard and acceptable.
- **Section 10.1** — Delaware governing law is acceptable.
- **Section 10.3–10.8** — Boilerplate provisions are standard and unproblematic.
