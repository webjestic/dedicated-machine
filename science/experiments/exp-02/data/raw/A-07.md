# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook structural failure: the cap exists on paper, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, and IP — the three categories that generate the largest and most likely claims in a federal contractor engagement of this type. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach disputes that rarely produce significant exposure on their own; the disputes that actually matter in this space are all uncapped. Additionally, Section 9.1's vendor indemnification obligation is drafted broadly enough to capture Vendor's own performance failures as first-party losses recharacterized as third-party claims, and the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 locks dispute resolution in Wilmington under AAA Commercial Rules without any carve-out for emergency injunctive relief — a structural gap that becomes critical the moment a confidentiality or IP breach requires immediate court intervention.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. The Liability Cap Is Structurally Hollow — Section 8.1 / 8.2

This is the primary defect and the one I would not let pass.

Section 8.1 establishes two protections: a consequential damages waiver and a fee-based aggregate cap (12 months of fees paid or payable). Both are standard. Both are reasonable in isolation. The problem is Section 8.2.

**The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove cap protection from:**

- All indemnification obligations (8.2(a))
- All confidentiality breaches (8.2(b))
- All IP infringement and misappropriation claims (8.2(c))
- All gross negligence and willful misconduct (8.2(d))
- All unpaid fees, expenses, and taxes owed by Client (8.2(e))

In a federal contractor software services engagement, the realistic universe of high-value disputes is: a data breach implicating confidential government information, an IP ownership fight over deliverables touching national security systems, a vendor performance failure that causes downstream federal contract harm, or a subcontractor creating IP exposure. Every one of those scenarios runs through indemnification, confidentiality, or IP. Every one of them is uncapped.

What remains inside the cap — the residual category Section 8.1 actually governs — is garden-variety breach of contract that doesn't implicate those provisions. That category rarely produces catastrophic exposure on its own. The cap is protecting the disputes that don't need a cap while leaving the disputes that do need one fully exposed.

**This is not a balanced commercial agreement. It is a cap that functions as a disclaimer of protection.**

**Requested change:** Negotiate a hard aggregate cap that applies to indemnification obligations, or at minimum establish a separate, defined ceiling on indemnification exposure (e.g., 2x or 3x fees paid in the preceding 12 months). Confidentiality and IP carve-outs are more defensible given the nature of this engagement, but they should be paired with a defined sublimit rather than unlimited exposure. The current structure gives Meridian no ceiling on its worst-case scenario.

---

### 2. Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad — Section 9.1(a) and 9.1(c)

Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of:

- **(a)** Vendor's performance of the Services
- **(c)** Vendor's breach of this Agreement

The combination of 9.1(a) and 9.1(c) is broader than it appears. "Arising out of or related to Vendor's performance" is expansive causal language. In litigation, opposing counsel will argue that nearly any third-party claim with a factual nexus to Vendor's work — including claims where Client's own conduct contributed — falls within this indemnity. Paired with the uncapped indemnification carve-out in 8.2(a), this creates a scenario where Vendor's indemnification obligation is both broad in scope and unlimited in amount.

The more immediate concern: 9.1(c) covers "Vendor's breach of this Agreement" without limiting the indemnity to third-party consequences of that breach. In practice, a vendor breach that causes Client to incur costs — internal remediation, contract penalties with a federal agency, reperformance costs — may be characterized as third-party losses flowing from the breach, pulling first-party damages into the indemnification framework and bypassing the cap entirely.

**Requested change:** Narrow 9.1(a) to claims arising from Vendor's negligent or wrongful acts in performing the Services, not all performance. Add proportional fault language: indemnification obligations should be reduced to the extent the claim arises from Client's own negligence or breach. Consider whether 9.1(c) should be removed or limited to third-party claims specifically caused by Vendor's breach, not all claims related to it.

---

### 3. No Injunctive Relief Carve-Out from Mandatory Arbitration — Section 10.2

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration for "any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement" with no exceptions. There is no carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in court.

In a federal contractor context, this is not a theoretical concern. If Vendor misappropriates Client's confidential information — particularly information touching national security systems or federal program data — the remedy is immediate injunctive relief. Waiting for AAA arbitration to be initiated, a panel seated, and a hearing scheduled is not a functional remedy. The harm will have occurred. The arbitration timeline does not match the urgency of the breach.

The same logic applies to IP misappropriation. If Vendor begins using Client's deliverables or proprietary methodologies outside the scope of this Agreement, the window for effective relief is short.

**Requested change:** Add a standard carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute. This is a routine provision and should not be controversial.

---

### 4. IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment — Section 4.2

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client "subject to full payment of all fees." This is a conditional assignment. Until every invoice is paid in full, title to the Deliverables does not transfer. In a multi-SOW engagement with rolling invoices, there will almost always be some amount in dispute or pending. That means there will almost always be a period — potentially an extended one — during which Client is using Deliverables it does not legally own.

In a federal contractor context, this creates a specific problem: if Client has incorporated Deliverables into a government system or deliverable under a prime contract, and a payment dispute arises, Vendor's retained ownership interest could be asserted against the government's rights in that work. Federal Acquisition Regulation considerations around IP and data rights make this more than a commercial inconvenience.

**Requested change:** Either remove the payment condition from the assignment and address non-payment through other remedies (suspension of services, termination, breach claim), or add a license-back provision that grants Client a continuing right to use Deliverables pending resolution of any payment dispute, with assignment completing upon resolution.

---

### 5. Subcontractor Liability Pass-Through Is Unenforceable in Practice — Section 2.3

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors and states that "Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement." This is standard language. The problem is that it is not backed by any contractual mechanism.

There is no requirement that subcontractors be disclosed to Client, approved by Client, or bound by flow-down provisions equivalent to those in this Agreement — particularly with respect to confidentiality (Section 5), IP assignment (Section 4), and indemnification (Section 9). Vendor's responsibility for subcontractor compliance is a promise, not a structural guarantee.

In a national security or federal services engagement, an undisclosed subcontractor with access to sensitive systems or data who is not bound by equivalent confidentiality obligations is a material security and legal exposure.

**Requested change:** Require Vendor to (a) obtain Client's prior written approval for subcontractors who will have access to Client systems, data, or Confidential Information; (b) flow down confidentiality, IP, and security obligations to all such subcontractors; and (c) remain directly liable for any subcontractor breach as if Vendor had committed the breach itself.

| # | Section | Issue | Priority |
|---|---------|-------|----------|
| 1 | 8.1 / 8.2 | Cap is structurally hollow — carve-outs cover all material dispute categories | **Critical** |
| 2 | 9.1(a)(c) | Vendor indemnification scope overbroad; no proportional fault reduction | **High** |
| 3 | 10.2 | No injunctive relief carve-out from mandatory arbitration | **High** |
| 4 | 4.2 | Conditional IP assignment creates ownership gap during payment disputes | **High** |
| 5 | 2.3 | Subcontractor obligations unenforceable without flow-down and approval requirements | **Moderate** |

---

*This agreement should not be executed in its current form. Items 1 through 3 represent structural defects that will not protect Meridian in the disputes most likely to arise from this engagement. Items 4 and 5 are correctable with targeted redlines. Recommend returning to DataStream with a marked draft addressing all five items before execution.*
