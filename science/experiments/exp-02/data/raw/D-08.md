**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this level — the liability carve-outs in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the IP assignment is clean, and the indemnification procedure in Section 9.3 includes the critical settlement consent requirement. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk or require material revision before execution in a federal contractor context: the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 conflicts with likely prime contract flow-down requirements and forecloses judicial remedies that may be mandatory under federal law; the confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is almost certainly inadequate given the sensitivity of information exchanged in national security-adjacent engagements; and the subcontractor authorization in Section 2.3, while superficially acceptable, lacks the security vetting, clearance, and notification controls that Meridian's federal obligations will demand. These are not negotiating preferences — they are structural deficiencies that could create compliance failures upstream.

## Detailed Analysis

### ✅ Provisions That Are Acceptable

**Section 4 — Intellectual Property**
The assignment structure in Sections 4.1–4.3 is workable. Pre-existing IP is properly ring-fenced, assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on full payment (appropriate leverage for Vendor), and the license-back for incorporated Vendor IP is perpetual and royalty-free. One minor note: "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables" is a narrow use restriction that could create friction if Client needs to extract, modify, or port Vendor IP components in future development. Flag for SOW-level negotiation if scope is likely to expand.

**Section 8 — Limitation of Liability**
The 12-month fee cap is standard and the carve-outs in Section 8.2 are well-constructed. Critically, indemnification obligations, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, and gross negligence/willful misconduct are all excluded from the cap — this is the correct architecture. Many vendor-submitted agreements attempt to cap indemnification exposure, which would be a hard rejection here. This one does not. The structure is approved as written, subject to the confidentiality survival issue noted below.

**Section 9 — Indemnification**
Vendor indemnification in Section 9.1 is broad and appropriately covers subcontractor conduct under 9.1(d). The settlement consent requirement in Section 9.3 is present and correctly drafted. Client indemnification in Section 9.2 is limited to unauthorized use, breach, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — this is a reasonable and symmetrical allocation.

**Section 6 — Representations and Warranties**
The "as is" disclaimer in Section 6.3 is aggressive but not unusual for a professional services vendor. The carve-out in Section 6.2 preserves the professional workmanship standard and the non-infringement representation. Acceptable as written.

### ⚠️ Provisions Requiring Revision

---

**Issue 1 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause**
*Risk Level: HIGH*

This clause requires binding arbitration for all disputes, administered by AAA, in Wilmington, Delaware. In a federal contractor context, this is potentially unenforceable and operationally dangerous for three reasons:

1. **Prime contract flow-down.** If Meridian is operating under a federal prime contract or subcontract, certain disputes — particularly those involving government data, security incidents, or performance failures — may be subject to mandatory administrative or judicial processes under the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), FAR Part 33, or agency-specific dispute resolution frameworks. A blanket arbitration clause does not override those obligations and may create a procedural conflict that delays or complicates resolution.

2. **Classified or controlled information.** If any dispute involves CUI, classified information, or proprietary government data, AAA arbitration proceedings — which are private but not government-controlled — may not be an appropriate or permissible forum. Introducing that information into an arbitration proceeding without proper controls could itself constitute a compliance violation.

3. **Enforcement asymmetry.** Arbitration clauses systematically disadvantage the party with the stronger legal position by eliminating appellate review, limiting discovery, and compressing timelines. In a high-value dispute, Meridian should preserve the option of federal or state court litigation.

**Requested revision:** Replace mandatory arbitration with a tiered dispute resolution clause: (1) senior executive escalation for 30 days; (2) mediation if escalation fails; (3) litigation in federal or Delaware state court, with each party retaining the right to seek injunctive or emergency relief without exhausting prior tiers. If Vendor insists on arbitration, carve out any disputes involving government data, federal compliance obligations, or claims exceeding a defined threshold (e.g., $500,000).

---

**Issue 2 — Section 5.3: Confidentiality Survival Period**
*Risk Level: HIGH*

A five-year post-termination confidentiality obligation is standard in commercial agreements. It is not adequate here. In national security and federal services engagements, the information exchanged between parties — system architectures, personnel data, security protocols, government client identities, contract structures — does not lose its sensitivity on a five-year clock. Competitors, adversaries, and bad actors do not operate on commercial timelines.

More practically: if Meridian is subject to NISPOM, DFARS 252.204-7012, or any agency-specific information handling requirements, those obligations may impose indefinite or program-duration confidentiality requirements that this agreement's five-year cap would directly contradict. A vendor agreement that expires confidentiality obligations before the underlying federal obligation does is a compliance gap, not a negotiated compromise.

**Requested revision:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of: (a) ten (10) years from termination, or (b) the duration of any applicable federal regulatory or contractual obligation governing the information in question. Add explicit language that nothing in Section 5.3 limits any obligation imposed by applicable law, regulation, or government contract.

---

**Issue 3 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization**
*Risk Level: HIGH*

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors to perform "portions of the Services" with no prior notice to Client, no Client approval right, and no security or clearance requirements. Vendor's only obligation is to remain "responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."

In a federal contractor context, this is inadequate on its face. Subcontractors who access Client systems, handle government data, or perform work on federal programs may be required to hold security clearances, satisfy CMMC requirements, comply with FAR/DFARS flow-down clauses, or be vetted under agency-specific rules. A blanket subcontracting authorization with no controls does not satisfy those obligations and, more importantly, does not give Meridian the visibility it needs to manage its own compliance posture.

The "remains responsible" language does not cure this. Responsibility after a breach is not a substitute for prevention before one.

**Requested revision:** Require Vendor to: (a) obtain Client's prior written approval before engaging any subcontractor who will access Client systems, facilities, or Confidential Information; (b) represent that all such subcontractors satisfy applicable security, clearance, and regulatory requirements; (c) flow down all material obligations of this Agreement, including confidentiality, IP assignment, and compliance requirements, to each subcontractor; and (d) promptly notify Client of any subcontractor change or termination that may affect Services continuity or compliance.

---

**Issue 4 — Section 5: Confidentiality — Absence of Government Data and CUI Provisions**
*Risk Level: MEDIUM-HIGH*

Section 5 is a standard commercial confidentiality clause. It does not address Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), government-furnished information, or data subject to federal handling requirements (e.g., ITAR, EAR, HIPAA if health data is involved, or FedRAMP if cloud services are implicated). The definition of "Confidential Information" in Section 5.1 relies on designation or reasonable inference — that standard is insufficient for federally regulated data categories where handling obligations attach by operation of law, not by party designation.

**Requested revision:** Add a Section 5.4 addressing government data and regulated information: (a) define a category of "Government Data" or "Regulated Information" that includes CUI and any information subject to federal handling requirements; (b) impose handling, storage, transmission, and destruction obligations consistent with applicable federal standards; (c) require Vendor to notify Client within 24–48 hours of any actual or suspected unauthorized access to such information; and (d) clarify that Vendor's obligations with respect to Government Data are not limited by the five-year survival period or any other commercial confidentiality limitation.

---

**Issue 5 — Section 7.2: Termination for Convenience — No Wind-Down or Transition Obligation**
*Risk Level: MEDIUM*

The 60-day termination for convenience notice period is standard. What is missing is any obligation on Vendor to cooperate with transition or wind-down activities following notice of termination. In a federal services context, abrupt vendor disengagement — even with 60 days' notice — can create mission continuity failures, particularly if Vendor holds access to systems, data, or ongoing deliverables that Client cannot immediately replicate.

**Requested revision:** Add a transition assistance obligation requiring Vendor, upon receipt of termination notice, to: (a) continue performing Services at Client's request for up to 90 days at the rates set forth in the applicable SOW; (b) cooperate in good faith with Client's transition to a successor vendor; (c) deliver all work in progress, data, and documentation in a format reasonably usable by Client; and (d) not take any action that would impair Client's ability to continue operations following termination.
