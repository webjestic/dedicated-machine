# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook structural problem: the cap exists on paper, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, and IP — the three categories that generate the most significant and most likely disputes in a professional services engagement of this type. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely produce material exposure on their own. The cap does not protect Meridian where protection is actually needed. Beyond the liability structure, several additional provisions introduce risk that is unacceptable in a federal contractor context: the IP assignment is conditioned on full payment in a way that creates a gap in ownership during active performance; the confidentiality survival period is inadequate given the sensitivity of federal work; the arbitration clause strips access to federal courts and imposes a venue that may conflict with government contract obligations; and the indemnification procedure grants the vendor unilateral control over defense and settlement of claims that could directly implicate Meridian's federal relationships and security posture.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. Limitation of Liability — Section 8.1 / 8.2
**Risk Level: High**

The cap in Section 8.1 is set at fees paid or payable in the twelve months preceding the claim. In a federal services engagement, that number may be substantial, but the cap is functionally irrelevant to the disputes that will actually arise. Section 8.2 removes the cap entirely for:

- Indemnification obligations (Section 9) — which cover third-party claims arising from services performance, IP infringement, breach, and gross negligence. These are not edge cases. These are the primary vectors of commercial dispute in a professional services relationship.
- Confidentiality breaches (Section 5) — in a national security context, a confidentiality breach is not a commercial inconvenience. It is a potential federal incident. Unlimited exposure here is asymmetric and unacceptable.
- IP infringement or misappropriation (Section 8.2(c)) — given that the deliverables in this context may incorporate sensitive government-adjacent work product, this carve-out is particularly dangerous.
- Gross negligence or willful misconduct (Section 8.2(d)) — a standard carve-out, but in combination with the others, it means the cap governs only ordinary negligence and minor breach. That is not a cap. That is a footnote.
- Unpaid fees (Section 8.2(e)) — this exception runs one direction only. It protects the vendor's revenue stream from the cap while Meridian's exposure in every other direction remains unlimited.

**Required Change:** Either negotiate a meaningful aggregate cap that applies to all categories of liability, including indemnification, or negotiate sub-caps for each carve-out category with defined maximums. At minimum, confidentiality and IP carve-outs must be subject to a cap that reflects the actual risk profile of this engagement — not left open-ended. If the vendor will not accept any cap on indemnification, negotiate a mutual indemnification cap set at a multiple of contract value (e.g., 2x or 3x total fees) to create a defined ceiling.

### 2. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment — Section 4.2
**Risk Level: High**

Section 4.2 assigns deliverables to Meridian "subject to full payment of all fees." This is a payment-contingent assignment. During active performance — before all invoices are paid and cleared — Meridian does not own the deliverables. In a federal contracting context, this creates a direct problem: if Meridian is delivering work product to a government client under a prime contract, and the IP assignment has not yet vested because a payment dispute exists with the vendor, Meridian may be in breach of its government obligations regarding IP flow-down.

The vendor's remedy for non-payment should be a breach claim and a right to suspend services — not retention of IP in deliverables that may already be in the government's hands.

**Required Change:** Remove the payment condition from the IP assignment. Replace with an unconditional assignment effective upon creation or delivery, with the vendor retaining a security interest in fees owed as a separate contractual remedy. Alternatively, structure the assignment to vest upon delivery of each milestone, independent of payment status.

---

### 3. Confidentiality Survival — Section 5.3
**Risk Level: High**

Five years is a standard commercial confidentiality survival period. It is not adequate for a federal contractor engagement. Information exchanged under this agreement may include details about government systems, security architecture, personnel, or program structure. The sensitivity of that information does not expire in five years. Many government contracts impose confidentiality obligations that survive indefinitely or for the life of the classification.

A vendor agreement that terminates confidentiality obligations in five years creates a gap between what Meridian owes its government clients and what it can enforce against its vendor.

**Required Change:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of: (a) ten years from disclosure, or (b) the period required by any applicable government contract or regulation. For information that constitutes trade secrets under applicable law, obligations should survive indefinitely. Add explicit language addressing government-sensitive information and any applicable federal regulatory requirements.

---

### 4. Arbitration Clause — Section 10.2
**Risk Level: Medium-High**

The mandatory arbitration clause raises two concerns in this context.

First, arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules eliminates access to federal courts. In a government contracting dispute, there may be federal jurisdictional questions, regulatory compliance issues, or government intervention scenarios where federal court access is not optional. Binding arbitration forecloses that.

Second, AAA arbitration is private. In a dispute involving government contract performance, there may be circumstances where Meridian needs to involve government stakeholders, seek injunctive relief on an emergency basis, or litigate in a forum that can compel third-party discovery from government entities. Arbitration limits all of these.

**Required Change:** At minimum, carve out disputes involving injunctive or equitable relief, disputes implicating government contract obligations, and any matter where a government agency is a necessary party. Consider negotiating for federal court jurisdiction in the District of Delaware as an alternative to arbitration, or a mutual right to elect litigation over arbitration for disputes above a defined threshold.

---

### 5. Indemnification Procedure — Section 9.3
**Risk Level: Medium-High**

Section 9.3 grants the indemnifying party "sole control of the defense and settlement." In a standard commercial context, this is unremarkable. In a federal contractor context, it is a problem.

If a third-party claim arises from vendor performance of services that touch government systems or programs, the vendor — DataStream Solutions — would have sole control over how that claim is defended and settled. DataStream may have no security clearance, no understanding of the government relationship, no visibility into what Meridian can and cannot disclose in litigation, and no incentive to protect Meridian's ongoing federal relationships. A settlement that DataStream considers commercially reasonable may be one that Meridian cannot accept without triggering government notification obligations or violating program security requirements.

The consent provision at the end of Section 9.3 provides some protection — the indemnifying party cannot settle in a way that imposes liability or obligation on the indemnified party without consent. But it does not address settlements that damage Meridian's federal relationships without technically imposing a legal obligation, and it does not address defense strategy decisions made before any settlement offer is on the table.

**Required Change:** Add a provision requiring the indemnifying party to consult with Meridian before making any material defense or settlement decision in claims that implicate government contracts, federal programs, or Meridian's security obligations. Add explicit language that Meridian retains the right to participate in the defense at its own expense and to withhold consent to any settlement that would adversely affect its government relationships, security clearances, or regulatory standing.

---

### 6. Subcontractor Provision — Section 2.3
**Risk Level: Medium**

Section 2.3 permits vendor to use subcontractors with no prior notice to or approval from Meridian, provided the vendor "remains responsible" for subcontractor performance. In a federal services context, this is insufficient. Subcontractors accessing systems, data, or facilities related to government work may require security clearances, background checks, or compliance with specific federal regulations. Vendor responsibility for subcontractor performance does not substitute for Meridian's ability to vet who is performing work on its behalf.

**Required Change:** Add a requirement for prior written approval of subcontractors, or at minimum prior written notice with a right to object. Require that subcontractors meet the same security, compliance, and background check requirements applicable to the vendor. Add flow-down language requiring subcontractors to be bound by confidentiality, IP assignment, and compliance obligations equivalent to those in this agreement.

---

### 7. Warranty Disclaimer — Section 6.3
**Risk Level: Medium**

The "as is" disclaimer in Section 6.3 disclaims all implied warranties, including non-infringement, except as expressly stated in Section 6.2. Section 6.2 provides only that deliverables will not "knowingly" infringe third-party IP. The word "knowingly" is a meaningful limitation — it means the vendor bears no responsibility for infringement it was unaware of, even if a reasonable investigation would have revealed it. In a federal context where deliverables may be deployed in sensitive systems, an unknowing infringement is still an infringement with real consequences for Meridian.

**Required Change:** Remove "knowingly" from the IP non-infringement warranty in Section 6.2, or replace it with a knowledge qualifier that includes constructive knowledge — what the vendor knew or reasonably should have known. Consider adding a warranty that deliverables comply with applicable federal regulations and security standards relevant to the engagement.
