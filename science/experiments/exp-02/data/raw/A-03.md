# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 is structurally present but functionally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that realistically arises in a professional services engagement of this nature — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap governing only a narrow residual of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure. For a government contracting firm operating in the national security space, this structure is not a balanced commercial agreement; it is an agreement where one party believes it has capped its exposure and the other party's counsel knows otherwise. Additional issues in Sections 4, 5, 9, and 10 compound the risk in ways that require resolution before execution.

---

## Section-by-Section Analysis

---

### Section 8 — Limitation of Liability
**Status: Reject as drafted**

This is the central problem in the agreement, and it requires the most direct treatment.

**Section 8.1** establishes two protections: a consequential damages waiver (no lost profits, no loss of data, no indirect or punitive damages) and an aggregate liability cap set at fees paid or payable in the trailing twelve months. On its face, this looks like a standard, balanced commercial cap. It is not.

**Section 8.2** then removes from the cap's protection:

- **(a)** All indemnification obligations under Section 9
- **(b)** All confidentiality breaches under Section 5
- **(c)** All IP infringement or misappropriation claims
- **(d)** Gross negligence or willful misconduct
- **(e)** All unpaid fees owed by Client

Now read those five categories against the actual dispute landscape in a federal professional services engagement. IP disputes — over deliverables, over pre-existing IP incorporated into deliverables, over ownership ambiguities in Section 4 — are among the most common and most expensive conflicts in vendor relationships of this type. Confidentiality breaches, particularly in the national security context, are not theoretical; they are the scenario that keeps clients up at night and generates the largest damages claims. Indemnification obligations under Section 9 are explicitly uncapped and cover Vendor's entire performance of Services, any IP allegation, any breach of the agreement, and any gross negligence — which is itself also carved out separately under 8.2(d). Unpaid fees are removed from the cap entirely, meaning Client's payment obligations face no ceiling.

What remains inside the cap? Ordinary, non-gross-negligence service delivery failures that do not involve IP, confidentiality, or indemnification triggers. In practice, that is a narrow category of minor performance disputes. The cap protects against the disputes that do not matter. It does not protect against the disputes that do.

This is not a drafting oversight. This is a cap structured to appear protective while preserving unlimited exposure across every commercially significant risk category. For a government contractor, where a single confidentiality incident or IP dispute can generate liability that dwarfs the contract value, executing this agreement as drafted means accepting effectively uncapped exposure while believing you have a cap.

**Required changes:**
- Indemnification obligations in 8.2(a) should be subject to a separate, defined aggregate ceiling — not removed from all limitation entirely. A multiplier of contract value (e.g., 2x or 3x trailing twelve-month fees) is a standard negotiating position.
- Confidentiality in 8.2(b) should remain carved out for Client's benefit given the national security context, but Vendor's confidentiality exposure should have a defined ceiling unless Client is willing to accept asymmetric treatment here.
- IP in 8.2(c) should be narrowed. Third-party infringement claims triggering indemnification are already addressed in Section 9.1(b). A freestanding, uncapped IP carve-out creates redundant and potentially broader exposure. Define the scope or collapse it into the indemnification framework.
- 8.2(d) for gross negligence and willful misconduct is a standard and defensible carve-out. No objection.
- 8.2(e) for unpaid fees should be removed entirely or capped. Client's fee obligations are already defined in Section 3. Removing them from the liability cap creates a mechanism for Vendor to pursue fee claims without ceiling, including in disputed termination scenarios.

### Section 4 — Intellectual Property
**Status: Request clarification and revision**

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but only "subject to full payment of all fees." This is a conditional assignment. If any fee dispute exists at the time of termination or delivery — and fee disputes are common in professional services engagements — the IP assignment may not have occurred. Client could be using, deploying, or building on Deliverables it does not legally own.

In a federal contracting context, where Deliverables may be incorporated into government systems or submitted as contract performance, the downstream consequences of a defective IP assignment are severe. A prime contractor delivering work product to a federal agency that it does not own is not a theoretical problem.

Section 4.3 grants Client a "non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license" to Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables, but only "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This restriction means Client cannot extract, modify, or use that underlying IP component independently — even if the Deliverable as a whole was assigned. If the Deliverable requires modification, enhancement, or integration work that touches the underlying Vendor IP layer, Client's license may not cover it.

**Required changes:**
- Remove or restructure the "subject to full payment" condition on IP assignment, or define a mechanism (e.g., escrow, partial assignment, license bridge) that protects Client's operational continuity during a fee dispute.
- Clarify the scope of the Pre-Existing IP license to address whether it covers derivative works, modifications, and integrations, or explicitly acknowledge the limitation so Client can evaluate whether it is acceptable.
- Consider requiring Vendor to identify Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables at the time of delivery, so Client is not discovering the license limitation after the fact.

### Section 5 — Confidentiality
**Status: Requires evaluation for adequacy in context**

The five-year survival period in Section 5.3 is below the standard for national security and federal contracting engagements. Confidential Information in this context may include information that remains sensitive indefinitely — system architectures, personnel data, security protocols, federal program details. A five-year window means that Vendor's confidentiality obligations expire while the underlying information may still be operationally sensitive and potentially classified or controlled.

The definition of Confidential Information in Section 5.1 relies on a "reasonably should be understood to be confidential" standard for unmarked information. In practice, this standard generates disputes. Vendor will argue that information not marked confidential was not understood to be confidential. Client will argue the opposite. In a national security context, this ambiguity is not acceptable.

**Required changes:**
- Extend confidentiality survival to the life of the agreement plus a defined post-termination period appropriate to the sensitivity of the engagement, or negotiate indefinite survival for information that is classified, controlled, or specifically designated as requiring extended protection.
- Strengthen the definition of Confidential Information to include specific categories relevant to the engagement (government program information, security architecture, personnel data) rather than relying solely on the reasonableness standard.

### Section 9 — Indemnification
**Status: Conditionally acceptable, with noted asymmetry**

Section 9.1 provides Client with broad indemnification coverage from Vendor: performance of Services, IP infringement, breach of agreement, and gross negligence/willful misconduct. This is favorable to Client on its face.

Section 9.2 provides Vendor with indemnification from Client for unauthorized use of Deliverables, Client's breach, and Client's gross negligence/willful misconduct. This is standard.

The structural problem, as noted above, is that both parties' indemnification obligations are entirely removed from the liability cap under Section 8.2(a). This means Vendor's indemnification exposure for its own performance of Services — Section 9.1(a) — is uncapped. For a vendor, this is a significant and often unintended exposure. For Client, it is favorable in theory, but it creates a counterparty risk issue: a vendor facing uncapped indemnification exposure on a large engagement may be unable to satisfy a judgment, making the contractual protection illusory.

Section 9.3's procedure clause grants the indemnifying party "sole control of the defense and settlement." In a government contracting context, this is problematic. A vendor controlling the defense of a claim that implicates Client's federal relationships, security clearances, or agency obligations may make settlement decisions that are commercially rational for the vendor but operationally catastrophic for Client. The restriction on settlement without Client's consent is present, but sole defense control is a separate and broader concern.

**Required changes:**
- Negotiate a mutual indemnification cap as discussed in Section 8 comments above.
- Modify Section 9.3 to require Client's consent not only for settlements that impose liability or obligation on Client, but also for any settlement that affects Client's federal relationships, agency obligations, or security posture.

---

### Section 10 — General Provisions
**Status: One issue requiring attention**

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules. For a government contracting firm, mandatory arbitration with a fixed venue creates a practical problem: certain disputes involving federal contracts, government data, or national security matters may be subject to federal jurisdiction requirements, agency oversight, or dispute resolution mechanisms that conflict with or preempt private arbitration. Executing a mandatory arbitration clause without carving out disputes that implicate federal law or government contract obligations creates a potential conflict that may not be resolvable at the time of the dispute.

**Required changes:**
- Add a carve-out from mandatory arbitration for disputes that are subject to federal jurisdiction, government contract dispute resolution requirements, or that involve classified or controlled information where arbitration proceedings would be procedurally incompatible with the applicable security requirements.
