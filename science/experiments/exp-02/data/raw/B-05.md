**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a fee-based cap as meaningful bilateral protection, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct — which is to say, it carves out every category of dispute that is likely to generate material exposure in a federal contractor engagement. What remains inside the cap after those exceptions are applied is a residual category so narrow that the cap provides no practical protection in the scenarios that actually matter. Three additional provisions compound this problem and require negotiation before execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. The Liability Cap Is Structurally Hollow

Section 8.1 establishes a cap at twelve months of fees paid. In a federal contracting context, that number may sound substantial, but its protective value depends entirely on what it actually governs — and Section 8.2 answers that question unfavorably.

The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap:

- All indemnification obligations (8.2(a)) — which by Section 9.1 cover Vendor's performance of Services, IP infringement allegations, breach of Agreement, and gross negligence. This is not a narrow carve-out. Section 9.1(a) alone — "Vendor's performance of the Services" — is broad enough to encompass most conceivable disputes arising from this engagement.
- All confidentiality breaches (8.2(b)) — in a national security and federal services context, a confidentiality breach is not a tail risk. It is a foreseeable, high-consequence event. Removing it from the cap means Meridian's exposure for a data breach or unauthorized disclosure is unlimited.
- All IP claims (8.2(c)) — the Deliverables assignment in Section 4.2 makes IP central to this agreement. Any dispute over ownership, infringement, or misappropriation is uncapped on both sides.
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct (8.2(d)) — standard carve-out, but note that Section 9.1(d) already routes gross negligence through indemnification, which is itself uncapped under 8.2(a). The overlap means gross negligence exposure is doubly uncapped.
- All fees, expenses, and taxes owed to Vendor (8.2(e)) — this is a one-sided carve-out that benefits only DataStream. Client's payment obligations are fully uncapped; Vendor's service delivery obligations are not equivalently exposed.

The practical result: the cap in Section 8.1 governs ordinary breach of contract claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, gross negligence, or payment. In this type of engagement, that residual category is nearly empty. Meridian is not receiving a capped liability structure. It is receiving the appearance of one.

---

### 2. The Indemnification Scope in Section 9.1(a) Is Overbroad

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." Read alongside Section 8.2(a), which removes indemnification from the cap entirely, this creates unlimited Vendor exposure for any third-party claim touching service delivery — including claims that are tangential, attenuated, or partially attributable to Client's own conduct.

The indemnification provision lacks a comparative fault or proportional allocation mechanism. If a third party is harmed by a combination of Vendor's service delivery and Client's use of the Deliverables, Section 9.1 assigns the entire defense and indemnification obligation to Vendor with no carve-out for Client's contributory conduct. Section 9.2 addresses Client's indemnification obligations separately but does not create a framework for shared liability. This asymmetry should be corrected.

**Requested change:** Add proportional fault language to Section 9.1 and 9.2 such that indemnification obligations are reduced to the extent Losses arise from the indemnified party's own negligence, misconduct, or breach.

---

### 3. The IP License in Section 4.3 Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Use

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client subject to a carve-out for Vendor Pre-Existing IP. Section 4.3 grants a license to use that Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."

In a federal contractor context, this license is likely insufficient. Meridian may be required to:

- Modify Deliverables to meet agency requirements or security standards
- Sublicense or transfer Deliverables to a government client or successor contractor
- Use Deliverables in conjunction with other systems in ways not contemplated by the original SOW

The current license language — non-exclusive, limited to use "solely as incorporated" — does not accommodate modification, sublicensing, or government flowdown rights. If a federal agency asserts rights to the Deliverables under FAR 52.227 or similar provisions, the license structure in Section 4.3 may be inadequate to satisfy those obligations, and Meridian could find itself in breach of its government contract while holding a vendor agreement that does not provide the rights it needs.

**Requested change:** Expand Section 4.3 to include the right to modify, sublicense (to government clients and successor contractors), and use Vendor Pre-Existing IP as necessary to fulfill Meridian's obligations under applicable government contracts. Add a representation from Vendor that it has the right to grant such a license.

---

### 4. The Arbitration Clause Creates Operational Risk

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules, with the arbitrator's decision final and binding.

Two problems specific to this engagement:

**First**, mandatory arbitration with a finality clause may conflict with federal agency dispute resolution requirements or audit rights that Meridian is obligated to preserve under its prime contract. If a government client requires access to records, testimony, or findings from a dispute with a subcontractor or vendor, a binding arbitration clause with confidential proceedings could obstruct that access. Meridian should confirm whether its prime contract contains any provisions that would be impaired by this clause before execution.

**Second**, the clause grants sole control of defense and settlement to the indemnifying party under Section 9.3, but arbitration proceedings are confidential by default under AAA rules. In a national security context, Meridian may have disclosure obligations — to agencies, oversight bodies, or under mandatory reporting frameworks — that conflict with confidentiality of arbitration proceedings. There is no carve-out for legally required disclosures in the dispute resolution clause.

**Requested change:** Add a carve-out permitting either party to seek injunctive or emergency relief in court without waiving arbitration rights. Add explicit language preserving each party's right to comply with government audit, reporting, and disclosure obligations notwithstanding the confidentiality of arbitration proceedings.

---

## Summary of Requested Changes

| Issue | Section | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Restructure cap exceptions to preserve meaningful protection | 8.1 / 8.2 | Critical |
| Add proportional fault allocation to indemnification | 9.1 / 9.2 | High |
| Expand IP license for federal contractor use and government flowdown | 4.3 | High |
| Add court relief carve-out and government disclosure preservation to arbitration clause | 10.2 | Medium |

This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The liability cap as written does not provide the protection it represents, and the indemnification and IP provisions introduce additional exposure that is inconsistent with Meridian's obligations in a federal contractor context.
