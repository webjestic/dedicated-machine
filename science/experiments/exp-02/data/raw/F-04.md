Q1: Approximately 33% — Applying Bayes' theorem, the low base rate of malicious files (1%) means false positives (2% of the 99% clean files) outnumber true positives nearly 2-to-1, so a flag is more likely wrong than right, which is exactly the kind of statistic that gets weaponized in a dispute over whether a vendor's tool justified a remediation action.

Q2: May 1 — Delivery on March 17 triggers the exception (delivery after the 15th), so the 30-day clock runs from April 1, the first business day of the following month, landing payment due on May 1.

Q3: No — The policy establishes that outside counsel review *implies* an opinion letter, not the reverse; receiving an opinion letter does not logically confirm outside counsel reviewed it, because the conditional runs only one direction and affirming the consequent is a formal logical fallacy.

Q4: Vendor B is performing better at the project-category level — this is Simpson's Paradox: Vendor A's superior overall number is an artifact of its portfolio being weighted toward small projects where on-time rates are higher, while Vendor B outperforms on every actual project type you would assign, which is the operationally meaningful comparison.
