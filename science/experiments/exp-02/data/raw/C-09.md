# CONTRACT REVIEW — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Senior Contract Review**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage, but it contains several provisions that create unacceptable or unquantified risk for Meridian Federal Partners in a federal contracting context. The most critical exposures involve the absence of federal compliance obligations, an arbitration clause that may conflict with government contract flow-down requirements, and IP assignment mechanics that are conditional on full payment in a way that creates downstream federal deliverable risk. These issues are not cosmetic — they are the kind of provisions that fail at the moment of a government audit, a prime contractor dispute, or a security incident.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. No Federal Compliance or Flow-Down Obligations (Missing Provision)**

There is no clause requiring Vendor to comply with federal regulatory requirements that are standard — and often mandatory — in a government contracting supply chain. For an engagement in the national security and federal services space, the absence of the following is a material deficiency:

- **FAR/DFARS compliance** (particularly if any SOW touches defense or intelligence work)
- **CMMC / NIST SP 800-171** cybersecurity standards
- **FedRAMP** if cloud services are involved
- **Export control compliance** (EAR/ITAR) — critical if Vendor personnel or subcontractors include foreign nationals or if deliverables have dual-use characteristics
- **Section 889 (NDAA)** prohibited telecommunications equipment representations

**Risk:** Meridian Federal Partners, as a government contractor, bears flow-down compliance obligations to its prime or directly to the government. If Vendor is operating in that supply chain without contractual compliance obligations, Meridian is exposed to cure notices, contract termination, and potential False Claims Act liability that it cannot pass upstream to Vendor under this agreement as written.

**Required Fix:** Add a dedicated federal compliance clause requiring Vendor to comply with all applicable federal laws, regulations, and flow-down clauses identified in applicable SOWs, and to provide certifications upon request.

---

**2. Arbitration Clause Conflicts with Potential Government Contract Requirements (Section 10.2)**

Mandatory binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is standard in commercial agreements. It is not standard — and may be impermissible — in federal contractor engagements. Specific concerns:

- Certain federal contracts and agency relationships require disputes to be resolved in federal court or through agency-specific dispute mechanisms
- The **False Claims Act** and **Anti-Kickback Act** create federal jurisdiction that cannot be waived by private arbitration agreement
- If Meridian's prime contract contains a disputes clause (FAR 52.233-1), mandatory arbitration of subcontract disputes may conflict with the prime's dispute resolution chain
- The clause as written provides **no carve-out** for government-mandated dispute processes, regulatory investigations, or injunctive relief in federal court

**Risk:** Meridian could find itself contractually bound to arbitrate a dispute that it is legally required — or strategically compelled — to litigate in federal court, or that a government agency has an independent interest in.

**Required Fix:** Add explicit carve-outs for: (a) disputes involving government contract compliance or federal regulatory matters; (b) injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction; and (c) any dispute resolution mechanism required by Meridian's prime contract or applicable federal law.

---

**3. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates Federal Deliverable Risk (Section 4.2)**

Section 4.2 states that IP assignment is *"subject to full payment of all fees."* This is a payment-contingent assignment — meaning title to Deliverables does not transfer until Vendor has been paid in full.

In a federal contracting context, this creates a serious structural problem:

- Meridian may be required to deliver work product to the government on a schedule that does not align with Vendor's invoice cycle
- If a payment dispute arises — even a legitimate one — Vendor retains title to Deliverables that Meridian has already delivered to the government
- The government may have independent rights to that work product under FAR 52.227 (Rights in Data) or similar clauses, creating a three-way title conflict that this agreement does not address
- There is no license bridge — no provision granting Meridian a temporary right to use and deliver Deliverables while a payment dispute is pending

**Risk:** A billing dispute with Vendor could retroactively cloud title to work product already in the government's hands. This is not a theoretical risk — it is the exact scenario that creates cure notices and contract termination events.

**Required Fix:** Either (a) remove the payment contingency and replace with a contractual right for Vendor to suspend future services upon non-payment, or (b) add an explicit license-pending-assignment provision granting Meridian full rights to use and deliver Deliverables during any payment dispute, with assignment completing upon resolution.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — High Priority, Negotiate Before Execution

---

**4. Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for National Security Context (Section 5.3)**

Five-year confidentiality survival is standard in commercial agreements. It is insufficient here. In federal contracting engagements:

- Classified or sensitive government information may carry indefinite non-disclosure obligations by law
- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) under 32 CFR Part 2002 has no expiration
- Proprietary program information, source selection data, and technical data packages routinely carry protection requirements that outlast any commercial confidentiality window

**Risk:** After year five, Vendor personnel are contractually free to disclose information that Meridian may still be legally obligated to protect. The agreement as written creates a gap between Meridian's legal obligations and Vendor's contractual ones.

**Required Fix:** Extend survival to the longer of (a) ten (10) years, or (b) the period required by applicable law or Meridian's prime contract obligations. Add a separate, indefinite carve-out for any information that constitutes CUI, classified information, or is subject to statutory non-disclosure requirements.

---

**5. Vendor IP License Is Dangerously Narrow (Section 4.3)**

The license granted for Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables is limited to use *"solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."* This creates three problems:

- Meridian cannot modify, maintain, or update the Deliverables without potentially exceeding the license scope
- If the government requires modifications to the Deliverables (standard in federal IT engagements), Meridian may need rights that this license does not grant
- There is no right to sublicense to the government, which may be required under Meridian's prime contract

**Risk:** Meridian delivers a product it cannot fully support, modify, or sublicense to its government customer. Vendor holds a structural veto over the useful life of the Deliverables.

**Required Fix:** Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, maintain, support, and sublicense (to government customers only) Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables, for the duration of Meridian's government contract obligations.

---

**6. Subcontractor Provision Lacks Security and Compliance Controls (Section 2.3)**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with only a general statement that Vendor "remains responsible." In a national security context, this is insufficient:

- No requirement for Meridian's prior written approval of subcontractors
- No prohibition on foreign nationals or entities in subcontractor roles without prior clearance review
- No requirement that subcontractors be subject to equivalent federal compliance obligations
- No flow-down of security, confidentiality, or IP obligations to subcontractors by name

**Risk:** Vendor could introduce a foreign-owned subcontractor into a sensitive engagement without Meridian's knowledge or consent. Meridian bears the compliance exposure; the agreement gives it no contractual control mechanism.

**Required Fix:** Require Meridian's prior written approval for any subcontractor engaged on work under this Agreement. Require all subcontractors to execute agreements containing equivalent compliance, confidentiality, IP, and security obligations. Prohibit use of subcontractors that would violate any applicable federal security or eligibility requirements.

---

**7. Indemnification Trigger for Vendor Performance Is Overbroad Against Meridian (Section 9.1(a))**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify for claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" — without any fault qualifier. Read literally, this could capture claims arising from Services that Vendor performed correctly but that caused harm due to Meridian's specifications or government direction.

Conversely, Section 9.2 (Client indemnification) is limited to *unauthorized* use, breach, or gross negligence. The asymmetry here, while facially favorable to Meridian on indemnification scope, could be challenged as unconscionable and may not hold up if Vendor's counsel raises it at the time of a claim.

**Risk:** An overbroad indemnification trigger that a court or arbitrator narrows at the moment of enforcement is worse than a properly scoped one that holds. Do not rely on overreach as a protection strategy.

**Required Fix:** Qualify Section 9.1(a) to apply to claims arising from Vendor's negligence, willful misconduct, or breach — not mere performance. This is more defensible and more likely to be enforced as written.

---

### 🟠 MODERATE — Address in Negotiation, Lower Execution Risk if Unresolved

---

**8. No Data Security or Breach Notification Obligation (Missing Provision)**

The agreement contains no data security standards, no obligation to maintain security controls, and no breach notification requirement. In an engagement where Vendor will have access to Meridian's systems and potentially government data, this is a significant gap. At minimum, the agreement should require:

- Vendor to maintain commercially reasonable (or NIST-aligned) security controls
- Prompt notification (72 hours or less) of any actual or suspected security incident
- Cooperation with incident response and forensic investigation
- Compliance with applicable breach notification laws

---
