# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it carries **five material issues** that create unacceptable or unquantified risk for Meridian Federal Partners in a federal contractor context. The most critical problems are: (1) an IP assignment conditioned on full payment that could leave Meridian without title to Deliverables during a payment dispute; (2) a confidentiality survival period of only five years, which is dangerously inadequate given the national security nature of this engagement; and (3) a mandatory arbitration clause that may conflict with federal contract flow-down requirements and forecloses judicial remedies in circumstances where they may be required. These are not cosmetic issues — each one can fail at exactly the moment it matters most.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment (§ 4.2)

**The Clause:**
> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**

This is a payment-contingent assignment. Title to Deliverables does not vest in Meridian until **all fees are paid in full**. In a federal contractor context, this creates a cascade of serious risks:

- During any billing dispute — even a legitimate one — Meridian holds **no ownership interest** in work product it may already be using or has already delivered to a government customer.
- If Vendor files for bankruptcy before payment is complete, the Deliverables become an asset of the bankruptcy estate. Meridian becomes an unsecured creditor competing for IP it commissioned and paid for substantially.
- Government contracts frequently require the contractor (Meridian) to warrant that it owns or has rights to all work product delivered. A contingent assignment means Meridian cannot make that representation cleanly.
- The phrase "all fees" is undefined in scope — it is ambiguous whether this means fees under a single SOW, all outstanding invoices across all SOWs, or the entire contract lifetime. Any ambiguity here favors Vendor in a dispute.

**Required Fix:**

Assignment should be **unconditional upon delivery**, or at minimum vest upon **substantial payment** with a clearly defined cure mechanism. If Vendor insists on a payment condition, the agreement must include: (a) an escrow or trust mechanism for IP during disputes; (b) an interim license of sufficient scope to allow Meridian to continue performing under its government contracts; and (c) a defined, limited definition of "all fees" tied to the specific SOW, not the entire agreement.

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Confidentiality Survival Period Inadequate for National Security Context (§ 5.3)

**The Clause:**
> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**

Five years is a standard commercial survival period. It is **not appropriate for a federal contractor engagement** involving national security, sensitive government information, or classified-adjacent data. Consider:

- Many federal programs have information sensitivity that extends **decades**, not years. A five-year clock means a former vendor is legally free to disclose sensitive program information in year six.
- If Meridian's government contracts incorporate confidentiality obligations that survive indefinitely or for the life of a program, this MSA creates a **gap in the protection chain** — Meridian remains bound to the government but cannot enforce corresponding obligations against its vendor after year five.
- Trade secrets, by definition under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, are protected for as long as they remain secret. A five-year contractual cap does not eliminate legal exposure; it just eliminates the contractual remedy while other liability theories remain live.
- There is no carve-out for information that remains classified, controlled unclassified information (CUI), or subject to ongoing federal regulatory obligations (e.g., ITAR, EAR, CMMC).

**Required Fix:**

Confidentiality obligations for information that is: (a) classified or CUI; (b) subject to federal regulatory controls; or (c) designated by Meridian as program-sensitive, should **survive indefinitely or for the life of the relevant government program**, whichever is longer. At minimum, the five-year period should be extended to **ten years** for all information, with a carve-out for perpetual protection of regulated categories. The agreement should also expressly incorporate any applicable federal information handling requirements by reference.

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Mandatory Arbitration Clause Conflicts with Federal Contractor Obligations (§ 10.2)

**The Clause:**
> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration... The arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding..."*

**The Problem:**

This clause is **overbroad and potentially incompatible** with Meridian's federal contracting environment:

- Federal contracts often contain mandatory dispute resolution procedures, including Disputes clauses under the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), agency-specific procedures, or requirements to preserve judicial remedies. A blanket arbitration clause in a downstream vendor agreement may conflict with those upstream obligations.
- "Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement" is an extremely broad scope. It would capture disputes involving IP, indemnification, and confidentiality — areas where judicial remedies (injunctive relief, emergency TROs) are often the only practical remedy. An arbitrator cannot issue a TRO with the speed a federal court can.
- The clause grants the arbitrator's decision immediate finality with no meaningful appeal right. In a dispute involving government-sensitive IP or a data breach, the inability to appeal a flawed arbitration decision is a material risk.
- Wilmington, Delaware venue for arbitration may be inconvenient or impractical depending on where Meridian's operations and government program activity are located.
- There is **no carve-out for emergency injunctive relief** in federal or state court. This is a standard and essential provision in any well-drafted arbitration clause.

**Required Fix:**

At minimum, the arbitration clause must include: (a) an explicit **carve-out for emergency injunctive or equitable relief** in any court of competent jurisdiction; (b) a **carve-out for disputes that are subject to mandatory federal dispute resolution procedures**; (c) a **limited appeal right** for awards exceeding a defined threshold; and (d) a venue provision that is operationally workable. Meridian should also evaluate whether a **litigation-first structure** with optional arbitration is more appropriate for this engagement type.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Vendor IP License Scope Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Use (§ 4.3)

**The Clause:**
> *"...Vendor grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license to use such Vendor Pre-Existing IP **solely as incorporated in the Deliverables**."*

**The Problem:**

The license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP is restricted to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This is a **narrower grant than Meridian likely requires** in practice:

- Federal contracts frequently require contractors to grant the government a license to use, modify, and reproduce software and related IP. If Vendor Pre-Existing IP is embedded in Deliverables that Meridian delivers to the government, the government's rights may exceed what Meridian itself holds — creating a rights gap that exposes Meridian to breach of its government contract.
- The restriction to use "as incorporated" means Meridian cannot extract, modify, or separately deploy Vendor Pre-Existing IP even if operationally necessary to maintain or update the Deliverables after the engagement ends.
- There is no **government purpose license** or flow-down provision, which is standard in federal contractor vendor agreements.
- The license is **non-exclusive**, meaning Vendor can license the same Pre-Existing IP to Meridian's competitors, including competitors on the same federal program.

**Required Fix:**

The license should be expanded to include: (a) the right to **modify, maintain, and create derivative works** of Vendor Pre-Existing IP as necessary to support the Deliverables; (b) an explicit **government purpose license** permitting Meridian to flow down necessary rights to the government customer consistent with applicable federal acquisition regulations; and (c) a **non-compete or exclusivity provision** for the specific program or agency, if operationally feasible.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Vendor Indemnification Scope Requires Tightening (§ 9.1)

**The Clause:**
> *"Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Client... arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**The Problem:**

Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Meridian for claims arising from "Vendor's performance of the Services." Read in conjunction with Section 9.2, which carves out Client's unauthorized use, there is a **gap in coverage**:

- There is no explicit indemnification for **data breaches or security incidents** caused by Vendor or its subcontractors. In a federal contractor engagement involving sensitive government data, a vendor-caused data breach is one of the highest-probability, highest-consequence risk events. The current language may or may not capture this depending on how a court or arbitrator characterizes the claim — that ambiguity is unacceptable.
- Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors and states Vendor "remains responsible" for them, but the indemnification in 9.1(d) covers gross negligence or willful misconduct of subcontractors — it does **not** cover ordinary negligence by subcontractors. This creates a coverage gap for subcontractor failures that fall short of gross negligence but still cause material harm.
- There is no indemnification for **regulatory violations** by Vendor (e.g., CMMC non-compliance, ITAR violations, CUI mishandling) that expose Meridian to government enforcement action.

**Required Fix:**

Section 9.1 should be amended to explicitly include: (a) **data breaches and security incidents** caused by Vendor or its subcontractors; (b) **ordinary negligence** of subcontractors, consistent with Vendor's assumption of responsibility under Section 2.3; and (c) **regulatory violations** by Vendor that result in government enforcement action or penalties against Meridian.
