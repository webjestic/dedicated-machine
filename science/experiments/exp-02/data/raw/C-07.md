# CONTRACT REVIEW — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by:** Senior Corporate Counsel, obotix.one
**Classification:** Pre-Execution Review

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and contains several well-drafted provisions — the IP assignment framework, the carve-out structure in Section 8.2, and the indemnification procedure in Section 9.3 are all above average for a commercial MSA. However, for a federal contractor engagement in the national security space, this agreement carries **four material deficiencies** that create unacceptable exposure before a single SOW is executed. The most critical issue is the complete absence of federal compliance and security obligations — a structural omission that cannot be papered over by SOW language alone. The agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Federal Compliance and Security Obligations: Entirely Absent

**The single most disqualifying deficiency in this agreement.**

This MSA contains **zero provisions** addressing federal regulatory compliance, data security, or national security obligations. For a government contracting firm operating in the federal services and national security space, this is not a gap — it is a structural failure.

**What is missing:**

- No reference to **FAR/DFARS** flow-down obligations, which are legally required to be passed to vendors when federal contract requirements are implicated
- No **CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information)** handling requirements or acknowledgment that CUI may be encountered
- No **CMMC (Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification)** compliance obligation or representation — mandatory for DoD-adjacent work
- No **NIST SP 800-171** or **800-53** security controls requirement
- No **FedRAMP** authorization requirement for any cloud-based tooling Vendor may deploy
- No **security incident notification** obligation — if Vendor suffers a breach involving Client data, there is currently no contractual obligation to notify Client within any defined window
- No **personnel security** requirements (background checks, clearance verification, foreign national restrictions)
- No prohibition on **data residency** violations or offshore processing of sensitive federal data

**Why this matters at the contract level, not the SOW level:**

The checklist marks this agreement as suitable for federal contractor engagement. If federal compliance obligations exist only at the SOW level — or not at all — Meridian Federal Partners faces the following exposure:

1. **Prime contractor liability** for subcontractor non-compliance with FAR/DFARS flow-downs that were never contractually imposed
2. **Regulatory enforcement risk** if CUI is mishandled by a vendor who was never told it was CUI
3. **Contract termination for default** on the prime contract due to vendor non-compliance that this MSA did nothing to prevent or require

**Required fix:** Add a dedicated federal compliance section establishing that Vendor shall comply with all applicable federal laws, regulations, and contract flow-down requirements identified in each SOW; that Vendor shall maintain required security certifications; that Vendor shall notify Client of any security incident within 72 hours of discovery; and that Vendor personnel accessing federal systems or sensitive data shall meet applicable clearance and background check requirements.

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for This Engagement

**Section 5.3** provides a five-year survival period for confidentiality obligations post-termination. The checklist treats this as a completed item. It is not adequate here.

**The problem:**

In national security and federal services engagements, confidential information routinely includes:

- Classified program structures and acquisition strategies
- Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and Protected Health Information (PHI) subject to statutory protection periods exceeding five years
- Trade secrets with indefinite protection under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA)
- Information subject to federal records retention requirements that may extend 10–20+ years

A five-year cliff creates a scenario where a vendor is legally free to disclose sensitive federal program information in year six. That is not a theoretical risk — it is a foreseeable one.

**Required fix:** Carve out trade secrets for indefinite protection (consistent with DTSA), extend the general survival period to a minimum of ten years for this class of engagement, and add explicit language that nothing in Section 5.3 limits any protection period required by applicable federal law or regulation.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates Operational Risk

**Section 4.2** states: *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables."*

The payment condition on IP assignment is a standard vendor-favorable construct. In a commercial SaaS or marketing context, it is unremarkable. In a federal contractor engagement, it is a trap.

**The problem:**

If a payment dispute arises — even a legitimate one, such as a disputed invoice or a government-side funding delay — the IP assignment is suspended. During that window:

- Deliverables already integrated into federal systems may be legally owned by the Vendor
- Client cannot grant the government the IP rights the prime contract likely requires it to deliver
- The government may have a claim against Meridian for failure to deliver clear title to work product it has already paid for at the prime level

This is compounded by the fact that Section 8.2(e) carves out fee obligations from the liability cap — meaning Vendor can pursue uncapped recovery for unpaid fees while simultaneously holding IP hostage.

**Required fix:** Decouple the IP assignment from the payment condition. IP should vest in Client upon creation or delivery. Vendor's remedy for non-payment should be a breach of contract claim — not a lien on federal work product. Alternatively, negotiate a mechanic where IP vests immediately but reverts only upon a final, uncured payment default following dispute resolution.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Arbitration Clause Is Incompatible with Federal Dispute Frameworks

**Section 10.2** mandates binding arbitration under AAA Commercial Rules in Wilmington, Delaware, with no exceptions.

**The problem:**

Federal contractor disputes — particularly those involving government-directed work, cost allowability, or compliance with federal regulations — may be subject to mandatory federal dispute resolution mechanisms including:

- The **Contract Disputes Act (CDA)**, which governs disputes on federal prime contracts and may impose obligations that flow to subcontractors
- **Agency-specific dispute resolution** requirements embedded in the prime contract
- **False Claims Act** exposure, which cannot be arbitrated away

A blanket mandatory arbitration clause with no federal law carve-out creates a direct conflict with these frameworks. If a dispute arises that implicates the prime contract or federal regulatory compliance, Meridian may be contractually committed to a forum that cannot provide the relief available — or may be required — under federal law.

Additionally, arbitration confidentiality could conflict with federal transparency and audit obligations.

**Required fix:** Add a carve-out from mandatory arbitration for: (a) disputes arising under or implicating federal law or the prime contract; (b) injunctive or emergency relief; and (c) any matter subject to mandatory federal dispute resolution. Retain arbitration as the default for purely commercial disputes between the parties.

---

### 🟠 MODERATE — Subcontractor Provision Lacks Security and Compliance Controls

**Section 2.3** permits Vendor to use subcontractors with only a general statement that Vendor "remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance."

**The problem:**

In a federal services context, this is insufficient. Vendor's general responsibility for subcontractor compliance means nothing if the subcontractors themselves are never required to meet federal security, clearance, or compliance standards. Meridian has no visibility into who is actually performing work, no right to approve or reject subcontractors, and no assurance that subcontractors are not foreign nationals, foreign-owned entities, or otherwise disqualified from accessing federal program information.

**Required fix:** Add Client approval rights for subcontractors performing work on federal engagements; require subcontractors to be bound by confidentiality, security, and compliance obligations at least as protective as those in this Agreement; prohibit use of subcontractors that are foreign-owned or controlled without prior written Client consent; and require Vendor to flow down all applicable federal compliance requirements to subcontractors.

---

### 🟢 PROVISIONS THAT ARE WELL-DRAFTED (No Changes Required)

The following provisions are noted as above-standard and should be preserved in negotiation:

| Section | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **8.2 — Liability Carve-Outs** | Comprehensive. Correctly excludes indemnification, confidentiality breach, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and fee obligations from the cap. This is better than most commercial MSAs. |
| **9.3 — Indemnification Procedure** | Clean and enforceable. The prohibition on settlement without indemnified party consent is correctly drafted. |
| **4.1/4.3 — Pre-Existing IP / License Back** | Appropriately structured. The perpetual, royalty-free license to incorporated Vendor IP is the correct construct. |
| **7.3 — Material Breach / Cure Period** | 30-day cure with immediate termination right upon failure is standard and appropriate. |
| **6.2 — IP Non-Infringement Warranty** | "Knowingly infringe" qualifier is vendor-favorable but acceptable given the carve-out in 8.2(c). |

## Summary of Required Changes

| Priority | Section | Issue |
|---|---|---|
| 🔴 Critical | New Section | Add federal compliance, CUI, CMMC, incident notification, and personnel security obligations |
| 🔴 Critical | §5.3 | Extend confidentiality survival; add trade secret carve-out for indefinite protection |
| 🟡 Significant | §4.2 | Decouple IP assignment from payment condition |
| 🟡 Significant | §10.2 | Add federal law and prime contract carve-out from mandatory arbitration |
| 🟠 Moderate | §2.3 | Add subcontractor approval rights, flow-down obligations, and foreign entity restrictions |

---

**Bottom line:** This agreement was drafted for a standard commercial software engagement. It was not drafted for a federal contractor. The gap between those two contexts is where Meridian's exposure lives. Do not execute without the changes above.
