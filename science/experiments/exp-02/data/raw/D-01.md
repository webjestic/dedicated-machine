**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability cap exceptions are appropriately carved out, IP assignment is conditioned on payment, and the indemnification procedure includes a consent-to-settlement requirement. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable or unresolved risk for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses contain internal tensions that will not survive a dispute. The agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 🔴 Critical Issues — Must Be Resolved Before Execution

---

**1. Mandatory Arbitration Clause (§ 10.2) — Incompatible with Federal Contracting Obligations**

The binding arbitration clause as drafted is overbroad and potentially unenforceable or operationally disqualifying in a federal contractor context.

**The problem:** Federal procurement law — including the False Claims Act, the Contract Disputes Act, and FAR-incorporated dispute resolution mechanisms — may govern disputes arising from work performed under or in support of a federal prime contract. Mandatory private arbitration cannot displace those statutory frameworks. If Meridian Federal Partners is a prime contractor or subcontractor on a federal award, and a dispute arises from work product delivered under this MSA, a court may void the arbitration clause entirely, or worse, Meridian may find itself in an inconsistent posture: bound to arbitrate with DataStream while simultaneously subject to a federal forum for the underlying contract dispute.

Additionally, the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for FY2010 (§ 844) and subsequent iterations restrict mandatory arbitration clauses in certain defense contracts. If this MSA supports a DoD engagement — which is plausible given the national security context — the clause may be facially non-compliant.

**Required fix:** Add a carve-out preserving each party's right to pursue claims in any forum required by applicable federal law, regulation, or prime contract terms. Consider replacing mandatory arbitration with a tiered dispute resolution clause (escalation → mediation → litigation) or making arbitration elective rather than mandatory. At minimum, add: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, nothing in this Section shall limit either party's rights under applicable federal law or the terms of any applicable prime contract or government contract."*

---

**2. Confidentiality Survival Period (§ 5.3) — Inadequate for National Security Engagements**

A five-year survival period for confidentiality obligations is standard in commercial SaaS or professional services agreements. It is not adequate here.

**The problem:** In a federal contractor context, Confidential Information exchanged under this MSA may include controlled unclassified information (CUI), export-controlled technical data (EAR/ITAR), personally identifiable information subject to Privacy Act obligations, or information bearing on national security programs. The legal obligation to protect such information does not expire in five years — in many cases it does not expire at all. A vendor who receives CUI or ITAR-controlled data under this MSA and then treats its confidentiality obligations as lapsed in year six is not protected by this agreement, and neither is Meridian.

**Required fix:** For information that constitutes CUI, classified information, export-controlled data, or information subject to a specific regulatory protection regime, confidentiality obligations must survive for the full period required by applicable law or regulation — not a fixed commercial term. Add: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, confidentiality obligations with respect to information subject to applicable federal law, regulation, or government contract requirements (including CUI, export-controlled information, or classified information) shall survive for the period required by such law, regulation, or contract, which may be indefinite."*

---

**3. Subcontractor Flow-Down (§ 2.3) — Dangerously Underspecified**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors and states that Vendor "remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement." This is insufficient.

**The problem:** In a federal contractor engagement, subcontractors may be subject to independent regulatory obligations — security clearance requirements, CMMC compliance, FAR/DFARS clause flow-down, background check requirements, citizenship restrictions, or facility clearance mandates. A generic statement that the vendor is "responsible" for subcontractor compliance does not ensure those obligations are actually flowed down, does not give Meridian visibility into who is performing the work, and does not allow Meridian to object to a subcontractor that is ineligible, foreign-owned, or otherwise disqualifying.

If a subcontractor accesses federal systems or handles sensitive data without proper authorization, Meridian — as the contracting party — bears the regulatory and contractual exposure. DataStream's general responsibility promise does not transfer that risk.

**Required fix:** Add provisions requiring: (a) prior written Client approval for subcontractors who will access Client systems, data, or facilities; (b) mandatory flow-down of all applicable regulatory and contractual obligations (including security, clearance, and compliance requirements); (c) Client's right to reject or remove a subcontractor for cause; and (d) Vendor's obligation to ensure subcontractors are bound by written agreements at least as protective as this MSA.

### 🟡 Significant Issues — Require Negotiation or Clarification

---

**4. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment (§ 4.2) — Creates Operational Risk**

The IP assignment is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." Until that condition is satisfied, ownership of the Deliverables remains with Vendor.

**The problem:** This is a standard vendor-favorable construct, but it creates a specific risk in a government contracting context. If Meridian delivers a Deliverable to a federal agency under a prime contract before all invoices are settled — which is operationally common — and a payment dispute subsequently arises, Meridian may be in the position of having delivered work product to the government that it does not legally own. The government's rights in that work product, and Meridian's ability to grant those rights, would be legally impaired.

**Required fix:** Negotiate for IP assignment to occur upon delivery and acceptance of each Deliverable, with payment obligations remaining as an independent contractual remedy (i.e., Vendor can sue for unpaid fees but cannot claw back IP already delivered). Alternatively, add a provision that Vendor's IP rights do not impair the government's rights in Deliverables delivered under a federal contract, consistent with FAR 52.227 requirements.

---

**5. "As Is" Disclaimer (§ 6.3) — Internally Inconsistent and Potentially Misleading**

Section 6.3 disclaims all warranties and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is." Section 6.2 provides an express warranty of professional performance and non-infringement. Section 8.2 carves out IP infringement from the liability cap.

**The problem:** The "as is" language in 6.3 is in direct tension with the express warranties in 6.2. A court interpreting this agreement will likely find that 6.3 cannot disclaim the warranties expressly given in 6.2 — but the drafting creates ambiguity that will be litigated. More importantly, the "as is" disclaimer, if read broadly, could be used by Vendor to argue that performance failures are not actionable — which directly undermines Meridian's ability to enforce delivery standards under any SOW.

**Required fix:** Revise 6.3 to read: *"Except as expressly set forth in Section 6.2, Vendor makes no additional warranties..."* — and remove the "as is" language entirely or limit it explicitly to third-party components not within Vendor's control. The current drafting is sloppy and will not hold up.

---

**6. Limitation of Liability Cap — 12-Month Lookback Is Inadequate for Long-Term Engagements**

The liability cap is set at fees paid in the twelve months preceding the claim.

**The problem:** In a multi-year engagement with back-loaded fee structures, early-phase work (architecture, system design, security configuration) may generate the highest-consequence failures while the fee base is lowest. A vendor who delivers a defective security architecture in month three of a five-year engagement is capped at three months of fees — which may be a fraction of the actual harm. For engagements involving national security systems or sensitive federal data, this cap may be structurally inadequate.

**Required fix:** Negotiate for a higher cap floor — either a fixed dollar minimum or a cap tied to total contract value rather than a rolling 12-month window. Consider a tiered cap structure: standard claims capped at 12-month fees; claims involving data breach, security failure, or regulatory non-compliance capped at a higher amount or uncapped.

---

**7. No Federal Compliance Representations (§ 6) — Material Gap**

The representations and warranties section contains no federal-specific compliance representations.

**The problem:** This agreement will support work performed in a federal contractor environment. There are no representations from Vendor regarding: CMMC compliance or cybersecurity posture; compliance with NIST SP 800-171 or applicable security frameworks; absence of foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) issues; compliance with export control laws (EAR/ITAR); or eligibility to receive or handle CUI. These are not aspirational — they are threshold requirements for performing work in this space. Their absence means Meridian has no contractual basis to terminate or seek indemnification if Vendor turns out to be non-compliant with a requirement that was never documented.

**Required fix:** Add a Section 6.4 with federal contractor-specific representations, including cybersecurity compliance, FOCI disclosure, export control eligibility, and agreement to comply with applicable FAR/DFARS clauses as directed by Client.

---

### 🟢 Provisions That Are Acceptable As Drafted

- **§ 8.2 Carve-outs:** The liability cap exceptions are well-structured. Carving out indemnification, confidentiality breach, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and fee obligations is appropriate and reflects competent drafting.
- **§ 9.3 Indemnification Procedure:** The consent-to-settlement requirement is present and properly placed. This is often missing in vendor-submitted agreements and its inclusion is noted.
- **§ 4.1 / 4.3 Pre-Existing IP:** The pre-existing IP carve-out with a perpetual, royalty-free license back is a reasonable construct and does not create unacceptable risk as drafted.
- **§ 7.3 Cure Period:** Thirty-day cure right before termination for cause is standard and acceptable.
- **§ 10.4 / 10.5 Amendment and Waiver:** Both are properly drafted.
