**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove indemnification, confidentiality, and IP from the cap entirely — which means the cap governs only the residual category of ordinary service delivery disputes, while every high-probability, high-severity exposure in a federal contractor engagement runs unlimited. Three additional provisions compound this problem in ways that are unacceptable before execution.

---

## Analysis

### The Liability Cap Does Not Protect Where Protection Is Needed

Section 8.1 establishes a cap at twelve months of fees paid. In a federal contracting context, that number may sound meaningful — but the question is never the size of the cap. The question is what the cap actually covers.

Section 8.2 removes from the cap:

- All indemnification obligations (Section 9)
- All confidentiality breaches (Section 5)
- All IP infringement or misappropriation claims
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct
- All unpaid fees

Now ask what remains inside the cap. Ordinary breach of contract claims for deficient service delivery. That is the residual category. In practice, that is rarely where the money is.

In a national security or federal services engagement, the disputes that generate material exposure are almost always IP ownership conflicts, confidentiality failures involving government-sensitive data, and indemnification triggers arising from third-party claims. All three are explicitly carved out. The cap does not apply to any of them. What Meridian Federal Partners is being offered is a cap that governs the least dangerous category of dispute while leaving unlimited exposure in every category that actually matters.

This is not a balanced commercial agreement. It is an agreement that uses the appearance of a cap to create the impression of mutual protection while preserving uncapped exposure in the directions that count.

---

### Specific Provisions Requiring Changes

**1. Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification Carve-Out Is Unlimited and Unbounded**

Removing indemnification from the cap entirely means that any third-party claim triggering Section 9 carries no ceiling on Meridian's exposure. Section 9.1 is drafted broadly — Vendor's indemnification obligation covers performance of Services, IP infringement allegations, breach of the Agreement, and gross negligence. That is nearly the entire agreement. If a third party brings a claim arising from Vendor's work on a federal program, Meridian's defense and indemnification costs are uncapped. There is no floor, no ceiling, no proportionality mechanism.

**Requested change:** Negotiate a separate aggregate cap on indemnification obligations, or at minimum require that indemnification exposure be capped at a multiplier of contract value (e.g., two to three times total fees paid), with carve-outs only for willful misconduct and fraud.

---

**2. Section 8.2(c) — IP Carve-Out Is Drafted Too Broadly**

The IP carve-out removes the cap for "infringement, misappropriation, or violation of either party's Intellectual Property Rights." This is not limited to third-party claims. It covers direct claims between the parties. Combined with the Pre-Existing IP structure in Section 4 — where Vendor retains ownership of Pre-Existing IP and grants only a license — there is a scenario in which Vendor asserts that Meridian's use of the Deliverables exceeds the scope of the license, characterizes that as an IP violation, and pursues uncapped damages. That is not a theoretical risk in a federal contracting environment where deliverables often incorporate vendor tooling, frameworks, or proprietary methodologies.

**Requested change:** Limit the IP carve-out to third-party infringement claims only, consistent with the indemnification structure in Section 9.1(b). Direct IP disputes between the parties should remain subject to the cap, or be subject to a separately negotiated ceiling.

---

**3. Section 8.2(e) — Unpaid Fees Are Carved Out of the Cap**

This carve-out exempts from the cap all amounts owed by Client for fees, expenses, and taxes under Section 3. The cap is mutual in its framing, but this exception runs only one direction — it protects Vendor's right to collect fees without limitation while Meridian's claims against Vendor for service failures remain capped. The asymmetry is structural. Meridian can owe Vendor unlimited amounts; Vendor's liability for deficient performance is capped at twelve months of fees. That is not a mutual cap. It is a cap on one party's exposure only.

**Requested change:** Either remove Section 8.2(e) and allow fee disputes to be governed by the cap like any other contractual obligation, or make the carve-out explicitly bilateral by adding a corresponding carve-out for Client's direct damages arising from material service delivery failures.

---

**4. Section 4.3 — License Scope Creates Operational Risk**

The license granted for Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." In a federal contracting context, Meridian may need to modify, extend, or integrate Deliverables into other systems — particularly if the government requires it under a prime contract. A license that restricts use to the Deliverables as delivered does not accommodate that. If Meridian modifies the Deliverables or integrates them into a broader government system, it may be operating outside the license scope, which under Section 8.2(c) is an uncapped IP exposure.

**Requested change:** Expand the license grant to include the right to modify, integrate, and use Vendor Pre-Existing IP as reasonably necessary to fulfill Meridian's obligations under applicable government contracts, including the right to grant sublicenses to government clients to the extent required by law or contract.

---

**5. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration in Wilmington Creates Operational Friction**

Binding arbitration under AAA Commercial Rules in Wilmington, Delaware is not inherently unreasonable, but it warrants scrutiny in a federal contractor context. If a dispute involves classified information, government-sensitive data, or matters subject to federal jurisdiction, private arbitration may not be the appropriate forum — and may conflict with obligations under applicable federal regulations or prime contract terms. This provision should be reviewed against Meridian's prime contract requirements before execution.

**Requested change:** Add a carve-out from mandatory arbitration for disputes involving classified information, matters subject to federal jurisdiction, or disputes where arbitration would conflict with applicable law or government contract requirements. Preserve the right to seek emergency injunctive relief in court without waiving arbitration rights.

---

### What This Agreement Gets Right

To be precise: the agreement is not entirely one-sided. The indemnification obligations in Section 9 are bilateral. The confidentiality obligations in Section 5 bind both parties. The IP assignment in Section 4.2 is clear and conditioned on payment, which is standard. The termination provisions are workable. The structural problems are concentrated in the liability framework and the license scope — but those are the provisions that govern exposure when the relationship breaks down, which is when they matter most.

---

### Bottom Line

Do not execute this agreement in its current form. The liability cap provides no meaningful protection in the dispute categories most likely to arise in a federal services engagement. The IP license is too narrow for operational use. The arbitration clause needs a federal jurisdiction carve-out before it can be evaluated against Meridian's prime contract obligations. These are not minor drafting issues. They are the provisions that will define Meridian's exposure if this engagement goes wrong — and in this operating environment, the cost of getting them wrong is not recoverable.
