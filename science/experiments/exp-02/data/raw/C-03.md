# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this tier — the liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately carved out, indemnification runs both directions, and IP assignment is conditioned on payment rather than automatic. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk for a federal contractor operating in the national security space, and two clauses contain a structural defect that could render their apparent protections illusory at the moment of maximum exposure. This agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Do Not Execute Without Resolution

---

**[FINDING 1] Section 8.2(a) Creates a Circular Indemnification Loop That May Collapse Under Stress**

The liability cap in Section 8.1 is excluded for indemnification obligations under Section 9 (per 8.2(a)). On its face, this looks protective — indemnification is uncapped, which is standard. The problem is structural: Section 9.1 defines Vendor's indemnification obligation as covering Losses arising from Vendor's *breach of this Agreement*. If that breach is, for example, a data security failure or a deliverable defect, the indemnification obligation is theoretically uncapped.

However, Section 6.3 disclaims all warranties except the narrow professional services warranty in 6.2. If Vendor's deliverable causes downstream harm — say, a software failure that disrupts a federal system — Client's ability to characterize that as a "breach" is constrained by the near-total warranty disclaimer. Vendor will argue there was no breach because no warranty was given. The indemnification carve-out from the cap then becomes inaccessible precisely when Client needs it most.

**Risk:** The warranty disclaimer and indemnification trigger are in structural tension. A sophisticated Vendor counsel will use 6.3 to defeat the indemnification claim, leaving Client exposed to the cap in 8.1 — which, in a low-fee early-stage engagement, could be trivially small.

**Required Fix:** Either (a) expand the warranty in Section 6.2 to include a functional conformance warranty tied to SOW specifications, or (b) add an explicit indemnification trigger for damages arising from defective Deliverables independent of breach characterization, or (c) both.

---

**[FINDING 2] Section 5.3 — Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Facially Inadequate for National Security Engagements**

The confidentiality obligation survives termination for five years. In a standard commercial SaaS or professional services context, this is acceptable. In a federal contractor engagement — particularly one involving national security, sensitive government data, controlled unclassified information (CUI), or anything touching classified program support — five years is legally and operationally insufficient.

Federal information handling obligations under NIST SP 800-171, CMMC, FAR 52.204-21, and related frameworks do not expire on a commercial timeline. If Vendor receives CUI or program-sensitive information in the course of performing Services, a five-year confidentiality window creates a gap between contractual obligation and regulatory requirement. Worse, it creates an affirmative argument by Vendor that its obligations lapsed — at year six, in the middle of a congressional inquiry or OIG investigation.

**Risk:** Contractual confidentiality obligation expires while regulatory and programmatic sensitivity of the information does not. This is a compliance gap and a litigation exposure gap simultaneously.

**Required Fix:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of (a) ten years or (b) the period required by applicable law or regulation, with a specific carve-out stating that obligations with respect to classified or CUI information survive indefinitely or as required by the relevant government authority.

---

**[FINDING 3] Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware Is Operationally Incompatible With Federal Contract Disputes**

This clause requires all disputes to be resolved by binding AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware. For a government contracting firm, this provision creates three distinct problems:

1. **Flow-down conflicts:** Prime contracts and subcontracts in the federal space frequently contain dispute resolution clauses mandated by FAR/DFARS, including the Disputes clause at FAR 52.233-1. Mandatory private arbitration may conflict with or be preempted by those clauses depending on how this MSA is positioned in the contract hierarchy.

2. **Government access and intervention:** If the federal government has an interest in the subject matter of a dispute — which is not uncommon in national security engagements — private binding arbitration forecloses government participation and may conflict with the government's rights under the prime contract.

3. **Finality risk:** The clause states the arbitrator's decision "shall be final and binding." There is no carve-out for disputes involving federal regulatory compliance, national security equities, or matters where a court must retain jurisdiction (e.g., injunctive relief for IP misappropriation or classified data breach).

**Risk:** Client may be contractually bound to a dispute resolution mechanism that is unenforceable, preempted, or incompatible with its prime contract obligations — discovered only after a dispute arises.

**Required Fix:** Add a carve-out permitting either party to seek injunctive or emergency relief in a court of competent jurisdiction. Add a provision subordinating this clause to any conflicting dispute resolution requirements in applicable prime contracts or federal regulations. Consider replacing mandatory arbitration with tiered escalation (executive negotiation → mediation → litigation) given the federal context.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Requires Negotiation Before Execution

---

**[FINDING 4] Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on "Full Payment of All Fees" Creates Operational Risk**

IP assignment to Client is expressly conditioned on full payment of all fees. This is a facially reasonable commercial provision, but in a federal contracting context it creates a gap: if a payment dispute arises mid-engagement — or if fees are disputed at completion — Client may be operating under government contracts using Deliverables to which it does not yet hold title. If the government has taken delivery of work product and a payment dispute later arises, Client could be in breach of its obligations to the government regarding IP ownership representations.

**Required Fix:** Add a provision granting Client an interim license to use Deliverables upon delivery (pending final payment), with full assignment triggering on payment. Alternatively, structure assignment as automatic upon delivery with a security interest in favor of Vendor pending payment — which is commercially equivalent but eliminates the title gap.

---

**[FINDING 5] Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization Is Unconditional and Lacks Security Requirements**

Vendor may use subcontractors freely, subject only to the requirement that Vendor "remains responsible" for their performance. In a national security or federal services context, this is insufficient. There is no requirement that:

- Subcontractors be disclosed to Client in advance
- Subcontractors be subject to security clearance or background check requirements
- Subcontractors be U.S. persons or entities (no foreign national / foreign entity restriction)
- Subcontractors be prohibited from accessing classified or CUI data without Client approval

**Risk:** Vendor could engage a foreign-owned subcontractor with access to sensitive federal program information, and Client would have no contractual basis to object or intervene until after the fact.

**Required Fix:** Require prior written Client approval for any subcontractor who will have access to Client systems, data, or Deliverables. Add affirmative representations that subcontractors are subject to equivalent security and compliance obligations, and add a right for Client to reject any subcontractor on security grounds without cause.

---

**[FINDING 6] Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Trigger (a) Is Overbroad in One Direction and Underprotective in Another**

Section 9.1(a) triggers Vendor indemnification for third-party claims arising from "Vendor's performance of the Services" — without a negligence or fault qualifier. This is actually favorable to Client and should be preserved. However, Section 9.1(d) limits indemnification for subcontractor conduct to *gross negligence or willful misconduct*. Given that Section 2.3 permits free use of subcontractors, this means ordinary negligence by a subcontractor — which could cause significant harm — falls outside the indemnification trigger and back inside the liability cap.

**Risk:** Subcontractor ordinary negligence causing third-party harm is not indemnified and is subject to the 12-month fee cap, which may be inadequate.

**Required Fix:** Remove the gross negligence/willful misconduct qualifier from 9.1(d) with respect to subcontractors, or alternatively require Vendor to carry sufficient insurance to cover subcontractor ordinary negligence claims and name Client as additional insured.

### 🔵 ADVISORY — Flag for Client Awareness, Negotiate If Possible

---

**[FINDING 7] No Insurance Requirements**

The agreement contains no requirement that Vendor maintain any insurance — no commercial general liability, no professional liability (E&O), no cyber liability, no workers' compensation. For a federal contractor engagement involving software and data handling, the absence of minimum insurance requirements means Client has no assurance that Vendor can satisfy its indemnification obligations if triggered.

**Recommendation:** Add a standard insurance exhibit requiring at minimum: (a) commercial general liability ($1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate), (b) professional liability / E&O ($2M per claim), (c) cyber liability ($2M), and (d) workers' compensation as required by law. Require Client to be named as additional insured on CGL and cyber policies.

---

**[FINDING 8] Section 6.2 — "Knowingly Infringe" Qualifier Weakens the IP Warranty**

The non-infringement warranty in Section 6.2 is limited to Deliverables that will not "*knowingly* infringe" third-party IP rights. The addition of "knowingly" is a significant limitation — it means Vendor bears no warranty obligation for inadvertent or negligent infringement, only deliberate infringement. In software development, most IP infringement is inadvertent (open-source license violations, third-party library incorporation, etc.). This qualifier effectively guts the practical value of the warranty.

**Recommendation:** Strike "knowingly" from Section 6.2. The indemnification in Section 9.1(b) covers IP infringement claims without a knowledge qualifier — the warranty should be consistent.
