# CONTRACT REVIEW — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Senior Contract Review**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it carries three material deficiencies that are unacceptable for a federal contractor engagement: (1) the indemnification trigger in Section 9.1(a) is overbroad and untethered from fault, exposing Client to indemnification disputes on claims arising merely from Vendor's *performance* regardless of Client's own conduct; (2) the confidentiality survival period of five years is dangerously inadequate for a national security or federal services context where classified, CUI, or operationally sensitive information may be involved; and (3) the mandatory arbitration clause in Section 10.2 creates a structural conflict with federal dispute resolution frameworks and potential prime contract flow-down obligations that could render this provision void or create compliance exposure. These issues must be resolved before execution.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**FINDING 1 — Section 9.1(a): Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Fault-Agnostic**

> *"...arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**The Problem:**
This trigger is untethered from any fault standard. "Arising out of or related to" Vendor's *performance* is one of the broadest causation formulations in commercial drafting. It would capture third-party claims that are causally connected to the Services even where Client's own conduct, specifications, or direction was the proximate cause of the harm. In a federal services engagement, where Client may be directing Vendor's work product toward sensitive government systems, this language could be weaponized against Vendor in ways that are commercially unreasonable — or, depending on how indemnification flows, could create ambiguity about who bears the cost of claims arising from Client-directed activities.

Critically, subsections (b), (c), and (d) of Section 9.1 are properly fault-scoped (IP infringement, breach, gross negligence/willful misconduct). The absence of a fault qualifier in (a) is not an oversight — it is a structural asymmetry that creates a gap large enough to swallow the rest of the indemnification framework.

**The Risk to Client:**
If a third party brings a claim arising from Vendor's performance of Services that was *directed by Client*, Vendor may argue that Client's indemnification obligation under Section 9.2(a) (unauthorized use) or 9.2(b) (breach) applies, while simultaneously Client cannot invoke Vendor's 9.1(a) obligation because the claim arose from performance, not gross negligence. This creates a coverage dispute at the worst possible moment — during active litigation.

**Required Fix:**
Narrow Section 9.1(a) to require a nexus to Vendor's negligence, willful misconduct, or material breach:

> *"(a) Vendor's negligent or wrongful performance of the Services, or Vendor's material breach of its obligations under any SOW;"*

---

**FINDING 2 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Context**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**
Five years is a standard commercial confidentiality tail. It is not appropriate here. Meridian Federal Partners operates in the national security and federal services space. Information exchanged under this agreement may include:

- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) subject to 32 C.F.R. Part 2002 and agency-specific handling requirements
- Information subject to NIST SP 800-171 or CMMC obligations with no defined expiration
- Operationally sensitive data tied to federal programs with classification lifespans that extend decades
- Export-controlled technical data under ITAR/EAR with indefinite protection requirements

A five-year contractual confidentiality window does not align with — and in some cases directly conflicts with — the regulatory obligations that attach to this category of information by operation of law. The contractual tail expiring does not extinguish the regulatory obligation. What it does is create ambiguity: a Vendor who has passed the five-year mark may reasonably believe its contractual obligations have lapsed, even while regulatory obligations persist. That ambiguity is a litigation risk and a compliance risk.

**Required Fix:**
Replace the fixed term with a tiered structure:

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of ten (10) years, except that obligations with respect to Confidential Information that constitutes trade secrets shall survive indefinitely, and obligations with respect to information subject to applicable federal law, regulation, or government contract requirements (including but not limited to CUI, ITAR-controlled data, or classified information) shall survive for the period required by such law, regulation, or contract, whichever is longest."*

**FINDING 3 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Creates Structural Conflict with Federal Contracting Framework**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Problem:**
This is a blanket, mandatory, binding arbitration clause with no carve-outs. In the federal contractor context, this is a structural problem on multiple levels:

1. **Prime Contract Flow-Down:** If Meridian Federal Partners holds a prime contract with a federal agency, that contract almost certainly contains dispute resolution clauses, Disputes Act provisions (41 U.S.C. §§ 7101–7109), or agency-specific requirements that govern how disputes touching on federal contract performance are resolved. A mandatory arbitration clause in a subcontract or vendor agreement that purports to govern "any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement" may directly conflict with those flow-down obligations. The AAA arbitration clause does not yield to federal dispute mechanisms — it competes with them.

2. **No Carve-Out for Injunctive Relief:** There is no emergency or injunctive relief carve-out. In IP misappropriation, confidentiality breach, or national security-sensitive scenarios, the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief from a court of competent jurisdiction pending arbitration is a material gap. The agreement does not preserve that right.

3. **Venue:** Wilmington, Delaware is an acceptable commercial venue, but it should be confirmed against any mandatory venue provisions in the prime contract or applicable agency regulations.

4. **Finality Risk:** The arbitrator's decision is "final and binding" with no appeal mechanism. In disputes involving federal program data, IP developed under government funding, or work product that may be subject to government rights (FAR 52.227 series), the inability to appeal an adverse arbitration award is a significant exposure.

**Required Fix:**
At minimum, add the following carve-outs:

> *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending resolution of a dispute by arbitration. Additionally, disputes arising under or relating to any prime contract, government contract, or federal regulatory obligation shall be resolved in accordance with the dispute resolution mechanisms required by such contract or regulation, which shall control in the event of conflict with this Section."*

Additionally, Client should confirm whether its prime contract(s) contain mandatory dispute resolution provisions that would render this arbitration clause unenforceable or create a compliance violation upon execution.

**FINDING 4 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment Conditioned on "Full Payment" Creates a Timing Gap**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**
The assignment is conditioned on full payment. Until that condition is satisfied, the assignment has not occurred — meaning Client is operating under a license-by-implication at best, or with no formal IP rights at worst, during the period between delivery and final payment. In a federal contractor context where Deliverables may be delivered to or used by a government agency before final invoicing is complete, this creates a gap in the chain of title that could affect the government's rights in the work product.

**Recommended Fix:**
Consider replacing with an automatic assignment that vests upon delivery, with a security interest or license-back mechanism protecting Vendor's payment rights:

> *"Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables upon delivery. To secure payment of all fees due under this Agreement, Vendor retains a security interest in the Deliverables until full payment is received, and Client grants Vendor a limited license-back to the Deliverables solely to enforce such security interest."*

Alternatively, negotiate a partial assignment that vests proportionally as payments are made against milestones.

**FINDING 5 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Approval Rights Are Absent**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor separately remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

**The Problem:**
Vendor has unilateral authority to engage subcontractors with no Client approval right. In a national security or federal services engagement, subcontractor identity may be material — particularly where subcontractors will have access to CUI, government systems, or sensitive program data. The absence of an approval right also creates exposure under CMMC and NIST 800-171 supply chain requirements, which impose obligations on the prime to vet and control subcontractor access.

**Recommended Fix:**
Add a prior written approval requirement for subcontractors who will have access to Client systems, data, or Confidential Information, and require that all such subcontractors be bound by security and compliance obligations equivalent to those imposed on Vendor under this Agreement and any applicable prime contract.
