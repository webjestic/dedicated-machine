**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability carve-outs in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the IP assignment is workable, and the indemnification procedure in Section 9.3 includes the settlement consent protection that is frequently omitted. However, several provisions introduce material risk that is unacceptable for a federal contractor engagement and must be resolved before execution. The issues identified below range from operationally disqualifying to significant enough to alter exposure in a dispute.

---

## Issues Requiring Resolution

### 🔴 Critical — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. Binding Arbitration Clause (§10.2) — Potential Conflict with Federal Contract Requirements**

The mandatory AAA arbitration clause may conflict with federal prime contract terms, agency dispute resolution requirements, or applicable statutes (e.g., the Contract Disputes Act, FAR 52.233 clauses, or agency-specific flow-down provisions). Federal contractors frequently cannot contractually waive access to courts or agency boards for disputes touching on government work. If Meridian's prime contract contains mandatory dispute resolution flow-downs, this clause is not merely inconvenient — it may be void as applied or create a compliance defect upstream.

**Required action:** Confirm that no applicable prime contract, FAR clause, or agency requirement prohibits or limits binding arbitration for disputes arising from this engagement. At minimum, carve out disputes implicating government contract performance, federal regulatory compliance, or any matter subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction. Consider replacing mandatory arbitration with a tiered dispute resolution clause (negotiation → mediation → litigation) with Delaware federal or state court as the forum.

---

**2. Confidentiality Survival Period (§5.3) — Inadequate for National Security Context**

A five-year survival period is commercially standard but operationally insufficient for engagements in the national security and federal services space. Classified information, controlled unclassified information (CUI), and sensitive government program data may carry legal protection obligations that extend indefinitely under federal law (e.g., 18 U.S.C. § 1030, Executive Order 13526, NISPOM, or agency-specific security requirements). A contractual five-year window does not extinguish those obligations, but it creates a gap between the parties' contractual duties and their regulatory ones — and that gap will be exploited in litigation.

**Required action:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of (a) ten years or (b) the period required by applicable law or regulation, with an explicit carve-out stating that nothing in §5.3 limits obligations imposed by federal law, regulation, or government contract terms. If classified information may be involved, a separate security annex should be attached.

---

**3. Subcontractor Provision (§2.3) — No Security or Compliance Vetting Requirement**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no requirement for prior written Client approval, no security clearance or background check obligation, no restriction on foreign nationals or foreign-owned entities, and no flow-down of federal compliance requirements. In a national security context, this is a significant gap. A subcontractor with foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) could trigger NISPOM violations, ITAR/EAR exposure, or prime contract breach — none of which Meridian would have visibility into until after the damage is done.

**Required action:** Amend §2.3 to require: (a) prior written Client approval for any subcontractor performing work on federal engagements; (b) Vendor representation that subcontractors meet applicable security, clearance, and compliance requirements; (c) explicit flow-down of all federal regulatory obligations (ITAR, EAR, CUI handling, etc.); and (d) prohibition on subcontracting to entities with unmitigated FOCI without Client consent.

---

### 🟡 Significant — Strongly Recommended Changes

---

**4. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment (§4.2) — Creates Leverage Risk**

The assignment of Deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This means that in any payment dispute — including a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy, scope creep, or fee calculation — Vendor retains IP ownership over work product that may already be integrated into a government deliverable. In a federal contracting context, this could create a situation where Meridian cannot fulfill its prime contract obligations because it does not legally own the work product it has already delivered to the government.

**Required action:** Either (a) remove the payment condition and replace it with a security interest or license-back mechanism that preserves Vendor's economic remedy without blocking IP transfer, or (b) add explicit language confirming that the assignment is effective upon delivery and that Vendor's remedy for non-payment is a breach of contract claim, not IP recapture. At minimum, add a provision confirming that any government license rights required by the prime contract are not affected by payment disputes between the parties.

---

**5. "As Is" Disclaimer (§6.3) Partially Conflicts with §6.2 Warranty**

Section 6.2 provides an express warranty that Services will be performed professionally and that Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is." The carve-out language ("except as expressly set forth in Section 6.2") preserves the express warranties, but the "as is" characterization of the Deliverables in §6.3 creates interpretive tension that a vendor's counsel will use to narrow §6.2 in a dispute. The phrase "as delivered" in §6.2 compounds this — it limits the warranty to the moment of delivery and arguably excludes latent defects discovered post-acceptance.

**Required action:** Clarify that the §6.3 disclaimer does not modify or limit the §6.2 warranties, and remove the "as delivered" qualifier from the non-infringement warranty or replace it with a representation that survives acceptance for a defined period.

---

**6. No Federal Compliance Representations**

For a vendor agreement in the federal services space, the representations in §6.1 and §6.2 are materially incomplete. There are no representations regarding: compliance with applicable federal laws and regulations (FAR, DFARS, ITAR, EAR, CUI requirements); data handling and cybersecurity standards (NIST SP 800-171, CMMC if applicable); non-debarment or suspension status; or absence of FOCI. These are not boilerplate additions — they are substantive protections that define whether Meridian can rely on this vendor in a regulated engagement.

**Required action:** Add a federal compliance representations section requiring Vendor to represent and warrant compliance with all applicable federal laws and regulations relevant to the engagement, including but not limited to export control, data security, and debarment status, with an obligation to notify Client promptly of any change in compliance status.

---

### 🔵 Clarification Needed

---

**7. Governing Law (§10.1) — Delaware Law, No Federal Law Supremacy Clause**

Delaware governing law is commercially standard and not objectionable on its face. However, the agreement contains no provision confirming that applicable federal law governs where it conflicts with or preempts state law. In a federal contracting context, federal statutes and regulations routinely preempt state contract law on issues including IP rights in government-funded work (Bayh-Dole), data rights (DFARS 252.227), and dispute resolution. The absence of a federal law supremacy clause creates ambiguity about which body of law controls in those areas.

**Required action:** Add a provision stating that nothing in this Agreement limits or supersedes the parties' obligations under applicable federal law, regulation, or government contract requirements, and that federal law governs to the extent it conflicts with or preempts any provision of this Agreement.

---

**8. Termination for Convenience — No Stranded Cost Protection for Client**

Section 7.2 permits either party to terminate for convenience on 60 days' notice, and §7.4 requires Client to pay for all Services performed and expenses incurred through termination. There is no cap on termination liability, no requirement that Vendor mitigate costs upon notice of termination, and no mechanism for Client to dispute invoiced expenses incurred after notice is given. In a government contracting context where Meridian's own prime contract may be terminated for convenience by the government, this creates a mismatch: Meridian may receive only partial reimbursement from its prime but owe full termination costs to Vendor.

**Required action:** Add a mitigation obligation requiring Vendor to use commercially reasonable efforts to minimize costs upon receipt of termination notice, and confirm that termination fees are limited to reasonable, documented costs actually incurred and not avoidable through mitigation.

---

## Summary Assessment

This agreement is not approvable in its current form for a federal contractor engagement. The arbitration clause, confidentiality survival period, and subcontractor provision each independently create compliance or operational risk that could affect Meridian's ability to perform on a government prime contract. The IP payment condition and missing federal compliance representations are significant secondary concerns. None of these issues are unusual to negotiate, and the agreement's overall structure is a workable foundation — but execution should not proceed until the critical items are resolved and the federal compliance gap is addressed.
