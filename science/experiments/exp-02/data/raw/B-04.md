**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection the four categories of dispute most likely to generate material exposure in a federal contractor engagement — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, and gross negligence — leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely produce the losses that make liability clauses matter in the first place. Before execution, three additional issues require resolution: the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(a) is drafted so broadly that Vendor's mere performance of Services triggers indemnification exposure regardless of fault; the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 mandates Wilmington venue without a carve-out for emergency injunctive relief, which is operationally unacceptable in a national security context where a confidentiality breach may require immediate judicial intervention; and the five-year confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is inadequate for a federal contractor engagement where classified or controlled unclassified information may carry indefinite protection obligations under applicable federal law and regulation.

---

**Detailed Analysis**

**Section 8 — Limitation of Liability**

The cap as written provides the appearance of bilateral protection. It does not provide the substance of it.

Section 8.1 caps total cumulative liability at fees paid in the preceding twelve months and excludes consequential damages. That structure is commercially standard. The problem is Section 8.2.

Read the exceptions list:

- **(a) Indemnification.** The indemnification obligations in Section 9 are carved out entirely. In practice, indemnification claims — particularly third-party IP infringement claims against a federal contractor — are the claims that generate seven-figure exposure. The cap does not touch them.

- **(b) Confidentiality.** Confidentiality breaches are carved out entirely. In a national security engagement, a confidentiality breach is not a commercial inconvenience. It is a regulatory event, potentially a federal law enforcement matter, and the source of the largest damages exposure in the relationship. The cap does not touch it.

- **(c) Intellectual Property.** IP infringement and misappropriation claims are carved out entirely. Given that Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client and Section 4.3 grants a license to embedded Vendor Pre-Existing IP, the boundary between licensed and assigned IP will be contested in any serious dispute. That contested boundary sits entirely outside the cap.

- **(d) Gross Negligence and Willful Misconduct.** This carve-out is common and defensible in isolation. In combination with the others, it closes the last gap a party might use to argue that egregious conduct should remain capped.

- **(e) Fees Owed.** Client's payment obligations are carved out. This is a unilateral carve-out that benefits Vendor exclusively. It is not bilateral protection. It is Vendor ensuring its revenue stream is uncapped while Client's recovery on every meaningful claim category is also uncapped — but in the other direction.

What remains inside the cap after these exceptions? Ordinary breach of contract claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or gross negligence. That is a narrow and largely theoretical category. In three years of practice I have not seen a commercial dispute in this space that lived exclusively in that residual category. The cap is not protecting the client. It is protecting the document's appearance of balance.

**Requested Change — Section 8:** Either (1) move indemnification and IP carve-outs to a separate, negotiated sublimit rather than full uncapped exposure, or (2) restructure the exceptions so that consequential damages exclusions remain in place even for carved-out categories, with only the aggregate cap lifted. At minimum, the confidentiality carve-out should be subject to a separate, defined sublimit — recommend no less than three times the trailing twelve-month fees — rather than unlimited exposure.

---

**Section 9.1(a) — Vendor Indemnification Scope**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of or related to "Vendor's performance of the Services." There is no fault requirement attached to this subsection. Subsections (b), (c), and (d) each carry limiting language — infringement allegations, breach, gross negligence — but (a) is drafted as a strict liability indemnification trigger tied solely to the act of performing Services.

This means that if a third party brings a claim against Client that is factually connected to Vendor's work — even if Vendor performed that work correctly, professionally, and in full compliance with the SOW — Vendor's indemnification obligation is triggered. A subcontractor injury claim, a regulatory challenge to a federal deliverable, a protest by a competing contractor: if the claim touches Vendor's performance, the obligation attaches.

This is not a balanced provision. It is a risk transfer mechanism that Vendor may not have intended and that Client should not rely on without understanding the reciprocal exposure it creates if Client ever seeks to enforce it and Vendor contests scope.

**Requested Change — Section 9.1(a):** Add a fault nexus. Suggested language: *"arising out of or related to Vendor's negligent or wrongful performance of the Services."* Alternatively, limit the trigger to claims arising from Vendor's failure to perform in accordance with the Agreement's standards.

---

**Section 10.2 — Arbitration Without Injunctive Relief Carve-Out**

The arbitration clause is mandatory and binding with no exception. In a national security engagement, confidentiality breaches and IP misappropriation events require the ability to seek emergency injunctive relief in federal court — immediately, without waiting for AAA panel constitution, preliminary scheduling, or Wilmington venue logistics.

AAA Commercial Rules do include an emergency arbitrator procedure, but that procedure is not equivalent to a federal court temporary restraining order in speed, enforceability, or practical effect when the underlying matter involves federal systems, controlled information, or national security equities.

**Requested Change — Section 10.2:** Add a standard injunctive relief carve-out. Suggested language: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending the resolution of any arbitration proceeding."* This is a market-standard provision. Resistance to including it is itself a negotiating signal worth noting.

---

**Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival Period**

Five years is a commercially standard survival period for a private commercial agreement. This is not a private commercial agreement in the ordinary sense. Meridian Federal Partners operates in the national security and federal services space. Depending on the nature of the Services and the information exchanged, applicable federal frameworks — including but not limited to CUI handling requirements under 32 C.F.R. Part 2002, NIST SP 800-171 obligations for covered defense information, and agency-specific data handling requirements — may impose confidentiality obligations that survive indefinitely or for periods substantially longer than five years.

A five-year contractual survival period does not override a federal regulatory obligation, but it creates a gap between the parties' contractual framework and their actual legal obligations. That gap produces ambiguity about remedies, enforcement, and the scope of surviving duties in any post-termination dispute.

**Requested Change — Section 5.3:** Extend survival to the longer of ten years or the period required by applicable law and regulation, with explicit language preserving any obligations imposed by federal law or agency requirement regardless of the contractual survival period.

---

**Additional Observation — Section 4.3, Pre-Existing IP License Scope**

The license granted to Client for Vendor Pre-Existing IP embedded in Deliverables is non-exclusive, perpetual, and royalty-free, but limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This restriction creates operational risk if Client needs to modify, update, or rebuild the Deliverables after the relationship ends. If the Vendor Pre-Existing IP is foundational to the Deliverable's function, Client's ability to maintain or enhance the system without Vendor's ongoing involvement may be contractually constrained.

In a federal contractor context where continuity of operations is a contractual obligation to the government client, this restriction deserves scrutiny before execution. This is flagged for client awareness rather than as a blocking issue, but it should be addressed in negotiation if the Deliverables are expected to require post-engagement modification.
