**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better than most commercial MSAs submitted for review in this space — the liability carve-outs in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the indemnification obligations are reciprocal and well-scoped, and IP assignment is clearly conditioned on payment. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk or are inadequate for a federal contractor engagement and must be addressed before execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 🔴 Critical Issues — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. Mandatory Arbitration (§10.2) — Incompatible with Federal Contracting Obligations**

The binding arbitration clause with venue fixed in Wilmington, Delaware is a hard problem in this context. Federal contractors routinely operate under prime contracts, subcontracts, and task orders that require disputes to be resolved through specific federal mechanisms — including the Contract Disputes Act, agency boards of contract appeals, or the Court of Federal Claims. A mandatory AAA arbitration clause could directly conflict with those upstream obligations.

Additionally, arbitration clauses in agreements touching classified or controlled unclassified information (CUI) environments raise counterintelligence and disclosure concerns — arbitral proceedings are not subject to the same access controls as federal court proceedings.

**Required change:** Either remove mandatory arbitration entirely and substitute litigation in a mutually agreed federal or state court, or carve out any dispute that implicates a federal prime contract, subcontract, or government-imposed obligation. At minimum, Meridian must retain the right to seek injunctive relief in court without waiving arbitration rights, and the clause must not be read to override any dispute resolution mechanism required by a prime contract or applicable federal law.

---

**2. Confidentiality Survival — Five Years Is Insufficient for This Engagement (§5.3)**

A five-year survival period is commercially standard. It is not adequate here. Meridian operates in the national security and federal services space. Information exchanged under this agreement may include CUI, export-controlled technical data, procurement-sensitive information, or details about federal systems and infrastructure. The sensitivity of that information does not expire on a commercial timeline.

**Required change:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of: (a) ten years, or (b) the period required by any applicable federal regulation, prime contract, or government-imposed obligation. For information that constitutes trade secrets under applicable law, obligations should survive indefinitely. If the parties cannot agree to indefinite protection for trade secrets, that must be flagged as an accepted risk and documented.

---

**3. Subcontractor Use — No Security or Compliance Vetting Requirement (§2.3)**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no restrictions beyond general performance responsibility. In a federal contractor context, this is a significant gap. Subcontractors may require security clearances, FOCI (Foreign Ownership, Control, or Influence) review, facility clearance, or compliance with CMMC, ITAR, EAR, or other regulatory frameworks depending on the nature of the work. Vendor's general responsibility for subcontractor "performance and compliance" does not address these requirements.

**Required change:** Add a provision requiring that (a) Vendor obtain Client's prior written approval before engaging any subcontractor on work performed under this Agreement; (b) all subcontractors meet any security, clearance, or regulatory requirements applicable to the engagement; and (c) Vendor flow down all applicable compliance obligations — including but not limited to CMMC, ITAR, FAR/DFARS clauses, and CUI handling requirements — to any subcontractor. Vendor's indemnification obligation under §9.1(d) already covers subcontractor gross negligence and willful misconduct, but that is a remedy after the fact. The goal here is prevention.

---

### 🟡 Significant Issues — Strong Recommendation to Address

---

**4. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment — Creates Operational Risk (§4.2)**

The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." Until that condition is satisfied, Client does not own the work product. In a federal contracting context, this creates a scenario where Deliverables that have already been integrated into a government system, delivered to an agency, or incorporated into a prime contract deliverable are not legally owned by Client. A payment dispute — even a good-faith one — could leave Client in breach of its upstream obligations to the government.

**Required change:** Either (a) remove the payment condition from the assignment and address non-payment exclusively through payment remedies and termination rights, or (b) add a provision clarifying that the assignment is effective upon delivery and that Vendor retains a security interest only, which is automatically released upon full payment. Client should not be in a position where a billing dispute creates a chain-of-title problem on work already delivered to a federal agency.

---

**5. "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts with Vendor's Warranty in §6.2 (§6.3)**

Section 6.2 provides an express warranty that Services will be performed professionally and that Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is." The "as is" language in §6.3 is facially inconsistent with the warranty in §6.2 and creates interpretive ambiguity about the scope of Vendor's actual commitments.

The disclaimer of the implied warranty of non-infringement in §6.3 is particularly problematic given that §6.2 only warrants against *knowing* infringement. Read together, these provisions could be construed to mean Vendor has no enforceable obligation with respect to non-knowing infringement — a meaningful gap in a software delivery context.

**Required change:** Revise §6.3 to expressly state that the "as is" disclaimer does not apply to the representations and warranties set forth in §6.2. Consider also strengthening the infringement warranty in §6.2 by removing the "knowingly" qualifier, or at minimum ensuring that the indemnification in §9.1(b) covers infringement claims regardless of Vendor's knowledge.

---

**6. No Regulatory Compliance Representation (§6)**

The representations and warranties section contains no representation that Vendor will comply with applicable federal laws and regulations. For a vendor performing services in the federal space, this is a material omission. Depending on the nature of the engagement, applicable requirements may include CMMC, NIST SP 800-171, ITAR, EAR, FAR/DFARS clauses, Section 889 (prohibition on certain telecommunications equipment), and others.

**Required change:** Add a representation by Vendor that it will perform the Services in compliance with all applicable federal, state, and local laws and regulations, including any specific regulatory frameworks identified in the applicable SOW. Consider adding a flow-down clause requiring Vendor to comply with any FAR/DFARS provisions that Meridian is required to flow down under its prime contracts.

---

### 🟢 Provisions That Are Acceptable — With Notes

---

**Limitation of Liability (§8.1 / §8.2):** The cap structure is reasonable and the carve-outs in §8.2 are appropriately broad. Excluding confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence, willful misconduct, and indemnification obligations from the cap is correct. No changes required here, though the value of the cap should be confirmed against the anticipated contract value in each SOW — a 12-month trailing fee cap may be inadequate if the engagement involves a large upfront deliverable with minimal ongoing fees.

**Indemnification (§9):** Reciprocal structure is acceptable. Vendor's indemnification covers performance, IP infringement, breach, and subcontractor misconduct. The notice and cooperation procedure in §9.3 is standard and includes appropriate protection against adverse settlements. No changes required.

**IP Framework (§4.1 / §4.3):** The Pre-Existing IP carve-out and the license-back for embedded Vendor IP are commercially standard and acceptable in structure, subject to the payment-condition issue noted above. Meridian should ensure that each SOW specifically identifies any Vendor Pre-Existing IP anticipated to be incorporated into Deliverables, so the scope of the license-back is defined in advance rather than disputed after delivery.

**Termination (§7):** Sixty-day convenience termination is acceptable. The 30-day cure period for material breach is standard. Survival of Sections 4, 5, 8, 9, and 10 is correct and complete.

**Governing Law (§10.1):** Delaware is acceptable as a governing law choice. No objection.
