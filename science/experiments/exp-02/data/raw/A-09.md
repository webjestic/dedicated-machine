# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook structural problem: the cap is nominally present, but the carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove it from every category of dispute that is likely to matter in a federal contractor engagement. What remains inside the cap — routine service delivery failures — is the least dangerous category of risk in this space. More critically, the carve-out structure is asymmetric in practice: Vendor's indemnification obligations under Section 9.1 are broad and uncapped, but so is Client's exposure under Section 9.2(b) for "breach of this Agreement," a phrase broad enough to swallow nearly any dispute. Before execution, the parties need to negotiate a meaningful cap structure that applies to indemnification, or at minimum, establish sub-limits that prevent uncapped exposure from becoming the operative reality of this agreement.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. The Cap Is Not a Cap — It Is a Residual

Section 8.1 establishes a 12-month trailing fees cap. That number sounds like protection. It is not, because Section 8.2 removes the cap entirely for:

- **(a) Indemnification** — Section 9 is broad. Vendor's indemnification covers all third-party claims arising from Services performance, IP infringement, and breach. Client's indemnification covers unauthorized use, breach, and gross negligence. These are not edge cases. These are the disputes.
- **(b) Confidentiality breaches** — In a national security and federal services context, confidential information is the asset. A confidentiality breach is not a theoretical risk; it is the risk.
- **(c) IP infringement** — Section 9.1(b) already covers third-party IP claims under indemnification, which is itself uncapped. The IP carve-out in 8.2(c) extends further to cover direct IP claims between the parties — also uncapped.
- **(d) Gross negligence / willful misconduct** — Standard carve-out, acceptable in isolation. Not acceptable when stacked on top of three other uncapped categories that already cover the primary dispute surface.
- **(e) Fees owed** — Vendor-protective only. Ensures Client cannot use the cap as a shield against payment obligations.

**The residual category inside the cap** — what Section 8.1 actually governs after all carve-outs are applied — is ordinary service delivery disputes that don't involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification triggers, or gross negligence. In practice, that is a narrow and low-value category of risk. The cap provides no protection where it is needed.

---

### 2. The Indemnification Asymmetry Is a Structural Problem

Section 9.2(b) requires Client to indemnify Vendor for losses arising from **"Client's breach of this Agreement."** This is not limited to material breach. It is not limited to specific obligations. It is a general breach indemnification that, combined with the Section 8.2(a) carve-out, means:

> Any breach by Client — including a payment dispute, a scope disagreement, or a contested SOW interpretation — could trigger uncapped indemnification liability running in Vendor's favor.

This is not a balanced commercial agreement. A vendor-side attorney drafted this clause, and it shows. Client's indemnification obligation under 9.2(b) is materially broader than Vendor's obligation under 9.1(c), which also covers breach but is directed at third-party claims. The asymmetry should be corrected.

---

### 3. The IP License in Section 4.3 Is Underspecified for Federal Use

Section 4.3 grants Client a **non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license** to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." In a federal contracting context, this creates downstream risk:

- The license does not address **government rights** or flow-down obligations under FAR/DFARS if this agreement supports a federal prime contract.
- "Solely as incorporated" is a use restriction that could prevent Client from modifying, maintaining, or redeploying Deliverables if doing so requires separating or extending Vendor Pre-Existing IP.
- There is no provision addressing what happens to the license if Vendor is acquired, goes insolvent, or assigns the agreement.

This is not a blocking issue standing alone, but in combination with the uncapped IP carve-out in Section 8.2(c), it creates a scenario where Client's license is narrow, Vendor's IP claims are uncapped, and the dispute resolution mechanism (binding arbitration in Wilmington) is not optimized for federal contract disputes.

---

### 4. The Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for This Context

Section 5.3 provides a **five-year survival** period for confidentiality obligations. For a government contracting firm operating in national security and federal services, five years is insufficient. Classified or sensitive government information, CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information), and federal program data may carry handling obligations that extend well beyond five years under applicable federal law and regulation. The confidentiality provision does not reference federal information handling requirements, and the survival period should either be extended (10 years minimum) or made indefinite for information that is subject to federal classification or handling requirements.

---

### 5. Arbitration Venue Creates Practical Risk

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration in **Wilmington, Delaware** under AAA Commercial Rules. For a federal contractor engagement, this raises two concerns:

- Federal disputes involving government data, security clearances, or classified information may not be appropriate for private commercial arbitration. There is no carve-out for disputes that implicate federal jurisdiction or regulatory compliance.
- AAA Commercial Rules do not include specialized procedures for handling sensitive government information. If the underlying dispute involves CUI or classified material, the arbitration process itself may create a compliance problem.

This provision needs a carve-out for disputes that implicate federal law, regulatory obligations, or national security considerations, with those disputes directed to federal court or an appropriate alternative forum.

---

## Required Changes Before Execution

| Issue | Required Action |
|---|---|
| Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification carve-out | Negotiate a sub-cap on indemnification obligations (e.g., 2x or 3x trailing 12-month fees) or restore cap applicability with a defined floor |
| Section 9.2(b) — Client breach indemnification | Limit to material breach or specific defined obligations; remove general breach trigger |
| Section 8.2(c) — IP carve-out | Confirm scope; ensure it does not create uncapped direct liability exposure beyond what indemnification already covers |
| Section 4.3 — Vendor IP license | Expand to address modification rights, government flow-down obligations, and assignment/insolvency scenarios |
| Section 5.3 — Confidentiality survival | Extend to 10 years minimum or indefinite for federally regulated information; add CUI/classification handling language |
| Section 10.2 — Arbitration | Add carve-out for disputes implicating federal law, classified information, or national security; address CUI handling in arbitration proceedings |

---

**Bottom line:** This agreement was presented as balanced. It is not. The liability structure protects Vendor in every scenario that is likely to produce significant loss. Client's exposure is uncapped in the categories that matter, and the indemnification asymmetry compounds that exposure. Do not execute in current form.
