# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a cap that is structurally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively exempt indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees — which is to say, they exempt every category of dispute that is likely to generate material exposure in a federal contractor engagement. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach claims that, in practice, will rarely be the vehicle through which serious losses are pursued. This is not a mutual cap; it is a drafting structure that creates the appearance of bilateral protection while leaving Meridian's actual exposure points uncapped and unlimited. Beyond the liability architecture, several additional provisions require correction before this agreement is executable in a government contracting context.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Limitation of Liability (Section 8)

**The cap does not function as a cap.**

Section 8.1 establishes a 12-month trailing fees cap on total cumulative liability. That number, depending on contract size, could be meaningful — or it could be a fraction of actual exposure. But the more fundamental problem is Section 8.2.

Read the five exceptions together:

- **(a) Indemnification (Section 9):** Vendor's indemnification obligations under Section 9.1 are broad. They cover all third-party claims arising from Services performance, IP infringement, any breach of the Agreement, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. Because indemnification is carved out of the cap entirely, a third-party IP claim or a breach-based indemnity demand carries **unlimited exposure** — not the 12-month cap Meridian believes it has.

- **(b) Confidentiality (Section 5):** In a national security and federal services context, confidentiality breaches are not edge cases. They are the scenario. A vendor who mishandles classified or sensitive federal information triggers this carve-out, and Meridian's recovery — or Meridian's exposure if the breach runs the other direction — is **uncapped**.

- **(c) IP Rights:** Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but Section 4.3 retains a license back for Vendor Pre-Existing IP. Any dispute about whether embedded IP was properly licensed, or whether Vendor's use of Client-provided materials constitutes misappropriation, falls outside the cap. **Uncapped**.

- **(d) Gross Negligence / Willful Misconduct:** This carve-out is standard and I do not object to it in principle. But note its interaction with Section 9.1(d): Vendor's indemnification obligation already covers gross negligence and willful misconduct. The carve-out in 8.2(d) therefore operates as a second, independent pathway to uncapped liability for the same conduct. The overlap is not necessarily harmful to Meridian, but it should be understood.

- **(e) Fees, Expenses, Taxes (Section 3):** Client's payment obligations are fully carved out. This means DataStream can pursue the full contract value — plus 1.5% monthly interest — outside the cap. In a multi-year federal engagement, that exposure can be substantial.

**The practical result:** The 12-month cap in Section 8.1 governs a residual category of direct breach claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, gross negligence, or unpaid fees. In my experience reviewing disputes that actually proceed to arbitration or litigation, that residual category is rarely where the money is. The cap provides comfort on paper and limited protection in practice.

**Required changes:**

1. Negotiate aggregate dollar caps on indemnification obligations, or at minimum a separate, higher cap specific to indemnification rather than unlimited exposure.
2. Negotiate a cap on confidentiality breach liability, or tie it to a defined multiple of contract value rather than leaving it unlimited.
3. Confirm whether IP carve-out in 8.2(c) is intended to be mutual and unlimited, or whether a separate IP liability cap is acceptable to both parties.
4. Ensure Meridian's internal risk team has reviewed and accepted the fee carve-out in 8.2(e) in light of projected contract value.

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Indemnification Scope (Section 9.1)

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's **performance of the Services**." This is exceptionally broad. It does not require a breach. A third-party claim that is merely connected to how Vendor performed — even if Vendor performed correctly — could trigger the indemnification obligation.

In a federal services context, where Vendor personnel may be operating on government systems or handling sensitive data, this language could sweep in claims that have nothing to do with Vendor fault. The standard formulation requires a nexus to breach, negligence, or wrongful conduct — not mere performance.

**Required change:** Narrow Section 9.1(a) to claims arising from Vendor's **negligent or wrongful** performance, or Vendor's **breach** of its obligations. Remove the standalone "performance of the Services" trigger or qualify it explicitly.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment (Section 4.2)

The assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a standard vendor-protective provision, but it creates a specific risk in a government contracting context: if a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — IP ownership reverts to or remains with Vendor during the pendency of the dispute. For Deliverables that may be incorporated into federal systems or submitted to a government customer, this creates a chain-of-title problem that could affect Meridian's downstream obligations to its government client.

**Required change:** Consider negotiating a license-back provision that grants Client a temporary license to use Deliverables during any payment dispute, pending resolution. Alternatively, condition the payment requirement on undisputed fees only.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Subcontractor Provision (Section 2.3)

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no restriction on who those subcontractors are, no requirement for Client approval, and no security or clearance requirements. In a national security and federal services engagement, this is not acceptable. Vendor personnel and subcontractors may require security clearances, may be subject to foreign ownership or control restrictions, or may need to comply with specific federal contractor requirements (e.g., CMMC, NIST 800-171, FAR/DFARS flow-downs).

**Required change:** Add a requirement that Vendor obtain Client's prior written approval before engaging subcontractors on work under this Agreement. Add a representation that subcontractors will comply with all applicable federal contractor requirements, including any security and clearance requirements specified in the applicable SOW.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Arbitration Venue and Federal Jurisdiction Considerations (Section 10.2)

Binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules is a standard commercial provision. However, for a federal contractor engagement, there are two concerns:

1. **Government contract disputes:** If any SOW involves work that is ultimately subject to the Contract Disputes Act or other federal procurement statutes, the arbitration clause may conflict with or be preempted by mandatory federal dispute resolution mechanisms. This needs to be evaluated against the specific government contract vehicles Meridian is operating under.

2. **Arbitrator qualifications:** For disputes involving sensitive or classified information, an unqualified arbitrator presents a security risk. The agreement should specify that any arbitrator must hold appropriate clearances or that proceedings involving classified information will be handled through a separate, agreed process.

**Required change:** Add a carve-out preserving Meridian's rights under applicable federal procurement law. Add arbitrator qualification requirements appropriate to the sensitivity of the engagement.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Confidentiality Survival Period (Section 5.3)

Five-year survival is standard in commercial agreements. It is not standard for a federal contractor engagement involving national security-related information. Depending on the classification level and nature of information exchanged, perpetual confidentiality obligations — or survival tied to the classification period of the information rather than a fixed term — may be required.

**Required change:** Evaluate whether the 5-year survival period is consistent with Meridian's obligations to its government customer. If the engagement involves controlled unclassified information (CUI) or classified information, the survival period should be extended or made perpetual for that category of information.

---

### 🟢 ACCEPTABLE — With Notes

- **Section 6.3 (Disclaimer of Warranties):** The "as is" disclaimer is standard and acceptable, but note that it is partially in tension with Section 6.2's affirmative warranty of professional performance and non-infringement. The disclaimer does not eliminate the Section 6.2 warranties, which is the correct result — but the interaction should be confirmed with Vendor's counsel to ensure both parties read it the same way.

- **Section 7.2 (Termination for Convenience):** 60-day notice for termination for convenience is reasonable. Confirm that this aligns with Meridian's ability to terminate its government contract obligations on comparable notice.

- **Section 3.2 (Late Fees):** 1.5% per month (18% annualized) is aggressive. Acceptable if Meridian has reliable payment processes, but flag for finance team.
