**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that is likely to arise in a federal contractor engagement of this type — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap governing only a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure in practice. This is not a mutual cap; it is an asymmetric risk allocation dressed in bilateral language, and it will not perform as protective when it is needed. Before execution, the indemnification trigger in Section 9.1(a) also requires tightening, and the arbitration clause needs evaluation against the client's federal contracting obligations.

## Detailed Analysis

### Section 8 — Limitation of Liability: The Core Problem

#### What the cap appears to provide

Section 8.1 presents as a standard, bilateral limitation of liability. It does two things: (1) excludes consequential, indirect, incidental, special, exemplary, and punitive damages; and (2) caps total cumulative liability at fees paid or payable in the twelve months preceding the claim. On its face, this looks like a conventional mutual cap. It is not.

#### What the carve-outs actually remove

Section 8.2 excepts the following from both the consequential damages exclusion and the fee-based cap:

- **(a) Indemnification obligations under Section 9.** Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of Vendor's *performance of the Services* — a scope so broad it encompasses nearly any dispute with a third-party dimension. More critically, because indemnification is carved out entirely, Vendor's indemnification exposure is uncapped and includes consequential damages and lost profits. The same is true in reverse under 9.2. Every significant commercial dispute in a services engagement eventually attracts a third-party claim or can be framed as one. Carving out indemnification wholesale means the cap is bypassed in the majority of high-stakes scenarios.

- **(b) Confidentiality breaches under Section 5.** In a national security and federal services context, confidentiality is not a peripheral obligation. The client's data environment almost certainly includes controlled unclassified information, sensitive federal program data, or information subject to agency-specific handling requirements. A confidentiality breach in this environment does not produce a dispute measured in months of fees. It produces regulatory exposure, contract termination, reputational damage, and potential downstream federal liability. Removing the cap from confidentiality breaches means the most catastrophic single-event exposure in this engagement is entirely uncapped.

- **(c) IP infringement and misappropriation.** The IP carve-out is bilateral. Given that Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client subject to a retained license for Vendor Pre-Existing IP, and that Section 4.3 grants only a non-exclusive license to embedded Vendor IP, there is a structural ambiguity about what constitutes Vendor Pre-Existing IP and what has been assigned. Any dispute about that boundary — which is a foreseeable dispute in any software services engagement — falls outside the cap entirely. For the client, this means that if a third party later claims the Deliverables infringe their IP, the resulting exposure (which flows through the Section 9.1(b) indemnity, itself carved out under 8.2(a)) is doubly uncapped.

- **(d) Gross negligence and willful misconduct.** This carve-out is defensible in isolation — courts frequently decline to enforce caps against willful misconduct as a matter of public policy, and including it explicitly avoids that ambiguity. The concern here is cumulative: when combined with the other four carve-outs, it eliminates the cap across the full spectrum of severity, from ordinary negligence (capped) to gross negligence (uncapped), with no gradation in between that meaningfully protects the client in a serious incident.

- **(e) Fees, expenses, and taxes under Section 3.** This carve-out is asymmetric in practical effect. It benefits only Vendor. Client has no equivalent obligation that would be carved out in its favor. The result is that Vendor's fee recovery is uncapped and not subject to the consequential damages exclusion, while Client's recovery for service failures is capped. This is a one-sided carve-out embedded in bilateral language.

#### What the cap actually governs

After removing indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, willful misconduct, and fee recovery, the cap applies to: ordinary breach of contract claims that do not involve IP, do not involve confidentiality, do not attract a third-party claim, and do not rise to the level of gross negligence. In a federal services engagement, that residual category is narrow. The cap provides real protection only in the least consequential disputes. It provides no protection in the disputes that matter.

This is the structural problem I have seen cause harm in practice. The CFO reads the cap number and builds it into risk modeling. The lawyers read the carve-outs and know the number is fictional for any dispute above a certain threshold. The gap between those two readings is where exposure lives.

### Section 9 — Indemnification: Scope of Vendor Trigger

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of "Vendor's performance of the Services." This is an unusually broad trigger. It does not require that Vendor be at fault, negligent, or in breach — only that the claim arise out of performance. In a federal contracting environment where subcontractors, teaming partners, and government agencies may all generate third-party claims touching on Vendor's work, this language could be read to impose indemnification obligations on Vendor for claims that are partially or primarily attributable to Client's own conduct or to third-party actions outside Vendor's control.

The standard formulation limits this trigger to claims arising from Vendor's *negligent or wrongful* performance, or from Vendor's *breach*. Section 9.1(c) separately covers breach, but 9.1(a) as drafted creates an independent, fault-free indemnification obligation tied to performance alone. Vendor's counsel will likely push back on this at some point. More importantly, because indemnification is carved out of the cap under 8.2(a), this broad trigger creates uncapped exposure for Vendor on a fault-free basis. That asymmetry should be resolved before execution, not in arbitration.

### Section 10.2 — Arbitration: Federal Contracting Conflict Risk

The mandatory arbitration clause requires all disputes to be resolved by AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware, with a final and binding award. This provision requires evaluation in light of the client's federal contracting obligations before execution.

Depending on the nature of the client's government contracts, certain disputes — particularly those involving allegations of fraud, false claims, or violations of federal procurement regulations — may be subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction or agency dispute resolution procedures that cannot be contractually waived. A blanket arbitration clause that purports to cover "any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement" may conflict with those obligations or create ambiguity about the forum for disputes with a federal nexus.

This is not a reason to reject arbitration categorically. It is a reason to add a carve-out preserving the client's ability to comply with federal dispute resolution requirements, and to confirm with the client's federal contracting counsel that no applicable agency regulations or prime contract flow-downs prohibit or limit arbitration of subcontract disputes.

---

### Section 4.3 — Pre-Existing IP License: Scope Limitation Risk

The license granted to Client for Vendor Pre-Existing IP embedded in Deliverables is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This restriction creates a practical problem: if the client needs to modify, update, or integrate the Deliverables with other systems — which is a routine operational need in a federal services context — any such use that goes beyond the Deliverables as originally delivered may fall outside the license scope. The client would then be using Vendor Pre-Existing IP without a license, creating an infringement exposure that is, under Section 8.2(c), entirely uncapped.

The license grant should be expanded to cover use, modification, and integration of the Deliverables in connection with Client's business operations, not merely use of the Deliverables in their delivered form.

---

## Required Changes Before Execution

**Priority 1 — Section 8.2 carve-outs:** The carve-outs must be restructured so that the cap provides meaningful residual protection. At minimum: (a) the indemnification carve-out should be narrowed to third-party IP infringement claims only, not all indemnification obligations; (b) the confidentiality carve-out should be subject to a separate, negotiated sublimit rather than fully uncapped; and (c) the fee carve-out in 8.2(e) should either be made bilateral or removed, as it currently benefits only Vendor.

**Priority 2 — Section 9.1(a):** Narrow the indemnification trigger from "arising out of Vendor's performance of the Services" to "arising out of Vendor's negligent or wrongful performance of the Services" to require fault as a predicate.

**Priority 3 — Section 10.2:** Add a carve-out preserving compliance with applicable federal dispute resolution requirements and confirm no prime contract flow-down conflicts with mandatory arbitration.

**Priority 4 — Section 4.3:** Expand the Pre-Existing IP license to cover use, modification, and integration of Deliverables in connection with Client's business operations, not solely use as incorporated in the delivered form.
