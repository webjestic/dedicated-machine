**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection the four categories — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — that account for virtually every high-stakes dispute that arises in a federal contractor engagement of this type. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary commercial claims that rarely produce significant exposure on their own. For Meridian, this means the agreement presents a cap that will not apply at the moment it is most needed. Three additional provisions compound that risk and require revision before execution.

---

### Issue 1 — The Liability Cap Is Effectively Illusory

**Section 8.1 / 8.2**

The cap is twelve months of fees paid. That number is stated clearly. What is not stated clearly — but becomes apparent the moment you read Section 8.2 — is that the cap does not govern indemnification (8.2(a)), confidentiality breaches (8.2(b)), IP claims (8.2(c)), gross negligence or willful misconduct (8.2(d)), or unpaid fees (8.2(e)).

In a federal services engagement, the disputes that generate material financial exposure are IP ownership disputes, confidentiality breaches involving government data, and indemnification obligations triggered by third-party claims. Those are precisely the categories this cap excludes. The residual category of disputes that the cap actually governs — ordinary contractual performance claims that do not rise to gross negligence and do not involve IP or confidentiality — represents a narrow band of commercial risk that rarely produces catastrophic exposure on its own.

The cap as written provides Meridian with the appearance of bounded exposure while leaving its actual exposure in the most dangerous categories entirely unlimited. That is not a balanced commercial agreement. That is a cap that functions as a marketing statement.

**Required change:** Either (a) bring indemnification and IP claims within the cap at an elevated ceiling — commonly two to three times annual contract value — or (b) negotiate mutual sublimits for each carved-out category so that no single category of claim carries genuinely unlimited exposure. The current structure is not acceptable for execution.

---

### Issue 2 — Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Asymmetric

**Section 9.1(a)**

Vendor's indemnification obligation covers third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." That language is not limited to Vendor's negligence or breach. It is triggered by the act of performance itself. A third-party claim arising from work Vendor performed correctly, on Client's instructions, using Client-provided specifications, could fall within this indemnification as drafted.

In a government contracting context, where Meridian may be directing Vendor's work product toward federal systems or end users, this creates a scenario where Meridian directs the work and Vendor indemnifies the consequences. That is not a reasonable allocation of risk, and it is not what the parties likely intend.

Compare Section 9.1(a) to Section 9.1(d), which correctly limits indemnification to gross negligence or willful misconduct for that specific category. The same limiting principle should apply to performance-based indemnification.

**Required change:** Revise Section 9.1(a) to limit Vendor's indemnification for performance-related claims to those arising from Vendor's negligence, breach, or failure to perform in accordance with the Agreement. Remove the unqualified "arising out of or related to performance" trigger.

---

### Issue 3 — IP Assignment Is Contingent and Creates Operational Risk

**Section 4.2**

Assignment of Deliverables to Client is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." Until that condition is satisfied, ownership of the Deliverables remains with Vendor. In a federal engagement, Meridian may be delivering work product to a government client under a prime contract before all invoices under this MSA are resolved. If a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — Vendor retains ownership of materials that Meridian has already delivered to the government.

This is not a theoretical risk. It is the exact scenario that produces disputes about who owns what at the moment a contract relationship deteriorates. The contingent assignment also interacts badly with Section 8.2(c), which carves IP claims out of the liability cap entirely. A payment dispute that triggers a Vendor IP ownership claim is now an uncapped liability event.

**Required change:** Replace the contingent assignment with an immediate assignment upon creation, with Vendor retaining a security interest in unpaid fees enforceable through separate contractual remedies. Alternatively, negotiate a license-back structure that gives Meridian full operational rights to Deliverables during any payment dispute period. Do not execute with a contingent assignment in a federal delivery context.

---

### Issue 4 — Confidentiality Survival Is Insufficient for Federal Contractor Context

**Section 5.3**

The five-year confidentiality survival period is standard in commercial agreements. It is not adequate here. Meridian operates in national security and federal services. Information exchanged under this agreement may include data subject to federal handling requirements — CUI, FOUO, or information governed by agency-specific security protocols — that carry obligations extending well beyond five years and in some cases indefinitely.

A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations creates a defined date on which Vendor's obligation to protect potentially sensitive federal information expires. That date may arrive while Meridian is still performing under related government contracts, still subject to federal oversight, or still bound by its own obligations to the government regarding that information.

**Required change:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of (a) ten years or (b) the period required by applicable law or regulation for information subject to federal handling requirements. Add explicit language requiring compliance with applicable federal data handling obligations, including CUI requirements, to the extent any such information is exchanged under the Agreement.

---

### Additional Observation — Arbitration Venue Creates Practical Friction

**Section 10.2**

Binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is not objectionable as a legal matter, but it warrants flagging. If Meridian's operations and personnel are not Delaware-based, mandatory arbitration in Wilmington imposes travel and logistical costs on every dispute regardless of magnitude. AAA Commercial Rules also carry filing fees and arbitrator compensation costs that can make small-to-mid-range disputes disproportionately expensive to pursue.

This is not a blocking issue, but Meridian should consider whether to negotiate a carve-out for claims below a defined threshold — typically $100,000 to $250,000 — permitting resolution in a court of competent jurisdiction without mandatory arbitration. This is a standard ask and reasonable counterparties accept it.

---

### Summary of Required Changes Before Execution

| Priority | Section | Issue |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | 8.1 / 8.2 | Cap is illusory — carve-outs cover all material risk categories |
| **Critical** | 4.2 | Contingent IP assignment creates ownership gap in federal delivery context |
| **High** | 9.1(a) | Vendor indemnification scope untethered from fault |
| **High** | 5.3 | Confidentiality survival inadequate for federal contractor context |
| **Advisory** | 10.2 | Arbitration venue and cost structure warrants review |

Do not execute this agreement as presented. The limitation of liability clause, as structured, does not provide the protection it represents. That gap will not be visible until a dispute arises — which is precisely when it will matter most.
