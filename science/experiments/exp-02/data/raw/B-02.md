**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection the four categories of dispute — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — that are most likely to generate material exposure in a federal contractor engagement, leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary commercial disputes where the stakes are comparatively low. For a national security and federal services context, where confidentiality breaches and IP disputes carry consequences that can dwarf contract value, executing this agreement means accepting unlimited bilateral exposure in precisely the scenarios that matter most. Three additional provisions require correction before execution: the IP license in Section 4.3 is non-exclusive and unassignable, which creates downstream problems if Meridian needs to transfer rights to a government client or successor; the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 mandates Wilmington, Delaware as the seat, which may conflict with federal venue requirements or agency-specific dispute resolution obligations that govern Meridian's prime contracts; and Section 9.1's indemnification scope — covering all third-party claims arising out of Vendor's "performance of the Services" without a materiality threshold or carve-out for Client-directed work — is broad enough to pull Vendor into indemnity obligations for disputes that originate from Client's own instructions or specifications, which is an asymmetry Meridian should not accept going into a government engagement where the scope of directed work is substantial.
