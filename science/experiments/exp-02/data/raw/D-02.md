**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and contains several well-drafted provisions, but presents four issues that require resolution before execution is appropriate for a federal contractor engagement. Two of those issues are significant enough to constitute unacceptable risk in the national security and federal services context in which Meridian Federal Partners operates.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### ✅ Provisions That Are Acceptable As Drafted

**Section 2.1 — Performance Standard**
"Professional and workmanlike manner consistent with industry standards" is a standard commercial formulation. Acceptable.

**Section 2.3 — Subcontractors**
Vendor retains responsibility for subcontractor performance and compliance. This is the correct structure. No flow-down language is required at the MSA level provided SOWs address it for sensitive engagements.

**Section 4.1–4.3 — Intellectual Property**
The assignment structure is commercially standard. The payment condition in Section 4.2 is noted — Meridian should ensure invoices are not allowed to age in a way that creates a gap in IP ownership. The license back to Vendor Pre-Existing IP in Section 4.3 is appropriately scoped ("solely as incorporated in the Deliverables"). Acceptable.

**Section 6.1–6.2 — Representations and Warranties**
The IP non-infringement warranty in Section 6.2 is qualified by "knowingly," which is a vendor-favorable limitation but within the range of what is commercially negotiable and not disqualifying on its own. Acceptable with awareness.

**Section 8.2 — Liability Cap Exceptions**
The carve-outs for indemnification obligations, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees are well-structured and represent a balanced exception framework. This is one of the stronger provisions in the agreement.

**Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure**
The settlement consent requirement in the final sentence of Section 9.3 is appropriately protective. Acceptable.

**Section 10.4–10.8 — General Provisions**
Boilerplate is clean and standard. No issues.

### ⚠️ Issues Requiring Resolution

---

#### Issue 1 — CRITICAL: Mandatory Arbitration Clause Conflicts with Federal Contractor Obligations (Section 10.2)

**The Problem:**

Section 10.2 requires that *all* disputes be resolved by binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules, with no exceptions. This is a blanket, unqualified arbitration mandate.

For a federal contractor operating in the national security space, this creates a direct structural conflict. Certain categories of disputes that arise in this context — including those implicating False Claims Act exposure, federal regulatory enforcement, government audit rights, or disputes in which the U.S. Government has an interest — are not properly resolved in private arbitration. Courts have consistently held that mandatory arbitration clauses cannot compel arbitration of FCA claims, and federal agencies retain independent rights that no private arbitration clause can extinguish.

More practically: if a dispute arises that touches on a government contract, a classified deliverable, or a federal compliance obligation, Meridian may find itself in a posture where it is contractually obligated to arbitrate a matter that cannot, legally or practically, be arbitrated. That creates procedural paralysis at the worst possible moment.

**Required Change:**

The arbitration clause must include an explicit carve-out for: (a) disputes in which the U.S. Government is a necessary or interested party; (b) claims arising under federal law where arbitration is prohibited or restricted by statute or regulation; and (c) injunctive or emergency relief in aid of arbitration. Meridian should also evaluate whether it prefers a litigation fallback for federal-nexus disputes rather than a modified arbitration structure.

---

#### Issue 2 — CRITICAL: Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Context (Section 5.3)

**The Problem:**

Section 5.3 limits confidentiality obligations to five (5) years post-termination. In a commercial software engagement between private parties, five years is a standard and often acceptable term. In a national security and federal services context, it is not.

Information exchanged in this engagement may include: system architecture for federal IT infrastructure, personnel security information, details of government program operations, or technical data subject to export control or classification-adjacent handling requirements. Five years is insufficient to protect this category of information. Some of it may have sensitivity that extends indefinitely. The agreement as written would permit DataStream to freely disclose Meridian's most sensitive operational information five years after the contract ends — including to competitors, foreign nationals, or in litigation — with no contractual recourse.

The checklist notation that confidentiality obligations are "present (5-year survival)" treats this as a completed item. It is not. Presence of a survival clause is not the same as adequacy of that clause.

**Required Change:**

Confidentiality obligations for information that is: (a) classified or classification-adjacent; (b) subject to export control regulations (EAR/ITAR); (c) related to government program operations; or (d) designated as sensitive by the applicable government customer — should survive indefinitely or for the duration of any applicable regulatory requirement, whichever is longer. At minimum, a tiered survival structure should be negotiated, with a longer or indefinite tail for identified sensitive categories.

---

#### Issue 3 — SIGNIFICANT: No Federal Flow-Down or Regulatory Compliance Obligation (Missing Provision)

**The Problem:**

This agreement contains no provision requiring DataStream to comply with federal contractor regulatory obligations that flow down from Meridian's prime contracts. This is not a gap that can be addressed at the SOW level alone. The MSA governs the entire relationship and establishes the baseline compliance framework.

Depending on the nature of Meridian's federal engagements, DataStream may be required to comply with: FAR/DFARS clauses (including cybersecurity requirements under DFARS 252.204-7012), CMMC requirements if handling Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), FedRAMP authorization requirements if providing cloud services, and applicable security clearance and background investigation requirements for personnel.

None of these obligations appear anywhere in this agreement. Section 2.1 references "applicable industry standards," which does not capture federal regulatory requirements. If DataStream suffers a cybersecurity incident involving CUI and there is no contractual obligation requiring DFARS-compliant incident reporting, Meridian bears the regulatory exposure with no contractual remedy against its vendor.

**Required Change:**

Add a Federal Compliance provision requiring Vendor to: (a) comply with all applicable federal laws, regulations, and contract flow-down requirements identified in applicable SOWs; (b) maintain required certifications (CMMC, FedRAMP, or other) as specified; (c) report cybersecurity incidents within required timeframes; and (d) cooperate with government audits and investigations. This provision should be expressly included in the survival clause.

---

#### Issue 4 — MODERATE: IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates Ownership Gap (Section 4.2)

**The Problem:**

Section 4.2 conditions IP assignment on "full payment of all fees." This means that until every invoice is paid in full, ownership of the Deliverables remains with DataStream. In a federal contractor context, this creates a scenario where Meridian may have delivered work product to a government customer — work product that Meridian does not yet legally own because of a disputed or delayed invoice. The government customer's rights, and Meridian's obligations to that customer, do not pause while a payment dispute is resolved.

This is not a disqualifying provision, but it is a risk that Meridian's finance and contracts teams must actively manage. It also creates leverage for DataStream in any fee dispute that is disproportionate to the underlying commercial disagreement.

**Required Change or Clarification:**

Negotiate for IP assignment to occur upon delivery of the Deliverables, with a license-back to DataStream for use pending full payment, rather than conditioning assignment on payment. Alternatively, ensure that Meridian's prime contract obligations do not require it to convey IP rights to the government on a timeline that could conflict with this payment condition.
