# CONTRACT REVIEW MEMORANDUM

**Document:** Master Services Agreement — DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC
**Reviewed By:** Senior Corporate Attorney, obotix.one
**Classification:** Pre-Execution Review — Federal Contractor Engagement

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it carries four material deficiencies that create unacceptable exposure for a federal contractor client operating in the national security space. The most critical issues are: (1) a confidentiality survival period that is facially inadequate for classified or CUI-adjacent engagements; (2) an IP assignment conditioned on full payment that creates a gap in government delivery obligations; (3) an arbitration clause that may conflict with federal dispute resolution requirements and lacks a carve-out for emergency injunctive relief; and (4) a vendor indemnification trigger scoped to "gross negligence or willful misconduct" that leaves ordinary negligence — the most common failure mode — unindemnified. None of these issues are fatal to the deal, but each must be resolved before execution.

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Issue 1: Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for This Engagement

**Provision:** Section 5.3

**What It Says:**
> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**

This is the single most dangerous provision in the agreement for a federal contractor client. Five years is a commercially standard survival period for ordinary commercial engagements. It is not adequate here.

In a national security or federal services context, the information exchanged under this agreement may include:

- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) governed by 32 C.F.R. Part 2002 and NIST SP 800-171, which imposes no sunset on protection obligations;
- Information subject to ITAR (22 C.F.R. Parts 120–130), which has no contractual expiration;
- Proprietary federal program data with classification-adjacent sensitivity;
- Personally Identifiable Information (PII) subject to the Privacy Act of 1974, which does not expire on a five-year clock.

A vendor who receives sensitive federal program information today is released from all contractual confidentiality obligations in five years. That is not a protection. That is a scheduled disclosure authorization.

The checklist notation that confidentiality obligations are "present (5-year survival)" is technically accurate but functionally misleading. The presence of a survival clause is not the same as an adequate survival clause.

**Required Fix:**

Replace Section 5.3 with tiered survival language:

> *"Confidentiality obligations shall survive termination of this Agreement: (a) indefinitely with respect to any information that constitutes a trade secret under applicable law; (b) for the duration required by applicable federal law or regulation with respect to any Controlled Unclassified Information, ITAR-controlled data, or other federally regulated information; and (c) for a period of seven (7) years with respect to all other Confidential Information."*

Additionally, insert a new Section 5.4:

> *"Notwithstanding any other provision of this Agreement, each party's obligations with respect to Confidential Information that is subject to federal law or regulation, including but not limited to CUI, ITAR-controlled technical data, or information subject to the Privacy Act, shall be governed by and comply with all applicable federal requirements, which shall control in the event of any conflict with this Agreement."*

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Issue 2: IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates a Federal Delivery Gap

**Provision:** Section 4.2

**What It Says:**
> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**

The payment-conditioned assignment is a standard vendor-protective mechanism in commercial agreements. In a federal contractor context, it is a structural trap.

Here is the failure scenario: Client delivers a Deliverable to a federal agency under a prime contract or task order. A payment dispute arises between Client and Vendor — not uncommon in government contracting, where invoicing cycles, contract modifications, and funding delays are routine. Vendor asserts that because full payment has not been received, no assignment has occurred, and Client does not own the Deliverable it has already delivered to the government.

The federal agency now holds a Deliverable to which Client may not have clear title. This creates:

- A potential breach of the prime contract (which almost certainly required delivery of government-owned or properly licensed work product);
- Exposure under the False Claims Act if Client represented to the government that it owned or had rights to deliver the work;
- A leverage position for Vendor that is wholly disproportionate to any payment dispute.

The payment condition also interacts badly with Section 8.2(e), which carves out fee obligations from the liability cap. Vendor can simultaneously hold title to Deliverables already delivered to the government and pursue uncapped recovery of fees. That is an asymmetric and unacceptable risk position.

**Required Fix:**

Remove the payment condition from the assignment trigger. Replace with:

> *"Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables upon creation. Client's obligation to pay fees is a separate covenant, and Vendor's remedy for non-payment is a breach of contract claim, not retention of title to Deliverables."*

If Vendor insists on payment protection, the appropriate mechanism is a security interest with a perfected lien, not a title-retention condition that creates downstream federal delivery risk.

### 🟠 SIGNIFICANT — Issue 3: Vendor Indemnification Scope Excludes Ordinary Negligence

**Provision:** Section 9.1(d)

**What It Says:**
> *"...arising out of or related to...the gross negligence or willful misconduct of Vendor or its subcontractors."*

**The Problem:**

Section 9.1 indemnifies Client for third-party claims arising from Vendor's performance of Services (9.1(a)), IP infringement (9.1(b)), and breach of the Agreement (9.1(c)). These are reasonable. However, Section 9.1(d) limits the negligence-based indemnification trigger to *gross* negligence or willful misconduct.

This means that if Vendor's ordinary negligence — a data handling error, a misconfigured system, a subcontractor's careless access to a federal system — causes a third-party claim against Client, Vendor has no indemnification obligation under 9.1(d). Client would need to rely on 9.1(a) (performance of Services) or 9.1(c) (breach of Agreement) to capture that scenario, and those theories may not be available depending on how the claim is framed.

In a federal services context, the most likely third-party claims are:

- A government agency asserting that a Vendor error caused a security incident;
- A subcontractor or downstream party asserting harm from negligent service delivery;
- A regulatory body pursuing Client for a Vendor-caused compliance failure.

None of these require gross negligence. Ordinary negligence is sufficient, and it is the most common failure mode.

The gross negligence limitation in 9.1(d) is also asymmetric: Section 9.2(c) (Client indemnification) uses the same "gross negligence or willful misconduct" standard for Client, which is appropriate because Client is the buyer. Applying the same standard to Vendor is not appropriate because Vendor is the party performing the work and creating the risk.

**Required Fix:**

Amend Section 9.1(d) to read:

> *"(d) the negligence or willful misconduct of Vendor or its subcontractors."*

Remove the "gross" qualifier from Vendor's indemnification obligation only. Client's standard in 9.2(c) may remain at gross negligence, as Client is not the performing party.

### 🟠 SIGNIFICANT — Issue 4: Mandatory Arbitration Clause Creates Federal Jurisdiction Conflict and Lacks Injunctive Relief Carve-Out

**Provision:** Section 10.2

**What It Says:**
> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration...The arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding and may be entered as a judgment in any court of competent jurisdiction."*

**The Problem:**

This clause has two distinct deficiencies.

**4(a) — Federal Jurisdiction Conflict:**

The agreement is silent on federal contract flow-down obligations, mandatory dispute resolution clauses in prime contracts, and the Contract Disputes Act (41 U.S.C. §§ 7101–7109). If Client's prime contract with a federal agency contains a mandatory disputes clause — which virtually all FAR-based contracts do — and a dispute arises that touches on federal contract performance, the arbitration clause in this MSA may be unenforceable as applied to that dispute, or worse, may create a procedural conflict that delays resolution at a critical moment.

The clause also does not address what happens when a dispute involves federal information, classified data, or CUI. Arbitration proceedings administered by the AAA are not cleared environments. Conducting arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware involving sensitive federal program information creates a potential security and regulatory compliance problem that the parties have not addressed.

**4(b) — No Injunctive Relief Carve-Out:**

The clause contains no carve-out for emergency injunctive or equitable relief. In a federal contractor context, the scenarios requiring immediate injunctive relief are not hypothetical:

- Vendor threatens to disclose CUI or proprietary federal program data;
- Vendor continues to access federal systems after termination;
- A subcontractor begins using Client's IP in an unauthorized manner.

In each of these scenarios, Client needs to be in court within hours, not weeks. The AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules do provide for emergency relief, but the process is slower and less certain than direct court access. The agreement should expressly preserve each party's right to seek emergency injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute.

**Required Fix:**

> *"Notwithstanding the foregoing: (a) either party may seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending arbitration, without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute; and (b) to the extent any dispute arises under or relates to a federal prime contract subject to the Contract Disputes Act or other mandatory federal dispute resolution mechanism, such dispute shall be resolved in accordance with the applicable federal requirements, which shall
