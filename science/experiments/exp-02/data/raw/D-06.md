**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted at this stage — the liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately carved out, the IP assignment is conditioned on payment, and the indemnification procedure includes a reasonable settlement consent requirement. However, several provisions create unacceptable exposure for a federal contractor engagement that must be resolved before execution: the mandatory arbitration clause in Section 10.2 conflicts with likely federal contract flow-down requirements and could impair Meridian's ability to satisfy dispute resolution obligations owed to the government; the five-year confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is materially inadequate for a national security context where CUI, ITAR-controlled data, or classified adjacents may be in scope; and the subcontracting authorization in Section 2.3 is insufficiently conditioned, permitting Vendor to engage subcontractors without prior written Client approval, background check requirements, or security clearance verification — a serious gap when the underlying work may touch sensitive federal systems or data.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 HIGH PRIORITY — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration / Dispute Resolution**

*Issue:* Binding arbitration administered by AAA with venue in Wilmington, Delaware is standard in commercial agreements but problematic here. Federal prime contracts frequently contain mandatory dispute resolution clauses, Disputes Act procedures (41 U.S.C. § 7103), or flow-down requirements that govern how disputes touching government work must be handled. A blanket arbitration clause in a subvendor agreement could:

- Conflict directly with flow-down dispute clauses from Meridian's prime contract;
- Prevent Meridian from joining or coordinating proceedings if the government becomes involved in a related dispute;
- Create confidentiality obligations through arbitration that conflict with government audit rights or congressional oversight obligations.

*Required Change:* Add a carve-out exempting disputes that implicate Meridian's obligations under any prime contract or government-mandated dispute process. At minimum, include language such as: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, nothing in this Section shall require arbitration of any dispute to the extent it arises under or is governed by the terms of a U.S. Government prime contract, task order, or applicable federal procurement regulation."* Counsel should also confirm whether Meridian's prime contract contains a flow-down clause that would supersede this provision by operation of law — if so, that should be stated expressly in the MSA.

---

**Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization**

*Issue:* As drafted, Vendor has unilateral authority to engage any subcontractor, subject only to the obligation that Vendor "remains responsible for subcontractor performance." This is commercially standard but operationally dangerous in a federal services context. The provision contains no requirement for:

- Prior written Client approval before engaging subcontractors;
- Verification that subcontractors hold required security clearances or are eligible for access to federal systems;
- Flow-down of security, compliance, or confidentiality obligations (including NIST 800-171, CMMC, or CUI handling requirements if applicable);
- Restrictions on foreign nationals or foreign-owned entities as subcontractors.

A vendor performing services on federal systems could introduce a foreign-national subcontractor or an entity with unresolved FOCI issues, and this clause would provide no contractual basis to object after the fact.

*Required Change:* Revise to require prior written Client approval for any subcontractor engagement. Add affirmative obligations that subcontractors: (a) are U.S. persons or otherwise cleared/eligible as required by applicable law or the prime contract; (b) execute confidentiality and security agreements at least as protective as this MSA; and (c) are bound by all applicable compliance obligations flowing from the prime contract. Vendor's responsibility for subcontractor performance is necessary but not sufficient — approval rights must be retained by Client.

---

**Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival Period**

*Issue:* A five-year post-termination survival period is standard in commercial agreements. It is not adequate here. In a federal contractor engagement, Vendor may receive or generate information that includes:

- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) subject to 32 C.F.R. Part 2002;
- Export-controlled technical data under ITAR (22 C.F.R. Parts 120–130) or EAR;
- Sensitive government program information with indefinite protection requirements;
- Personally Identifiable Information (PII) subject to the Privacy Act or agency-specific handling rules.

None of these categories expire in five years. A vendor who receives CUI in Year 1 and terminates in Year 2 would have contractual confidentiality obligations that lapse in Year 7 — potentially while the underlying government program remains active and classified.

*Required Change:* Revise Section 5.3 to provide that confidentiality obligations with respect to any information subject to applicable law, regulation, or government-mandated protection requirements (including CUI, ITAR-controlled data, classified information, and PII) survive indefinitely or for the period required by applicable law, whichever is longer. The five-year commercial default may be retained for ordinary business confidential information not subject to regulatory protection requirements, but it must not govern the full scope of information exchanged in this engagement.

---

### 🟡 MODERATE PRIORITY — Significant Risk, Addressable by Clarification or Amendment

---

**Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment**

*Issue:* Assignment of Deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a common vendor-protective mechanism, but it creates a gap: if a payment dispute arises mid-engagement — including a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — Vendor retains ownership of all Deliverables until resolution. In a federal engagement where Deliverables may include software integrated into government systems or operational infrastructure, Meridian could find itself in breach of its prime contract obligations while a payment dispute is pending, with no ownership rights in work already delivered and potentially already in use.

*Required Change:* Negotiate an escrow or conditional assignment mechanism: Deliverables should be assigned upon delivery (or upon substantial completion), with Vendor retaining a security interest or license-back right in the event of non-payment, rather than withholding assignment entirely. Alternatively, define a clear cure period and dispute resolution pathway that preserves Meridian's operational access to Deliverables during any payment dispute.

---

**Section 6.3 — Broad "As Is" Disclaimer**

*Issue:* The disclaimer in Section 6.3 disclaims all implied warranties and provides that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is," subject only to the narrow express warranty in Section 6.2. The Section 6.2 warranty is limited to: (a) professional performance consistent with industry standards; and (b) no *knowing* infringement. The "knowingly" qualifier on the IP warranty is a meaningful limitation — it means Vendor has no obligation with respect to infringement it was unaware of, even if a reasonable investigation would have revealed it.

In a federal engagement, Deliverables may be integrated into mission-critical systems. An "as is" disclaimer combined with a knowledge-qualified IP warranty provides minimal assurance of fitness for the actual use case.

*Recommended Change:* Negotiate removal of the "knowingly" qualifier from Section 6.2(b), or add a representation that Vendor has conducted reasonable diligence regarding third-party IP in the Deliverables. Consider adding a fitness warranty specific to the use case described in each SOW, rather than relying solely on the generic "industry standards" formulation.

---

**Section 9.1 — Scope of Vendor Indemnification**

*Issue:* Vendor's indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(a) covers third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." This is broad, but note that indemnification under Section 9 is carved out from the liability cap under Section 8.2(a). This is the correct structure. However, Section 9.1 does not expressly address indemnification for regulatory violations, data breaches, or security incidents caused by Vendor — categories of loss that are particularly material in a federal services context and that may generate government-imposed fines, audit costs, or remediation obligations that would not fit neatly within "third-party claims."

*Recommended Change:* Add explicit indemnification coverage for: (a) Vendor's failure to comply with applicable data security, CUI handling, or regulatory requirements; and (b) security incidents or data breaches caused by Vendor's systems, personnel, or subcontractors, including costs of notification, remediation, and regulatory response.

---

### 🟢 LOW PRIORITY — Noted for the Record, No Blocking Issue

---

**Section 10.1 — Governing Law (Delaware)**

Delaware governing law is commercially standard and not objectionable on its face. Note that certain federal procurement regulations and statutes will govern by operation of law regardless of the contractual choice of law clause. This should be acknowledged in the agreement — consider adding: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, nothing in this Section shall limit the application of applicable federal law or regulation to the extent required by law."*

---

**Section 7.2 — Termination for Convenience (60-Day Notice)**

Sixty days is reasonable for commercial agreements. In a federal engagement, Meridian's prime contract may permit the government to terminate for convenience on shorter notice, potentially leaving Meridian contractually obligated to DataStream for 60 days after Meridian's own contract has been terminated. Confirm that the notice period is consistent with Meridian's prime contract termination provisions, or negotiate a flow-down termination right that mirrors the government's rights.

---

**Section 3.2 — Late Payment Interest at 1.5% Per Month**

1.5% per month (18% annualized) is at the upper range of commercially acceptable late fees. Not a blocking issue, but flag for the client — in a government engagement where invoice disputes or payment delays may arise from government audit holds or contracting officer disputes, this rate could generate significant accrued interest on amounts that are legitimately in dispute.
