# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

---

## Summary

The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a cap that is structurally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively exclude indemnification, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence and willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees — which is to say, the exceptions swallow every category of dispute that is likely to arise in a professional services engagement of this nature. What remains inside the cap is a narrow residual of ordinary contract disputes where neither party acted badly, no IP was touched, no confidential information was mishandled, and all invoices were paid — a fact pattern that describes a relationship that does not need a cap because nothing went wrong. For a federal contractor operating in the national security space, this structure provides no meaningful ceiling on exposure where it actually matters.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. The Liability Cap Is Functionally Illusory

**Section 8.1** establishes a cap equal to fees paid in the prior twelve months and excludes consequential damages. On its face, this reads as standard commercial protection. The problem is not the cap itself — it is what Section 8.2 removes from it.

**Section 8.2 carve-outs, mapped to dispute probability:**

| Carve-Out | Category Excluded | Likelihood of Arising |
|---|---|---|
| 8.2(a) | All indemnification obligations | High |
| 8.2(b) | All confidentiality breaches | High — especially in national security context |
| 8.2(c) | All IP infringement / misappropriation | High — Vendor is building Deliverables |
| 8.2(d) | Gross negligence / willful misconduct | Medium — but covers the worst outcomes |
| 8.2(e) | All unpaid fees and taxes | Certain — every engagement involves fees |

The residual category that the cap actually governs — disputes that do not involve indemnification, confidentiality, IP, misconduct, or fees — is not a realistic dispute profile for this type of engagement. The cap exists on paper and nowhere else.

This is the specific scenario I have had to explain to a CFO after the fact. The clause looked like protection. It was not.

---

### 2. Confidentiality Carve-Out Is Especially Dangerous Here

Section 8.2(b) removes the cap entirely for confidentiality breaches, with no sub-limit and no floor. In a national security and federal services context, Vendor will have access to sensitive government-related information, client operational data, and potentially information subject to federal handling requirements. An uncapped confidentiality breach exposure — running in both directions — with no defined ceiling is not a balanced commercial term. It is an open-ended liability event.

The five-year survival period in Section 5.3 extends this exposure well beyond contract performance. That is a long window for an uncapped obligation.

**Recommended fix:** Negotiate a sub-cap on confidentiality breach liability — a defined multiple of contract value or an agreed fixed ceiling — rather than leaving it entirely outside the cap structure.

---

### 3. IP Carve-Out Interacts Poorly With Section 4

Section 8.2(c) removes cap protection for any IP infringement or misappropriation claim. Section 4.3 grants Client only a non-exclusive license to Vendor's Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables. The boundary between what constitutes a Deliverable and what constitutes Vendor Pre-Existing IP is not defined with precision — it is resolved by the phrase "incorporated therein," which will be contested in any dispute.

If Vendor later claims that a core functional component of a Deliverable was Pre-Existing IP and that Client's use exceeded the license scope, that claim falls under 8.2(c) — entirely outside the cap, with no consequential damages exclusion either. The combination of a vague IP boundary in Section 4 and an uncapped IP carve-out in Section 8.2 creates a structural trap.

**Recommended fix:** Require Vendor to identify and schedule Pre-Existing IP at SOW execution. Ambiguity in IP ownership should not be resolved against Client in an uncapped exposure environment.

---

### 4. Indemnification Carve-Out Removes the Cap From the Broadest Liability Trigger

Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising from: Services performance, IP infringement, any breach of the Agreement, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. Section 9.2 mirrors this for Client. Both obligations are carved out of the cap entirely under Section 8.2(a).

The practical effect: any third-party claim that touches Vendor's performance — which describes most serious disputes — bypasses the cap entirely through the indemnification pathway. A plaintiff's attorney does not need to plead around the cap. The agreement does it for them.

---

### 5. Section 8.2(e) — Unpaid Fees Are Uncapped

This carve-out is vendor-favorable and asymmetric in effect. Fees, expenses, and taxes owed under Section 3 are excluded from the cap. Combined with the 1.5% monthly interest provision in Section 3.2, a payment dispute on a long-running engagement can accumulate exposure that exceeds the cap's twelve-month baseline before the dispute is even filed.

This carve-out is not inherently unreasonable — vendors legitimately need fee recovery — but in the context of a cap that is already structurally hollow, it adds another layer of uncapped exposure that should be acknowledged and evaluated.

---

### 6. Arbitration Venue and Governing Law — Operational Note

Section 10.2 mandates binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules. For a federal contractor, this raises a practical question: does this forum and governing law create any conflict with federal procurement regulations, agency-specific contract requirements, or applicable federal law that may govern disputes touching on government work product? This agreement does not address that intersection.

This is not a blocking issue on its face, but it warrants a separate review against any applicable FAR provisions or agency-specific requirements before execution.

---

## Required Changes Before Approval

1. **Restructure Section 8.2 to include sub-caps on carve-outs.** Indemnification and confidentiality obligations should carry defined liability ceilings — a reasonable multiple of contract value — rather than operating as fully uncapped exceptions. The current structure does not provide Client with a meaningful liability ceiling in any realistic dispute scenario.

2. **Define the Pre-Existing IP boundary in Section 4.** Require Vendor to schedule Pre-Existing IP at SOW execution. The interaction between the vague IP boundary in Section 4 and the uncapped IP carve-out in Section 8.2(c) creates unacceptable exposure.

3. **Evaluate the confidentiality carve-out in the context of information sensitivity.** Given the national security operating environment, negotiate a defined sub-limit for confidentiality breach liability or, at minimum, a mutual acknowledgment of the uncapped nature of this obligation with appropriate operational controls.

4. **Confirm arbitration clause compatibility with applicable federal procurement requirements.** Verify that mandatory AAA arbitration in Delaware does not conflict with agency-specific dispute resolution requirements or federal law applicable to the underlying work.

---

*This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The limitation of liability structure, as drafted, does not provide the protection it represents itself as providing.*
