# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
**Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Senior Contract Review**

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement is structurally competent and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs at this tier, but it contains several provisions that are materially inadequate or affirmatively dangerous for a federal contractor operating in the national security space. The most critical failures are: (1) a confidentiality survival period that is facially insufficient for classified or CUI-adjacent engagements; (2) an IP assignment conditioned on full payment that creates a gap period of unassigned deliverables with potential federal program implications; and (3) a mandatory binding arbitration clause that conflicts with the dispute resolution frameworks governing many federal contract relationships. These are not cosmetic issues — they are structural vulnerabilities that will surface at the worst possible moment.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. Confidentiality Survival: 5 Years Is Legally Insufficient (§ 5.3)**

The agreement caps confidentiality obligations at five years post-termination. In a federal contractor context — particularly one touching national security, defense, or intelligence-adjacent work — this is a significant exposure gap.

- Many federal information handling requirements (NISPOM, DFARS 252.204-7012, FISMA, and agency-specific security protocols) impose indefinite or substantially longer protection obligations on controlled unclassified information (CUI), covered defense information (CDI), and other sensitive categories.
- A vendor whose contractual confidentiality obligation expires in year six is no longer bound by this agreement's protections, regardless of what the underlying data sensitivity requires.
- The five-year cap also creates a misalignment with the IP survival provisions (§ 4 survives indefinitely), meaning trade secrets embedded in deliverables lose their contractual confidentiality protection while IP ownership rights persist — a structural inconsistency that could be exploited.

**Required Change:** Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of: (a) the period required by applicable federal law, regulation, or agency directive; or (b) ten (10) years. For any information classified or designated as CUI, survival should be indefinite or tied to declassification.

---

**2. IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment Creates a Dangerous Gap (§ 4.2)**

The assignment of deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This is a standard vendor-protective clause in commercial agreements. In a federal contracting context, it is a trap.

- During the period between delivery and full payment, title to deliverables remains with the vendor. If a dispute arises, the vendor terminates, or payment is delayed for any administrative reason, the client holds deliverables it does not legally own.
- If those deliverables are incorporated into a federal program, system, or contract performance, the government's rights may be implicated — and the client could be in breach of its prime contract obligations regarding IP flow-down.
- The "full payment" trigger also creates leverage for the vendor to assert ownership over partially paid deliverables in a dispute scenario, which is precisely when you least want ambiguity.

**Required Change:** Assignment should be effective upon delivery and acceptance of each deliverable, with payment obligations remaining as a separate contractual covenant enforceable independently. Alternatively, a license-back structure that grants full ownership rights upon delivery with a security interest reverting to vendor for non-payment is more commercially defensible than a suspended assignment.

---

**3. Mandatory Binding Arbitration Conflicts with Federal Contractor Dispute Posture (§ 10.2)**

The agreement requires all disputes to be resolved by AAA binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware. This provision is problematic on multiple levels for a federal contractor:

- Federal contractors are frequently subject to the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), agency-specific dispute resolution procedures, or prime contract flow-down clauses that mandate specific forums or procedures. A blanket arbitration clause can conflict directly with these obligations.
- Arbitration forecloses access to federal courts for disputes that may have public law dimensions — including disputes over government data, security incidents, or performance failures on federally funded work.
- The "final and binding" language with no carve-out for emergency injunctive relief is particularly dangerous. If a vendor misappropriates IP or breaches confidentiality, the client cannot seek emergency injunctive relief in court without first arguing around this clause.
- AAA Commercial Rules do not include procedures calibrated to national security sensitivities. Arbitration proceedings involving sensitive federal information create their own disclosure and handling risks.

**Required Change:** At minimum, carve out: (a) the right of either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights; (b) any dispute governed by or arising under federal law or a prime contract dispute resolution clause. Strongly recommend replacing mandatory arbitration with litigation in federal district court, or at minimum a tiered dispute resolution process (negotiation → mediation → litigation).

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — High Risk, Requires Negotiation

---

**4. Vendor IP License Is Use-Restricted in a Way That May Not Survive Program Evolution (§ 4.3)**

The license granted for Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into deliverables is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This restriction means:

- The client cannot extract, modify, or reuse vendor background IP even for purposes directly related to the same federal program, unless it is using the deliverable as a whole.
- As programs evolve, systems are upgraded, or deliverables are integrated into larger architectures, this restriction will create friction and potential infringement exposure.
- In a government contracting context, the government may assert rights to use, modify, and reproduce deliverables under FAR 52.227 clauses. If the client cannot grant those rights because its own license is use-restricted, there is a flow-down failure.

**Required Change:** Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, integrate, and create derivative works of the Vendor Pre-Existing IP to the extent necessary to exercise full rights in the deliverables and fulfill obligations under applicable government contracts.

---

**5. Subcontractor Provision Lacks Security and Compliance Flow-Down Requirements (§ 2.3)**

Section 2.3 permits subcontracting with only a general statement that Vendor "remains responsible" for subcontractor performance. In a national security context, this is inadequate.

- There is no requirement that subcontractors be subject to equivalent security clearance, background investigation, or personnel vetting requirements.
- There is no prohibition on use of foreign nationals, foreign-owned entities, or subcontractors operating in jurisdictions that present counterintelligence risk.
- There is no client approval right over subcontractor selection, which is standard in sensitive federal engagements.
- "Remains responsible" is a performance guarantee, not a security guarantee. The vendor can be financially liable for a subcontractor breach while the underlying security damage is already done.

**Required Change:** Add: (a) client prior written approval for any subcontractor with access to client systems, data, or facilities; (b) mandatory flow-down of all security, confidentiality, and compliance obligations; (c) prohibition on use of subcontractors who are foreign nationals or foreign-owned entities without client consent; (d) right of client to require removal of any subcontractor for security reasons.

---

**6. "Knowingly" Qualifier on IP Non-Infringement Warranty Materially Weakens Protection (§ 6.2)**

The warranty that deliverables "will not knowingly infringe" third-party IP rights is a significantly weakened standard. The word "knowingly" converts an objective warranty into a subjective one — the vendor only breaches if it was actually aware of the infringement.

- This means a vendor who failed to conduct any IP clearance or freedom-to-operate analysis cannot be held in breach, because it never "knew" of the infringement.
- In a federal program context, an IP infringement claim against deliverables can halt program performance, trigger government indemnification demands, and expose the client to substantial liability — none of which is mitigated by the vendor's subjective ignorance.

**Required Change:** Remove "knowingly." The warranty should be that deliverables, as delivered, do not infringe the intellectual property rights of any third party. If vendor insists on a knowledge qualifier, require vendor to represent that it has conducted reasonable IP clearance procedures and is not aware of any infringement risk.

---

**7. Termination for Convenience Does Not Address Federal Program Continuity (§ 7.2)**

The 60-day termination for convenience provision is facially standard but operationally problematic in a federal engagement:

- There is no obligation on Vendor to provide transition assistance, knowledge transfer, or continuity support following notice of termination.
- In a federal program context, a vendor who walks away after 60 days without transition obligations can leave the client unable to perform on its prime contract — with no contractual remedy beyond the general damages framework, which is capped at 12 months of fees.
- There is no provision addressing what happens to government-furnished property, government data, or access credentials upon termination.

**Required Change:** Add a mandatory transition assistance period (minimum 90 days post-termination notice) during which Vendor must provide reasonable knowledge transfer, documentation, and cooperation. Add specific obligations for return or destruction of government data and revocation of system access upon termination.

---

### 🟠 MODERATE — Address if Possible

---

**8. Liability Cap Based on Trailing 12-Month Fees May Be Inadequate for Early-Stage Engagements (§ 8.1)**

The 12-month trailing fee cap is standard commercial language, but it creates a perverse result: in the early months of a contract, when the most significant implementation risks exist, the cap may be nominal. A vendor who causes a catastrophic failure in month two of a multi-year engagement is capped at two months of fees.

**Recommended Change:** Establish a minimum floor for the liability cap — either the total contract value under all active SOWs or a fixed dollar amount — whichever is greater.

---

**9. No Data Security or Incident Notification Obligations**

The agreement contains no provisions addressing: (a) data security standards applicable to vendor handling of client information; (b) breach notification timelines; (c) vendor obligations following a security incident. For a federal contractor, DFARS 252.204-7012 requires 72-hour reporting of cyber incidents to DoD. This agreement is silent on all of it.
