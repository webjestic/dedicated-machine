**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that is statistically likely to arise in a federal services engagement — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap governing only a residual band of ordinary breach claims where damages are typically modest anyway. Three additional provisions require targeted revision before this agreement is acceptable for execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### Section 8 — Limitation of Liability: The Core Problem

Read Section 8.1 in isolation and it looks like standard commercial protection: a consequential damages waiver and a 12-month fee cap. Read Section 8.2 immediately after, and the cap largely disappears.

**What the carve-outs actually remove:**

| Carve-Out | What It Covers in Practice |
|---|---|
| 8.2(a) — Indemnification | Any third-party claim arising from services performance, IP infringement, or breach. This is the most common litigation vector in a services engagement. |
| 8.2(b) — Confidentiality | Any breach of Section 5, which in a national security context can involve classified or CUI-adjacent information with catastrophic downstream exposure. |
| 8.2(c) — IP Rights | Any dispute over ownership, infringement, or misappropriation of IP — directly implicated by Section 4's assignment structure and the Pre-Existing IP carve-out. |
| 8.2(d) — Gross Negligence / Willful Misconduct | Broad enough that opposing counsel will argue gross negligence in virtually any serious performance failure. The standard is not as high as it sounds in practice. |
| 8.2(e) — Fees and Taxes | All amounts owed under Section 3, meaning Vendor's payment claims are entirely uncapped. |

**What remains inside the cap:** Ordinary breach of contract claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or gross negligence — and that do not implicate unpaid fees. In a federal services context, that residual category is nearly empty. The disputes that actually reach litigation or arbitration in this space are IP disputes, confidentiality breaches, indemnification triggers, and performance failures serious enough to be characterized as gross negligence. The cap provides meaningful protection for the category of dispute least likely to occur.

This is not a theoretical concern. If Vendor's subcontractor causes a data incident involving Client's federal systems, the claim will be framed as indemnification under 9.1(a), confidentiality breach under Section 5, and gross negligence under 8.2(d) — simultaneously. All three carve-outs apply. The 12-month fee cap is irrelevant to that dispute.

**Required change:** Negotiate either (1) a meaningful aggregate cap that applies to indemnification and confidentiality obligations — even if set higher than the standard 12-month figure — or (2) sub-caps for specific carve-out categories with defined ceilings. A confidentiality breach cap at 2x or 3x trailing fees is defensible. An unlimited confidentiality obligation in a national security engagement is not. At minimum, 8.2(d) must be narrowed: "gross negligence" should be replaced with "willful misconduct" alone, or gross negligence should be defined to require a heightened showing, because as drafted it creates a pleading-stage escape hatch from the cap in any serious dispute.

### Section 4 — Intellectual Property: Structural Risk in the Assignment

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but carves out Vendor Pre-Existing IP. Section 4.3 grants a license to use that Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."

**The problem:** There is no definition of what constitutes Vendor Pre-Existing IP, no disclosure obligation requiring Vendor to identify Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables at the time of delivery, and no mechanism for Client to assess what it actually owns versus what it is merely licensed to use. In a federal contracting context, this matters for two reasons:

First, if the government requires assignment of all IP in a deliverable — as it often does under FAR clauses — Client cannot assign what it does not own. A deliverable that is 40% Vendor Pre-Existing IP is a deliverable Client cannot fully convey to its federal customer.

Second, the license in 4.3 is non-exclusive and use-restricted. If Vendor is acquired, goes insolvent, or terminates this agreement, Client's continued right to use the Pre-Existing IP embedded in its own deliverables depends on the survival and enforceability of that license grant.

**Required change:** Add a disclosure obligation — Vendor must identify any Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables at or before delivery, in writing. Consider adding a license survival clause that explicitly survives termination and is binding on successors. If federal flow-down requirements apply to any SOW, add a representation that Vendor's Pre-Existing IP licensing structure is compatible with those requirements.

### Section 5 — Confidentiality: Five-Year Survival Is Inadequate for This Context

The five-year confidentiality survival period in Section 5.3 is standard for commercial agreements. It is not appropriate for a national security or federal services context.

Information disclosed in connection with federal engagements — system architectures, personnel data, operational methodologies, access credentials, security protocols — does not become non-sensitive after five years. A vendor who receives this information and faces no confidentiality obligation in year six has received a time-limited obligation that does not match the nature of what was disclosed.

**Required change:** Extend survival to the life of the agreement plus ten years, or add a carve-out providing that Confidential Information that constitutes trade secrets, classified information, or information subject to federal regulatory protection obligations survives indefinitely. The five-year period can remain for ordinary commercial confidential information.

### Section 10.2 — Arbitration: Venue and Process Concerns

Binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules is not inherently problematic. However, two issues warrant attention:

First, there is no carve-out for injunctive or emergency relief. In a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation scenario, Client may need immediate injunctive relief from a court of competent jurisdiction. As drafted, the mandatory arbitration clause could be used to delay or contest emergency court proceedings. Standard practice is to include an explicit carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency equitable relief in court without waiving arbitration rights.

Second, AAA Commercial arbitration is expensive. For a dispute involving a small SOW, the arbitration costs may exceed the value in controversy, effectively denying Client a practical remedy for smaller breaches. Consider adding a threshold — disputes below a defined dollar amount (e.g., $50,000) may be resolved in Delaware courts rather than arbitration — or specifying expedited AAA procedures for lower-value claims.

**Required change:** Add an injunctive relief carve-out. Consider a small-claims threshold or expedited procedures provision.

---

### Items That Are Acceptable As Drafted

- **Section 2.3** (subcontractor responsibility): Vendor retains responsibility for subcontractor performance. Acceptable, though consider adding a flow-down requirement for confidentiality and security obligations specifically.
- **Section 6.2** (IP non-infringement warranty): The "knowingly infringe" qualifier limits this warranty. Acceptable for commercial purposes, but note it does not cover unknowing infringement.
- **Section 9.3** (indemnification procedure): Standard and balanced. The sole-control provision is appropriate with the consent-to-settlement protection included.
- **Section 7.3** (cure period): 30-day cure period for material breach is standard and acceptable.

### Summary of Required Changes

| Priority | Section | Issue |
|---|---|---|
| Critical | 8.1 / 8.2 | Cap is effectively nullified by carve-outs; negotiate sub-caps or narrow exceptions |
| Critical | 8.2(d) | "Gross negligence" carve-out creates pleading-stage escape from cap; narrow to willful misconduct or define standard |
| High | 4.2 / 4.3 | No Pre-Existing IP disclosure obligation; license survival and federal flow-down compatibility unaddressed |
| High | 5.3 | Five-year confidentiality survival inadequate for national security context |
| Moderate | 10.2 | No injunctive relief carve-out; no small-claims threshold or expedited procedure |

This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The limitation of liability structure, as written, provides Meridian with the appearance of capped exposure while leaving its actual exposure in the most probable dispute categories entirely unlimited.
