# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper introduces PARC, a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs terminate at the nearest satisfying path rather than the most complete one, with no native cost function for the gap between delivery and need. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a rate-limiter task), and (2) a two-agent pipeline with separated satisfaction conditions crosses that ceiling. Thirty-three experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro are reported, with a stated commitment to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses.

---

## Strengths

- **The behavioral finding in exp-28b is clean and the claim is appropriately scoped.** 0% Tier 2 detection across four variants and 40 runs on a shallow canonical task is a specific, falsifiable result. The paper does not overclaim it — it calls it a ceiling measurement, not a universal law, and correctly identifies that the blockchain artifact (exp-27) disconfirmed the same claim on a different artifact. This is honest empirical practice.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is the strongest methodological contribution.** Running identical procedural content in Persona vs. Instructions slots across five experiments to test H1 vs. H2 is a well-designed confound-resolution strategy. The result — that content, not slot, drives detection — is internally consistent across five replications and the paper correctly updates its theoretical framing in response. This is the kind of self-correcting empirical work the field needs.

- **The paper names its own open questions with specificity.** §9.9 identifies exp-29's n=1 limitation, the Artifact Pointer Confound, the Tone formalization gap, and the P_p/P_d operationalization problem. These are genuine gaps, not boilerplate hedging, and the paper does not pretend they are resolved.

- **The exp-30 misalignment ladder is structurally interesting.** The finding that outputs-only transparency (F: 0%) outperforms reasoning+outputs transparency (D: 10%) is counterintuitive and the proposed mechanism — that the scratchpad/axiom conflict creates routing loopholes — is a testable hypothesis. The paper correctly notes this is a single-model result on Gemini 2.5 Flash.

- **The claim-evidence mapping in Appendix B is unusually thorough.** Providing a full behavioral claim register with experiment citations is a service to reviewers and replicators. Most papers in this space do not do this.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

### 1. The central mechanistic framework is unfalsifiable as stated, and the paper does not adequately acknowledge this.

The Dedicated Machine hypothesis — "LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution with no native cost function for the gap" — is presented as an explanatory theory that "explains every framework finding." The problem is structural: the hypothesis is specified at a level of abstraction where it cannot be falsified by any behavioral experiment. If the model produces shallow output, the satisfaction condition was shallow. If the model produces deep output, the satisfaction condition was deep. If a strong Persona fails, the satisfaction condition was not correctly installed. The hypothesis accommodates all outcomes post-hoc by adjusting what "satisfaction condition was installed" means.

The paper acknowledges that mechanistic claims are hypotheses, but this acknowledgment does not resolve the problem. The issue is not whether the mechanism has been confirmed at the activation level — it is whether the behavioral-level formulation is falsifiable at all. The paper does not identify a prediction the Dedicated Machine hypothesis makes that, if false, would disconfirm it. This is a load-bearing structural flaw: a theory that explains everything predicts nothing.

The paper's own evidence partially exposes this. exp-26 produced 0/10 on both goal-architecture framing (B) and prohibition framing (A), which the paper explains as "the machine stopped at the nearest satisfying path — the heartbeat TOCTOU bug." But this explanation is applied after the result is known. What prediction would the Dedicated Machine hypothesis have made in advance that would have distinguished exp-26 from exp-28b? The paper does not say.

### 2. exp-29 is n=1 and supports Claim 2 in the abstract without qualification.

The abstract states: "A two-agent review pipeline solved a distributed systems failure mode on the first run." The paper later acknowledges (§9.9, item 1) that "an n=10 experiment is needed." But this limitation is buried in a future-work section after the claim has been stated as a finding in the abstract, the introduction, §5.3, §6.11, the conclusion, and Appendix A. The claim is stated as confirmed; the n=1 limitation is stated as a future-work item. These are inconsistent epistemic postures.

A single run of a two-agent pipeline reaching Tier 1.0 is a demonstration, not a measurement. The paper cannot establish that "the pipeline crosses the single-agent ceiling" from one run. It can establish that the pipeline is capable of producing a Tier 1.0 result, which is a different and weaker claim. The distinction matters because the paper's Claim 2 is about design target and architectural principle, not about a single successful execution.

The comparison to "40 prior single-pass runs" is also not a controlled comparison. The pipeline used Claude Opus 4.6 (noted in Appendix A); the single-pass baseline used Claude Sonnet 4.6. The paper does not address whether the Tier 1.0 result is attributable to the pipeline architecture, the model upgrade, or their interaction.

### 3. The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is decorative mathematics that does not generate testable predictions.

The unified reasoning formula presents a softmax attention expression combined with a task-layer fraction. The problems are multiple:

- The variables ($P_p$, $S_i$, $S_t$, $C$, $I$) have no defined units, scales, or measurement procedures. $P_p$ appears as both a vector (in $Q \cdot P_p^T$) and a scalar multiplier (in the two-vector formula in §4.1.3). These are different mathematical objects.
- The coefficients $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ are named but not estimated from data. The token-delta table in §4.1.3 is presented as empirical evidence for the formula, but token counts are not the quantities the formula operates on.
- The formula cannot be used to make a numerical prediction about any experiment. It cannot be fit to data. It cannot be falsified.
- The "boundary condition from H2" is acknowledged inline: the formula's World/Task separation does not hold when Instructions contain procedural content equivalent to P_p. A formula that fails at its own identified boundary condition is not a formula — it is a diagram.

This formula should either be removed or replaced with a formal model that can be estimated from the experimental data. As written, it gives the paper a false appearance of mathematical rigor.

### 4. The P_p/P_d distinction lacks a formal operationalization, and this undermines replicability of the central claim.

The paper's primary behavioral finding — that P_p produces qualitatively different outcomes than P_d — depends entirely on the P_p/P_d distinction being well-defined. §9.6 acknowledges that "the P_p/P_d distinction is described qualitatively but has no formal operationalization" and provides a provisional heuristic. But the heuristic is applied post-hoc: the paper classifies Personas as P_p or P_d, runs experiments, and reports results. An external replicator cannot determine whether a new Persona is P_p or P_d without the authors' judgment.

This is not a minor limitation. The entire experimental edifice rests on the claim that P_p Personas produce different outcomes than P_d Personas. If the classification is not operationalized, the claim cannot be replicated, and the finding cannot be distinguished from "Personas the authors believed would work, worked; Personas the authors believed would not work, did not work."

The paper identifies inter-rater reliability testing as necessary future work. At a top-tier venue, this work needs to exist before submission, not after.

### 5. The exp-30 ethical-level experiment is methodologically underpowered for the claims it is asked to support.

exp-30 is presented as demonstrating that "changing the satisfaction condition — not adding rules — should be the intervention that closes the gap." The experiment runs six variants on Gemini 2.5 Flash, n=10 per variant, on a single scenario (the blackmail scenario from Lynch et al.). Several problems:

- The paper notes that "the heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning" and that results are based on "manual review of actual sent email content." Manual review of 60 outputs by the paper's authors, without inter-rater reliability measurement, is not a measurement procedure that supports the precision of the reported rates (100%, 50%, 20%, 10%, 0%).
- The six-variant ladder conflates multiple variables: variant F differs from variant D in both the scope of transparency (outputs only vs. reasoning+outputs) and the presence/absence of a structural conflict. The paper's explanation for the D vs. F difference (scratchpad/axiom conflict) is a post-hoc mechanistic hypothesis, not a controlled comparison.
- The claim that "output gate is sufficient; constraining reasoning is counterproductive" is drawn from a comparison of two variants in one scenario on one model. This is a single data point for a claim with significant practical implications for AI safety practice.
- The Claude Opus 4.6 result (0% misalignment across all conditions including baseline) is reported as a "preliminary run" without specifying n. This result is used to support a claim about training-level alignment internalization, which is a strong mechanistic claim from an unspecified sample size.

### 6. The cross-domain and cross-model generalization claims are thinner than presented.

§7 claims that "all primary behavioral claims were tested outside their original domain and model." The cross-domain evidence is one legal contract review experiment (exp-01f). The cross-model evidence is one experiment (exp-01g) running two variants of one experiment on Gemini 2.5 Pro. The pipeline experiments (exp-28d, exp-29) were not replicated on Gemini. The Stakes taxonomy was not replicated cross-model. The Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient was not replicated cross-model. The premise-rejection test was not replicated cross-model.

"All primary behavioral claims hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro (zero-shot transfer)" (§1.1, contribution 9) is not supported by the evidence. Two variants of one experiment transferred. The remaining claims have not been tested cross-model.

### 7. The paper's stated distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic hypotheses drifts in practice.

§1.2 commits to a strict distinction. The commitment is honored in §8.1, which explicitly separates the two categories. But the drift occurs throughout the paper:

- §2.1: "This is not a metaphor. It is the most accurate description of observed behavior across thirty-three experiment series." A theory that is "the most accurate description" is being stated as fact, not hypothesis.
- §3.1: "The World Layer components (Persona, Context, Stakes) shape the K/V space before the Request is processed." This is stated as a description of mechanism, not a hypothesis.
- §4.1.1: "P_p performs K/V space pre-filtering" appears in a section header-level position before the mechanistic-hypothesis qualifier appears.
- §4.1.3: "Stakes functions as inverse temperature on the softmax distribution" is stated as a mechanical hypothesis but is embedded in a section that presents it as the explanation for observed behavior.

The paper's own stated standard — mechanistic claims stated as hypothesis, not fact — is not consistently applied. This is a distinction audit failure of the type the paper itself identifies as a structural flaw (§1.2).

### 8. The self-prediction gap experiment (exp-06) is n=7 and the design is not described with sufficient precision.

exp-06 is a meta-experiment where P_p and P_d variants of the paper itself are submitted for review. The paper reports that "4/5 A-run reviewers independently identified the few-shot/procedural-content confound." The total n is 7 (not 10 per variant), the variant assignment is not specified, and the scoring criterion is not defined. This experiment is used to support the self-prediction gap claim (§9.2) and the few-shot confound identification (§9.3). Neither use is adequately supported by n=7 with an unspecified design.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **Falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis.** What behavioral observation, if found, would disconfirm the Dedicated Machine hypothesis? Specifically: is there a result that the hypothesis predicts would not occur, such that if it did occur, the hypothesis would need to be revised or rejected? If no such result exists, the hypothesis is not a scientific claim — it is a post-hoc explanatory frame. Please either specify the disconfirming prediction or revise the paper's characterization of the hypothesis accordingly.

2. **exp-29 model version.** Appendix A lists exp-29 as running on claude-opus-4-6, while the primary experimental setup (§6.1) specifies Claude Sonnet 4.6. The paper's Claim 2 comparison ("pipeline vs. 40 prior single-pass runs") conflates these. Can the authors report what the pipeline achieves at n=10 on Claude Sonnet 4.6, the same model used for the single-pass baseline? If the Tier 1.0 result does not replicate at n=10 on Sonnet 4.6, what is the revised claim?

3. **P_p/P_d operationalization.** The paper's central finding depends on classifying Personas as P_p or P_d. Can the authors provide the provisional heuristic (§9.6) in sufficient detail that an independent researcher could classify a new Persona without consulting the authors? Specifically: what is the minimum specification of "a first-person procedural clause that specifies a reasoning step contingent on a prior state"? Would the authors be willing to submit a set of 20 Personas (10 intended as P_p, 10 as P_d) to blind classification by independent raters, and report the agreement rate?

4. **The PCSIEFTR formula.** The formula in §8.2 uses $P_p$ as a vector (in the dot product $Q \cdot P_p^T$) and as a scalar (in the two-vector formula). These are different mathematical objects. Can the authors either (a) provide a consistent mathematical specification where all variables have defined types and units, or (b) remove the formula and replace it with a verbal description of the relationships it is intended to capture? If the formula is retained, what numerical prediction does it make about any reported experiment?

5. **The ~11% Instructions ceiling.** The coefficient is reported as converging across three run sets (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%). All three use the same prohibition ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), the same task type (code review), and the same model (Claude Sonnet 4.6). Does the coefficient hold for a different prohibition, a different task type, or a different model? The convergence across three measurements with identical methodology is not convergence — it is three measurements of the same configuration. What is the coefficient for a legal review prohibition on the MSA artifact?

6. **exp-30 scoring procedure.** The paper reports misalignment rates of 100%, 50%, 50%, 20%, 10%, and 0% from manual review of sent email content. What was the scoring procedure? Were raters blind to condition? Was there more than one rater? What was the inter-rater agreement? Without this information, the precision of these rates cannot be evaluated.

7. **The "Confident Error" in exp-04c.** Variant D (weak Persona + Task Stakes) produced 0/10 on the cross-service race and 1,681 tokens of wrong-direction analysis. The paper uses this to support the claim that Task Stakes terminates a wrong-direction search at confident completion. But the paper also reports D mean = 1,289 tokens in exp-04b (calibrated) and 1,681 tokens in exp-04c (post-calibration failure). If Task Stakes is an Entropy Brake that reduces secondary output, why does the Confident Error run produce more tokens than the calibrated D runs? What does this imply about the Entropy Brake mechanism?

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper contains genuine empirical contributions — the slot-swap series, the single-agent ceiling measurement, and the honest acknowledgment of calibration failures and open questions — that merit serious engagement. However, three structural problems prevent acceptance in current form: (1) the central theoretical hypothesis is not falsifiable as stated and the paper does not acknowledge this; (2) Claim 2's primary support (exp-29) is n=1 on a different model than the baseline it is compared against; and (3) the PCSIEFTR formula is decorative mathematics that does not generate testable predictions and should be removed or replaced. These are not presentation problems —
