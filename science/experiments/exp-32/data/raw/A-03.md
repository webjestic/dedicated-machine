# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper introduces PARC, a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs terminate at the nearest satisfying path rather than failing due to knowledge gaps. The central empirical apparatus consists of two ordered claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a rate-limiter task), and (2) a two-agent pipeline that crosses that ceiling on the first run. The paper reports thirty-three experiment series, proposes a P_p/P_d distinction, a two-vector Stakes formula, an ~11% Instructions elaboration coefficient, and a vocabulary specificity threshold, and explicitly commits to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses.

---

## Strengths

- **Explicit epistemic commitment, partially honored.** The paper states clearly in §1.2 and §8.1 that mechanistic claims are hypotheses, not confirmed facts. This is the correct posture for behavioral ML work, and the paper maintains it more consistently than most in this genre. The claim classification table in §8.1 is a genuine structural contribution.

- **The ~11% Instructions coefficient is the paper's cleanest finding.** Three independent run sets (exp-03b/c/d) produce converging ratios (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) on a well-defined binary outcome. The convergence is real, the measurement is precise, and the claim is appropriately scoped. This is what empirical prompt engineering work should look like.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is methodologically sound and the conclusion is appropriately humble.** The paper ran the experiment that could falsify its own central claim (H1: slot is load-bearing), found H2 (content is load-bearing) is the better account, and updated accordingly. The willingness to report this and revise the mechanistic framing is a structural strength.

- **The "bridge is the artifact" finding in exp-28d is specific and testable.** The claim that Agent 2 misses exactly what Agent 1 missed is a precise, falsifiable prediction, and the section-level trace provided constitutes genuine evidence for it. This is a useful design principle grounded in a real observation.

- **The exp-30 ladder design is well-structured.** Six variants forming a P_d→P_p gradient on a single scenario, with the finding that output-gate-only (F) outperforms reasoning+output gate (D), is a non-obvious result with a coherent mechanistic explanation. The acknowledgment that the heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives and that results rest on manual review is appropriate disclosure.

- **The self-prediction gap (§9.2) is a genuine and underreported phenomenon.** The observation that the authoring model underestimates P_p Persona strength is interesting, the proposed mechanism (descriptive vs. enactive register) is plausible, and the design implication (evaluate empirically, not by inspection) is practically useful.

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

### 1. The central causal claim is not identified by the experimental design.

The paper's core theoretical claim is that Persona changes *termination behavior* — specifically, that P_p installs a satisfaction condition that prevents premature stopping. The experimental design cannot distinguish this from the simpler hypothesis that P_p prompts embed task-relevant procedural reasoning content that functions as implicit chain-of-thought, and that this content — not any identity-installation mechanism — drives the behavioral difference.

The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) was designed to test this and found H2: content in either slot produces equivalent detection. The paper acknowledges this in §9.3 and §9.5. But the acknowledgment does not go far enough. If H2 is correct, then the "Dedicated Machine" framing — satisfaction conditions, termination mechanisms, horizon blindness — is a narrative overlay on a content-quality effect. The paper cannot simultaneously claim (a) H2 is the better account and (b) the Dedicated Machine hypothesis is the mechanistic explanation for the findings. These are in tension. The paper does not resolve this tension; it notes it and moves on.

This is the load-bearing problem. The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is presented as a *unifying mechanistic explanation* (§1.1 Contribution 1, §2.1, §10). If the operative variable is procedural domain vocabulary in any slot, the unifying explanation is "procedural domain vocabulary activates relevant reasoning chains," which is a content-quality account, not a satisfaction-condition account. The paper needs to either defend the satisfaction-condition account against H2 or reframe the Dedicated Machine hypothesis as a useful design metaphor rather than a mechanistic explanation.

### 2. Claim 2 rests on n=1 for its primary demonstration.

exp-29 — the zombie-write pipeline, which the paper calls "Claim 2 in its clearest form" and the "canonical PARC pipeline example" — ran one agent pair, one time. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 as an open question, but the acknowledgment is insufficient given the weight the claim carries in the abstract, introduction, and conclusion.

A single run cannot establish that the pipeline "crossed the horizon no single-pass prompt can consistently reach." It establishes that the pipeline crossed it once. The paper reports that the best single-pass result in 40 exp-09 runs was 1/10 (10%), which makes the n=1 pipeline result striking — but striking results from n=1 are exactly the results that require replication before they can carry the weight of a central claim. The paper presents exp-29 as confirmation of Claim 2 and corroboration of exp-28d. It is neither: it is a single data point that is consistent with Claim 2. exp-28d (n=10 pipeline runs) is the actual evidentiary basis for Claim 2, and exp-29 is an illustration.

The abstract states: "A two-agent review pipeline solved a distributed systems failure mode on the first run." This is accurate but misleading as a claim anchor. "On the first run" is not a property of the pipeline — it is a property of one execution of the pipeline. The paper should not present exp-29 as evidence for Claim 2 without n≥10 replication.

### 3. The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is not falsifiable as stated.

The hypothesis is: "AI language models optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution of whatever goal is currently installed, with no native cost function for the gap between what was delivered and what was needed."

What would falsify this? The paper does not say. Every experimental result is interpreted as confirmation: P_p works because it installs a better satisfaction condition; P_d fails because its satisfaction condition is met too early; Stakes amplifies because it raises the cost of premature termination; the pipeline works because it separates satisfaction conditions. When a framework can absorb every result as confirmation, it is not functioning as a scientific hypothesis — it is functioning as an interpretive vocabulary.

The paper partially acknowledges this in §8.1 by distinguishing behavioral claims from mechanistic hypotheses. But the Dedicated Machine hypothesis is presented in §1.1 as Contribution 1 and in §10 as the frame that "makes every other finding make sense." A hypothesis that makes every finding make sense without specifying what would not make sense is not a scientific contribution — it is a post-hoc narrative. The paper needs to specify at least one experimental result that would have disconfirmed the Dedicated Machine hypothesis, or explicitly reclassify it as a design metaphor rather than a mechanistic claim.

### 4. The unified formula (§8.2) is not derived from the data and cannot be.

The PCSIEFTR formula presents a mathematical structure that implies quantitative relationships between components. The formula includes terms like $S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T)$, $\beta_{brake} S_t$, and $\text{Ceiling}(I)$. These are not measured quantities — $P_p^T$, $S_i$, $S_t$, and $C$ have no defined units, no measurement procedure, and no empirical coefficients derived from the experimental data. The only coefficient that is actually measured is the ~11% Instructions elaboration ratio, which appears in the formula as $\text{Ceiling}(I)$ but is not formally connected to the denominator structure.

The formula is presented in the style of an empirically grounded equation but functions as a conceptual diagram. This is a presentation problem with structural consequences: readers at NeurIPS/ICLR/ACL will read this formula as making quantitative claims. The paper should either (a) remove the formula and replace it with a component interaction diagram that accurately represents the qualitative relationships, or (b) define measurement procedures for each variable and derive the formula from data. The current presentation overstates the formalization of the framework.

### 5. The exp-30 (ethical level) section is structurally disconnected from the rest of the paper.

exp-30 runs on Gemini 2.5 Flash, uses a different task domain (blackmail scenario), applies a different scoring methodology (manual review of sent email content after classifier failure), and tests a different dependent variable (misalignment rate) than all other experiments. The paper presents it as a demonstration that the Dedicated Machine hypothesis extends to the ethical level.

The problem is not that the experiment is uninteresting — the output-gate finding (F=0% vs. D=10%) is genuinely notable. The problem is that the experiment cannot bear the weight of "same structure, different blast radius" (§2.3). The three-level structure (housekeeping → operational → ethical) implies that the same mechanism operates across all three. But the experimental evidence for Level 3 comes from a different model, a different task type, a different scoring method, and a different research group's framework (Lynch et al.). The paper has not established that the mechanism is the same — it has established that a similar design principle (output gate vs. prohibition) produces a similar directional result in a different setting. That is suggestive, not confirmatory.

The ethical level section should either be separated as a preliminary finding requiring independent replication, or the paper should remove the "same structure" framing and present it as an exploratory extension.

### 6. The P_p/P_d distinction lacks a formal operationalization, and this undermines replicability.

§9.6 acknowledges this directly: "The P_p/P_d distinction is described qualitatively but has no formal operationalization." The provisional heuristic offered (a Persona is P_p if it contains at least one first-person procedural clause contingent on a prior state) is a starting point, not a coding scheme. Without inter-rater reliability testing, external researchers cannot reliably classify Personas as P_p or P_d, which means the central distinction cannot be independently replicated.

This is not a minor limitation — it is a replication barrier for the paper's primary contribution. The paper identifies this as future work (§9.9 item 4), but at a venue like NeurIPS/ICLR/ACL, a central construct that cannot be reliably operationalized by independent researchers is a submission-level problem, not a future-work item.

### 7. The single-model dominance of the experimental record is underacknowledged.

Thirty-one of thirty-three experiment series run on Claude Sonnet 4.6 or Claude Opus 4.6. Cross-model replication consists of exp-01g (Gemini 2.5 Pro, n=20, two variants) and exp-30 (Gemini 2.5 Flash, n=60, one task domain). The paper claims in §7 that "all primary behavioral claims were tested outside their original domain and model." This overstates the cross-model evidence. The consideration-set mechanism, P_p/P_d distinction, Stakes taxonomy, Instructions coefficient, and pipeline principle are all established on Claude and illustrated on Gemini. The cross-model evidence base is thin relative to the generality of the claims.

The paper should not claim cross-model generalization for findings that have been tested on one non-Claude model in one or two experiments. The correct framing is: "Primary claims established on Claude Sonnet 4.6; preliminary cross-model evidence from Gemini 2.5 Pro (exp-01g) is consistent with generalization; systematic cross-model replication is future work."

### 8. The scoring methodology is not independently validated.

All scoring is binary on a ground-truth criterion defined by the authors before each run. The paper does not report inter-rater reliability for any scoring decision, does not describe a blinding procedure for scorers, and does not report whether the authors who designed the prompts also scored the outputs. For a paper making strong quantitative claims (0/10, 10/10, 100% detection rates), the absence of any validation of the scoring procedure is a methodological gap. Binary scoring on author-defined criteria without blinding is susceptible to confirmation bias in borderline cases, and the paper does not establish that there were no borderline cases.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **The H2 / Dedicated Machine tension.** The slot-swap series found that procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to the Persona slot. If content is the operative variable independent of slot, what is the experimental prediction of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis that differs from the prediction of "procedural domain vocabulary activates relevant reasoning chains"? Please specify a result that would have confirmed H1 (slot is load-bearing) and explain why H2 does not undermine the satisfaction-condition framing.

2. **exp-29 n=1.** The zombie-write pipeline is presented as the canonical demonstration of Claim 2. What is the expected Tier 1.0 rate for Agent 1 across n=10 runs, and what is the expected infrastructure failure mode coverage rate for Agent 2? If Agent 1 achieves Tier 1.0 on, say, 4/10 runs, does the pipeline claim hold? What is the minimum Agent 1 consistency rate at which the pipeline claim is still supported?

3. **Falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis.** Please specify one experimental result — ideally one that was plausible before the experiments were run — that would have disconfirmed the Dedicated Machine hypothesis. If no such result exists, please clarify whether the hypothesis is intended as a scientific claim or as a design metaphor.

4. **The unified formula.** What are the units of $P_p^T$, $S_i$, $S_t$, and $C$ as they appear in the PCSIEFTR formula? How would an independent researcher measure these quantities for a new prompt? If these quantities cannot be measured independently of the output $R$, the formula is circular — please clarify what predictive or prescriptive work the formula is doing that a component interaction diagram would not do.

5. **Scoring methodology.** For the experiments with the strongest quantitative claims (exp-01e J=0/10, exp-28b Tier 2=0%, exp-28d load_test=100%), were the outputs scored by the prompt authors or by independent raters? Were scorers blind to which variant produced each output? For binary criteria, what was the rate of borderline cases, and how were they resolved?

6. **The ~11% coefficient.** The three run sets producing 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% are described as "converging." All three use the same prohibition ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), the same model, and the same task domain. Is the coefficient expected to hold across different prohibition types, different task domains, or different models? What is the theoretical basis for expecting convergence at ~11% rather than at a task-specific value?

7. **P_p/P_d operationalization for replication.** The provisional heuristic for P_p classification (first-person procedural clause contingent on a prior state) — can you apply it to three Personas from the paper and show the coding? Specifically: (a) Variant I from exp-01e, (b) Variant J from exp-01e, and (c) the Agent 1 Persona from exp-29. If the heuristic produces ambiguous classifications for any of these, what additional criteria resolve the ambiguity?

8. **exp-30 model selection.** Claude Opus 4.6 produced 0% misalignment across all conditions including the baseline. The paper states this is "consistent with the hypothesis." But if the model used for the primary pipeline experiments (Claude Opus 4.6 in exp-29) has already internalized the output gate at the weight level, what does the exp-29 result demonstrate about PARC's contribution versus the model's baseline alignment? Please clarify whether the pipeline finding in exp-29 would replicate on a model with non-trivial baseline misalignment.

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision.**

The paper contains genuine empirical contributions — the ~11% Instructions coefficient, the slot-swap series and its honest H2 conclusion, the "bridge is the artifact" finding, and the exp-30 output-gate result — that are worth publishing. The experimental discipline is above average for prompt engineering work, and the explicit commitment to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses is the correct posture.

However, three problems require substantive revision before the paper can be accepted at this venue. First, the tension between H2 (content is the operative variable, slot is not) and the Dedicated Machine hypothesis (satisfaction conditions installed by identity framing) is unresolved and load-bearing — the paper cannot claim both without reconciling them.
