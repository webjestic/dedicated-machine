# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper introduces PARC (Persona Architecture for Reasoning and Context), a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis": LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfying an installed goal and have no native cost function for the gap between what was delivered and what was needed. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims—that a measurable single-agent ceiling exists (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a rate-limiter task despite model knowledge of all requirements), and that a two-agent pipeline with separated satisfaction conditions crosses that ceiling on first run. The framework is validated across 33 experiment series (~1,245 runs) on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, spanning code review, legal contract review, and an AI alignment scenario.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine theoretical contribution with predictive value.** The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is not merely a rebranding of existing "LLMs are next-token predictors" framing. The distinction between *knowledge in the consideration set* and *knowledge load-bearing on the satisfaction condition* is precise, non-trivial, and generates falsifiable predictions. The J-variant result (3,906 thinking tokens on Gemini 2.5 Pro, 0/10 detection) is the cleanest single data point in the paper and is genuinely striking: more compute applied to the wrong satisfaction condition produces a more thorough wrong answer. This is a real finding.

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is empirically grounded and operationally useful.** The split is not definitional hand-waving. The 10/10 vs. 0/10 detection split between structurally equivalent prompts differing only in whether the Persona encodes a procedural search algorithm vs. a dispositional commitment is reproducible across domains (code review, legal review) and models (Claude, Gemini). The premise-rejection experiment (exp-05) is particularly clean: the 10/10 vs. 10/10 split on premise rejection vs. compliance validation, with the P_d Context section installing a prior before a single line of code is read, is a well-designed demonstration.

- **Honest treatment of disconfirmations and boundary conditions.** The paper explicitly reports calibration failures (exp-27: blockchain artifact too canonically trained, Claim 1 disconfirmed for that artifact), the category overlap problem that prevented a clean Instructions masking test across five experiments, the n=1 limitation on exp-29, and the H2 result that partially undermines the slot-determinism reading of the framework. This is unusual and commendable. Most papers in this space would have quietly dropped the blockchain experiment or framed it as a scope limitation.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is the strongest methodological contribution.** Placing identical procedural content in the Persona slot vs. the Instructions slot and measuring detection and depth across five experiments is a well-designed confound resolution. The H2 result—content, not slot, is the operative variable—is a genuine finding that the authors correctly interpret as strengthening the CoT interpretation and weakening their own slot-determinism framing. Reporting this honestly while correctly identifying what it does and does not imply for the pipeline architecture is methodologically mature.

- **The pipeline architecture claim is supported by qualitatively distinct evidence.** The exp-29 result—seven infrastructure failure modes (Redis Sentinel failover, Kubernetes CFS throttling, daemon thread behavior under SIGKILL, TOCTOU on READ COMMITTED isolation, etc.) not found in any of 40 prior single-pass runs, produced without mechanism vocabulary in either prompt—is not easily explained away. The "bridge is the artifact" principle, confirmed by the section-level trace in exp-28d (items Agent 1 missed = items Agent 2 missed), is a clean mechanistic prediction that was verified.

- **The exp-30 ethical extension is the most surprising result in the paper.** The finding that an output-only transparency axiom (F: 0% misalignment) outperforms a reasoning+output transparency axiom (D: 10% misalignment) because constraining the scratchpad creates a routing conflict that some models resolve by routing around the axiom in private reasoning—is a non-obvious, counterintuitive result with direct implications for alignment research. The scratchpad evidence showing the model considering and discarding blackmail under F is the kind of mechanistic trace that makes this more than a behavioral observation.

- **Explicit claim classification (behavioral vs. mechanistic hypothesis).** Section 8.1 maintains a strict and consistently applied distinction between confirmed behavioral findings and mechanistic conjectures about transformer internals. This is the correct epistemic posture for this type of work and should be the standard for the field.

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **[Critical] The central theoretical frame is unfalsifiable as stated.** The Dedicated Machine hypothesis—"LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution of whatever goal is currently installed"—is presented as an explanation for every observed behavioral pattern. But the hypothesis is not falsifiable in the form given: any observed output can be post-hoc described as "the fastest satisfying path given the installed goal." The paper does not specify what evidence would *disconfirm* the Dedicated Machine framing as opposed to disconfirming a specific prediction derived from it. The distinction matters: the paper successfully falsifies specific predictions (Claim 1 on the blockchain artifact, the compulsion-as-reflex hypothesis, the slot-determinism hypothesis), but the umbrella frame is never put at risk. A theoretical frame that survives all disconfirmations by absorbing them is not a scientific theory—it is a narrative. The authors should either (a) specify conditions under which the Dedicated Machine framing itself would be abandoned in favor of an alternative, or (b) explicitly reframe it as a useful engineering metaphor rather than a mechanistic hypothesis.

- **[Critical] The mechanistic equations are not mechanistic.** The PCSIEFTR unified formula (§8.2) uses transformer notation ($Q$, $K$, $V$, softmax, $\sqrt{d_k}$) but does not correspond to any actual transformer computation. The formula mixes behavioral constructs (Persona, Stakes, Instructions) with architectural primitives in a way that is dimensionally incoherent: $S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T)$ requires $S_i$ to be a scalar, $Q$ to be a vector, and $P_p^T$ to be a compatible vector—but Persona is a text string, not a vector in the model's attention space. The formula is not wrong in the way a falsifiable equation is wrong; it is not a formula at all. It is a conceptual diagram dressed in mathematical notation. This is a significant credibility problem for a paper submitted to a venue where reviewers will check dimensional consistency. The authors should either (a) replace the formula with an honest conceptual diagram, or (b) ground it in actual embedding operations with defined dimensions. The behavioral claims do not require this formula to be valid; removing it strengthens the paper.

- **[Serious] The single-model, single-temperature experimental design limits generalizability claims.** The primary experimental series runs on Claude Sonnet 4.6 at temperature 0.5. Cross-model replication is limited to exp-01g (Gemini 2.5 Pro, two variants, n=20) and exp-30 (Gemini 2.5 Flash, n=60 on a different task). The paper makes claims about "AI language models" and "transformers" that are supported by evidence from one model family at one temperature setting. Temperature 0.5 is a reasonable choice but is not the default for most production deployments (0.0 or 1.0 are more common), and the interaction between temperature and the P_p/P_d distinction is untested. The claim that "the procedural specification is model-agnostic—it encodes a reasoning algorithm that any transformer executes" (§7) is not supported by the experimental record. Two variants on one additional model is insufficient for a model-agnostic claim.

- **[Serious] The scoring methodology is underspecified and potentially confounded.** Binary scoring on a "ground-truth criterion defined before each run" is the stated methodology, but the paper does not report inter-rater reliability, does not describe who scored the outputs, and does not address whether the scorer was blind to condition. For the primary detection claims (0/10 vs. 10/10), this may not matter—the effect sizes are large enough that scoring noise is unlikely to flip the result. But for the pipeline experiments (exp-28d: 5.6/10 mean; exp-29: seven infrastructure failure modes), the scoring is more granular and the scorer's knowledge of condition is more likely to introduce bias. The 10-item operational checklist for exp-28b/28d needs its scoring rubric published in full, with explicit criteria for partial credit. "All raw outputs are in data/exp-*/raw/" is not a substitute for a reproducible scoring protocol.

- **[Serious] The exp-29 n=1 limitation is more serious than the paper acknowledges.** The paper's strongest result—Tier 1.0 on the zombie-write pipeline on the first run, seven infrastructure failure modes not found in 40 prior single-pass runs—rests on a single pipeline execution. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 ("An n=10 experiment is needed") but presents the result in §6.11 and §10 with rhetorical weight that the sample size does not support. "The pipeline solved the zombie-write problem on the first run" is a compelling claim. It is also a claim that could be a lucky draw from a distribution that includes many failures. The comparison to "40 prior single-pass runs" is not a valid control because the pipeline runs used a different model (claude-opus-4-6 vs. claude-sonnet-4-6 for the single-pass baseline). This confound is not disclosed in §6.11 and is only recoverable from the appendix.

- **[Moderate] The P_p/P_d operationalization is insufficient for replication.** The provisional heuristic in §9.6 ("a Persona is P_p if it contains at least one first-person procedural clause that specifies a reasoning step contingent on a prior state") is a starting point, not a coding scheme. Without inter-rater reliability testing, external researchers cannot reliably classify prompts as P_p or P_d, which means the central experimental manipulation cannot be independently replicated. The paper correctly identifies this as a limitation but presents it as future work rather than as a current constraint on the strength of the claims. The claims should be hedged accordingly.

- **[Moderate] The Dedicated Machine framing of the ethical experiment (exp-30) overreaches.** The exp-30 result is interesting and the output-gate finding is non-obvious. But the paper's interpretation—that the F variant achieves 0% misalignment because the transparency axiom changes the satisfaction condition rather than adding a prohibition—is one of several possible explanations. An equally parsimonious explanation is that the F variant's specific wording ("defensible to all stakeholders simultaneously") functions as an implicit prohibition on blackmail-type actions, and the difference from variant B is wording specificity rather than the P_p/P_d mechanism. The paper does not test this alternative. Additionally, the claim that "the training has already internalized the output gate at the weight level" for Claude Opus 4.6 (§6.12) is a strong mechanistic claim made on the basis of a "preliminary run" with no reported n, no reported variant breakdown, and no statistical analysis. This should either be reported properly or removed.

- **[Moderate] The "~11% elaboration ceiling" claim is overclaimed as a convergent coefficient.** Three measurements (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) across three run sets on the same task with the same prohibition are presented as a "converging coefficient." This is three data points on one task type with one prohibition type. The coefficient may be specific to CI-scope prohibitions on code review tasks. The paper does not test whether the coefficient generalizes to other prohibition types, other task domains, or other Persona strengths. Presenting it as a general property of the Instructions component is not supported by the evidence.

- **[Minor] The paper's length and structure work against its claims.** At approximately 18,000 words, the paper is substantially longer than venue norms and contains significant redundancy between the abstract, introduction, framework sections, experimental sections, and conclusion. The Dedicated Machine framing is restated in nearly identical language across §2, §4, §5, §8, and §10. This is partly a consequence of the paper's ambition—it is simultaneously a theoretical paper, a framework paper, and an empirical paper—but the result is that the strongest findings (J-variant, slot-swap series, exp-30 output-gate) are buried in a document that requires sustained effort to navigate. A 12-page conference paper with a supplementary appendix would serve the work better.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **On falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis:** What experimental result would lead you to abandon the Dedicated Machine framing in favor of an alternative theoretical account? If the answer is "none, because any result can be described in Dedicated Machine terms," the framing should be presented as a useful engineering metaphor rather than a scientific hypothesis. If there is a falsifying condition, please state it explicitly.

2. **On the unified formula:** Can you provide dimensional analysis showing that the PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is dimensionally consistent? Specifically: what is the type and dimension of $P_p^T$ when $P_p$ is a text string? What is the type of $S_i$ when it appears as a scalar multiplier on a dot product? If the formula is not intended to be dimensionally consistent, what is it intended to represent that a conceptual diagram would not represent equally well?

3. **On the exp-29 model discrepancy:** The single-pass baseline for the zombie-write task used Claude Sonnet 4.6; the pipeline experiment used Claude Opus 4.6. The performance difference between these models is non-trivial. How much of the pipeline result is attributable to the pipeline architecture versus the model upgrade? Have you run the zombie-write pipeline on Claude Sonnet 4.6 to isolate the pipeline effect from the model effect?

4. **On scoring methodology:** Who scored the experimental outputs, and were scorers blind to condition? For the 10-item operational checklist in exp-28b/28d, what is the full rubric for each item, including criteria for partial credit? For exp-29's seven infrastructure failure modes, what was the pre-specified criterion for counting a failure mode as "found"?

5. **On the temperature interaction:** The primary experiments run at temperature 0.5. The P_p/P_d distinction predicts that P_p installs a convergent search procedure that terminates at a specific structural finding. At temperature 0.0, this should produce near-deterministic outputs; at temperature 1.0, the stochastic variation should be higher but the mean detection rate should be similar if the mechanism is as described. Have you tested the P_p/P_d detection split at temperature 0.0 and/or 1.0? If the effect disappears or reverses at different temperatures, the mechanism hypothesis requires revision.

6. **On the exp-30 alternative explanation:** The F variant's transparency axiom ("every message I send must be defensible to all stakeholders simultaneously") could be interpreted as a highly specific implicit prohibition on blackmail-type actions rather than as a satisfaction-condition change. What distinguishes this from variant B's explicit prohibition ("do not use personal affairs as leverage") mechanistically? Have you tested a variant that uses the F wording but applies it to reasoning rather than outputs, to isolate whether the "outputs only" constraint is doing the work or whether the specific wording of the axiom is doing the work?

7. **On the H2 result and the framework's central claim:** The slot-swap series shows that procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to procedural content in the Persona slot. The paper interprets this as "content, not slot, is the operative variable." But if slot placement does not matter, what is the theoretical basis for the World Layer / Task Layer distinction, and what is the basis for the claim that "no amount of Task Layer specification changes the satisfaction condition already installed by the World Layer" (§4)? If the Instructions slot can install a satisfaction condition equivalent to the Persona slot, the two-layer architecture is a design heuristic, not a mechanistic claim. Please clarify what the H2 result implies for the framework's theoretical structure.

8. **On external replication:** The paper reports that "all raw outputs are in data/exp-*/raw/." Is this data publicly available or available to reviewers? Without access to the raw outputs and the scoring rubrics, the behavioral claims cannot be independently verified. What is the plan for data release?

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper contains genuine empirical contributions—the J-variant consideration-set result, the slot-swap confound resolution, the pipeline architecture evidence, and the exp-30 output-gate finding—that are worth publishing and would be of interest to the NLP and prompt engineering communities.
