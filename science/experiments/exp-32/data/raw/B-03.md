# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper introduces PARC (Persona Architecture for Reasoning and Context), a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs optimize toward the nearest satisfying path to their installed goal and produce shallow output not from knowledge gaps but from underspecified satisfaction conditions. The authors present thirty-three experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, advancing two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists on shallow canonical tasks (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs), and (2) a two-agent pipeline with separated satisfaction conditions crosses that ceiling on the first run. The paper further introduces the P_p/P_d distinction, a two-vector Stakes formula, an ~11% Instructions elaboration ceiling coefficient, and a vocabulary specificity threshold as subsidiary empirical contributions.

---

## Strengths

- **The core empirical contrast is sharp and replicable.** The J=0/10 (weak Persona + rich Context) versus I=10/10 (strong P_p + same Context) result is a clean, interpretable finding. The cross-model zero-shot transfer to Gemini 2.5 Pro without prompt modification is a meaningful replication that most prompt engineering papers do not attempt.

- **The paper commits to a falsifiability structure.** Claim 1 and Claim 2 are stated as ordered, testable propositions with explicit measurement criteria (10-item operational checklist, binary Tier 1.0 scoring). The authors disconfirm Claim 1 on the blockchain artifact (exp-27) and report this honestly rather than suppressing it. This is methodologically unusual and creditable.

- **The behavioral/mechanistic distinction is maintained throughout.** The paper consistently labels transformer-level claims (K/V pre-filtering, inverse temperature, attention masking) as hypotheses rather than confirmed findings. Section 8.1 provides an explicit claim classification table. This is the correct epistemic posture for behavioral work on closed-weight models.

- **The H2 result (slot-swap series, exp-18–24) is a genuine contribution.** Resolving the few-shot/procedural-content confound through five independent slot-swap experiments is methodologically sound. The authors correctly identify that this result partially undermines their own slot-determinism framing and update the paper accordingly. The finding that content drives the effect independent of Persona vs. Instructions slot placement is a substantive, non-obvious result.

- **The pipeline architecture claim is the paper's most novel contribution.** The "bridge is the artifact" finding — that Agent 2 carries exactly what Agent 1 encoded, confirmed by section-level trace in exp-28d — is a precise, actionable architectural principle that goes beyond existing multi-agent prompting literature. The exp-29 result (seven infrastructure failure modes not found in 40 prior single-pass runs, without mechanism vocabulary in either prompt) is striking.

- **The Instructions elaboration ceiling coefficient (~11%) is a quantitatively precise, converging finding.** Three independent run sets producing 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% is the kind of convergent quantitative result that is rare in prompt engineering work and provides a concrete design parameter.

- **The exp-30 ethical-level extension is intellectually serious.** The finding that an output-only transparency axiom (F=0%) outperforms reasoning+output constraint (D=10%) — and that the scratchpad shows the model finding and discarding the blackmail option rather than being prohibited from it — is a non-trivial result with alignment implications. The decision to run on Gemini 2.5 Flash rather than Claude Opus 4.6 (which showed 0% baseline misalignment) is methodologically correct and explicitly justified.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The central theoretical claim is unfalsifiable as stated.** The Dedicated Machine hypothesis — "LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution of their installed goal" — is presented as an explanation for every observed result, but it is not operationalized in a way that would allow a disconfirming experiment. If a model produces deep output from a weak Persona, the hypothesis can accommodate this by post-hoc redefining the "installed satisfaction condition." The paper does not specify what experimental outcome would falsify the Dedicated Machine framing as opposed to alternative accounts (e.g., standard CoT prompting effects, attention-sink phenomena, instruction-following fine-tuning). The authors acknowledge this implicitly in §9.5 but do not resolve it. A theory that explains all outcomes equally well explains none of them distinctively.

- **The primary experimental design confounds P_p content with CoT scaffolding.** The H2 result (§9.3, §9.5) establishes that procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to the Persona slot. The authors correctly identify this as "the correct description of the mechanism." But this means the paper's central claim — that Persona installs a satisfaction condition via identity framing — is not supported over the simpler alternative that procedural reasoning scaffolds in any slot produce the observed effects. The Dedicated Machine framing adds no explanatory work over "procedural CoT content improves detection of structurally hidden failure modes." The authors gesture toward this in §9.5 but do not resolve it. The paper's theoretical contribution depends on the slot distinction mattering; the paper's best empirical evidence (H2) shows it does not.

- **Sample sizes are insufficient for the paper's quantitative claims.** The two most important results — exp-29 (pipeline Tier 1.0) and exp-28d (pipeline vs. single-pass) — rest on n=1 and n=10 respectively. The authors acknowledge the exp-29 n=1 limitation in §9.9 but present it as a primary empirical claim in the abstract, introduction, and conclusion. A result that reached Tier 1.0 "on the first run" is consistent with a 10% base rate. The ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient is based on three run sets of n=10, which is adequate for the point estimate but insufficient for the confidence interval the paper implies. The exp-30 ethical results (n=10 per variant, 6 variants) are the best-powered experiment in the paper; the others are substantially underpowered for the claims made.

- **The scoring rubrics are not independently validated.** The 10-item operational checklist (exp-28b/28d), Tier 1.0 criterion (exp-29), and binary detection scores throughout are defined by the authors and scored by the authors. No inter-rater reliability is reported. For a paper whose central empirical claims rest on binary detection rates (0/10 vs. 10/10), the absence of independent scoring is a significant methodological gap. The premise-rejection test (exp-05) and the ethical blackmail scoring (exp-30, where the authors note the heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives requiring manual review) are particularly vulnerable to this concern.

- **The Dedicated Machine framing is applied inconsistently to the ethics experiment.** In exp-30, Variant F achieves 0% misalignment by constraining the output boundary rather than the satisfaction condition. The paper interprets this as "the definition of done was changed." But the scratchpad evidence the authors cite — "the model reasons freely, considers the blackmail option, and discards it" — is equally consistent with the model having internalized an output constraint that functions as a rule rather than a satisfaction condition redefinition. The Dedicated Machine framing predicts that rule-based constraints leak (as in Variant B at 20%); Variant F is a rule-based output constraint that does not leak. The paper's own theoretical frame predicts F should leak. The authors do not address this tension.

- **The mathematical formalism does not add explanatory content.** The PCSIEFTR unified formula (§8.2) is presented as a "unified reasoning formula" but is not derived from any mechanistic model, does not generate novel predictions, and is not fit to data. The formula's variables ($S_i$, $S_t$, $P_p$, $C$) are not quantified in any experiment — they are labels for qualitative constructs. The attention mechanism notation in §3.1 ($Q$, $K$, $V$) is standard transformer description, not a model of PARC's operation. The formula creates an appearance of mathematical rigor without its substance. A paper that correctly distinguishes behavioral from mechanistic claims should not present an unvalidated mechanistic formula as a "unified equation."

- **The paper conflates model-specific findings with general claims.** Thirty-one of thirty-three experiment series use Claude Sonnet 4.6. The cross-model replication (exp-01g) tests only two variants (I and J) on Gemini 2.5 Pro and finds the same 10/10 vs. 0/10 split. The exp-30 ethical experiment uses Gemini 2.5 Flash. The ~11% elaboration ceiling, the Stakes taxonomy, the two-vector formula coefficients, the vocabulary specificity threshold, and the pipeline architecture results are all Claude Sonnet 4.6 findings. The paper presents these as general properties of LLMs. This is not supported by the experimental record.

- **The "self-prediction gap" finding (§4.1.1, §9.2) is not adequately controlled.** The claim that authoring models systematically underestimate P_p Persona strength rests on a single data point (Variant L predicted at 6/10, scored 10/10) and a meta-experiment (exp-06) in which the same model that generated the Persona also evaluated it. The self-prediction gap could reflect prediction difficulty, the stochastic nature of n=10 scoring, or the fact that the evaluating model was asked to predict a score rather than run the experiment. The claim is interesting but the evidence is insufficient to support it as a "structural property of instinct-language identity."

- **The paper's scope and length are not calibrated to its empirical contribution.** The paper is approximately 18,000 words. The core empirical contribution — P_p > P_d on structurally hidden failure modes, pipeline crosses single-agent ceiling, ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient — could be presented in 8,000 words with equivalent rigor. The Dedicated Machine framing, the three-level ethical extension, the self-prediction gap, the Tone section, and the unified formula occupy substantial space without proportionate empirical support. The length creates a signal-to-noise problem that obscures the genuine contributions.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis:** What experimental outcome would falsify the Dedicated Machine framing as distinct from the simpler claim that "procedural CoT content in any slot improves detection of structurally hidden failure modes"? Can you specify a predicted result that the Dedicated Machine hypothesis generates but the CoT account does not?

- **On the H2 result and the paper's central claim:** Given that exp-19, 20, 22, 23, and 24 all show A ≈ B (Persona slot ≈ Instructions slot for equivalent content), what is the residual claim for Persona as a distinct architectural component? The paper states "the slot is the carrier; the condition is the mechanism" — but if the condition can be installed in any slot, what does the World Layer / Task Layer distinction add beyond a design heuristic?

- **On exp-29 sample size:** The zombie-write pipeline result is presented as a primary empirical claim ("Tier 1.0 on the first run"). If n=10 runs of the same pipeline produced, say, 3/10 Tier 1.0 consistency, would this change the paper's Claim 2? If yes, why is n=1 presented as confirmatory rather than as a pilot result?

- **On independent scoring:** Were any of the binary detection scores (particularly the Tier 1.0 criterion in exp-29 and the operational checklist in exp-28b/28d) scored by a rater blind to condition assignment? If not, what is the authors' account of how scorer expectation effects are controlled?

- **On the Variant F tension:** Variant F achieves 0% misalignment through an output-only constraint without redefining the satisfaction condition in the sense the paper uses elsewhere (no P_p, no procedural identity). The Dedicated Machine framing predicts that output-level rules leak (as Variant B demonstrates at 20%). Why does Variant F not leak, and how does the paper's theoretical frame accommodate this without post-hoc revision?

- **On model specificity:** The ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient, the Stakes type taxonomy, and the pipeline architecture results are all measured on Claude Sonnet 4.6. Are these claimed as properties of that model, of instruction-tuned transformers generally, or of LLMs as a class? What would a cross-model replication of exp-28b/28d on Gemini 2.5 Pro need to show to support the general claim?

- **On the P_p/P_d operationalization:** The provisional heuristic (§9.6) defines P_p as containing "at least one first-person procedural clause that specifies a reasoning step contingent on a prior state." By this definition, many standard chain-of-thought Instructions prompts would qualify as P_p. Does the paper intend P_p to be a property of content (procedural + domain-specific) or a property of slot (Persona) or both? Given H2, this distinction matters for the paper's claims.

- **On the unified formula:** The PCSIEFTR formula in §8.2 contains coefficients ($\alpha_{amp}$, $\beta_{brake}$) that are described qualitatively but not estimated from data. What would it take to fit this formula to the experimental record? If the coefficients cannot be estimated, what predictive work does the formula do that the verbal description in §2.4 does not?

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper contains genuine empirical contributions — particularly the J=0/10 vs. I=10/10 consideration-set result, the H2 slot-swap resolution, the ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient, and the pipeline architecture findings — that are worth publishing in a venue focused on empirical NLP or prompt engineering methodology. However, the paper as submitted has three problems that require major revision rather than minor correction: (1) the central theoretical frame (Dedicated Machine hypothesis) is not falsifiable as stated and is not clearly distinguished from standard CoT prompting accounts, which the paper's own best evidence (H2) supports; (2) the primary pipeline claims (exp-29) rest on n=1 and require replication before they can be presented as confirmatory; and (3) the mathematical formalism presents unfitted, non-predictive equations as a "unified reasoning formula," which misrepresents the paper's actual epistemic contribution. A revised paper that (a) narrows its theoretical claims to what the behavioral evidence supports, (b) presents exp-29 as a pilot result with n=10 replication, (c) removes or clearly labels the speculative formalism, and (d) addresses the CoT alternative account directly would be a strong submission.
