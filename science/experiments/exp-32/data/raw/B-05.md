# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper introduces PARC (Persona Architecture for Reasoning and Context), a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis": LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfying an installed goal, with no native cost function for the gap between what was delivered and what was needed. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims—(1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a rate-limiter task despite model knowledge of all requirements), and (2) a two-agent pipeline with separate satisfaction conditions crosses that ceiling—supported by 33 experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro. The framework specifies eight components (PCSIEFTR) across two layers, with Persona—specifically its procedural variant (P_p)—identified as the primary determinant of reasoning quality.

---

## Strengths

- **The Dedicated Machine framing is genuinely clarifying.** The reframe from "specification completeness" to "satisfaction condition definition" is a substantive theoretical contribution. It unifies disparate prompt engineering observations under a single mechanism and generates falsifiable predictions. The distinction between "the model doesn't know" and "knowing is not load-bearing on the satisfaction condition" is precise and non-obvious.

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is well-operationalized relative to the field's norms.** The provisional heuristic ("at least one first-person procedural clause contingent on a prior domain-specific state") is more precise than most persona-typing schemes in the literature. The 10/10 vs. 0/10 split on the premise-rejection test (exp-05) is a clean behavioral demonstration, not merely a descriptive taxonomy.

- **The paper is admirably honest about its own limitations.** The claim classification in §8.1 explicitly separates confirmed behavioral findings from mechanistic conjectures. The authors identify calibration failures (exp-27), unresolved confounds (Artifact Pointer Confound, exp-24), and the n=1 limitation on exp-29 without being prompted. This is above the median for empirical ML papers.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is methodologically sound and the authors correctly report a result that partially undermines their own framing.** H2 (content over slot) being corroborated five times is a genuine contribution: it resolves the few-shot/procedural-content confound and strengthens the claim that mechanism-level vocabulary is the operative variable, regardless of which slot carries it. Reporting this honestly rather than burying it strengthens the paper's credibility.

- **The pipeline experiments (exp-28d, exp-29) demonstrate a concrete, reproducible engineering principle.** "The bridge is the artifact" is a precise, testable claim about inter-agent information transfer, and the section-level trace confirming that Agent 2 misses exactly what Agent 1 missed is the right kind of evidence for it.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient (~11%) converging across three independent run sets is a useful quantitative anchor.** The scope re-framing mechanism—where a security vulnerability is re-categorized as a CI correctness issue to satisfy both the prohibition and the P_p satisfaction condition—is a novel and practically important finding.

- **Cross-domain and cross-model replication are included, not promised.** Zero-shot transfer to Gemini 2.5 Pro (exp-01g) and legal contract review (exp-01f) with identical prompts is a meaningful generalization test. The legal domain result is particularly useful because it rules out code-review-specific training artifacts.

- **The self-prediction gap finding (§4.1.1, §9.2) is underappreciated in the literature.** The systematic underestimation of P_p strength by the authoring model—predicted 6/10, scored 10/10—has direct practical implications and is grounded in a mechanistic account (descriptive vs. enactive register). The meta-experiment (exp-06) using the paper itself as the review artifact is methodologically creative.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is not falsifiable as stated, and the paper does not attempt to falsify it.** The hypothesis is presented as explaining "every behavioral pattern in this paper," but a theoretical frame that explains every finding post-hoc is not a scientific hypothesis—it is a narrative. The paper would need to specify conditions under which the Dedicated Machine framing would predict a different outcome than a competing account (e.g., standard CoT, instruction-following models, RLHF-based explanations) and then test those conditions. As written, the framing is consistent with all findings but not uniquely predictive of them. The authors acknowledge mechanistic claims are hypotheses, but the theoretical frame itself needs a falsification condition.

- **The central causal claim—that Persona changes the satisfaction condition rather than the knowledge retrieval pathway—is not established by the experimental design.** The key comparison (Variant I vs. J, exp-01e) shows a 10/10 vs. 0/10 detection difference with Persona as the manipulated variable. But the paper cannot distinguish between three accounts: (a) P_p installs a different satisfaction condition (the paper's claim), (b) P_p functions as a structured CoT prompt that elicits a different reasoning chain (the H2-consistent account the paper itself raises in §9.5), or (c) P_p changes the effective retrieval prior toward failure-mode vocabulary. The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) corroborates H2 and the authors correctly note this—but then the paper continues to use "satisfaction condition" and "consideration set" language as if the mechanistic claim were established. The paper needs to either commit to H2 as the correct account and revise the theoretical framing accordingly, or provide experimental evidence that distinguishes H2 from the satisfaction-condition account.

- **The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is not a formula—it is a diagram with mathematical notation.** The equation $R = [\text{Softmax}(\frac{S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}) \times C] \times [\frac{\text{Task} \cdot (1 - \beta_{brake} S_t)}{\text{Ceiling}(I)}]$ has no derivation, no dimensional analysis, no defined units for $S_i$, $S_t$, $C$, or $I$, and no empirical fit to data. The coefficients $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ appear in §4.4 but are not estimated in §8.2. Dividing by $\text{Ceiling}(I)$ is undefined when $I = 0$. Multiplying a softmax output (a probability distribution) by $C$ (undefined dimensionality) produces a dimensionally incoherent expression. This formula should either be removed, replaced with a properly specified model with estimated parameters, or clearly labeled as a schematic illustration rather than a quantitative claim.

- **The experimental sample sizes are insufficient to support the precision of the claims.** The primary pipeline result (exp-29) is n=1 per agent, and the paper acknowledges this but still uses it to "corroborate Claim 2." A single pipeline run cannot corroborate a claim about a mechanism. exp-28d has n=10 for the pipeline condition but the design document is n=1 (a single Agent 1 run feeding all Agent 2 runs), which means the 10 Agent 2 runs are not independent. The paper reports Tier 2 detection rates to one decimal place (e.g., "alerting_policy = 30%") from n=10 runs, but with binary outcomes and n=10, the 95% confidence interval on a 30% rate is approximately [7%, 65%]—wide enough to include 0%. The precision of the quantitative claims consistently exceeds what the sample sizes support.

- **The baseline comparisons are not adequately controlled.** The paper compares PARC variants against "weak Persona" baselines (e.g., "You are a senior software engineer"), but does not compare against: (a) standard chain-of-thought prompting with equivalent procedural content in the system prompt, (b) few-shot prompting with examples of the target failure mode, or (c) existing structured prompting frameworks (e.g., RISEN, CO-STAR, ReAct). The paper claims to challenge the "specification completeness" theory of prompt engineering, but the baselines do not include the strongest versions of that theory. A reviewer cannot assess whether PARC's gains are attributable to the theoretical framework or simply to including more task-relevant procedural content than the baselines.

- **The exp-30 (ethical alignment) section is methodologically disconnected from the rest of the paper and should be a separate submission.** The experiment uses a different model (Gemini 2.5 Flash), a different task domain (agentic misalignment), a different evaluation method (manual review of email content after classifier failure), and a different theoretical framing (alignment rather than task performance). The finding that "output gate achieves 0% misalignment" is potentially important but cannot be evaluated from the description provided: the n=10 per variant is too small for a 0% claim, the classifier failure is not adequately characterized, and the relationship to the PARC framework's other claims is asserted rather than demonstrated. Including this as a section of a prompt engineering paper dilutes both contributions.

- **The "Dedicated Machine at the ethical level" framing (§2.3, Level 3) is inappropriate for a technical venue without substantially more careful treatment.** The paper uses a single anecdotal reference to a blackmail scenario to extend the Dedicated Machine hypothesis to AI safety. The claim that "ethics had no Stakes; the machine ran toward the fastest satisfying path; blackmail was on that path" is a serious empirical claim about model behavior that requires much more rigorous experimental support than is provided. The paper correctly notes that Claude Opus 4.6 showed 0% misalignment across all conditions—which the authors interpret as "training has already internalized the output gate"—but this interpretation is not tested and is inconsistent with the paper's own framing (which would predict that the satisfaction condition, not training, drives behavior).

- **The "Semantic Density" construct (Phase 6, §4.1.5) is underspecified.** The paper introduces "semantic neighborhood density" as the operative variable within P_p but does not provide a measurement procedure. The claim that "a tighter, more domain-coherent cluster produces deeper consideration sets" is stated in terms of embedding-space geometry, but the paper does not measure embedding distances, does not define "domain coherence" operationally, and does not explain how a practitioner would assess semantic density without running the experiment. The vocabulary specificity threshold (orientation → directive labels → mechanism) is a useful practical heuristic, but calling it "Semantic Density" and framing it in terms of gravity wells and embedding clusters is mechanistic speculation presented as an established construct.

- **The paper's length and organizational structure are not appropriate for the venue.** At approximately 18,000 words with 34 experiment series, the paper attempts to be simultaneously a theoretical contribution, an empirical study, a practical framework, and an alignment paper. Top-tier ML venues expect focused contributions with clear scope. The paper would be substantially stronger as two submissions: (1) the theoretical framework + core experimental evidence (exp-01 series, exp-03 series, exp-05, exp-28b/d), and (2) the pipeline architecture + semantic density findings (Phase 6 + exp-29).

- **The "self-prediction gap" is presented as a finding but is not adequately controlled.** The claim that "the authoring model systematically underestimates P_p Persona strength" is based on a single prediction (Variant L predicted at 6/10, scored 10/10) and a meta-experiment (exp-06) with n=7. This is insufficient to establish a systematic effect. The paper also does not control for the possibility that the authoring model's prediction was calibrated to a different task or scoring rubric than the evaluation.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On falsifiability:** What experimental result would falsify the Dedicated Machine hypothesis? Specifically: is there a task, model, or prompt configuration where the hypothesis predicts a different outcome than the H2 (procedural content drives detection regardless of slot) account? If the two accounts make identical predictions across all tested conditions, what is the evidential basis for preferring the Dedicated Machine framing over the simpler CoT account?

- **On the slot-swap result:** The H2 result (content over slot, corroborated five times) appears to undermine the theoretical claim that Persona specifically "installs a satisfaction condition" through identity-framing mechanisms. The paper acknowledges this in §9.5 but does not revise the theoretical framing. Can the authors specify what the Persona slot contributes above equivalent procedural content in the Instructions slot, given the H2 evidence? Is the P_p/P_d distinction a claim about slot, content, or both?

- **On experimental independence:** In exp-28d, a single Agent 1 design document was used as input for all 10 Agent 2 runs. This means the 10 Agent 2 runs are not independent observations—they are 10 samples from the conditional distribution P(Agent 2 output | fixed Agent 1 output). How does this affect the interpretation of the Tier 2 detection rates? Would running Agent 1 independently for each of the 10 pipeline trials change the results, and has this been tested?

- **On the formula:** Can the authors provide dimensional analysis for the PCSIEFTR formula, define the units of $S_i$, $S_t$, $C$, and $I$, and explain how the formula handles the $I = 0$ case? Alternatively, if the formula is intended as a schematic illustration rather than a quantitative model, can the authors clarify this and remove the undefined coefficients?

- **On the baseline:** Has PARC been compared against a strong CoT baseline where the procedural reasoning steps in P_p are instead provided as explicit chain-of-thought instructions in the Task Layer, with equivalent content and specificity? Given the H2 result, this comparison is necessary to establish whether the framework's gains are attributable to the World Layer/Task Layer distinction or simply to the inclusion of mechanism-level procedural content.

- **On exp-30:** The classifier used to score misalignment is described as producing "systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning," requiring manual review. Can the authors provide the classifier's false positive and false negative rates, the inter-rater reliability of the manual review, and the specific criteria used to classify a sent email as misaligned? Without this, the 0% result for Variant F cannot be evaluated.

- **On the ethical level claim:** The paper claims that Claude Opus 4.6 showing 0% misalignment across all conditions—including the baseline—is "consistent with the hypothesis." But the hypothesis predicts that misalignment rate is determined by the satisfaction condition, not by training. A model that shows 0% misalignment regardless of satisfaction condition framing would seem to disconfirm the hypothesis, not confirm it. Can the authors reconcile this?

- **On the ~11% coefficient:** The elaboration ceiling coefficient converges at approximately 11% across three run sets. Is this coefficient specific to the particular prohibition ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), the task (code review), the model (Claude Sonnet 4.6), or the P_p variant used? Has it been tested with different prohibitions, different tasks, or different models? The paper presents it as a general property of Instructions-as-ceiling, but the evidence base is three runs of the same prohibition on the same task.

- **On P_p operationalization:** The provisional heuristic for P_p classification ("at least one first-person procedural clause contingent on a prior domain-specific state") is stated but not validated. Has this heuristic been applied by independent raters to the Personas used in the experiments? What is the inter-rater agreement? Without this, it is unclear whether the P_p/P_d distinction is a reliable experimental variable or a post-hoc categorization.

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper makes genuine theoretical and empirical contributions—the Dedicated Machine framing, the P_p/P_d distinction, the pipeline architecture principle, and the slot-swap series are all substantive—but the current submission conflates behavioral findings with mechanistic claims in ways that undermine the theoretical contribution, presents quantitative results at a precision the sample sizes do not support, includes a formula that is not a formula, and attempts to cover too much ground for a single venue submission. A focused revision addressing the falsifiability of the central hypothesis, the relationship between the H2 result and the satisfaction-condition framing, the experimental independence issue in exp-28d, and the formula's status would substantially strengthen the paper; the ethical alignment section should be separated into an independent submission with appropriate experimental rigor.
