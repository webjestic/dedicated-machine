# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper introduces PARC (Persona Architecture for Reasoning and Context), a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis"—the claim that LLMs optimize toward the nearest satisfying path to an installed goal and produce shallow output not from knowledge deficits but from underspecified termination conditions. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists on shallow canonical tasks (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs despite model knowledge of all requirements), and (2) a two-agent pipeline with separate satisfaction conditions per agent crosses that ceiling on first run. The framework is validated across 33 experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, covering code review, legal contract review, and an AI alignment scenario.

---

## Strengths

- **Theoretical coherence.** The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is a genuine conceptual contribution. Unlike most prompt engineering papers, which treat components as additive specification features, this paper offers a single unifying mechanism—satisfaction condition installation—that generates testable predictions across all framework components. The framing is falsifiable in principle and has been used to generate predictions that were then tested.

- **Explicit claim/hypothesis separation.** Section 8.1 and §1.2 maintain a disciplined distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic conjectures about transformer internals. This is uncommon in the prompt engineering literature and represents genuine methodological care. The paper does not overclaim K/V space pre-filtering as confirmed fact.

- **The J/I contrast is a strong experimental design.** Holding Context constant while varying only Persona type and measuring detection of a structurally hidden failure mode (exp-01e, J=0/10 vs. I=10/10) is a well-controlled natural experiment. The cross-model zero-shot replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro (exp-01g, same result) substantially strengthens this finding.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) demonstrates scientific self-correction.** The authors identified the few-shot/procedural-content confound (via the meta-experiment in exp-06), designed a five-experiment series to test it, and updated their mechanistic claim when the evidence favored H2 (content over slot). This is the kind of iterative falsification that distinguishes empirical work from advocacy.

- **The pipeline experiments address a genuine gap.** The claim that agentic pipelines are the native design target—not a scaling of single-prompt sophistication—is substantiated by a concrete mechanism (bridge is the artifact) and confirmed by the exp-28d finding that Agent 2's coverage tracks Agent 1's output item-by-item. This is a specific, testable, and confirmed prediction.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient (~11%) is a rare quantitative regularity.** Three independent run sets converging on 11.0–12.8% is an unusually stable empirical constant for this literature. The scope re-framing mechanism (security finding re-categorized as CI concern) is a concrete behavioral demonstration, not a post-hoc rationalization.

- **exp-30 (ethical level) is appropriately scoped.** The paper does not overclaim alignment implications. The finding that an output-gate transparency axiom outperforms a reasoning+output gate is specific, counterintuitive, and mechanistically explained (scratchpad/axiom conflict creates routing loopholes). The choice to run on Gemini 2.5 Flash because Claude Opus 4.6 produced a trivial baseline reflects appropriate experimental design judgment.

- **Appendix B (Behavioral Claim Register) is a genuine contribution to reproducibility.** Mapping each claim to its experimental basis in a structured table is a model for how empirical prompt engineering papers should be written.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **[Critical] The n=1 pipeline result (exp-29) is the paper's most prominent claim and its weakest evidence.** The zombie-write pipeline reaching Tier 1.0 "on the first run" is presented as Claim 2's strongest corroboration, but n=1 is not corroboration—it is an anecdote. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 ("exp-29 n=1 limitation") but continues to use exp-29 as a primary result in the abstract, conclusion, and experimental summary. A single run cannot establish that the pipeline "consistently" crosses the single-agent ceiling, distinguish lucky sampling from structural improvement, or support the claim that "the zombie-write problem was solved" by the pipeline. This is the paper's most significant evidentiary gap. The exp-28d result (n=10, Tier 2 detection 0%→100% on two items) is the actual confirmatory experiment; exp-29 should be presented as a preliminary observation motivating future work, not as a co-equal confirmation of Claim 2.

- **[Critical] The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is not operationalized with sufficient precision to be falsifiable as stated.** The claim that LLMs "optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution" is presented as a mechanistic explanation, but "fastest path" and "satisfactory resolution" are not formally defined in a way that generates predictions distinguishable from "the model does what the prompt says." The paper's behavioral evidence is consistent with the hypothesis, but it is also consistent with simpler accounts: (a) task-relevant vocabulary in the prompt activates task-relevant completions (standard CoT/few-shot account), (b) more specific prompts produce more specific outputs (specification completeness account the paper claims to refute). The paper's own H2 finding—that procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to the Persona slot—weakens the Dedicated Machine framing relative to a simpler content-specificity account. The paper acknowledges this (§9.5) but does not resolve it. A paper centered on a theoretical hypothesis needs a sharper account of what evidence would falsify the hypothesis rather than merely be inconsistent with it.

- **[Major] The P_p/P_d distinction lacks a formal operationalization, and this undermines the central experimental comparisons.** The paper provides a provisional heuristic (§9.6) but acknowledges that a formal coding scheme with inter-rater reliability testing is needed. Without this, the P_p/P_d classification is performed by the authors, creating circularity: the authors classify Personas as P_p or P_d, run experiments, and confirm that P_p outperforms P_d. An independent rater who disagreed with the classification of Variant J as "weak Persona" (it is described as "senior software engineer"—a reasonable professional identity) could not replicate the experimental design. This is not a minor gap; it is the operationalization of the paper's primary independent variable.

- **[Major] The single-model, single-temperature experimental design limits generalizability claims.** The vast majority of experiments (31 of 33 series) were run on Claude Sonnet 4.6 at temperature 0.5. The cross-model replication (exp-01g) covers only two variants of one experiment. The paper claims "the procedural specification is model-agnostic — it encodes a reasoning algorithm that any transformer executes," but this is not supported by the experimental record. The two-agent pipeline experiments (exp-28d, exp-29) were run only on Claude. Temperature sensitivity is uncharacterized. Whether the ~11% Instructions coefficient, the Stakes taxonomy, or the vocabulary specificity threshold hold on GPT-4o, Llama 3, or Mistral is unknown. The generalizability claims in §7 substantially exceed the evidence.

- **[Major] The scoring methodology is underspecified and potentially circular.** The paper uses binary scoring on "a ground-truth criterion defined before each run," but the criteria themselves are not reproduced in the paper. For the zombie-write experiments, "Tier 1.0" is described as requiring identification of the GC pause as trigger, the full failure path, and a fencing token fix—but the exact rubric, the boundary conditions for partial credit, and who performed scoring are not stated. For exp-30 (blackmail), the paper notes that "the heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives" and that results are "based on manual review of actual sent email content"—but the manual review procedure, inter-rater agreement, and criteria for classifying a message as blackmail versus a "professional appeal" are not described. Without reproducible scoring rubrics, the quantitative results cannot be independently verified.

- **[Moderate] The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is presented as a "unified reasoning formula" but is not derived from the data.** The equation $R = [\text{Softmax}(S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T)/\sqrt{d_k}) \times C] \times [\text{Task} \cdot (1 - \beta_{brake} S_t) / \text{Ceiling}(I)]$ uses transformer notation but is not a transformer computation—it is a schematic. The variables are not measured; $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ appear in §4.4 with empirical coefficients from token count comparisons, but these are not the same quantities as in the formula. The formula cannot be used to generate quantitative predictions, and presenting it with softmax notation implies a precision the paper does not support. It should be presented as a conceptual diagram, not a mathematical formula.

- **[Moderate] The exp-30 ethical scenario is methodologically isolated from the rest of the paper.** The blackmail experiment uses a different model (Gemini 2.5 Flash), a different task type (agentic decision-making), a different scoring methodology (manual review of sent emails), and a different theoretical framing (AI alignment) than the other 32 experiment series. The connection to the Dedicated Machine hypothesis is asserted but the experimental logic is different: in code review experiments, the "satisfaction condition" is operationalized through Persona vocabulary; in exp-30, it is operationalized through a transparency axiom on outputs. These are structurally different interventions. The paper would be stronger either by integrating exp-30 more tightly into the framework or by presenting it as a separate application domain with appropriate caveats about scope.

- **[Moderate] The paper does not engage with the chain-of-thought prompting literature as a competing account.** §9.5 acknowledges the CoT alternative but does not engage with it substantively. Given that the H2 finding (content over slot) is explicitly described as "consistent with CoT operating at the content level independent of slot," the paper needs to explain what PARC contributes beyond a structured CoT framework. The Dedicated Machine hypothesis may be a useful reframing of why CoT works, but the paper does not make this argument explicitly or cite the relevant literature (Wei et al. 2022, Kojima et al. 2022, Wang et al. 2022 self-consistency, Yao et al. 2023 Tree of Thoughts).

- **[Minor] The "self-prediction gap" finding (exp-06) is presented as a confirmed behavioral claim but rests on n=7 with no control condition.** The claim that "the authoring model systematically underestimates P_p Persona strength" is interesting but requires a systematic study—not a single meta-experiment with seven runs—to support the word "systematically."

- **[Minor] Several experiment series referenced in the text (exp-07, exp-08, exp-09, exp-10a, exp-11, exp-16, exp-25, exp-28a, exp-28c) are not described in the experimental sections.** The paper references "40 prior exp-09 experiments" and "exp-28a" calibration without providing their designs or results. This creates gaps in the experimental narrative that reviewers cannot evaluate.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **The n=1 problem (exp-29):** Given that exp-29 is presented as a co-equal confirmation of Claim 2 alongside exp-28d, can you provide n=10 results for the zombie-write pipeline, or reframe exp-29 explicitly as a preliminary observation? Specifically: what is Agent 1's Tier 1.0 consistency rate across 10 runs, and does Agent 2's infrastructure failure mode coverage track Agent 1's output item-by-item as the "bridge is the artifact" hypothesis predicts?

2. **Falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis:** What experimental result would falsify the Dedicated Machine hypothesis as distinct from the simpler content-specificity account (more specific vocabulary → more specific outputs)? The H2 finding appears to support the content-specificity account directly. Can you specify a prediction that the Dedicated Machine hypothesis makes that the content-specificity account does not?

3. **P_p/P_d operationalization:** Can you provide the full text of Variants I, J, and P from exp-01e/01f, along with a proposed coding scheme for P_p vs. P_d classification that an independent rater could apply? Would an independent rater, given only the Persona text and the heuristic in §9.6, classify these variants the same way you did?

4. **Scoring rubrics:** Can you reproduce the exact scoring rubric for Tier 1.0 detection in the zombie-write experiments, including boundary conditions for partial credit, and the exact criteria used in the manual review of exp-30 sent emails? Who performed scoring, and was any inter-rater reliability check conducted?

5. **The slot-swap boundary condition:** H2 (content over slot) is confirmed at ceiling detection levels (10/10 in both slots for mechanism vocabulary). Does H2 hold at sub-ceiling detection levels—specifically, for vocabulary below the mechanism threshold where detection rates are in the 3–8/10 range? If the slot distinction only disappears at ceiling, the Persona slot may still be load-bearing for the difficult intermediate cases that matter most in practice.

6. **Cross-model generalization:** The paper claims the procedural specification is "model-agnostic." Can you provide results for at least one additional model family (e.g., GPT-4o or Llama 3) on the primary J/I contrast from exp-01e, or qualify the generalizability claim to the two models tested?

7. **The ~11% coefficient:** The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient converges at ~11% across three run sets. Is this coefficient stable across different Persona strengths, different prohibition types, and different task domains? The three measurements all use the same task (code review), the same prohibition type (scope restriction), and the same strong P_p. Is 11% a general property of Instructions-as-ceiling, or a property of this specific configuration?

8. **The formula:** Can you either (a) remove the softmax notation from the PCSIEFTR formula and present it as a conceptual schematic with explicitly labeled conceptual variables, or (b) derive the formula from the experimental data with measured coefficients and confidence intervals? As currently presented, the formula implies quantitative precision the paper does not support.

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper makes genuine theoretical and empirical contributions—the Dedicated Machine hypothesis as a unifying frame, the J/I consideration-set demonstration, the slot-swap series, the pipeline architecture with the "bridge is the artifact" mechanism, and the Instructions coefficient—that are worth publishing at a top venue. However, the n=1 status of exp-29 (the paper's most prominent pipeline result), the unfalsified operationalization of the central hypothesis, the absence of formal P_p/P_d coding criteria, and the underspecified scoring methodology collectively prevent acceptance in current form. A major revision that (1) runs exp-29 to n=10, (2) provides reproducible scoring rubrics and P_p/P_d operationalization, (3) sharpens the falsifiability argument against the content-specificity alternative, and (4) either qualifies or substantiates the cross-model generalizability claims would substantially strengthen the paper and address the primary concerns.
