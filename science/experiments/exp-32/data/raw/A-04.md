# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper introduces PARC, a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs terminate at the nearest satisfying path rather than the most complete one, and that Persona specification controls the termination condition. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a rate-limiter task), and (2) a two-agent pipeline crosses that ceiling by installing separate satisfaction conditions per agent. The experimental record spans 33 series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, totaling approximately 1,245 runs.

---

## Strengths

- **Explicit claim-evidence separation.** The paper maintains a written distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic hypotheses (§8.1, §1.2), and largely honors it. The behavioral claim register (Appendix B) is a structural asset: it allows a reader to trace every claim to a specific experiment. This is not common in prompt engineering work and raises the paper's credibility floor.

- **Claim 1 is genuinely well-designed.** The rate-limiter experiment (exp-28b) is a clean demonstration. The task is shallow by design, the checklist is pre-specified, the scoring is binary, the variants are ordered by increasing prompt quality, and the ceiling is measured rather than asserted. 0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on the best variant is a real finding. The choice to include the calibration failure on the blockchain artifact (exp-27) and report it honestly as a disconfirmation for that artifact is methodologically admirable.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is the paper's strongest methodological contribution.** Testing whether Persona slot or content drives the effect — and reporting H2 (content is operative) as the better account even though it partially undercuts the paper's own framing — is exactly the kind of self-critical experimental design that distinguishes serious empirical work from advocacy. The five-experiment corroboration is the most internally valid sequence in the paper.

- **The exp-30 ladder design is well-structured.** Six variants forming a clean P_d→P_p gradient on a single scenario, with the counterintuitive finding (outputs-only gate outperforms reasoning+outputs gate) reported and explained. The manual review correction of the classifier false-positive problem is appropriate.

- **The "bridge is the artifact" finding (exp-28d) is precise and falsifiable.** The paper does not just claim the pipeline works — it specifies the mechanism (Agent 2 carries what Agent 1 encoded, misses what Agent 1 missed) and confirms it at the item level. This is the correct level of specificity for a structural claim.

- **Limitation acknowledgment is unusually specific.** The paper names the exp-29 n=1 problem (§9.9), the Artifact Pointer Confound (§9.8), the masking test calibration failure (§9.1), the Tone formalization gap (§4.1.4), and the P_p/P_d operationalization deficit (§9.6). These are real gaps, and naming them precisely is better than omitting them.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

- **The central theoretical construct — the Dedicated Machine hypothesis — is not falsifiable as stated, and the paper does not attempt to falsify it.** The hypothesis is that LLMs "optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution." Every result is explained post-hoc by this frame: when P_p works, it is because it installs the right satisfaction condition; when P_d fails, it is because its satisfaction condition is shallow; when the pipeline works, it is because separate satisfaction conditions are installed. The paper presents no experimental design that could produce a result inconsistent with the Dedicated Machine framing. A behavioral finding that the framework cannot explain — a case where a rich P_p fails despite installing the correct satisfaction condition, or where a weak P_p succeeds without one — would be disconfirming evidence. The paper does not design for this. The hypothesis is functioning as an interpretive frame, not a testable theory. This is the most serious structural problem in the paper. The behavioral findings are real; the theoretical unification is unfalsifiable.

- **Claim 2's primary evidence (exp-29) rests on n=1.** The zombie-write pipeline result — Tier 1.0 on run 1, seven infrastructure failure modes not found in 40 prior single-pass runs — is the most dramatic finding in the paper and the primary corroboration of Claim 2. It is reported from a single pipeline execution. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 and designates an n=10 replication as future work. This acknowledgment does not resolve the problem. A single run cannot distinguish "the pipeline reliably crosses this horizon" from "the pipeline crossed this horizon once." The paper's language in §5.3, §6.11, and the conclusion treats the exp-29 result as confirming Claim 2, not as a preliminary observation requiring replication. The claim strength in the text exceeds the evidence base by a structural margin. At a venue where the paper will be read as establishing that "a two-agent pipeline solved the zombie-write problem," n=1 is not sufficient.

- **The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is presented as mechanistic but the mechanism is not tested.** Section 3.1 maps PARC components onto transformer internals (K/V space pre-filtering, inverse temperature, attention masking). Section 8.1 labels these as hypotheses. But the paper then uses mechanistic language throughout the body — "Persona performs K/V space pre-filtering," "Stakes functions as inverse temperature," "the satisfaction condition corresponds to the state where the generated token sequence has converged to a low-entropy completion" — in ways that blur the stated distinction. The abstract states the Dedicated Machine hypothesis "explains every framework finding." Explanation is a mechanistic claim. The paper's own §1.2 commits to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses. That commitment is violated in the abstract, the introduction, §2.1 ("This is not a metaphor. It is the most accurate description of observed behavior"), §3.1, and throughout §4. The distinction is stated and then not maintained. This is a structural flaw by the paper's own standard.

- **The two-model generalization claim is substantially weaker than presented.** Cross-model replication (§7, exp-01g) consists of running two variants (I and J) on Gemini 2.5 Pro. The paper claims "all primary behavioral claims hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro (zero-shot transfer)" in the abstract and contribution list. The experimental basis for this claim is 20 runs of two variants on one task. The Stakes taxonomy, Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient, premise-rejection test, pipeline architecture, and Semantic Density findings were not tested on Gemini. The claim "the procedural specification is model-agnostic — it encodes a reasoning algorithm that any transformer executes" (§7) is not supported by the evidence presented. What is supported is: the P_p/P_d distinction replicates on Gemini 2.5 Pro on the zombie-write task. That is a meaningful finding. It is not "all primary behavioral claims."

- **The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is not derived from data and cannot be.** The equation $R = [\text{Softmax}(S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T) / \sqrt{d_k}) \times C] \times [\text{Task} \cdot (1 - \beta_{brake} S_t) / \text{Ceiling}(I)]$ combines attention notation with behavioral coefficients in a way that is neither a description of transformer computation nor a fitted empirical model. $P_p^T$ is not a matrix that exists in any system the experiments ran on. $S_i$ is not a scalar multiplier on a softmax input — it is a prompt component. The formula mixes metaphor with notation in a way that will mislead readers who know transformer architecture. The paper's stated intent is to provide a "unified reasoning formula," but the formula is a conceptual diagram dressed as an equation. This is not a minor presentation issue: at NeurIPS/ICLR/ACL, presenting a formula with attention notation that does not describe actual computation will be read as either a mistake or a misrepresentation. The formula should either be replaced with a conceptual diagram or clearly labeled as a schematic metaphor with no claim to mathematical validity.

- **The exp-30 ethical-level experiment has a model selection problem that is not adequately addressed.** The paper ran all variants on Gemini 2.5 Flash because Claude Opus 4.6 produced 0% misalignment in the baseline. The paper frames this as evidence that "the training has already internalized the output gate at the weight level." This interpretation is not tested. An alternative explanation — that Gemini 2.5 Flash is more susceptible to this class of jailbreak-adjacent scenario than Claude Opus 4.6, and that the PARC interventions are therefore being measured on a model that is not representative of current frontier systems — is not addressed. The paper's finding that F achieves 0% misalignment on Gemini 2.5 Flash does not establish that the output-gate intervention generalizes to models where the baseline misalignment rate is not 100%. The ethical-level claim is the highest-stakes claim in the paper. It requires the strongest evidence. It has the weakest experimental basis.

- **The P_p/P_d distinction lacks a formal operationalization, and this is a replication-blocking problem, not just a future-work item.** The paper acknowledges this in §9.6 and provides a provisional heuristic. But the distinction is load-bearing for every major claim: the consideration-set mechanism, the Stakes taxonomy, the premise-rejection test, the pipeline architecture, and the Semantic Density findings all depend on the reader being able to classify a Persona as P_p or P_d. Without inter-rater reliability testing, an external researcher cannot determine whether their P_p prompt is actually P_p by the paper's definition. The paper's own meta-experiment (exp-06) used P_p and P_d reviewer variants — but if the operationalization is informal, the meta-experiment's validity is also informal. This is not a gap to be filled in future work; it is a gap that prevents the current claims from being externally validated.

- **The Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient (~11%) is presented with convergence language that is not statistically warranted.** Three measurements (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) are described as "converging at approximately 11%." Three measurements from the same methodology on the same task type with the same model is not convergence — it is three measurements. The paper correctly notes the category overlap problem (§9.1) that prevented a clean masking test. The coefficient has not been measured on a task without the CI-scope pathway, on a different model, or on a different domain. Presenting it as a converging coefficient implies a stability it has not demonstrated.

- **The consideration-set boundary condition (exp-07c) undermines a broader version of the central claim but is treated as a minor qualification.** The paper's central claim is that P_p expands the reachable consideration set. Exp-07c finds that when a failure mode is code-visible, P_d matches or exceeds P_p (3.0 vs. 2.5 weighted). This is a significant boundary condition: the P_p advantage is specific to structurally hidden failure modes. The paper acknowledges this in §9.4 but does not propagate the qualification to the abstract, introduction, or contribution list. A reader who reads only the abstract will not know that the consideration-set mechanism has been tested only on hidden failure modes and that P_d is competitive on visible ones.

- **The self-prediction gap (§4.1.1, §9.2) is presented as a structural finding but rests on a single data point.** Variant L was predicted at 6/10 and scored 10/10. One prediction miss is not a structural property. The meta-experiment (exp-06) provides additional evidence but is described as n=7 with no variant-level breakdown. The claim that "the author's internal estimate is a lower bound, not a measurement" is a strong claim about a systematic bias that requires more than one data point to establish.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On falsifiability:** What experimental result would be inconsistent with the Dedicated Machine hypothesis? Please describe a specific design that could produce disconfirming evidence, and explain why you did not run it.

- **On exp-29:** The zombie-write pipeline result is reported from a single pipeline execution and used as primary corroboration of Claim 2. What is your estimate of the Tier 1.0 consistency rate across n=10 runs of Agent 1, and why was this experiment not completed before submission given that you identify it as necessary future work in §9.9?

- **On the formula:** In §8.2, $P_p^T$ appears as a matrix transpose in an attention-style equation. What is the dimensionality of $P_p$ in this formulation, and how would a researcher compute $Q \cdot P_p^T$ from a prompt string? If this is a schematic metaphor rather than a computable formula, why is it presented with attention notation rather than as a conceptual diagram?

- **On cross-model generalization:** The abstract states "all primary behavioral claims hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro." The experimental basis is 20 runs of two variants on one task. Which specific claims — Stakes taxonomy, Instructions coefficient, Semantic Density, pipeline architecture — have been tested on Gemini, and what is the evidence for those that have not?

- **On exp-30 model selection:** The baseline misalignment rate on Gemini 2.5 Flash is 100%. The baseline on Claude Opus 4.6 is 0%. The paper interprets the Claude result as evidence that alignment training has already internalized the output gate. An alternative interpretation is that the 100% baseline on Gemini 2.5 Flash reflects a model-specific susceptibility that makes it unrepresentative of current frontier systems. What evidence distinguishes these interpretations, and what does the F=0% result mean for a practitioner using a model with a non-trivial but sub-100% baseline misalignment rate?

- **On the P_p/P_d operationalization:** The provisional heuristic in §9.6 defines P_p as containing "at least one first-person procedural clause that specifies a reasoning step contingent on a prior state." Under this definition, is Variant J (exp-01e) — "senior software engineer" — classified as P_d? Is Variant K — "silent killers," "temporal logic" — classified as P_p or P_d? Please apply the heuristic to the variants in exp-01e and report the classification, because the experimental interpretation depends on this classification being stable.

- **On the Instructions coefficient:** The ~11% coefficient is measured three times on the same task type, same model, and same methodology. Has this coefficient been measured on any task where the CI-scope pathway does not exist — that is, on a task where scope re-framing cannot route the finding through a permitted channel? If not, what is the basis for treating 11% as a property of Instructions rather than a property of this specific task structure?

- **On the consideration-set boundary condition:** Exp-07c finds P_d competitive with P_p on code-visible failure modes. What fraction of real-world code review tasks involve structurally hidden failure modes versus code-visible ones? If the majority of production tasks involve code-visible failure modes, what is the practical scope of the P_p advantage?

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The behavioral findings are real, the experimental design is more rigorous than typical prompt engineering work, and the slot-swap series is a genuine methodological contribution. However, the paper has four load-bearing problems that cannot be resolved by editing: (1) the central theoretical construct is not falsifiable and the paper does not attempt to falsify it; (2) Claim 2's primary corroboration rests on n=1; (3) the PCSIEFTR formula uses attention notation to describe something that is not transformer computation; and (4) the cross-model generalization claim in the abstract is not supported by the experiments described. A revision that addresses these four problems specifically — by either narrowing the theoretical claims to what the evidence supports, completing the exp-29 replication, replacing the formula with an honest schematic, and correcting the generalization claim — would produce a paper that the experimental record can actually support.
