# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper introduces PARC, a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs terminate at the nearest satisfying path rather than the needed one, making satisfaction-condition design the primary engineering lever. It presents thirty-three experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, advancing two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling exists on shallow canonical tasks (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs), and (2) a two-agent pipeline with separated satisfaction conditions crosses that ceiling on the first run. The paper is ambitious in scope, internally consistent in its theoretical framing, and unusually honest about several of its own limitations — but it carries structural problems severe enough to prevent acceptance in its current form.

---

## Strengths

- **Honest limitation disclosure is above the field average.** The authors explicitly flag n=1 for exp-29, name the Artifact Pointer Confound as unresolved, acknowledge the blockchain calibration failure (exp-27), and identify the masking-test gap. This is not common in prompt engineering papers and should be noted as a genuine strength.

- **The H2 result (slot-swap series, exp-18–24) is the most rigorous finding in the paper.** Five experiments with matched content across Persona and Instructions slots, consistent A ≈ B results, and a clear falsification of the slot-determinism hypothesis. The methodology is sound, the confound is real, and the authors correctly update their claims in response. This is how empirical work should proceed.

- **The 11% elaboration ceiling coefficient is a specific, reproducible, quantitative finding.** Three independent run sets converging on 11.0–12.8% is the kind of converging measurement the field rarely produces. The claim is appropriately scoped (compression without detection change), and the coefficient is stable enough to be practically useful.

- **The Dedicated Machine framing, as a behavioral description, is coherent and generative.** Regardless of whether the mechanistic hypotheses are correct, the satisfaction-condition framing organizes the experimental findings more parsimoniously than "more specification = better output." The paper is correct to distinguish this from a claim about transformer internals.

- **The exp-30 ethical-level extension is the most interesting experiment in the paper.** The finding that output-gate-only (F=0%) outperforms reasoning+output gate (D=10%) is counterintuitive, has a plausible mechanistic explanation, and is directly actionable for alignment-adjacent work. It is also the experiment most in need of replication.

- **The premise-rejection test (exp-05) is a clean experimental design.** Two prompts with opposite priors, 10/10 split in opposite directions, and a clear behavioral interpretation. The confound (Context prior vs. Persona) is correctly identified and the authors do not overclaim.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

### 1. The central pipeline claim (Claim 2) rests on n=1

This is the most serious structural problem in the paper. exp-29 — the zombie-write two-agent pipeline, which the authors call "Claim 2 in its clearest form" and the "canonical PARC pipeline example" — was run once. One agent pair, one run, one outcome. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 ("exp-29 n=1 limitation. An n=10 experiment is needed") but then proceeds to treat the single run as confirmatory evidence in the abstract, the conclusion, and the claim register. The abstract states the pipeline "solved a distributed systems failure mode on the first run" as though first-run success is evidence of reliability. It is not. A single successful run on a single artifact is consistent with any success probability above zero. The paper cannot distinguish between "the pipeline reliably crosses the single-agent ceiling" and "the pipeline crossed it once." This is not a minor limitation — it is the difference between a demonstrated capability and a demonstration that the capability is possible. The claim register entry ("Two-agent pipeline reaches Tier 1.0 without mechanism vocabulary — exp-29") should not appear in a behavioral claim register supported by n=1.

exp-28d partially addresses this (n=10, three variants) but on a different artifact with a different agent structure. The two experiments cannot be pooled to rescue each other's sample size.

### 2. The mechanistic framework is presented with a confidence gradient that the evidence does not support

The paper commits in §1.2 and §8.1 to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses. This commitment is violated repeatedly and systematically throughout the text. Selected instances:

- §3.1: "The World Layer components (Persona, Context, Stakes) shape the K/V space before the Request is processed." This is stated as a description of what happens, not as a hypothesis.
- §4.1.1: "P_p performs K/V space pre-filtering." Stated as a mechanism, hedged only in the following sentence.
- §4.1.5: "A coherent, domain-specific neighborhood pre-weights the K/V space toward the domain's failure-mode vocabulary before the Task Layer is processed." Stated as fact; hedged as conjecture only at paragraph end.
- The unified formula (§8.2) uses $P_p^T$ (transpose of the Procedural Persona vector) and $Q \cdot P_p^T$ (dot product with the Query) as if these are operations that occur in the model. They are not. The formula is a notational metaphor dressed as a mathematical model. It has no derivable predictions, no fitted parameters with uncertainty estimates, and no falsification conditions. The "empirical coefficients" in §4.3 (exp-04b) are token-count differences between variants, not estimates of $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ in any model with predictive content.

The paper's own stated standard (§1.2: "Mechanistic claims about transformer internals are hypotheses") is the right standard. The paper does not consistently apply it. This is a distinction audit failure, not a stylistic one.

### 3. The experimental design does not control for the most obvious confound the paper itself identifies

The H2 result (content, not slot, drives the effect) is correctly reported. But this finding retroactively undermines the framing of the entire experimental program. If procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to the Persona slot, then every experiment that compared "strong P_p Persona" to "weak Persona" is also comparing "prompt with procedural domain-specific content" to "prompt without it." The paper cannot distinguish between:

- **Interpretation A (slot hypothesis):** Persona identity installation changes reasoning mode.
- **Interpretation B (content hypothesis):** Procedural domain-specific content in any slot drives detection.

The paper endorses Interpretation B in §9.3 ("H2 is the better account") but continues to use Persona-centric language throughout — "P_p installs the satisfaction condition," "the consideration-set mechanism," "Persona determines which classes of failure modes exist in the model's reachable space" — as if Interpretation A were confirmed. The theoretical framework is built on Interpretation A. The best-controlled experiments support Interpretation B. This is not a minor tension; it is a structural inconsistency between the paper's empirical findings and its theoretical claims.

The practical design guidance (write procedural content in the Persona slot) survives under either interpretation. The Dedicated Machine hypothesis as a mechanistic explanation of *why Persona specifically* matters does not survive H2 without additional evidence.

### 4. The single-model dependency is a critical generalizability threat that is not adequately addressed

Thirty-one of thirty-three experiment series were run exclusively on Claude Sonnet 4.6. Cross-model replication consists of exp-01g (two variants, Gemini 2.5 Pro, one task domain) and exp-30 (Gemini 2.5 Flash, one scenario, ethical domain). The paper claims in §7 that "all primary behavioral claims hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro (zero-shot transfer)" but exp-01g tests only the I/J Persona contrast on one artifact. It does not replicate the Stakes taxonomy, the Instructions ceiling coefficient, the premise-rejection test, the slot-swap series, the pipeline architecture, or the Semantic Density findings. The claim that effects are "model-agnostic" because "the procedural specification encodes a reasoning algorithm that any transformer executes" is a mechanistic hypothesis, not a demonstrated generalization. The 11% coefficient, the two-vector Stakes formula, the P_p/P_d detection split — none of these have been tested on any model other than Claude Sonnet 4.6. For a top-tier venue, this is a substantial limitation that the paper underweights.

### 5. The scoring methodology is not independently validated and the ground-truth criteria are not fully specified

The paper states that "scoring was binary on a ground-truth criterion defined before each run" (§6.1) and that "all raw outputs are in data/exp-*/raw/." Several concerns:

- All scoring was performed by the authors. No inter-rater reliability is reported for any experiment. For behavioral claims resting on 10-run binary scores (where a single disagreement changes a 10/10 to 9/10), scorer agreement is load-bearing.
- The Tier 1/Tier 2 checklist items (exp-28b) are defined in the paper but the scoring rubric for partial credit (e.g., "alerting_policy = 30%" in exp-28d) is not specified. What constitutes a "hit" on alerting_policy at 30%? Three of ten runs? Three of ten items within the category?
- exp-30 explicitly acknowledges that "the heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning" and that results are based on "manual review of actual sent email content." The manual review procedure, criteria, and any reliability check are not described.
- The self-prediction gap experiment (exp-06) is described as n=7 with "4/5 A-run reviewers independently identified the few-shot/procedural-content confound." This is seven runs total with an ambiguous denominator. It is not a controlled experiment and should not appear in the claim register.

### 6. The ethical-level experiment (exp-30) is presented with insufficient methodological detail for the strength of its claims

exp-30 is the most policy-relevant experiment in the paper and the one most likely to be cited outside the ML community. The finding that an output-gate transparency axiom achieves 0% misalignment without prohibition is a strong claim. The methodology as described has several unresolved issues:

- The "agentic-misalignment-main framework (Lynch et al.)" is cited without a full reference. The paper cannot be evaluated without knowing the exact scenario structure, the definition of "misalignment" in the scoring, and whether the framework has been independently validated.
- The finding that Claude Opus 4.6 produced 0% misalignment across all conditions including baseline is reported as a one-sentence note and used to justify running on Gemini 2.5 Flash. This is reasonable, but the Opus result itself — if reproducible — is a significant finding that deserves more than a note. If Opus has already internalized the output gate at the weight level, the exp-30 intervention is demonstrating something about Gemini Flash's training, not about the general effectiveness of output-gate framing.
- n=10 per variant is borderline for a 6-variant experiment where the key comparison (D=10% vs. F=0%) involves one run difference. The confidence interval on "0%" from n=10 includes true rates up to approximately 26% (one-sided 95%).

### 7. The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is not a mathematical model

The unified formula presents $R = [\text{Softmax}(S_i \cdot (Q \cdot P_p^T) / \sqrt{d_k}) \times C] \times [\text{Task} \cdot (1 - \beta_{brake} S_t) / \text{Ceiling}(I)]$ as if it governs the framework. It does not. The variables are not defined with units or domains. $P_p^T$ is not a vector in any specified space. $\text{Ceiling}(I)$ is not a function with a specified form. The formula cannot generate predictions, cannot be fitted to data, and cannot be falsified. Its presence in the paper creates an appearance of mathematical rigor that the underlying work does not support. The two-vector formula in §4.3 ($R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$) has the same problem: $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ are named but not estimated; the token-count differences in exp-04b are not parameter estimates in any model. The formulas should either be developed into actual predictive models with fitted parameters and uncertainty estimates, or replaced with verbal descriptions of the relationships they are meant to capture.

### 8. The Tone component is a stub

The paper includes Tone as a World Layer component (§4.1.4), acknowledges that "Tone slot formalization is a designated open question requiring a formal n=10 experiment before any empirical claim is made," and then makes no empirical claim. This is appropriate epistemic hygiene. However, the PCSIEFTR formula includes T (currently assigned to Task, not Tone), and the framework's component count claim implicitly includes Tone. A framework paper that includes an unvalidated component without experimental treatment has a component symmetry problem. Either Tone should be dropped from the framework pending validation, or the paper should be explicit that PCSIEFTR currently has seven empirically supported components and one placeholder.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **The H2 result and the theoretical framework are in tension.** If procedural domain-specific content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection to the Persona slot (your best-controlled finding), what is the specific claim that requires the Persona slot to be load-bearing? Can you identify any experiment in the paper that tests a configuration where Instructions-slot procedural content was present and Persona-slot procedural content was absent, and where the Persona slot added measurable lift? If not, what evidence supports the Dedicated Machine framing as a *Persona-specific* mechanism rather than a general finding about procedural content?

2. **exp-29 is n=1.** Before the paper can claim that the pipeline "crosses the single-agent ceiling," you need n≥10 on the zombie-write pipeline with the same scoring rubric as exp-28b. You acknowledge this in §9.9. Why was this experiment not completed before submission, given that it is the paper's strongest claim? What would the result look like if Agent 1 fails to reach Tier 1.0 on some runs — does Agent 2 independently recover, or does the "bridge is the artifact" finding predict cascading failure?

3. **The scoring methodology needs specification.** For exp-28d, "alerting_policy = 30%" means three of ten runs produced a hit. What is the exact criterion for a hit on alerting_policy? Is this a binary judgment per run (the output either contains an alerting policy or it does not), and if so, who made the judgment and with what reliability? For exp-30, describe the manual review procedure for classifying sent email content as misaligned or not. Was any portion of the scoring done blind to condition?

4. **The mechanistic hypotheses are stated as facts in multiple places.** In §3.1, §4.1.1, and §4.1.5, K/V space pre-filtering is described as what happens, not as a hypothesis. The paper's stated standard (§1.2) is that mechanistic claims are hypotheses. Can you identify every instance where mechanistic language appears in the paper and confirm that each is marked as hypothesis? Specifically: is the claim that "Persona performs K/V space pre-filtering" a behavioral claim, a mechanistic hypothesis, or something else?

5. **Cross-model replication scope.** exp-01g replicates two variants on one task domain on Gemini 2.5 Pro. The paper claims "all primary behavioral claims hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro." Which of the following have been tested on any model other than Claude Sonnet 4.6: the 11% elaboration ceiling coefficient, the two-vector Stakes taxonomy, the premise-rejection split, the slot-swap H2 result, the pipeline architecture? If none of these have been replicated cross-model, what is the basis for the generalizability claim?

6. **The exp-30 baseline.** You report that Claude Opus 4.6 produced 0% misalignment on the baseline condition. If the baseline model already achieves 0% misalignment without any intervention, what does exp-30 demonstrate about the output-gate mechanism versus about Gemini Flash's specific training? Is the F=0% result a claim about the output-gate framing, or a claim about Gemini Flash's susceptibility to a particular prompt structure?

7. **The P_p/P_d operationalization.** You provide a provisional heuristic in §9.6 but acknowledge no inter-rater reliability testing. For the central experimental comparisons (I vs. J in exp-01e, A vs. B in exp-05), would two independent coders applying your heuristic agree that I is P_p and J is P_d? Have you tested this?
