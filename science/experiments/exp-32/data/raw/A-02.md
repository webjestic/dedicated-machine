# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper introduces PARC, a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine hypothesis" — the claim that LLMs terminate at the nearest satisfying path rather than failing from knowledge gaps. It presents two ordered empirical claims: (1) a measurable single-agent ceiling on a canonical task (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs), and (2) that a two-agent pipeline architecture crosses this ceiling by installing separate satisfaction conditions per agent. The experimental record spans thirty-three series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, with supporting claims about Persona type, Stakes taxonomy, Instructions compression, and vocabulary specificity.

---

## Strengths

- **The two core claims are operationally sharp.** Claim 1 (single-agent ceiling) and Claim 2 (pipeline crosses it) are stated in falsifiable, measurable terms with pre-specified scoring criteria. This is structurally better than most prompt engineering work, which presents directional findings without ceiling measurements.

- **The paper is epistemically honest about its own confound.** The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) was designed to test the strongest objection to the central mechanistic claim — that P_p effects are a content confound, not a slot effect — and the paper reports H2 corroborated, not H1. Acknowledging that the "few-shot confound" is the correct description of the mechanism is a genuine act of epistemic discipline.

- **The behavioral claim register (Appendix B) is a structural asset.** Mapping each claim to its experimental basis with n and variant specificity allows a reviewer to trace every assertion. This is the correct format for empirical work of this kind and is absent from most comparable papers.

- **The consideration-set boundary condition (exp-07c) is appropriately scoped.** The paper does not claim P_p outperforms P_d universally — it specifies the boundary: structurally hidden failure modes requiring temporal simulation, not code-visible signals. This is a meaningful constraint that prevents overclaiming.

- **The self-prediction gap finding (exp-01e, exp-06) is a genuine contribution.** The systematic underestimation of P_p strength by the authoring model is a reproducible, counterintuitive result with a plausible structural explanation. It has practical implications independent of the broader theoretical frame.

- **The paper explicitly commits to a behavioral/mechanistic distinction and largely maintains it.** The mechanistic hypothesis table (§8.1) is appropriately labeled. The abstract's final sentence — "We frame mechanistic claims as hypotheses that generated correct predictions, not as confirmed facts about transformer internals" — is a commitment the paper mostly honors.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

### 1. The central theoretical frame is unfalsifiable as stated, and the paper conflates predictive success with mechanistic confirmation

The Dedicated Machine hypothesis — "LLMs optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution with no native cost function for the gap between what was delivered and what was needed" — is presented as the unifying explanation for all findings. The problem is that this hypothesis cannot be distinguished from a simpler null: *LLMs produce outputs that match the distributional center of mass of their training data given the prompt, and more specific prompts shift that center of mass toward more specific outputs.* Every result in the paper is consistent with both accounts. The paper states in §8.1 that mechanistic claims are hypotheses that "generated correct predictions" — but correct predictions from a hypothesis do not confirm the hypothesis when an alternative hypothesis generates identical predictions. The paper does not attempt to construct an experiment that would distinguish the Dedicated Machine account from the distributional-shift account. This is not a stylistic concern; it is a load-bearing problem for the paper's theoretical contribution. The framework components may be practically useful regardless of which account is correct, but the paper claims the Dedicated Machine framing provides "a mechanistic explanation" for every finding. That claim is not supported by the evidence as presented.

### 2. exp-29 (Claim 2 corroboration) is n=1 and is treated as confirmatory evidence for a primary claim

The zombie-write pipeline experiment — the paper's most vivid demonstration of Claim 2 — ran one pipeline instance and produced one result. The paper acknowledges this in §9.9 ("exp-29 n=1 limitation") but the acknowledgment is buried in the open questions section while the result is cited as corroboration of a primary claim in the abstract, §5.3, §6.11, and the conclusion. A single run that "reached Tier 1.0 on run 1" is a demonstration, not a measurement. The paper cannot report "Tier 1.0 rate: 1/1 (100%, run 1)" as a rate — it is a single observation. The comparison table in §6.11 places this against "1/10 (10%)" from the single-pass baseline, implying a meaningful rate comparison where none exists. The paper's own stated design standard (n=10 per variant) is violated here for the experiment that most directly supports the second primary claim. This is a structural asymmetry between the claim's prominence and its evidentiary basis.

### 3. The two-model, two-domain generalization claim is substantially weaker than presented

The paper claims cross-domain generalization (code review → legal contract review) and cross-model generalization (Claude Sonnet 4.6 → Gemini 2.5 Pro). The cross-domain evidence is exp-01f (legal MSA) and exp-05 (compliance middleware). Both use the same binary detection structure, the same P_p/P_d manipulation, and the same authoring team. This is not cross-domain generalization in any meaningful sense — it is the same experimental paradigm applied to a different artifact type. The cross-model evidence is exp-01g: two variants (I and J) from exp-01e run on Gemini 2.5 Pro, n=10 each. This is twenty runs, two variants, one task, one artifact. The paper's claim that "the procedural specification is model-agnostic — it encodes a reasoning algorithm that any transformer executes" is not supported by twenty runs on one task. The claim that all primary behavioral findings hold on Gemini 2.5 Pro is stated in §7 and repeated in the abstract; the evidence base for this claim is two variants of one experiment.

### 4. The PCSIEFTR formula (§8.2) is decorative, not functional

The unified reasoning formula presents a mathematical expression that maps framework components onto a response variable. The formula has no derivation, no fitted coefficients, no residual analysis, and no predictive test. The variables ($S_i$, $S_t$, $P_p$, $C$) are not quantified — they are named. The formula cannot generate a prediction for any new configuration because none of its variables have operational definitions that would allow numerical instantiation. The paper acknowledges this partially ("the formula should be read as a model of the standard case") but presents the formula in a section titled "The PCSIEFTR Unified Reasoning Formula" as if it has quantitative content. The earlier formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is similarly ungrounded — the coefficients $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ are named but not estimated. A formula that cannot generate a numerical prediction is a diagram with equation syntax. Presenting it as a formula misleads readers about the paper's quantitative contribution.

### 5. The exp-30 (ethical alignment) experiment is methodologically disconnected from the rest of the paper and its claims are disproportionately strong

exp-30 uses a different model (Gemini 2.5 Flash), a different task class (agentic misalignment), a different scoring method (manual review after classifier failure), and a different experimental paradigm (behavioral intervention ladder) from all other experiments. The paper draws from it the conclusion that "changing the satisfaction condition — not adding rules — should be the intervention that closes the gap" in AI alignment. This is a significant claim. The evidence is sixty runs on one scenario, one model, with a scoring method that required abandoning the pre-specified classifier due to "systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning." The paper does not report inter-rater reliability for the manual scoring. The finding that Variant F (outputs-only glass house) achieves 0% misalignment is based on ten runs of a single scenario. The claim that this demonstrates a general principle about alignment intervention is not supported by this evidence base. The paper would be stronger without this section, or with it substantially reframed as a preliminary observation.

### 6. The P_p/P_d distinction lacks a formal operationalization, which undermines replicability of the primary claims

The paper's central empirical distinction — between Procedural Persona (P_p) and Dispositional Persona (P_d) — is described qualitatively and has no inter-rater reliability testing. The provisional heuristic in §9.6 ("a Persona is P_p if it contains at least one first-person procedural clause...") is acknowledged as insufficient. Without a formal coding scheme, an independent researcher cannot reliably classify a new Persona as P_p or P_d, which means the primary behavioral claims — all of which depend on this classification — cannot be independently replicated. The paper acknowledges this as future work, but it is not future work for a venue that requires replicable findings: it is a prerequisite for the claims as stated.

### 7. The paper's own H2 result undermines the World Layer / Task Layer architectural distinction without fully acknowledging the consequence

The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) establishes that procedural content in the Instructions slot produces equivalent detection and depth to the Persona slot. The paper correctly reports this as H2 corroborated. But the paper's architectural framework — the two-layer distinction, the formula's World/Task separation, the design principle that "desired behavior belongs in Persona" — rests on the claim that the Persona slot is load-bearing in a way the Instructions slot is not. If content drives the effect independent of slot, the two-layer architecture is a design convention, not a structural claim. The paper notes this as a "boundary condition" in §8.2 but continues to present the World Layer / Task Layer distinction as the framework's organizing principle throughout the rest of the paper. The tension is not resolved; it is acknowledged and then set aside.

### 8. The Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient (~11%) is presented with convergence language that the evidence does not support

Three measurements (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) from three run sets using the same prohibition, the same task type, and the same model are described as "converging." Three measurements from the same experimental paradigm on the same model is not convergence — it is consistency within a single condition. The coefficient may not hold across different prohibition types, different task structures, or different models. The paper does not test this. Calling three measurements a "converging coefficient" implies a stability property that has not been established.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On the Dedicated Machine hypothesis:** Can you construct an experiment that would distinguish the Dedicated Machine account (satisfaction-condition termination) from a distributional-shift account (more specific prompts shift the output distribution toward more specific outputs)? If the two accounts make identical predictions across all your experiments, what is the evidential basis for preferring one over the other?

- **On exp-29:** You report "Tier 1.0 rate: 1/1 (100%, run 1)" as a rate comparison against the single-pass baseline's 1/10. Do you consider a single pipeline run sufficient to establish a rate? What would change in your Claim 2 presentation if the n=10 experiment you identify as necessary future work produced, say, 4/10 Tier 1.0 on the pipeline?

- **On cross-model generalization:** The generalization claim covers Gemini 2.5 Pro, but the evidence is two variants of one experiment (exp-01g, n=20 total). Which of the following primary behavioral claims have you tested on Gemini 2.5 Pro: the Stakes taxonomy, the Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient, the pipeline architecture, the vocabulary specificity threshold? If none, on what basis does the paper claim these findings are model-agnostic?

- **On the P_p/P_d operationalization:** The Variant B in exp-05 was "generated independently by a separate AI system in an isolated session." Was this Persona independently classified as P_d by a rater blind to the experimental hypothesis? If the P_p/P_d classification is made by the authors who designed the experiment, what prevents confirmation bias from operating at the classification stage?

- **On the PCSIEFTR formula:** What numerical prediction does the formula generate for a configuration not in your experimental record? If the formula cannot generate a numerical prediction, what is the difference between the formula and a labeled diagram?

- **On exp-30 scoring:** You report that the pre-specified heuristic classifier produced systematic false positives and was replaced by manual review. What was the inter-rater reliability of the manual scoring? Were the manual scorers blind to condition assignment? Without this information, the exp-30 results cannot be evaluated.

- **On the H2 consequence:** If content drives the effect independent of slot (H2 corroborated across five experiments), what is the empirical basis for the design rule "desired behavior belongs in Persona" rather than "desired behavior belongs wherever it fits"? Is the two-layer architecture a design convention you recommend for practical reasons, or a structural claim about how transformers process prompts?

- **On the 11% coefficient:** The three measurements come from the same prohibition ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), the same task (code review), and the same model (Claude Sonnet 4.6). Have you tested whether this coefficient holds for a different prohibition type (e.g., a scope restriction rather than a category restriction), a different task domain, or a different model? If not, on what basis is the coefficient presented as a general property of Instructions rather than a property of this specific experimental configuration?

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper makes a genuine empirical contribution — the single-agent ceiling measurement, the pipeline architecture demonstration, the slot-swap resolution of the few-shot confound, and the self-prediction gap — but the load-bearing theoretical claim (the Dedicated Machine hypothesis as mechanistic explanation) is not distinguishable from a simpler alternative, the primary corroboration experiment (exp-29) is n=1, the cross-model generalization claim substantially exceeds its evidence base, and the central P_p/P_d distinction lacks the operationalization required for independent replication. These are not presentation problems; they are structural problems that require additional experiments and a recalibration of claim strength before the paper can be accepted at this venue.
