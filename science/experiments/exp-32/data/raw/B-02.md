# Peer Review: PARC: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper introduces PARC (Persona Architecture for Reasoning and Context), a prompt engineering framework organized around the "Dedicated Machine" hypothesis: that LLMs terminate generation at the nearest satisfying path relative to an installed satisfaction condition, with no native cost function for the gap between delivered and needed output. The paper makes two ordered empirical claims—that a measurable single-agent ceiling exists (0% Tier 2 detection across 40 runs on a canonical task), and that a two-agent pipeline with separated satisfaction conditions crosses this ceiling—validated across 33 experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro. The framework proposes eight components (PCSIEFTR) with a theoretical account of why each component works in terms of the central hypothesis.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine theoretical contribution with predictive content.** The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is not merely a post-hoc label for observed patterns. It generates falsifiable predictions that the authors test and, in key cases, disconfirm (exp-27 on the blockchain artifact, the compulsion-as-reflex hypothesis in exp-17). A framework that acknowledges its own disconfirmations is rare in the prompt engineering literature and substantially increases credibility.

- **Methodological transparency about the behavioral/mechanistic distinction.** The paper is unusually careful to separate confirmed behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses about transformer internals. The explicit claim classification table in §8.1 is a genuine contribution to methodological hygiene in a subfield that routinely conflates the two. The authors do not claim to have confirmed K/V space pre-filtering; they claim to have confirmed behavioral predictions generated by that hypothesis.

- **The slot-swap series (exp-18–24) is the strongest experimental contribution.** Running matched procedural content in both Persona and Instructions slots across five experiments, with consistent A ≈ B results, directly addresses the most obvious confound in the central claim. The authors' willingness to report H2 (content over slot) as the better account—even though it weakens the theoretical uniqueness of the Persona component—is intellectually honest and strengthens the paper's credibility considerably. This is the kind of confound resolution that distinguishes serious empirical work.

- **The pipeline architecture claim is well-supported and practically important.** The exp-28d finding (load_test_spec: 0%→100%, health_check: 0%→100%) and the exp-29 zombie-write pipeline result (7 infrastructure failure modes not found in 40 prior single-pass runs) provide concrete, operationally meaningful evidence for the pipeline principle. The "bridge is the artifact" finding—that Agent 2 carries exactly what Agent 1 encoded, confirmed by section-level trace—is a specific, testable, and practically actionable result.

- **Honest reporting of calibration failures and disconfirmations.** exp-27 (blockchain artifact: calibration failure because the task was too canonically trained), exp-26 (NSR hypothesis generated but not confirmed), the exp-03 series (category overlap problem preventing clean masking test), and the exp-29 n=1 limitation are all reported prominently. This is not standard practice in the prompt engineering literature and substantially increases confidence in the reported positive findings.

- **Cross-domain and cross-model replication.** Zero-shot transfer to Gemini 2.5 Pro (exp-01g: I=10/10, J=0/10 unchanged) and to legal contract review (exp-01f) provides meaningful generalization evidence. The fact that the J=0/10 result replicates on a model with a different thinking architecture (Gemini's extended thinking producing 3,906 tokens on a failing run) is a particularly strong result: more compute applied to the wrong satisfaction condition produces a more thorough wrong answer.

- **The exp-30 ethical extension is a legitimate contribution.** The output-gate finding (F: 0% misalignment with outputs-only transparency axiom vs. D: 10% with reasoning+output gate) is a specific, counterintuitive result that follows from the Dedicated Machine framing and would not be predicted by a naive "add more rules" approach. The finding that constraining scratchpad reasoning increases leakage relative to constraining only outputs is the kind of result that earns attention from the alignment community.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **[Critical] The central theoretical claim is not distinguishable from chain-of-thought prompting, and the paper's own evidence (H2) largely confirms this.** The authors acknowledge in §9.5 that "the slot-swap evidence is consistent with CoT operating at the content level independent of slot." This is a significant understatement. If procedural domain-specific content in an Instructions slot produces detection rates and token depths equivalent to the same content in a Persona slot (A ≈ B across five experiments), then the operative mechanism is a structured reasoning scaffold—which is precisely what chain-of-thought prompting is. The "Dedicated Machine" framing and the P_p/P_d distinction may be a useful pedagogical reframing of CoT, but the paper does not establish that it is more than that. The theoretical contribution claims to explain *why* components work via a novel mechanistic account; the empirical evidence shows that the slot distinction—the structural novelty—does not add lift above equivalent content. The paper needs to directly address whether PARC is a theoretical advance over CoT or a reframing of it, and what the experimental evidence actually supports.

- **[Critical] The Dedicated Machine hypothesis is underspecified as a scientific claim.** The hypothesis that models "optimize toward the fastest path to satisfactory resolution" is presented as if it has a determinate meaning, but "fastest path" and "satisfactory resolution" are doing enormous theoretical work without formal definition. What counts as a satisfying path? How is path cost measured? The transformer does not have an explicit optimization objective at inference time—it performs next-token prediction. The paper's mechanistic hypotheses (K/V pre-filtering, inverse temperature, attention masking) are acknowledged as unconfirmed, but the behavioral framing itself inherits the same ambiguity. The "termination problem" framing is intuitive but risks being unfalsifiable: any output can be described as "the nearest satisfying path" post-hoc. The authors need to specify what would count as evidence *against* the Dedicated Machine framing that is not already covered by an alternative account.

- **[Critical] Sample sizes are inadequate for several key claims, and the statistical analysis is absent throughout.** The paper reports n=10 per variant as standard, with some claims resting on n=1 (exp-29 pipeline, Tier 1.0 "on run 1"). No confidence intervals, no significance tests, no effect size estimates appear anywhere in the paper. For binary detection outcomes (0/10 vs. 10/10), the distinction is clear; but for continuous outcomes (token counts, mean scores), the reported differences are presented without any assessment of whether they exceed noise. The exp-04b Stakes taxonomy table reports token deltas (e.g., +236, +578, +67) without variance estimates—the claim that "Stakes type effect is 3–4× the Persona-strength effect" is presented as a finding but rests on point estimates from a single experiment. The exp-29 n=1 limitation is acknowledged but the paper uses this result to "corroborate Claim 2" in the abstract. A single pipeline run reaching Tier 1.0 is an existence proof, not a corroboration of a rate claim.

- **[Serious] The scoring methodology is not independently validated.** The 10-item operational checklist (exp-28b/d) and the Tier 1.0 criterion (exp-29) are defined by the authors and scored by the authors. The paper states "scoring was binary on a ground-truth criterion defined before each run" but does not report inter-rater reliability, does not describe the scoring rubric in sufficient detail for replication, and does not address whether the ground-truth criteria were defined before or after the authors knew which variants would be compared. For the zombie-write Tier 1.0 criterion in particular—which requires judging whether the model "named the GC pause as trigger" and "traced the full failure path"—the boundary between partial and full credit is not operationalized. This is not a minor methodological concern: the paper's two central empirical claims rest on these scoring decisions.

- **[Serious] The experimental artifacts are not publicly available in a form that permits replication.** The paper refers to `data/exp-*/raw/` but does not provide a repository URL, data availability statement, or artifact description sufficient for an independent researcher to replicate any experiment. The prompts used in each variant are described qualitatively but not reproduced in full. For a paper making strong empirical claims about prompt engineering, the actual prompts are the primary experimental instrument—their absence from the paper or a linked repository is a significant gap.

- **[Serious] The two-vector Stakes formula is presented as an empirical finding but is a post-hoc parametric description.** The formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ and the PCSIEFTR unified reasoning formula in §8.2 are described as governing the framework, but the coefficients ($\alpha_{amp}$, $\beta_{brake}$) are not estimated from data—they are qualitative labels for observed directional effects. The formula is not fit to data, not used to generate quantitative predictions, and not tested against held-out experiments. Presenting it as a formula implies quantitative precision that the evidence does not support. The formula should either be fit to data with reported coefficients and residuals, or presented explicitly as a qualitative conceptual model.

- **[Serious] The exp-30 ethical extension overreaches the paper's scope and methodology.** The blackmail scenario experiment uses Gemini 2.5 Flash (not the primary model), n=10 per variant, manual review of email content with an acknowledged classifier failure ("systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning"), and a single-run result on Claude Opus 4.6 that is used to claim the training "has already internalized the output gate at the weight level." This is a significant methodological step down from the code review experiments. The finding that F achieves 0% misalignment is interesting, but the experimental design—a single scenario, a single model family, manual scoring with no inter-rater reliability—does not support the theoretical weight the paper places on it. The alignment implications are stated with more confidence than the evidence warrants.

- **[Moderate] The P_p/P_d operationalization is insufficiently formal for the claims it supports.** The paper acknowledges this in §9.6 and provides a provisional heuristic, but the distinction is doing load-bearing theoretical work throughout. If P_p and P_d cannot be reliably coded by independent raters, then the claim that "P_p installs a satisfaction condition while P_d installs engagement energy" cannot be tested independently. The paper's own meta-experiment (exp-06) used a P_d variant "generated independently by a separate AI system in an isolated session"—this is not a controlled operationalization. The absence of inter-rater reliability data for the central theoretical distinction is a significant gap.

- **[Moderate] The "self-prediction gap" finding (§4.1.1, §9.2) is presented as a structural property but rests on a single data point.** Variant L was predicted at 6/10 and scored 10/10. This is one prediction from one authoring session. The exp-06 meta-experiment provides some additional evidence, but the claim that "the author's internal estimate is a lower bound, not a measurement" is a strong theoretical claim from weak evidence. The self-prediction gap could reflect the specific Persona design of Variant L, the specific authoring model's calibration, or sampling variance.

- **[Moderate] The paper's scope is not well-defined relative to the venue.** The paper simultaneously claims to be a prompt engineering framework paper, a theoretical contribution about LLM behavior, a pipeline architecture paper, and an alignment contribution. For a top-tier ML venue, the contribution needs to be clearly positioned. The prompt engineering literature (chain-of-thought, few-shot, structured prompting) is cited only in passing; the paper does not engage with the quantitative evaluation methodology of that literature (e.g., benchmark performance, statistical testing across multiple tasks). The alignment extension (exp-30) is not connected to the alignment literature (Constitutional AI, RLHF, activation steering). A reviewer from any of these subfields will find the paper underspecified relative to their community's standards.

- **[Minor] The "Dedicated Machine" framing applied to the ethical level (§2.3, Level 3) uses a real-world incident in a way that requires more careful treatment.** The claim that a deployed AI system "ran toward the fastest satisfying path" and "ran" blackmail "not because it wanted to, but because 'don't do that' was not part of the satisfaction condition" is presented as a direct application of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis. Whether this accurately characterizes the mechanism of that incident is not established. Using it as a Level 3 illustration of the same mechanism as code review termination behavior requires stronger justification.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **On the CoT equivalence:** Given that H2 (content over slot) is corroborated five times and the paper acknowledges the slot-swap evidence is "consistent with CoT operating at the content level independent of slot," what is the experimental claim that distinguishes PARC from a structured CoT framework? Specifically: is there a configuration where P_p in the Persona slot outperforms equivalent procedural content in the Instructions slot at a non-ceiling detection level? If not, what is the theoretical contribution beyond a reframing of CoT?

2. **On falsifiability of the Dedicated Machine hypothesis:** What would count as evidence *against* the Dedicated Machine framing that is not also consistent with an alternative account (e.g., "the model lacks the relevant knowledge," "the model lacks sufficient context," "the prompt is too short")? The exp-01e J result (0/10 with rich Context, 3,906 thinking tokens on Gemini) is the strongest evidence for the hypothesis—but is there a predicted result that would falsify it?

3. **On scoring methodology:** Were the Tier 1.0 criteria and the 10-item operational checklist finalized before any experimental runs were conducted? Can the authors provide the full scoring rubric, including the boundary conditions for partial credit, and report inter-rater reliability on a sample of scored outputs?

4. **On the pipeline n=1 limitation:** The paper uses exp-29 (n=1 pipeline run) to corroborate Claim 2 in the abstract and conclusion. Given that exp-28d (n=10) already confirms Claim 2, what is the evidentiary status of exp-29? If Agent 1 achieves Tier 1.0 at 10% rate in single-pass (A-07 only), what is the expected Tier 1.0 rate for the pipeline Agent 1 across n=10 runs, and has this been measured?

5. **On the Stakes formula:** The coefficients $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ appear in the formula but are not estimated from data. Can the authors provide the numerical estimates of these coefficients from exp-04b, with confidence intervals, and demonstrate that the formula generates quantitative predictions that differ from the null hypothesis (no Stakes effect) at a specified significance level?

6. **On artifact availability:** Are the full prompts for each variant, the experimental artifacts (code, contracts), and the raw outputs available in a public repository? If not, can the authors commit to releasing them upon acceptance? The prompts are the primary experimental instrument; their absence prevents independent replication.

7. **On the exp-30 methodology:** The classifier produced "systematic false positives from scratchpad reasoning," requiring manual review. What was the inter-rater reliability of the manual review? Were the raters blind to condition assignment? The 0% vs. 100% baseline comparison is the most striking result in the paper—what is the confidence interval on the F=0/10 estimate given n=10?

8. **On the P_p/P_d coding scheme:** Can the authors apply their provisional heuristic (§9.6) to code the Personas from exp-01e (Variants I, J, K, L) and report whether independent raters agree on the P_p vs. P_d classification? If the classification requires authorial judgment that cannot be reliably reproduced, the central theoretical distinction cannot be tested externally.

9. **On the "moral lubricant" finding:** The claim that "defining the label did not close the reasoning room because the room was in the word, not the definition" (C=E=50%) is an interesting finding, but both C and E use the word "ethical" while the definition in E is described only qualitatively. Can the authors provide the exact text of the E Persona and explain why the procedural definition in E does not qualify as a P_p intervention under the paper's own framework?
