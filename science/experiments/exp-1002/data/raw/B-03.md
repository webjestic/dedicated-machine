**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains three structural defects that will materially undermine Meridian's position in any dispute that actually matters. First, Section 9.1's indemnification scope is dangerously overbroad as written against Meridian: Vendor's indemnity obligation covers "Vendor's performance of the Services" and "Vendor's breach of this Agreement" without a carve-out for Client's contributory fault, which means in a shared-fault scenario Meridian could find itself bearing defense costs and litigation exposure it did not anticipate — but more critically, the Client indemnity in Section 9.2(a) sweeps in any use of Deliverables "not authorized by this Agreement," a phrase that is undefined and elastic enough to be weaponized against Meridian in any dispute where Vendor contests the scope of authorized use. Second, the liability cap in Section 8.1 is pegged to fees paid in the preceding twelve months, which in the early stages of a multi-year engagement — precisely when integration risk is highest — may represent a fraction of Meridian's actual exposure; the cap should be floored at a fixed minimum that reflects the total contract value or at minimum the full annual contract year, not a trailing window. Third, and most structurally dangerous, Section 8.2 carves out indemnification obligations from the liability cap entirely, which on its face appears protective, but because the indemnification provisions in Section 9 are themselves uncapped and cover Vendor's own performance failures, this creates an asymmetric risk structure: Vendor's exposure for direct breach is capped, but Meridian's indemnification obligations to Vendor — including for unauthorized use under 9.2(a) — are uncapped and unlimited, a posture that is unacceptable in a federal services engagement where scope disputes are routine and consequential. These provisions must be renegotiated before execution.
