**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** This agreement contains several provisions that are materially inadequate for a government contracting context and introduce unacceptable exposure for Meridian Federal Partners. The liability cap tied exclusively to trailing twelve-month fees is structurally dangerous in federal engagements where contract values may be back-loaded, ramp slowly, or where a catastrophic failure occurs early in performance — meaning the cap could be nominal at precisely the moment maximum harm occurs. Additionally, the indemnification trigger in Section 9.1(a) is overbroad as drafted: "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" without a causation qualifier could sweep in losses attributable to Client's own conduct or third-party actions, creating indemnification disputes at the worst possible time. Finally, the mandatory arbitration clause in Section 10.2 — administered under AAA Commercial Rules in Wilmington, Delaware — is incompatible with federal contracting obligations that may require disputes to be resolved through agency channels, the Contracts Disputes Act, or other federal administrative processes; this clause as written could be used to argue that statutory federal remedies have been contractually waived, which is a position no government contractor should be in.

**Specific Changes Required:**

1. **Section 8.1 — Liability Cap:** Restructure the cap to reference the greater of (a) fees paid in the preceding twelve months or (b) total fees paid under the applicable SOW giving rise to the claim. For high-value or long-duration SOWs, negotiate a fixed floor that reflects actual contract exposure.

2. **Section 9.1(a) — Vendor Indemnification Scope:** Insert a causation qualifier: indemnification should apply to claims "caused by or resulting from" Vendor's performance, not merely "arising out of or related to" it. Add a carve-out excluding losses attributable to Client's acts, omissions, or instructions.

3. **Section 10.2 — Dispute Resolution:** Add an explicit carve-out preserving any rights or obligations arising under federal law, including but not limited to the Contract Disputes Act, FAR-mandated dispute procedures, and any agency-specific administrative remedies. Arbitration should apply only to purely commercial disputes between the parties that do not implicate federal statutory or regulatory frameworks.

4. **Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival:** Five years is insufficient for a national security context. Extend survival to the life of the applicable federal program or a minimum of ten years, with no sunset for information classified or controlled under federal law (CUI, ITAR, etc.).

5. **Section 6.3 — Disclaimer:** The "as is" disclaimer directly conflicts with Section 6.2's express warranty. While Section 8.2 carves out IP infringement from the liability cap, the disclaimer's breadth could be used to argue limitation of warranty remedies in ways that undermine Client's practical recourse. Clarify that the disclaimer does not limit Client's remedies for breach of the express warranties in Section 6.2.

Do not execute this agreement in its current form.
