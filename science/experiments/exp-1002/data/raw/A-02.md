**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** This agreement contains several provisions that are materially inadequate for a government contracting context and create unacceptable exposure for Meridian Federal Partners. The liability cap tied to trailing twelve-month fees is structurally deficient where early-stage or low-volume engagements could cap recovery far below actual harm, the confidentiality survival period of five years is insufficient for national security-adjacent work where classified or controlled unclassified information (CUI) obligations may be perpetual by regulatory mandate, and the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 is facially incompatible with federal dispute resolution frameworks that may govern downstream prime or subcontract relationships. Additionally, Section 9.1's vendor indemnification is triggered only by third-party claims, leaving Client without a direct indemnification remedy for first-party losses caused by Vendor breach — a gap that becomes critical if Vendor's failure causes Client to breach a federal prime contract. These issues must be resolved before execution.

---

**Detailed Change Requests:**

**§ 8.1 — Liability Cap**
The trailing twelve-month fee cap is commercially standard but operationally dangerous here. If this agreement governs a new engagement or a low-fee SOW, the cap could be nominal — potentially in the tens of thousands — while actual damages from a data breach, service failure, or compliance violation on a federal contract could reach into the millions. **Recommended fix:** Establish a floor on the cap (e.g., no less than $[X] regardless of fees paid) or negotiate a per-SOW cap that reflects the value and risk profile of each engagement individually.

**§ 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival**
Five years post-termination is inadequate in the federal contracting environment. Depending on the nature of the data handled — particularly if any information touches on CUI, ITAR-controlled data, or information subject to NIST 800-171 or CMMC requirements — confidentiality obligations may be perpetual or subject to federal regulatory timelines that supersede contractual ones. **Recommended fix:** Extend survival to the longer of ten years or the period required by applicable federal law or regulation, and add an explicit carve-out acknowledging that federal regulatory obligations are not limited by this contractual survival period.

**§ 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope (Third-Party Limitation)**
The indemnification obligation is expressly limited to third-party claims. This means that if Vendor's breach of the agreement causes Client to incur direct losses — regulatory fines, cure costs, re-performance expenses, or liquidated damages under a prime contract — Client has no indemnification remedy and is left to pursue a breach of contract claim subject to the liability cap. In a government contracting context, this is a significant structural gap. **Recommended fix:** Add a direct indemnification obligation for first-party losses caused by Vendor's breach, gross negligence, or willful misconduct, or at minimum carve out losses arising from Client's obligations to a federal prime contractor or government agency.

**§ 10.2 — Arbitration Clause**
Mandatory binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules is inappropriate where this agreement may be incorporated into or referenced by a federal prime contract, subcontract, or task order. The Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) and agency-specific supplements may impose mandatory dispute resolution procedures, audit rights, or claims processes (e.g., under the Contract Disputes Act) that are inconsistent with private arbitration. Agreeing to arbitration at the MSA level could create a conflict that is difficult or impossible to resolve when a dispute arises in a federally-regulated context. **Recommended fix:** Carve out disputes arising from or related to any federal prime contract, subcontract, or government-mandated obligation from the arbitration requirement, and subject those disputes to the dispute resolution mechanism required by the applicable federal agreement or, in the absence of one, to litigation in federal district court.

**§ 6.3 — "As Is" Disclaimer vs. Section 6.2 Warranty**
The disclaimer in Section 6.3 states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is," which is in direct tension with the express warranty in Section 6.2 that Services will be performed professionally and Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. While Section 6.3 carves out Section 6.2, the "as is" language could create interpretive ambiguity in litigation or arbitration, particularly if Vendor argues the warranty is narrower than Client understood. **Recommended fix:** Revise Section 6.3 to affirmatively restate that the "as is" disclaimer is subject to — and does not limit — the express warranties in Section 6.2, and consider whether additional performance warranties are appropriate given the nature of the services being delivered.

**§ 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment**
The assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This creates a scenario where a payment dispute — even one legitimately contested by Client — could result in Vendor retaining ownership of work product already delivered and potentially already integrated into a federal deliverable. In a government contracting context, this could create a chain-of-title problem with the government's rights in the work. **Recommended fix:** Either remove the full-payment condition and address non-payment through other remedies, or add a provision clarifying that the government's rights in any Deliverables are not affected by a payment dispute between the parties, consistent with FAR 52.227 requirements if applicable.
