**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains three structural defects that will materially impair Meridian's position in any dispute that actually matters. First, Section 9.1's vendor indemnification is fatally circular: Section 8.2(a) exempts indemnification obligations from the liability cap, but Section 9.1 defines those obligations using the term "Losses," which in turn captures attorneys' fees and damages — meaning the cap's removal is real only if indemnification is triggered, and the indemnification trigger for general performance failures under 9.1(a) is broad enough to swallow claims Vendor will argue belong under the capped regime instead, creating a dispute about which bucket applies at the worst possible moment. Second, the liability cap in Section 8.1 is indexed to fees paid in the preceding twelve months, which in a multi-year federal engagement with a back-loaded payment structure or a low-burn early period means the cap could be functionally trivial at the time of a major breach — Meridian needs either a floor tied to total contract value or a rolling lookback that includes forward-committed fees. Third, Section 10.2's mandatory arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is almost certainly incompatible with Meridian's federal contracting obligations: disputes touching national security data services may implicate the Contract Disputes Act, agency-specific dispute resolution requirements, or federal court jurisdiction that cannot be contractually waived, and executing an agreement that purports to bind Meridian to private arbitration for claims that a federal agency or the government itself may be a real party in interest creates a structural conflict Meridian cannot resolve after signing.
