**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** This agreement contains several provisions that create unacceptable exposure for a government contracting client operating in the national security space. The liability cap tied to trailing twelve-month fees is structurally inadequate where a single catastrophic breach — particularly a data or security incident — could dwarf total contract value, and while Section 8.2 carves out confidentiality breaches and gross negligence from the cap, ordinary negligence causing significant harm remains capped, which is insufficient in a federal services context. Additionally, the agreement is silent on regulatory compliance obligations critical to this environment (FAR/DFARS flow-downs, CMMC, FedRAMP, data handling under CUI frameworks), the arbitration clause in Delaware is operationally problematic for a Virginia-based federal contractor and may conflict with mandatory dispute resolution mechanisms in downstream government contracts, and the five-year confidentiality survival period is likely too short given the classification sensitivity and lifecycle of federal program data.

**Specific Changes Required:**

1. **Section 8.1 — Liability Cap:** The twelve-month fee cap must be increased or restructured for high-severity breach categories. At minimum, a tiered cap should be negotiated — e.g., two to three times total contract value for data security incidents, IP loss, or regulatory violations — rather than a single flat cap that may represent a fraction of actual exposure.

2. **Section 8.2 — Exceptions:** Ordinary negligence causing data loss or security incidents should be added as a carve-out from the cap, not just gross negligence. In a national security context, the distinction between ordinary and gross negligence is frequently litigated and the current drafting leaves Client exposed.

3. **Missing Clause — Regulatory Compliance:** A dedicated compliance provision must be added requiring Vendor to comply with all applicable federal regulations, including FAR 52.224-series privacy clauses, DFARS 252.204-7012 (Safeguarding Covered Defense Information), CMMC requirements at the applicable level, and any CUI handling obligations under 32 CFR Part 2002. Vendor's failure to flow these obligations to subcontractors under Section 2.3 is a specific gap.

4. **Section 10.2 — Arbitration:** The mandatory arbitration clause should be reviewed against Client's prime contract terms, which may require disputes to be resolved in federal court or under specific federal jurisdiction. Delaware arbitration venue is also inconsistent with Client's operational base and should be renegotiated to Virginia or made flexible. Consider whether a carve-out for injunctive relief in federal court is needed for IP or data security emergencies.

5. **Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival:** Five years is inadequate for federal program data. Survival should extend to the longer of ten years or the applicable regulatory retention period, with no sunset for information that remains classified or subject to government-imposed handling restrictions.

6. **Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope:** The indemnification trigger for "Vendor's performance of the Services" is broad but should be clarified to explicitly include data breaches, security incidents, and regulatory violations caused by Vendor, to avoid any argument that these are not covered as written.
