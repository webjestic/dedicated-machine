**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains a structural defect that will materially harm Meridian in any significant dispute. Section 8.2(a) carves indemnification obligations entirely outside the liability cap, which appears protective on its face — but Vendor's indemnification obligation under Section 9.1 is drafted so broadly that it covers Vendor's own breach of the agreement, its performance of services, and its subcontractors' conduct, meaning the cap never applies to the claims most likely to generate real exposure. The asymmetry becomes critical when you read Section 8.2(e): amounts owed by Client for fees and taxes are also carved out of the cap, so Vendor's payment rights are fully uncapped while Client's most important damage claims — service failures, data incidents, subcontractor misconduct — will be funneled through the indemnification carve-out and argued to be uncapped as well, a position Vendor's counsel will take in any dispute. Additionally, the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 mandates Wilmington, Delaware as the exclusive venue with no carve-out for injunctive or emergency relief, which is operationally untenable for a federal services engagement where Meridian may need to seek immediate judicial relief to protect classified or sensitive federal data under applicable regulatory obligations. Before execution, Meridian should require: (1) a defined aggregate ceiling on indemnification exposure for both parties, expressed as a multiple of fees paid; (2) deletion or narrowing of Section 8.2(e) so that uncapped fee recovery does not create a one-sided liability structure; and (3) an explicit carve-out from the arbitration clause preserving each party's right to seek emergency injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights for the underlying dispute.
