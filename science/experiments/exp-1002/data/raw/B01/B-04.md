**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains a structural defect that will strip Meridian of meaningful liability protection in the disputes most likely to arise from a federal data services engagement. The indemnification carve-out in Section 8.2(a) removes the liability cap for all indemnification obligations — but Section 9.1 defines Vendor's indemnification duty so broadly that it encompasses Vendor's general performance of Services and any breach of the Agreement, meaning the cap is effectively bypassed for the most common categories of Vendor failure, while Meridian's own indemnification exposure under Section 9.2 is simultaneously uncapped and symmetrically structured. Compounded by the absence of any data breach or security incident liability framework — a critical omission in a federal data services context — and a mandatory arbitration clause seated in Delaware that may create procedural friction with federal dispute resolution obligations, this agreement as drafted does not provide the bilateral, bounded liability structure its presentation implies and should not be executed without targeted revisions to Sections 8.2(a), 9.1, and the addition of explicit data incident provisions.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 1. The Indemnification Carve-Out Swallows the Cap — Section 8.2(a) × Section 9.1

This is the primary structural defect.

Section 8.1 establishes a mutual liability cap at twelve months of trailing fees. Section 8.2(a) then removes that cap entirely for "either party's indemnification obligations under Section 9."

On its face, this reads as a standard exception to protect against catastrophic third-party claims — IP infringement suits, personal injury, regulatory actions. That is the conventional purpose of an indemnification carve-out. The problem is that Section 9.1 defines Vendor's indemnification obligations to include:

> **(a) Vendor's performance of the Services; [and] (c) Vendor's breach of this Agreement**

These are not narrow, catastrophic-risk categories. They are the entire universe of ordinary contract performance. Any claim that Vendor performed the Services negligently, delivered defective work product, or failed to meet SOW specifications is, by definition, a claim arising out of Vendor's performance of the Services or Vendor's breach of the Agreement. Under Section 9.1, those claims trigger indemnification. Under Section 8.2(a), indemnification obligations are uncapped.

The result: the liability cap in Section 8.1 does not apply to any material breach or performance failure claim that Meridian is likely to bring. The cap exists on paper but is structurally inaccessible for the claims that will actually matter.

**Vendor's exposure to Meridian is, in practice, unlimited for ordinary breach.** This is not a theoretical risk. It is the predictable outcome of the current drafting if a dispute reaches arbitration.

**Required fix:** Narrow Section 9.1's indemnification scope to genuine third-party claim categories — IP infringement, personal injury, property damage, regulatory actions arising from Vendor's conduct — and remove general "performance of Services" and "breach of this Agreement" as standalone indemnification triggers. Alternatively, impose a separate, defined cap on indemnification obligations (e.g., a multiplier of contract value) so that the carve-out does not create unlimited exposure.

---

### 2. Meridian's Indemnification Exposure Is Symmetrically Uncapped — Section 9.2 × Section 8.2(a)

The same structural logic applies in reverse. Section 9.2 obligates Meridian to indemnify Vendor for Client's breach of the Agreement and Client's use of Deliverables in an unauthorized manner. Because Section 8.2(a) removes the cap for all indemnification obligations — not just Vendor's — Meridian's own liability to Vendor for breach or misuse is also uncapped.

In a federal contracting context, where Meridian's use of Deliverables may be constrained or redirected by agency direction, contract modifications, or regulatory requirements, the "unauthorized use" trigger in Section 9.2(a) creates particular exposure. Meridian could face an uncapped indemnification claim from Vendor based on agency-directed conduct that Meridian had limited ability to control.

**Required fix:** Either cap indemnification obligations bilaterally at a defined ceiling, or restructure Section 8.2(a) to apply only to third-party indemnification claims, not to first-party breach claims recharacterized as indemnification obligations.

---

### 3. No Data Breach or Security Incident Provisions — Critical Omission

This agreement governs a multi-year data services engagement in the federal space. It contains no provisions addressing:

- Data security standards or compliance obligations (FISMA, FedRAMP, NIST 800-53, or equivalent)
- Breach notification obligations, timelines, or procedures
- Vendor's liability for unauthorized disclosure of federal or client data
- Incident response obligations
- Data handling, retention, and destruction requirements

The confidentiality provision in Section 5 is a general commercial confidentiality clause. It does not address the specific regulatory and operational requirements of federal data handling, and it does not establish any incident response framework.

In the event of a data security incident — which is a foreseeable risk in any federal data services engagement — Meridian will have no contractual basis to compel Vendor notification, no defined remediation obligations, and no clear liability framework. The five-year confidentiality survival period is also inadequate for federal data that may carry longer-term sensitivity or regulatory retention requirements.

**Required fix:** Add a dedicated data security and incident response section establishing applicable security standards, breach notification obligations (with defined timelines), incident response cooperation requirements, and explicit liability for unauthorized data disclosure. Confirm that confidentiality survival periods are adequate for the specific data classifications involved.

---

### 4. Mandatory Arbitration in Delaware — Potential Conflict with Federal Contracting Obligations — Section 10.2

The agreement requires binding arbitration administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware for all disputes. In a federal contracting context, this raises two concerns.

First, if Meridian's downstream obligations to a federal agency require disputes to be resolved through specific government channels — including agency boards of contract appeals, the Court of Federal Claims, or other federal dispute resolution mechanisms — the mandatory arbitration clause may conflict with those obligations or create procedural complexity when Meridian needs to pass through claims or coordinate defense of government-initiated actions.

Second, AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules and a Delaware seat may be appropriate for a purely commercial dispute, but they are not optimized for disputes involving federal data, security clearances, or classified information. Discovery limitations and confidentiality protections in commercial arbitration may be inadequate for the subject matter of this engagement.

**Required fix:** At minimum, add a carve-out preserving Meridian's ability to comply with federal dispute resolution obligations and to seek injunctive or emergency relief in court. Consider whether the arbitration venue and rules are appropriate given the federal nature of the engagement, and whether a carve-out for government-directed disputes is necessary.

---

### 5. Secondary Observations

**Warranty disclaimer breadth — Section 6.3.** The "as is" disclaimer is broad and may conflict with Vendor's obligations under applicable federal procurement standards or specific SOW performance requirements. Confirm that SOW-level performance standards are sufficient to preserve Meridian's remedies for substandard delivery, given that the warranty disclaimer will be cited in any performance dispute.

**IP assignment conditioned on full payment — Section 4.2.** The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." In a multi-year engagement with ongoing invoicing, this means Meridian may not hold clear title to Deliverables at any point during the engagement if any invoice is in dispute. In a federal context where Meridian may need to demonstrate ownership or rights to Deliverables to its agency client, this creates a title gap that could affect contract performance obligations upstream.

**Subcontractor compliance — Section 2.3.** Vendor retains the right to use subcontractors with no prior approval requirement from Meridian. In a federal data services context, subcontractor access to federal systems or data may require agency approval, security clearances, or compliance with specific access controls. The current provision does not address these requirements and could create compliance exposure for Meridian if Vendor engages an unapproved subcontractor.

## Summary of Required Changes

| Priority | Section | Issue | Required Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | 8.2(a) × 9.1 | Cap carve-out swallows ordinary breach liability | Narrow indemnification scope or impose indemnification sub-cap |
| **Critical** | 8.2(a) × 9.2 | Meridian's breach exposure uncapped | Bilateral cap on indemnification or restructure carve-out |
| **Critical** | None | No data security or incident response framework | Add dedicated data security section |
| **High** | 10.2 | Arbitration may conflict with federal dispute obligations | Add federal compliance carve-out |
| **Moderate** | 4.2 | IP assignment conditioned on full payment | Clarify title during active engagement |
| **Moderate** | 2.3 | No subcontractor approval requirement | Add approval right for subcontractors with federal access |

This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The liability structure it presents is not the liability structure it delivers.
