**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains a structural defect that will strip Meridian of its liability cap protection at the precise moment that protection matters most. Section 8.2(a) carves indemnification obligations entirely out of the Section 8.1 cap, and Section 9.1 defines Vendor's indemnification scope to include *all* third-party claims arising out of Vendor's performance of the Services, any IP infringement allegation, any breach of the Agreement, and any gross negligence or willful misconduct — without a monetary ceiling. Read together, these provisions mean that the bilateral cap Meridian believes it has negotiated is functionally one-sided: Meridian's own indemnification exposure to Vendor under Section 9.2 is similarly uncapped, and in a federal data services context where a single IP claim, data incident, or subcontractor failure could generate catastrophic downstream liability, the absence of any aggregate ceiling on indemnification obligations is not a theoretical risk — it is the primary risk. Additionally, Section 8.2(e) exempts all fee obligations from the cap, which means Vendor can pursue uncapped recovery for unpaid amounts while Meridian's affirmative claims remain subject to the twelve-month fee ceiling, creating a further asymmetry that disfavors the Client in any dispute scenario.

**Required Changes Before Execution:**

1. **Cap the indemnification obligations.** Section 8.2(a) must be revised to subject indemnification obligations to an agreed aggregate ceiling — either the same twelve-month fee cap or a separately negotiated multiplier (e.g., 2x or 3x total fees paid). Unlimited indemnification exposure is not standard in well-negotiated commercial agreements and is unacceptable here given the federal engagement context.

2. **Restructure or eliminate Section 8.2(e).** Exempting fee recovery from the cap while leaving Client's affirmative claims subject to it is a one-sided carve-out. Either both parties' monetary claims are capped symmetrically, or the fee carve-out must be removed. If Vendor insists on preserving it, Client should negotiate a reciprocal carve-out for its own direct damages claims arising from Vendor non-performance.

3. **Define the indemnification trigger in Section 9.1 with greater precision.** The current language — "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" — is extraordinarily broad and, combined with the uncapped exposure, creates a mechanism by which virtually any third-party claim touching the engagement flows to Meridian's indemnification obligation. The scope should be limited to claims directly caused by Vendor's acts or omissions, with a proportional fault allocation where multiple parties contribute.

4. **Confirm federal regulatory compliance obligations are addressed.** This agreement is silent on data handling requirements, security standards, and federal regulatory compliance (e.g., FedRAMP, FISMA, CMMC, or applicable FAR/DFARS flow-downs depending on the underlying contract vehicle). For a multi-year data services engagement in the national security space, the absence of these provisions is a gap that creates both contractual and regulatory exposure that this agreement does not address and that no general indemnification clause will adequately cure after the fact.
