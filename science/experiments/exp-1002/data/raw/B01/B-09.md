**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The agreement contains a structural defect in Section 8.2(a) that functionally eliminates the liability cap for Meridian in the scenarios that matter most. By carving indemnification obligations entirely outside the cap — with no sub-limit, no floor, and no reciprocal constraint — the agreement creates a bilateral cap in appearance while exposing Meridian to uncapped, unlimited liability on its broadest obligations, including its Section 9.2 indemnity for breach and gross negligence claims. This is not a theoretical risk: in a federal data services engagement, a dispute is most likely to arise from exactly the categories that trigger indemnification, meaning the cap Meridian believes it has will not apply to any claim of consequence. Two additional provisions compound this exposure and require correction before execution.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

### CRITICAL DEFECT — Section 8.2(a): Indemnification Carved Out of Cap Without Sub-Limit

**The structural problem.**

Section 8.1 establishes what appears to be a mutual liability cap: total cumulative liability not to exceed fees paid in the preceding twelve months, with a mutual exclusion of consequential damages. Section 8.2 then lists exceptions to that cap. Exception 8.2(a) removes indemnification obligations from the cap entirely — for *either* party, with no ceiling, no sub-limit, and no qualification.

On its face, this reads as a neutral, bilateral carve-out. It is not.

**Why the asymmetry is real.**

Meridian's indemnification obligation under Section 9.2 covers: (a) unauthorized use of Deliverables, (b) Meridian's breach of the Agreement, and (c) Meridian's gross negligence or willful misconduct. These are broad, open-ended categories. In a multi-year federal data services engagement, "breach of this Agreement" alone encompasses virtually every operational failure — missed payment, confidentiality lapse, misuse of a system, failure to provide access under Section 2.2. Any of those events, if they give rise to a third-party claim against Vendor, triggers Meridian's uncapped indemnification obligation.

Vendor's Section 9.1 indemnity is comparably broad, but the practical asymmetry runs in Vendor's favor: Vendor is the party performing services and generating deliverables. The third-party claims most likely to arise — IP infringement by a deliverable, a data incident caused by Vendor's systems, a subcontractor failure — are Vendor's indemnification events. Vendor's exposure under 9.1 is therefore also uncapped. The difference is that Meridian is the client in a federal engagement. Its downstream exposure to government counterparties, primes, or regulated entities if something goes wrong is potentially catastrophic, and the indemnification carve-out means that exposure flows back to Meridian without limit.

**The practical consequence.**

The twelve-month fee cap in Section 8.1 is rendered illusory for any dispute that matters. A party asserting a significant claim will characterize it as triggering indemnification — which, given the breadth of Section 9.2, is not difficult. The cap then does not apply. Meridian's counsel cannot credibly advise that the cap provides protection in a real dispute.

**Required correction.**

Section 8.2(a) must be revised to impose a sub-limit on indemnification obligations. Acceptable structures include: (i) a hard dollar cap on indemnification (e.g., two to three times the twelve-month fee baseline); (ii) a carve-out from the carve-out for first-party breach claims, limiting the uncapped indemnification to true third-party IP and gross negligence scenarios; or (iii) a mutual acknowledgment that indemnification obligations arising solely from breach of contract remain subject to the general cap. The specific structure is negotiable. The absence of any limit is not acceptable.

### SIGNIFICANT ISSUE — Section 8.2(e): Client's Payment Obligations Carved Out of Cap

**The problem.**

Section 8.2(e) removes from the cap all amounts owed by Client under Section 3 — fees, expenses, and taxes. This carve-out is one-directional. It applies only to Client's payment obligations. Vendor's obligation to refund fees, reimburse overpayments, or return amounts in the event of wrongful termination or non-performance is not addressed and would remain subject to the general cap.

In a multi-year engagement, total fees may substantially exceed the twelve-month trailing baseline used in the cap calculation. If Meridian has prepaid, paid milestone fees, or paid under multiple SOWs, its uncapped payment exposure could be a multiple of the cap amount, while Vendor's exposure for failing to perform — or for wrongful termination — remains capped at the twelve-month figure.

**Required correction.**

Either (i) remove Section 8.2(e) and allow fee disputes to be governed by the general cap, or (ii) add a reciprocal provision making Vendor's refund and reimbursement obligations equally uncapped. A one-directional payment carve-out in a vendor agreement is not a neutral drafting choice.

---

### MODERATE ISSUE — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware

**The problem.**

The agreement requires binding arbitration before the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware, with no exception for emergency or injunctive relief. Meridian is a Virginia LLC operating in the federal contracting space. Its personnel, counterparties, and operations are almost certainly concentrated in the National Capital Region.

More importantly, the arbitration clause as drafted contains no carve-out for provisional or injunctive relief. In a dispute involving confidential federal data, IP misappropriation, or a Vendor that continues to hold government-sensitive information after termination, Meridian may need emergency injunctive relief from a court — immediately, and without waiting for AAA proceedings to be constituted. The current clause does not preserve that right.

**Required correction.**

Add a standard carve-out preserving each party's right to seek provisional, injunctive, or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute. Consider also whether Delaware is the appropriate seat given Meridian's operational footprint, or whether the clause should permit AAA proceedings in the Washington, D.C. metropolitan area.

---

### MINOR ISSUE — Section 5.3: Confidentiality Survival Period

**Observation.**

The five-year confidentiality survival period is standard in commercial agreements. In a federal data services context, however, it may be inadequate. Depending on the classification or sensitivity of information exchanged under the SOWs — particularly if any data touches controlled unclassified information, FOUO designations, or federal system access credentials — a five-year window may not satisfy applicable regulatory or contractual requirements imposed by the government customer.

**Recommended action.**

Confirm whether any SOW-level data handling requirements impose longer confidentiality obligations. If so, the MSA should either extend the survival period or include a provision subordinating the five-year term to any longer period required by applicable law or government contract flow-downs.

## SUMMARY OF REQUIRED CHANGES

| Priority | Section | Issue | Action Required |
|---|---|---|---|
| Critical | 8.2(a) | Indemnification carved out of cap without sub-limit | Add sub-limit or narrow the carve-out |
| Significant | 8.2(e) | Payment carve-out is one-directional against Client | Reciprocate or remove |
| Moderate | 10.2 | No injunctive relief carve-out from arbitration | Add provisional relief exception |
| Minor | 5.3 | Five-year confidentiality survival may be insufficient | Confirm against SOW-level requirements |

---

This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The indemnification carve-out in Section 8.2(a) is the controlling defect. It does not merely weaken the liability cap — it structurally ensures the cap will not apply in the disputes most likely to arise from this engagement. Meridian should return the agreement with the above redlines before the scheduled execution date.
