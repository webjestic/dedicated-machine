**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The agreement contains a structural defect that will strip Meridian of its liability cap protection at the precise moment it matters most. Section 8.2(a) carves indemnification obligations entirely out of the Section 8.1 cap — meaning that when Vendor triggers its indemnification rights under Section 9.2 (Client's breach, unauthorized use of Deliverables, or Client's gross negligence), Vendor's recovery against Meridian is **uncapped and unlimited**, while the cap language reads as bilateral and reciprocal. This asymmetry is compounded by Section 8.2(e), which also removes the cap on all fees, expenses, and taxes owed under Section 3 — a provision that runs only one direction, in Vendor's favor, with no Client-side analog. Taken together, the two exceptions that operate exclusively or predominantly against Client account for the majority of realistic dispute scenarios in a federal data services engagement; the cap Meridian believes it has is largely illusory in practice.

**Specific Changes Required:**

1. **Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification Carve-Out:** Either (a) eliminate the carve-out entirely and subject indemnification obligations to the 12-month fee cap, or (b) negotiate a separate, defined indemnification cap — expressed as a fixed multiple of fees paid (e.g., 2× or 3× trailing 12-month fees) — that applies symmetrically to both parties' indemnification exposure. An unlimited indemnification carve-out in a multi-year federal engagement can expose Meridian to liability orders of magnitude beyond the contract value.

2. **Section 8.2(e) — Fees Exception:** This exception is unilateral. It protects Vendor's right to collect unpaid fees without any cap, but provides no corresponding protection for Client. If Meridian overpays, is double-billed, or is owed a refund or credit, those claims remain subject to the cap. Either remove this exception entirely — unpaid fees are direct damages recoverable under the cap — or add a symmetric Client-side exception for amounts owed to Client under the Agreement.

3. **Section 8.2(b) and (c) — Confidentiality and IP Carve-Outs:** These exceptions are facially bilateral, but in a federal data services context, the practical exposure is asymmetric. Meridian will be handling sensitive federal data; a confidentiality breach by Meridian triggers uncapped liability, while a breach by Vendor — who holds Meridian's federal client data and system access — also runs uncapped. This is acceptable only if Meridian has robust internal controls and the SOWs clearly define data handling obligations. Confirm this is understood and accepted as a business matter before execution.

4. **Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure:** The requirement that Meridian grant Vendor "sole control of the defense and settlement" is problematic in a federal contracting context. Vendor could settle a third-party claim in a manner that implicates Meridian's federal relationships, security clearances, or agency obligations without Meridian's consent. The existing carve-out — requiring written consent before any settlement that "imposes liability or obligation" on the indemnified party — is insufficient. Revise to require Meridian's prior written consent for **any** settlement that (a) involves admission of fault by Meridian, (b) affects Meridian's federal contracts or agency relationships, or (c) involves injunctive or non-monetary relief binding on Meridian.

5. **Section 10.2 — Arbitration:** Binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is standard and not objectionable on its face, but confirm that this clause does not conflict with any dispute resolution requirements in Meridian's underlying federal contracts or applicable agency regulations. Some federal prime contracts impose mandatory dispute resolution procedures that flow down to subcontractors and vendors. If such requirements exist, this clause needs a federal contract compliance carve-out.

**Bottom Line:** Do not execute this agreement in its current form. The liability structure presents as bilateral and capped but operates, in the scenarios most likely to produce actual disputes, as uncapped against Meridian. These are not minor drafting issues — they are the provisions that will govern the outcome of any serious claim under this engagement.
