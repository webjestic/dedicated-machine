# CONTRACT REVIEW — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Contract Review Division*

---

## Decision: Request Changes

## Summary

This agreement presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains several structural deficiencies and latent risk provisions that are unacceptable for a federal contractor engagement. Most critically, the IP assignment is payment-contingent in a way that creates a dangerous gap in ownership during performance, the confidentiality survival period is inadequate given the national security context, and the mandatory arbitration clause in Delaware creates a venue and process conflict that could compromise our client's ability to assert federal defenses or comply with agency reporting obligations. These issues are not cosmetic — they go to the core of how exposure is allocated in a high-stakes government services context.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Be Resolved Before Execution

---

**1. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Is Contingent on Full Payment**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest..."*

This is a trap buried in plain sight. The assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on **full payment of all fees** — meaning if any invoice dispute arises, even a good-faith one, Vendor retains ownership of work product that may already be integrated into a federal system or delivered to a government agency. In a government contracting context, this creates a scenario where:

- The government customer may have already taken delivery and begun relying on the Deliverable;
- A payment dispute — however minor — could retroactively cloud title to work product;
- Client could be in breach of its prime contract obligations if it cannot certify clear IP ownership to the agency.

**Required Fix:** IP assignment must be effective upon delivery of each Deliverable, with Vendor retaining a security interest or license-back as a payment remedy — not a title-retention mechanism. Alternatively, assignment should vest upon delivery with a license reverting to Vendor only upon a final, uncured payment default following dispute resolution.

---

**2. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Binding Arbitration**

This clause is facially standard but operationally problematic for a federal contractor:

- **No carve-out for regulatory or agency-mandated proceedings.** If a dispute involves conduct that triggers mandatory reporting obligations (e.g., FAR 52.203-13, whistleblower statutes, cybersecurity incident reporting under DFARS 252.204-7012), forcing that dispute into private AAA arbitration creates a direct conflict with federal compliance obligations.
- **Wilmington, Delaware venue** is inconvenient and potentially inaccessible depending on where performance occurs and where agency oversight is centered.
- **"Final and binding" language with no appeal mechanism** eliminates the ability to challenge an erroneous arbitral award on legal grounds, which is particularly dangerous where federal law or agency regulations may be implicated.
- **No emergency injunctive relief carve-out.** If Vendor misappropriates IP or breaches confidentiality, Client must wait for AAA proceedings rather than seeking immediate injunctive relief in court.

**Required Fix:** Add explicit carve-outs for (a) emergency injunctive relief in any court of competent jurisdiction, (b) disputes involving federal regulatory compliance obligations, and (c) claims arising under federal law where arbitration may be preempted or inappropriate. Reconsider venue or add flexibility.

---

**3. Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

Five years is commercially standard. It is **not** appropriate here. In a national security and federal services context:

- Sensitive government information, system architecture details, personnel data, and security configurations may retain their sensitivity for decades;
- Many federal contracts require confidentiality protections that survive indefinitely or for the life of the classification;
- A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means a vendor who receives CUI, PII, or operationally sensitive data in Year 1 is free to disclose it in Year 6.

**Required Fix:** Confidentiality obligations for information that constitutes trade secrets should survive indefinitely (consistent with applicable trade secret law). For all other Confidential Information in a federal services context, survival should be extended to a minimum of **ten (10) years**, with a carve-out preserving obligations for the duration of any applicable federal regulatory requirement.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — High Risk, Requires Negotiation

---

**4. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Rights Are Broadly Permissive**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

In a federal contractor context, this is insufficient on its face:

- **No prior written consent requirement.** Vendor can unilaterally introduce subcontractors without Client approval, which is a direct conflict with typical prime contract flow-down requirements and security clearance obligations.
- **No flow-down of federal compliance obligations** (e.g., DFARS cybersecurity requirements, background check requirements, export control compliance under ITAR/EAR).
- **No prohibition on foreign nationals or foreign-owned subcontractors** — a significant national security exposure.

**Required Fix:** Require Client's prior written approval for any subcontractor. Mandate that all subcontractors be bound by the same compliance obligations as Vendor, including any applicable federal regulatory requirements. Add explicit prohibition on subcontractors who are foreign nationals or entities without appropriate clearances where the work involves controlled or sensitive information.

---

**5. Section 4.3 — Vendor Pre-Existing IP License Is Dangerously Narrow**

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

The license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This creates a material operational risk:

- If Client needs to **modify, update, or maintain** the Deliverable after delivery, any such modification that touches Vendor Pre-Existing IP arguably exceeds the license scope;
- If the Deliverable is **integrated into a larger government system**, use of the embedded Vendor IP in that broader context may be outside the license;
- Vendor could assert license violation based on routine operational use.

**Required Fix:** Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, maintain, and create derivative works of the Vendor Pre-Existing IP as embedded in or necessary to use the Deliverables for Client's internal and government contract purposes.

---

**6. Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope Has a Structural Gap**

Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Client for claims arising out of "Vendor's performance of the Services" broadly — but this creates an interpretive tension with 9.1(d), which limits indemnification for negligence to **gross** negligence. A competent opposing counsel will argue that ordinary negligence claims arising from performance fall under 9.1(a) but are simultaneously limited by the gross negligence standard in 9.1(d), creating ambiguity that a court or arbitrator could resolve against Client.

Additionally, there is **no indemnification for data breach or cybersecurity incidents** caused by Vendor — a glaring omission in any federal services engagement where Vendor may have access to government systems or sensitive data.

**Required Fix:** Clarify that 9.1(a) covers all negligence in performance without requiring the gross negligence threshold. Add an explicit indemnification obligation for data breaches, cybersecurity incidents, and unauthorized disclosure of government or sensitive data caused by Vendor or its subcontractors.

---

### 🟠 MODERATE — Warrants Attention and Clarification

---

**7. Section 7.2 — Termination for Convenience Is Symmetric But Asymmetrically Harmful**

Both parties have equal termination for convenience rights on 60 days' notice. In practice, this is asymmetric in impact:

- Vendor terminating for convenience mid-performance on a federal engagement could leave Client in breach of its prime contract with no recourse beyond fees paid;
- There is **no obligation on Vendor to complete in-progress work** or transition deliverables upon convenience termination;
- No transition assistance obligation exists anywhere in this agreement.

**Required Fix:** Add a transition assistance obligation requiring Vendor to cooperate in transitioning work to Client or a successor vendor for a defined period following notice of termination. Consider limiting Vendor's termination for convenience right during active SOW performance periods.

---

**8. Section 6.3 — "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts With Section 6.2**

The agreement simultaneously provides an express warranty in Section 6.2 (professional performance, no knowing IP infringement) and then disclaims all warranties in Section 6.3, including non-infringement. The internal inconsistency between these provisions — particularly the disclaimer of non-infringement warranty immediately after providing a non-infringement representation — creates interpretive ambiguity that could be exploited to narrow the warranty actually provided.

**Required Fix:** Section 6.3 should explicitly carve out the warranties expressly stated in Section 6.2 to eliminate any argument that the "as is" disclaimer swallows the express warranty.

---

**9. Section 10.1 — Delaware Governing Law**

Delaware law is Vendor-favorable and was almost certainly chosen by Vendor's counsel. For a federal contractor engagement, consider whether the law of the state where performance primarily occurs, or where Client is domiciled, is more appropriate. More importantly, **there is no acknowledgment that federal law governs where applicable** — a significant gap when federal statutes, regulations, or agency requirements may directly control the parties' obligations.

**Required Fix:** Add a provision clarifying that to the extent federal law applies to any aspect of this Agreement or the Services, federal law shall control. Consider renegotiating governing law to a jurisdiction more favorable to or neutral for Client.
