**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 is structurally present but practically hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that is likely to arise in a federal contractor software engagement — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure in practice. This is not a balanced commercial agreement; it is an agreement where the vendor's most significant liability vectors are uncapped and the client's primary payment obligations are also uncapped, while the cap itself provides the appearance of mutual protection without the substance. Three additional issues require attention before execution: the IP assignment in Section 4.2 is conditioned on full payment, meaning a payment dispute mid-engagement could cloud title to partially completed deliverables in a federal contracting context where ownership continuity matters; the "as is" disclaimer in Section 6.3 is in direct tension with the affirmative warranty in Section 6.2 and will create interpretive conflict if the Deliverables underperform; and the mandatory arbitration venue in Wilmington, Delaware may conflict with dispute resolution requirements applicable to federal contractor engagements that should be confirmed before execution.

---

### Detailed Analysis

---

#### The Limitation of Liability Clause Does Not Function as a Cap

This is the central problem with the agreement and the one that requires the most direct attention before any other issue is addressed.

Section 8.1 establishes two protections: a consequential damages waiver and a 12-month fees cap on total cumulative liability. Read in isolation, this looks like a standard commercial limitation of liability provision. It is not, because Section 8.2 immediately removes from those protections the following categories:

- All indemnification obligations (Section 9)
- All confidentiality breaches (Section 5)
- All IP infringement or misappropriation claims (Section 4)
- Gross negligence or willful misconduct
- All unpaid fees, expenses, and taxes (Section 3)

The practical effect of this carve-out structure is that the cap governs a residual category of claims that is nearly empty in a real dispute. Consider what actually generates material liability in a software professional services engagement: a vendor delivers code that infringes third-party IP — carved out. A vendor's subcontractor mishandles client data in a federal environment — confidentiality breach, carved out, and likely triggers indemnification, also carved out. A vendor performs negligently in a way that rises to gross negligence — carved out. The client refuses to pay — carved out. What remains inside the cap is a narrow band of ordinary breach claims — missed deadlines, substandard deliverables that don't rise to gross negligence, minor scope disputes — which are precisely the disputes that rarely produce damages anywhere near the cap level in the first place.

The vendor has structured this so that the cap applies where it doesn't matter and doesn't apply where it does. This is not a mutual protection; it is asymmetric risk allocation dressed in bilateral language.

**What to request:** Either (1) negotiate the carve-outs down to gross negligence/willful misconduct and a narrowly defined IP indemnification obligation, bringing confidentiality breaches and general indemnification back inside the cap with a higher multiplier (e.g., 2x or 3x trailing 12-month fees), or (2) if the vendor insists on this carve-out structure, demand a substantially higher base cap — the 12-month fees figure is only meaningful if the cap actually applies to the disputes that matter.

---

#### IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment — Section 4.2

Section 4.2 reads: *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables."*

The payment condition on assignment creates a title problem that is particularly acute in federal contractor engagements. If a payment dispute arises mid-engagement — which is not uncommon when scope creep, disputed invoices, or termination-for-convenience scenarios occur — the vendor retains ownership of partially completed Deliverables until the dispute is resolved. In a federal context, where the government may have flow-down rights, data rights requirements under FAR/DFARS, or continuity-of-operations needs, a cloud on title to partially completed work product is not a theoretical risk. It is a leverage point the vendor can exercise at exactly the moment the client is most vulnerable.

The "as delivered" qualifier in Section 6.2 compounds this: the warranty applies only to Deliverables "as delivered," which means pre-delivery work product sits in a zone where neither the assignment nor the warranty has fully attached.

**What to request:** Remove the payment condition from the assignment clause or replace it with an escrow or license-back mechanism that preserves client access to work product during a payment dispute while protecting the vendor's economic interest. The assignment should vest upon creation or delivery, with the vendor retaining a security interest in unpaid fees as a separate remedy rather than a condition on title transfer.

---

#### The "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts With the Affirmative Warranty — Section 6.3

Section 6.2 provides an affirmative warranty: the Services will be performed professionally and the Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties except those "expressly set forth in Section 6.2" and states the Services and Deliverables are provided "as is."

The tension here is not merely cosmetic. The "as is" language in Section 6.3 is a standard disclaimer that courts in some jurisdictions read broadly, and the carve-out for Section 6.2 warranties is narrow. If a client attempts to assert a warranty claim based on professional performance standards — say, that the delivered software does not meet the specifications in the SOW — the vendor will argue that the "as is" disclaimer limits the warranty to the two specific representations in Section 6.2 and nothing more. The client will argue the SOW specifications are incorporated by reference. This is a dispute that should be resolved in the contract, not in arbitration.

Additionally, the warranty in Section 6.2 is qualified by "knowingly" with respect to IP non-infringement. A vendor who conducts no clearance review cannot "knowingly" infringe. This qualifier significantly weakens the warranty's practical value in a federal engagement where IP chain-of-title matters.

**What to request:** Clarify that SOW specifications constitute express warranties, remove or narrow the "as is" disclaimer to apply only to implied warranties not otherwise addressed, and remove the "knowingly" qualifier from the IP non-infringement warranty or replace it with a reasonable investigation standard.

---

#### Federal Contractor Context Requires Additional Scrutiny

The agreement is described as intended for a federal contractor engagement, but the agreement contains no provisions addressing:

- **FAR/DFARS flow-down obligations**, which may be required depending on the nature of the prime contract
- **Data rights and government license rights**, which can override the IP assignment structure in Section 4 if federal funds are involved in development
- **Security and compliance obligations** applicable to federal contractor data environments (e.g., CMMC, FedRAMP, CUI handling requirements)
- **Mandatory dispute resolution procedures** that may apply to federal contractor disputes and could conflict with the private AAA arbitration clause in Section 10.2

None of these are necessarily dealbreakers, but their absence from an agreement described as suitable for a federal contractor engagement is a gap that needs to be addressed either in the MSA or in the SOW template before execution.

---

#### Minor Issues Worth Noting

- **Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure:** The requirement to grant the indemnifying party "sole control" of defense and settlement is standard but requires a carve-out ensuring the indemnified party can participate in its own defense in matters affecting its ongoing business operations or federal contractor status. The existing consent requirement for settlements that impose obligations is present but may not be sufficient.

- **Section 7.2 — Termination for Convenience:** The 60-day notice period is reasonable, but the agreement does not address whether the vendor is entitled to wind-down costs or early termination fees beyond work performed. In a federal context where funding can be cut with little notice, this should be clarified.

- **Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival:** Five years is below the standard for federal contractor engagements involving sensitive government information, which often requires indefinite protection for certain categories of information. Confirm whether the 5-year period is adequate given the nature of the data involved.

#### Summary of Required Changes Before Execution

| Issue | Priority | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| Limitation of liability carve-outs render cap ineffective | **Critical** | Renegotiate scope of exceptions or increase base cap substantially |
| IP assignment conditioned on full payment | **High** | Remove payment condition; use security interest mechanism instead |
| "As is" disclaimer conflicts with affirmative warranty | **High** | Clarify warranty scope; remove "knowingly" qualifier |
| Federal contractor compliance provisions absent | **High** | Add FAR/DFARS flow-down, data rights, and security provisions |
| Sole control of indemnification defense | **Moderate** | Add participation rights carve-out |
| Confidentiality survival period | **Moderate** | Confirm 5-year period is adequate for data involved |
| Termination for convenience — wind-down costs | **Low** | Clarify vendor entitlement upon client-initiated termination |

Do not execute this agreement in its current form. The limitation of liability structure alone is sufficient reason to return it for renegotiation, and the federal contractor context adds compliance exposure that the agreement does not address.
