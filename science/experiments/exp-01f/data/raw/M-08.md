**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection the four categories of claim — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — that represent the overwhelming majority of disputes that actually arise in a professional services engagement of this type. What remains inside the cap after those exceptions are carved out is a narrow residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely generate significant exposure on their own. The cap as written does not provide the protection it appears to promise. Additionally, for a government contracting context, the agreement is missing several provisions that are not optional: there is no flow-down clause addressing federal regulatory obligations, no FAR/DFARS compliance representation from the Vendor, no data handling or security standards clause appropriate for federal information environments, and the mandatory arbitration provision in Section 10.2 raises enforceability concerns in disputes involving federal contract performance where agency interests may be implicated. These are not cosmetic issues — they are structural gaps that would leave Meridian Federal Partners exposed in the precise scenarios this agreement is meant to govern.

---

**Detailed Findings**

**Section 8 — Limitation of Liability: The Cap Does Not Function as a Cap**

Read Section 8.1 in isolation and it looks reasonable: a 12-month fee lookback, consequential damages excluded, mutual application. Standard commercial terms. Then read Section 8.2.

The exceptions remove from the cap:

- All indemnification obligations (§9) — which by their own terms cover third-party claims arising from Vendor's *performance of the Services*, IP infringement allegations, and breach of the agreement generally. This is not a narrow carve-out. Vendor indemnification under §9.1(a) encompasses the entire scope of what Vendor does under this contract.
- All confidentiality breaches (§5) — in a federal services context, where Confidential Information will include sensitive government data, program information, and potentially controlled unclassified information, this is the highest-risk category of breach.
- All IP claims (§8.2(c)) — framed broadly as "infringement, misappropriation, or violation" of either party's IP rights, with no cap on exposure.
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct (§8.2(d)) — which opposing counsel will argue covers any serious performance failure, since the standard for gross negligence is not high and is fact-specific.
- All unpaid fees (§8.2(e)) — which removes the cap from Vendor's primary affirmative claim in any dispute.

The residual category that the cap actually governs — ordinary breach claims that do not touch indemnification, confidentiality, IP, or gross negligence — is not where the money is in a dispute of this type. I have seen this structure before. The cap is real on paper and effectively nonexistent in practice.

**Recommended changes:**

1. Negotiate a separate, defined sublimit for indemnification obligations rather than a blanket carve-out — e.g., indemnification capped at a multiple of the 12-month fee lookback (2x or 3x), with the uncapped treatment reserved for third-party IP infringement claims only.
2. Retain the confidentiality carve-out but define it as applying only to *material* breaches or breaches resulting in actual disclosure to unauthorized parties, not technical violations.
3. The IP carve-out in §8.2(c) should be narrowed to third-party infringement claims. First-party IP disputes between the parties should remain subject to a defined cap.
4. The gross negligence carve-out should require a judicial or arbitral finding of gross negligence before the cap is lifted — not merely an allegation.

---

**Section 4 — Intellectual Property: Pre-Existing IP License Is Inadequate for Federal Use**

Section 4.3 grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." In a government contracting context, this is a problem. If Deliverables are ultimately used to support a federal agency, the government may require broader rights — including the right to have the work maintained, modified, or reproduced by other contractors. A license scoped to use "solely as incorporated" does not accommodate that. If Meridian has downstream obligations to a federal customer regarding deliverable rights, this license will not satisfy them.

**Recommended change:** Add a provision confirming that the Pre-Existing IP license includes sufficient rights to permit Client to fulfill its obligations to any federal agency customer, including rights of reproduction, modification, and sublicense to the government to the extent required by applicable federal acquisition regulations.

---

**Section 5 — Confidentiality: No Security Standards, No Incident Response Obligation**

The confidentiality provision is structurally standard but operationally insufficient for a federal services context. There is no requirement that Vendor implement specific security controls, no reference to NIST 800-171, CMMC, or any other applicable federal information handling standard, and critically — no breach notification obligation. If Vendor suffers a security incident involving Client's Confidential Information, this agreement does not require Vendor to notify Client within any defined timeframe. In a government contracting environment, that is not a gap — it is a liability.

**Recommended changes:**

1. Add a data security addendum or clause specifying minimum security standards applicable to any federal or government-sensitive information.
2. Add a mandatory breach notification provision requiring Vendor to notify Client within 72 hours (or sooner if required by applicable law or contract) of any actual or suspected unauthorized access to Confidential Information.
3. Specify that Confidential Information includes any Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) and that handling of CUI is subject to applicable federal requirements.

---

**Section 9 — Indemnification: Vendor's Indemnity Scope Creates Asymmetric Risk**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of "Vendor's performance of the Services." This is broad enough to cover virtually any third-party claim connected to the engagement. That is favorable to Client in isolation. The problem is that because indemnification is carved out of the liability cap entirely under §8.2(a), this provision — read together with the cap structure — means Vendor's exposure for indemnification is theoretically unlimited. Vendors who recognize this will either refuse to sign or will seek to narrow the indemnification scope aggressively in negotiation. It is better to address this now with a defined sublimit than to have it become a point of failure in execution or dispute.

The Client indemnification in §9.2 is appropriately narrower, but note that §9.2(b) — indemnification for Client's "breach of this Agreement" — is also uncapped. In a scenario where Vendor alleges Client breach, Vendor's indemnification claim against Client is unlimited. This is a symmetric structural problem that cuts both ways.

---

**Section 10.2 — Arbitration: Enforceability Concerns in Federal Context**

Mandatory binding arbitration with AAA in Wilmington, Delaware is a standard commercial dispute resolution clause. In a purely commercial context, it is unremarkable. In a government contracting context, it creates complications that should be evaluated before execution:

1. If disputes arise that implicate federal contract performance, agency interests, or regulatory compliance, federal courts — not private arbitrators — may have jurisdiction that cannot be contractually waived.
2. If Meridian's prime contract with a federal agency contains a disputes clause (as most FAR-based contracts do), that clause may govern disputes that flow downstream, and a private arbitration agreement between Meridian and its subcontractor/vendor may conflict with or be subordinate to it.
3. Arbitration limits discovery, which in a dispute involving federal program data or performance failures may be a significant disadvantage to the party with less documentary control.

**Recommended change:** Add a carve-out from the arbitration obligation for disputes that are subject to mandatory federal dispute resolution procedures, and evaluate whether the prime contract's disputes clause requires any modification to this provision.

---

**Missing Provisions for Federal Contracting Context**

The following provisions are absent and should be added before execution:

| Gap | Risk |
|---|---|
| No FAR/DFARS flow-down clause | Meridian may be unable to satisfy its prime contract compliance obligations through this agreement |
| No Vendor compliance representation | No contractual basis to require Vendor to comply with applicable federal regulations |
| No CUI handling requirements | Exposure if federal information is mishandled by Vendor |
| No security incident notification | No obligation on Vendor to report breaches in a timely manner |
| No government audit rights | Federal agencies may require audit access that this agreement does not preserve |
| No anti-kickback / ethics representations | Standard requirement in federal contracting chain |

---

**Bottom Line**

This agreement is not approvable in its current form for a federal contractor engagement. The liability cap is structurally deficient, the confidentiality and data security provisions are inadequate for the operating environment, and the agreement lacks the federal contracting compliance infrastructure that Meridian's obligations almost certainly require. These are not points to raise in post-execution negotiation. They need to be resolved before signature.
