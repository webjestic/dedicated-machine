**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a 12-month fee cap as meaningful bilateral protection, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct — which are, in practice, the categories that generate the largest and most likely disputes in a software professional services engagement. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure; the clause provides your client with the appearance of capped liability while leaving uncapped exposure precisely where the risk concentrates. Additionally, for a federal contractor engagement, the arbitration venue and governing law selections, the absence of any federal regulatory compliance obligations on the vendor, and the breadth of Vendor's indemnification trigger under Section 9.1(a) — which sweeps in *any* Losses arising from performance of Services without a materiality threshold — warrant specific attention before execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### Section 8 — Limitation of Liability: The Core Problem

#### What the cap says it does

Section 8.1 establishes two protections:

1. **Consequential damages waiver** — neither party liable for indirect, incidental, special, exemplary, punitive, or consequential damages, including lost profits or data loss.
2. **Aggregate liability cap** — total cumulative liability capped at fees paid or payable in the 12 months preceding the claim.

On its face, this reads as a standard, balanced commercial cap. It is not.

#### What the carve-outs actually do

Section 8.2 removes the following from both the consequential damages waiver and the aggregate cap:

| Carve-Out | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **(a) Indemnification obligations (§9)** | Indemnification is the mechanism through which the largest third-party claims flow. A software vendor facing an IP infringement suit against your client, or your client facing a data breach claim, will litigate under indemnification. The cap does not apply. |
| **(b) Confidentiality breaches (§5)** | In a federal contractor context, confidential information frequently includes controlled unclassified information, proprietary government data, or sensitive client data. A confidentiality breach is uncapped. |
| **(c) IP infringement/misappropriation (§8.2(c))** | Software deliverables carry inherent IP risk. Any allegation that Vendor's code infringes a third-party patent or incorporates misappropriated material is uncapped — and this carve-out is broader than the indemnification trigger because it is not limited to third-party claims. |
| **(d) Gross negligence / willful misconduct** | Opposing counsel in any significant dispute will plead gross negligence. This carve-out means the cap dissolves the moment that allegation is made, regardless of whether it is ultimately proven. |
| **(e) Fees, expenses, taxes (§3)** | One-sided in practical effect: this protects Vendor's right to collect payment without cap, but Client has no equivalent asymmetric protection. |

#### The residual category inside the cap

Strip out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and fee recovery. What remains inside the cap? Ordinary, non-gross service delivery failures — a missed deadline, a deliverable that does not meet specifications, a garden-variety breach of the workmanlike performance warranty in Section 6.2 — where the damages are direct and modest. That is the category the cap governs. It is not the category that generates material commercial disputes.

**The clause does not cap liability. It caps a category of claims that is unlikely to be the subject of significant litigation.**

---

### Specific Requested Changes

#### 1. Restructure or narrow the Section 8.2 carve-outs

The carve-outs as written are not inherently unreasonable in isolation — many commercial agreements exclude IP and confidentiality from caps. The problem is their **cumulative scope**. Recommend one or more of the following:

- **Sub-cap for IP and confidentiality:** Rather than fully uncapped, negotiate a higher fixed cap (e.g., 2× or 3× the 12-month fee amount) for IP and confidentiality claims. This preserves some ceiling on exposure.
- **Narrow the gross negligence carve-out:** As drafted, the carve-out activates on an *allegation* of gross negligence, not a finding. Consider requiring a final judicial or arbitral determination before the cap is lifted, or limiting the carve-out to willful misconduct only.
- **Remove or limit 8.2(c) as a standalone carve-out:** IP infringement is already addressed through the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(b). Having a separate, broader IP carve-out in 8.2(c) that is not limited to third-party claims creates an uncapped direct claim pathway for IP disputes that the indemnification structure does not require.

#### 2. Section 9.1(a) — Vendor indemnification scope

Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Client against Losses "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" without any limiting principle. Combined with the indemnification carve-out in 8.2(a), this creates uncapped Vendor exposure for any third-party claim touching Service performance. While this benefits Client, it is worth flagging:

- Vendor will likely push back on this scope during negotiation, and understanding the exposure is important for assessing deal risk.
- The "arising out of or related to" standard is expansive. If Vendor's subcontractor causes an incident, Vendor's indemnification obligation is triggered under 9.1(a) and is uncapped. Confirm this is the intended allocation.

#### 3. Federal contractor context — missing provisions

The agreement is described as intended for a federal contractor engagement. The following are absent and should be addressed before execution:

- **No federal regulatory compliance obligations on Vendor.** If Vendor will access federal systems, handle controlled unclassified information (CUI), or perform work subject to DFARS, CMMC, FedRAMP, or similar frameworks, those obligations must be expressly stated. Their absence does not mean they do not apply — it means the contract does not allocate responsibility for compliance failures.
- **No data security or breach notification obligations.** Section 5 addresses confidentiality but contains no data security standards, incident response obligations, or breach notification timelines. For a federal contractor context, this is a significant gap.
- **Flow-down obligations.** If this agreement is downstream of a federal prime contract, applicable FAR/DFARS clauses may need to flow down to Vendor. The agreement contains no flow-down mechanism.

#### 4. Section 6.3 — Disclaimer scope relative to Section 6.2

The warranty in Section 6.2 provides that Deliverables "will not *knowingly* infringe" third-party IP. The disclaimer in Section 6.3 then disclaims all implied warranties including non-infringement. The interaction of these two provisions is that Vendor's only IP warranty is a knowledge-qualified representation, and all other non-infringement protection is disclaimed. In a software context, this means Vendor has no obligation to conduct freedom-to-operate analysis or take affirmative steps to ensure non-infringement — only to avoid *knowing* infringement. This should be strengthened to require reasonable investigation, particularly for Deliverables that will be deployed in a federal environment.

#### 5. Arbitration venue and governing law

Delaware governing law and Wilmington arbitration are standard Vendor-favorable selections. For a federal contractor client, consider:

- Whether the client's prime contract specifies governing law or dispute resolution requirements that would conflict.
- Whether arbitration is appropriate at all for disputes that may involve federal contract performance, CUI, or classified information — AAA arbitration proceedings may create disclosure complications.

---

### What Does Not Require Changes

The following provisions are commercially standard and do not present material concerns:

- SOW structure and services scope (§2) — adequate.
- Payment terms and late fees (§3) — standard; 1.5%/month is market.
- IP assignment structure (§4) — the Pre-Existing IP carve-out with license-back is appropriate and expected.
- Termination provisions (§7) — 60-day convenience termination and 30-day cure period are reasonable.
- Indemnification procedure (§9.3) — prompt notice, sole control, and consent-to-settlement provisions are well-drafted.
- General provisions (§10) — boilerplate is clean and unproblematic.

---

### Summary Recommendation

Do not execute in current form. The limitation of liability clause misrepresents the protection it provides — the cap is structurally sound but functionally hollow because the carve-outs cover the categories of dispute that matter. Before execution, negotiate sub-caps for IP and confidentiality claims, narrow the gross negligence carve-out to require a final determination rather than mere allegation, and address the federal contractor compliance gaps. The indemnification and warranty provisions are workable but should be reviewed against the specific federal engagement requirements before the agreement is finalized.
