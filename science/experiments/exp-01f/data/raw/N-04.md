# Contract Review — Vendor Professional Services Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

---

## Executive Summary

This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review. The liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately carved out, the indemnification obligations are reasonably balanced, and the IP assignment mechanics are functional. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk or ambiguity for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses contain structural defects that would undermine their stated protections at the moment of enforcement. The agreement cannot be executed as written.

---

## Issue Log

### 🔴 Critical — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**Issue 1 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization Is Unconditional**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**Problem:** In a federal contractor context, this provision is operationally dangerous. It grants Vendor blanket authority to delegate any portion of the Services to any subcontractor without Client's prior knowledge or approval. Vendor's retained "responsibility" is a post-hoc remedy — it does not prevent a subcontractor with disqualifying characteristics (foreign ownership, debarment, adverse security posture) from accessing Client systems, personnel, or classified-adjacent information before Client is ever notified.

For an entity operating in the national security space, this is not a theoretical risk. A subcontractor touchpoint can trigger facility access issues, FOCI concerns, or supply chain integrity problems that cannot be remediated after the fact.

**Required Change:** Insert a prior written consent requirement for subcontractor engagement. At minimum: *"Vendor shall not engage any subcontractor to perform Services under this Agreement without Client's prior written approval, which shall not be unreasonably withheld."* Consider also requiring flow-down of all security, confidentiality, and compliance obligations to approved subcontractors by written agreement.

---

**Issue 2 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment Is Contingent on Full Payment and Excludes Embedded Vendor IP Without Adequate Definition**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables, excluding any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein."*

**Problem:** This provision contains two compounding defects.

First, the payment contingency means IP assignment does not occur until all fees are paid in full. In a multi-SOW engagement with disputed invoices — a common occurrence — this creates a window during which Client may be operating Deliverables it does not legally own. In a government performance context, this could impair Client's ability to fulfill prime contract obligations or transfer deliverables to the government as required.

Second, "Vendor Pre-Existing IP" is undefined in scope. There is no obligation on Vendor to identify, disclose, or enumerate what Pre-Existing IP will be incorporated into a given Deliverable prior to or during SOW execution. The license in Section 4.3 is perpetual and royalty-free, but it is non-exclusive and scoped to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." If Vendor later asserts that a core functional component is Pre-Existing IP, Client's ability to modify, extend, or maintain the Deliverable may be constrained or require renegotiation.

**Required Changes:**
- Remove or restructure the payment contingency. Assignment should be effective upon delivery, with Vendor retaining a security interest in unpaid fees through separate contractual mechanism if necessary.
- Require Vendor to identify and disclose any Pre-Existing IP intended for incorporation into Deliverables at the SOW stage, prior to execution of that SOW.
- Expand the Section 4.3 license to include the right to modify, maintain, and create derivative works of embedded Vendor Pre-Existing IP, at minimum for internal operational purposes.

---

**Issue 3 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for This Engagement Class**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**Problem:** A five-year post-termination confidentiality window is standard in commercial software agreements. It is not appropriate for agreements involving national security-adjacent work, federal program information, or sensitive government client data. Information disclosed in this context may retain its sensitivity indefinitely. A five-year sunset effectively licenses disclosure of operationally sensitive information after that window closes.

**Required Change:** Either extend survival to the life of the information's sensitivity (impractical to define) or, more practically, carve out a category of "Government Sensitive Information" or "Classified-Adjacent Information" for which confidentiality obligations survive indefinitely or until the information enters the public domain through no fault of the Receiving Party. At minimum, negotiate survival to ten (10) years for all Confidential Information, with no sunset for information subject to government handling requirements.

---

**Issue 4 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause May Conflict with Federal Contractor Obligations**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration... The arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding and may be entered as a judgment in any court of competent jurisdiction."*

**Problem:** Binding arbitration with finality is a standard commercial provision. In a federal contractor context, it is potentially incompatible with several obligations. Disputes touching on government data, contract performance, or regulatory compliance may implicate the Contract Disputes Act, agency oversight requirements, or mandatory reporting obligations that cannot be subordinated to a private arbitration proceeding. An arbitrator's confidential, final ruling may also conflict with Client's transparency obligations to a government customer.

Additionally, the clause contains no carve-out for emergency injunctive relief. Given the IP and confidentiality provisions in this agreement, the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction pending arbitration is a material gap.

**Required Changes:**
- Insert an explicit carve-out: *"Nothing in this Section shall limit either party's right to seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending resolution of a dispute."*
- Evaluate whether the mandatory arbitration clause should be narrowed to exclude disputes arising from or implicating federal contract performance, government data, or regulatory compliance obligations.

### 🟡 Significant — Should Resolve Before Execution

---

**Issue 5 — Section 6.3: Disclaimer of Implied Warranties Conflicts with Section 6.2 Representation**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**Problem:** The "as is" language in Section 6.3 is in direct tension with the affirmative representation in Section 6.2 that Services will be performed in a "professional manner consistent with applicable industry standards." These provisions are not cleanly reconciled. A vendor facing a warranty claim will invoke the "as is" disclaimer; Client will point to the Section 6.2 standard. This ambiguity will be litigated.

More substantively, the disclaimer of fitness for a particular purpose is problematic in an SOW-driven engagement where Vendor is explicitly engaged to deliver a specific functional outcome. If the Deliverable fails to perform its intended purpose, Vendor's position will be that it delivered something "as is" and met the general workmanlike standard. That is not an adequate protection for Client.

**Required Change:** Add a limited fitness warranty tied to the SOW: *"Vendor warrants that the Deliverables will materially conform to the functional specifications set forth in the applicable SOW for a period of [90/180] days following acceptance."* This is a standard and reasonable ask that most vendors will accept.

---

**Issue 6 — Section 9.1(a): Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Will Be Contested**

> *"Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Client... arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**Problem:** Indemnification for "Vendor's performance of the Services" without a negligence or fault qualifier is facially overbroad. A vendor will argue — correctly — that this provision, read literally, would require Vendor to indemnify Client for any third-party claim arising from the engagement regardless of fault allocation. This will be a flashpoint in any coverage dispute and may cause the provision to be narrowed or voided by an arbitrator.

The provision is also asymmetric in a way that may not survive scrutiny: Client's indemnification obligation in Section 9.2 is fault-qualified (gross negligence or willful misconduct), while Vendor's performance-based indemnity in 9.1(a) is not.

**Required Change:** Qualify Section 9.1(a) to limit indemnification to claims arising from Vendor's negligent or wrongful performance of the Services. This is a more defensible and enforceable standard, and it eliminates the asymmetry that a court or arbitrator may use to rebalance the provision against Client.

---

**Issue 7 — Section 7.2: Termination for Convenience Is Symmetric but Operationally Asymmetric**

> *"Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' written notice..."*

**Problem:** Sixty days' notice is a reasonable commercial standard. However, in a federal contractor context where Client's obligations to a government customer are defined by a prime contract with fixed performance periods, a vendor's right to terminate for convenience on 60 days' notice may be insufficient to allow Client to identify, onboard, and transition to a replacement vendor without a gap in performance. A performance gap at the prime contract level can trigger cure notices, liquidated damages, or termination for default by the government customer.

**Required Change:** Consider negotiating an extended notice period for Vendor-initiated termination for convenience (90–120 days), or insert a transition assistance obligation: *"Upon any termination, Vendor shall provide reasonable transition assistance services for a period of up to [90] days at Client's request, at Vendor's then-current rates."* This is a standard ask and protects continuity of performance.

### 🟢 Noted — Low Risk, No Change Required

**Issue 8 — Section 3.2: Late Payment Interest Rate**

The 1.5% per month rate (18% annualized) is at the high end of commercial practice but is capped at the legal maximum and is standard in vendor agreements. No change required, but flag for client awareness.
