**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally present but operationally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that is likely to arise in a professional services engagement of this type — indemnification, confidentiality breach, IP infringement, gross negligence or willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary breach claims where the damages are typically modest anyway. For a federal contractor engagement where data sensitivity, IP ownership, and compliance exposure are the primary risk vectors, this means the cap provides no meaningful ceiling on the disputes that actually matter. Before execution, the client needs to understand that they are accepting uncapped exposure across the full surface area of foreseeable risk, and the agreement should be renegotiated to either narrow the carve-outs or establish sub-caps that impose defined ceilings on the excepted categories.

---

**Supporting Analysis**

**The cap as written:**

Section 8.1 establishes two protections: (1) exclusion of consequential, indirect, and punitive damages, and (2) a total liability ceiling equal to fees paid in the preceding twelve months. On first read, this looks like standard commercial protection. It is not, once you read Section 8.2.

---

**What Section 8.2 removes from the cap:**

| Carve-Out | What It Exposes |
|---|---|
| **(a) Indemnification (§9)** | Vendor's indemnity obligation covers all third-party claims arising from Services performance, IP infringement, breach, and gross negligence — with no dollar ceiling. Client's indemnity obligation for unauthorized use or its own breach is equally uncapped. |
| **(b) Confidentiality breach (§5)** | In a federal contractor context, confidential information likely includes government data, CUI, or proprietary program information. A confidentiality breach here is not a theoretical risk — it is the risk. Uncapped. |
| **(c) IP infringement or misappropriation** | Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but Section 4.3 carves out Vendor Pre-Existing IP with only a license. If Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is later disputed or if a third party asserts infringement, exposure is uncapped in both directions. |
| **(d) Gross negligence or willful misconduct** | This carve-out is common and often defensible in principle. The problem is that in litigation, plaintiffs routinely plead gross negligence alongside ordinary negligence. The moment opposing counsel characterizes conduct as grossly negligent — even as an alternative theory — the cap argument collapses. |
| **(e) Unpaid fees (§3)** | This one runs only in Vendor's favor. Client's payment obligations are fully uncapped, including accrued interest at 1.5% per month. This is not symmetric. |

---

**What remains inside the cap:**

After the carve-outs, the cap realistically governs only garden-variety service delivery failures — a missed deadline, a Deliverable that does not meet workmanlike standards, a minor breach of a non-IP, non-confidentiality obligation. These are the disputes where actual damages tend to be quantifiable, bounded, and often less than the cap anyway. The cap is protecting the category of risk that needed the least protection.

---

**The federal contractor dimension:**

The agreement is described as intended for a federal contractor engagement. That context materially elevates the risk profile of the uncapped categories:

- **Confidentiality:** Government-related data may carry regulatory obligations (FAR, DFARS, CUI requirements) that create liability exposure well beyond the contractual relationship. A confidentiality breach is not just a contract claim — it can trigger regulatory consequences, agency notification obligations, and reputational harm that dwarfs the contract value.
- **IP:** Federal procurement contexts often involve background IP, government purpose license rights, and data rights assertions by the government. The Pre-Existing IP carve-out in Section 4.3 combined with the uncapped IP exception in Section 8.2(c) creates a scenario where a downstream IP dispute could expose Client to unlimited liability with no contractual ceiling.
- **Indemnification scope:** Section 9.1(a) indemnifies against third-party claims arising from Vendor's *performance of the Services* — a broad formulation. In a federal engagement, "third parties" could include the government itself as a claimant or intervenor. No cap applies.

---

**Specific changes to request:**

1. **Narrow or sub-cap the indemnification carve-out.** If indemnification must remain outside the consequential damages exclusion, negotiate a separate aggregate cap on indemnification obligations — typically a multiple of contract value (e.g., 2x or 3x total fees). Uncapped indemnification in a services agreement is not standard; it is a vendor-favorable position dressed as standard.

2. **Sub-cap the confidentiality carve-out.** Confidentiality breaches should have a defined ceiling. A common approach: cap confidentiality liability at a fixed multiple of fees or at a negotiated dollar amount that reflects the actual sensitivity of the information being shared. The five-year survival period in Section 5.3 combined with uncapped liability is a significant long-tail exposure.

3. **Tighten the gross negligence carve-out.** Consider requiring that gross negligence be established by final judicial or arbitral determination before the cap is lifted, rather than allowing the mere allegation of gross negligence to remove the cap during litigation. Alternatively, negotiate a sub-cap for gross negligence claims short of willful misconduct.

4. **Address the fee asymmetry in Section 8.2(e).** Client's payment obligations are uncapped while Vendor's service delivery obligations are capped (to the extent the cap applies at all). If the cap is mutual in name, it should be mutual in effect. Either remove this carve-out or add a corresponding carve-out for Client's damages arising from Vendor's material failure to deliver.

5. **Clarify Pre-Existing IP scope before execution.** Section 4.3's license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP is non-exclusive and limited to use "as incorporated in the Deliverables." In a federal contractor context, Client needs to understand what Vendor Pre-Existing IP will be incorporated and whether the license terms are sufficient for the intended government use. This is a diligence issue, not just a drafting issue.

---

**Bottom line for the client:**

Do not execute this agreement under the assumption that the liability cap provides meaningful protection. As written, it does not apply to the disputes you are most likely to face. The agreement can be made acceptable with targeted revisions, but it should not be signed in its current form for a federal contractor engagement.
