# Contract Review — Software Professional Services Vendor Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it presents four issues that require resolution before execution is appropriate for a federal contractor engagement. Two of those issues are material risk items; two are clarifications that could become material depending on program context. The agreement should not be executed as written.

### ⚠️ ISSUES REQUIRING RESOLUTION

---

#### ISSUE #1 — MATERIAL RISK
**§5.3 | Confidentiality Survival: Five-Year Cap Is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Engagements**

**The Problem:**

The confidentiality obligation survives termination for only five (5) years. In a federal contractor context, this is a significant structural deficiency. Depending on the program, Client may be handling Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), Sensitive But Unclassified (SBU) materials, proprietary program data, or information subject to federal regulatory retention requirements that extend well beyond five years. A fixed five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means Vendor is contractually free to disclose Client's sensitive program information after that window closes — regardless of whether the information remains sensitive, remains in Vendor's possession, or remains subject to federal handling requirements.

This is not a theoretical risk. It is a structural gap that becomes a live exposure the moment the five-year clock expires.

**Required Change:**

Confidentiality obligations for information that constitutes trade secrets should survive indefinitely, consistent with applicable trade secret law. For all other Confidential Information, the survival period should be extended to a minimum of seven (7) years, or for the duration of any applicable federal regulatory retention requirement, whichever is longer. Alternatively, Client should negotiate for survival "until the information enters the public domain through no fault of the Receiving Party," which is the more protective standard.

**Proposed Revised Language (§5.3):**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement (a) indefinitely with respect to information constituting trade secrets under applicable law, and (b) for a period of seven (7) years with respect to all other Confidential Information, or for such longer period as may be required by applicable federal law or regulation."*

#### ISSUE #2 — MATERIAL RISK
**§10.2 | Mandatory Binding Arbitration: Incompatible with Federal Contract Obligations and Potential Regulatory Requirements**

**The Problem:**

The agreement requires all disputes to be resolved by binding arbitration administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware, with no carve-outs. This provision is facially incompatible with federal contractor obligations in several respects:

1. **Federal prime contract flow-downs.** If Client's work under this agreement is performed in support of a federal prime contract, that prime contract almost certainly contains dispute resolution clauses, Disputes Act provisions (41 U.S.C. § 7101 et seq.), or other federal requirements that govern how disputes touching on federal contract performance are resolved. A mandatory private arbitration clause in a subcontract or vendor agreement cannot override those federal requirements and creates a direct conflict.

2. **False Claims Act and whistleblower exposure.** Mandatory arbitration clauses in federal contractor agreements have been scrutinized in connection with False Claims Act (FCA) matters. Certain FCA claims cannot be compelled to arbitration, and a broad arbitration clause that does not carve out statutory claims creates ambiguity about the scope of arbitrable disputes.

3. **Injunctive relief.** The arbitration clause contains no carve-out for emergency injunctive or equitable relief. In the event of a confidentiality breach, IP misappropriation, or other time-sensitive harm, Client's ability to seek immediate injunctive relief in a court of competent jurisdiction is effectively eliminated as drafted. This is a significant practical problem in the national security context where speed of response to a breach can be outcome-determinative.

4. **Venue.** Wilmington, Delaware is not an unreasonable choice for a Delaware-governed agreement, but Client should evaluate whether this creates operational burden given its actual place of business and program locations.

**Required Change:**

At minimum, the arbitration clause must include: (a) an express carve-out for injunctive and equitable relief in courts of competent jurisdiction; (b) an express carve-out for disputes arising under or implicating federal law, federal contract requirements, or federal regulatory obligations; and (c) a carve-out for any claims that are non-arbitrable as a matter of applicable law.

**Proposed Revised Language (§10.2):**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement that does not involve federal law, federal contract requirements, or federal regulatory obligations shall be resolved by binding arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association under its Commercial Arbitration Rules, with arbitration taking place in Wilmington, Delaware. Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending resolution of any dispute. Nothing in this Section shall require arbitration of any claim or dispute that is non-arbitrable as a matter of applicable federal or state law, or that arises under or implicates Client's obligations under any federal prime contract or applicable federal regulation."*

#### ISSUE #3 — CLARIFICATION REQUIRED
**§4.3 | License to Vendor Pre-Existing IP: Scope Limitation May Create Operational Risk**

**The Problem:**

Section 4.3 grants Client a license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This is a narrow license. It means Client is licensed to use the Pre-Existing IP only in the specific form and context in which it appears in the Deliverables as delivered. If Client needs to modify the Deliverables, integrate them with other systems, port them to a different environment, or use the underlying Pre-Existing IP components in a derivative work, that use may fall outside the scope of the license as written.

In a federal contractor context, where Deliverables may need to be modified to comply with evolving agency requirements, integrated into government systems, or maintained over a multi-year program lifecycle, this restriction creates a meaningful operational constraint that Client may not fully appreciate at execution.

**Required Clarification:**

Client should confirm whether the anticipated use of the Deliverables is limited to use "as delivered" or whether Client expects to modify, adapt, integrate, or build upon the Deliverables over time. If the latter, the license in §4.3 must be expanded to cover modifications and derivative works.

**Proposed Revised Language (§4.3) if broader use is anticipated:**

> *"To the extent any Vendor Pre-Existing IP is incorporated into the Deliverables, Vendor grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license to use, reproduce, modify, and create derivative works of such Vendor Pre-Existing IP solely to the extent necessary for Client to use, maintain, modify, and support the Deliverables."*

#### ISSUE #4 — CLARIFICATION REQUIRED
**§9.1 | Vendor Indemnification Scope: "Arising Out of Vendor's Performance" Is Overbroad in One Direction and Potentially Underinclusive in Another**

**The Problem:**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." This language is broad, which is generally favorable to Client. However, it creates two issues that should be addressed:

1. **Contributory fault allocation.** The indemnification obligation in §9.1(a) is not qualified by fault. If a third-party claim arises from the Services but is caused in whole or in part by Client's own conduct, specifications, or instructions, Vendor's indemnification obligation as written still applies. This creates a potential windfall for Client and a corresponding overexposure for Vendor that may be resisted at the time of a claim, leading to litigation over the scope of the indemnity. A proportionate fault carve-out is commercially standard and reduces dispute risk.

2. **Data breach / security incident coverage.** The indemnification provision does not expressly address third-party claims arising from a data breach or security incident caused by Vendor's systems, personnel, or subcontractors. Given that Vendor will have access to Client's systems and potentially sensitive federal program data (per §2.2), this is a gap. In the event of a breach, Client may face regulatory exposure, agency notification obligations, and third-party claims. Vendor's indemnification obligation should expressly extend to this scenario.

**Required Clarification:**

Client should confirm whether the absence of a data breach indemnification provision is acceptable given the nature of Vendor's system access. If Vendor will have access to sensitive or regulated data, an express data breach indemnification obligation should be added to §9.1.
