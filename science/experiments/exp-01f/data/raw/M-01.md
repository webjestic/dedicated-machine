**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally present but operationally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively exempt indemnification, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence, willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees from the cap — which is to say, they exempt every category of dispute that is likely to generate material exposure in a federal contractor engagement. What remains inside the cap is a narrow residual of ordinary service delivery disputes where damages are modest by definition; the cap provides no meaningful protection against the claims that actually threaten the business. Additionally, the indemnification obligations in Section 9 are uncapped and mutual, but the asymmetry of real-world risk between a software vendor and a federal contractor means that Client's exposure under Section 9.2 — particularly for unauthorized use of Deliverables — could be triggered by conduct that is ambiguously defined and difficult to control in a government performance environment. Before execution, the following changes are required:

---

**1. Section 8.2 — Carve-Out Scope Must Be Narrowed**

The current carve-out structure removes the cap from every commercially significant risk category. This is not a balanced allocation — it is a cap that exists on paper and nowhere else.

- **Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification:** Indemnification obligations should themselves be subject to an aggregate ceiling. Recommend negotiating a separate, higher cap on indemnification obligations — for example, two to three times the trailing twelve-month fees — rather than leaving them entirely uncapped. Unlimited indemnification exposure in a federal services context, where third-party IP and security claims can be catastrophic, is not acceptable.

- **Section 8.2(b) — Confidentiality:** A blanket carve-out for all confidentiality breaches is overbroad. Recommend limiting uncapped exposure to breaches involving gross negligence or willful misconduct, or to breaches that result in unauthorized disclosure of classified or controlled unclassified information (CUI). Inadvertent breaches should remain subject to the cap.

- **Section 8.2(c) — IP Rights:** This carve-out is written symmetrically but functions asymmetrically. Vendor's IP exposure arises from what it delivers; Client's IP exposure arises from how it uses those deliverables in a federal environment. Recommend limiting this carve-out to third-party IP infringement claims addressed through the indemnification mechanism in Section 9.1(b), rather than creating a freestanding uncapped IP liability category that could be invoked independently of the indemnification procedure.

- **Section 8.2(e) — Unpaid Fees:** This carve-out is reasonable in principle — a cap should not shield a party from its payment obligations — but it should be expressly limited to undisputed fees. As drafted, it could be used to bypass the cap on any fee dispute, including contested invoices or change order disagreements.

---

**2. Section 9 — Indemnification Obligations Require a Defined Ceiling**

Because indemnification is carved out of the liability cap entirely, Section 9 currently imposes unlimited exposure on both parties. In a national security or federal services context, third-party claims arising from Vendor's performance — particularly IP infringement claims or claims arising from subcontractor conduct under Section 2.3 — can reach figures that bear no relationship to the contract value. Recommend inserting a defined aggregate cap on indemnification obligations, stated as a multiple of fees paid, with a separate, higher sublimit for IP indemnification claims given their frequency in software engagements.

---

**3. Section 9.2 — Client Indemnification Scope Is Ambiguous and Overbroad**

Section 9.2(a) requires Client to indemnify Vendor for use of Deliverables "in a manner not authorized by this Agreement." In a federal contractor context, Client's use of Deliverables is frequently directed by agency requirements, task orders, or contracting officer instructions that may not be anticipated at the time of MSA execution. The phrase "not authorized by this Agreement" is undefined and could be read to capture use cases that Client has no practical ability to refuse. Recommend adding language that limits this indemnification obligation to uses that are both unauthorized and the result of Client's knowing or willful conduct, and that expressly excludes uses required by law, regulation, or government direction.

---

**4. Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival Period**

A five-year survival period is standard in commercial agreements. It is potentially inadequate in a federal contractor context where Confidential Information may include CUI, export-controlled data, or information subject to agency-specific handling requirements that impose longer or indefinite protection obligations. Recommend either extending the survival period to match the longest applicable regulatory requirement or carving out a separate, indefinite survival obligation for information that is classified, CUI, or otherwise subject to federal handling requirements.

---

**5. Section 10.2 — Arbitration Clause Requires Scrutiny for Federal Contract Compatibility**

Binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware is a standard commercial choice. In a federal contractor context, it creates two issues worth flagging before execution. First, certain disputes arising from federal contract performance — particularly those touching on agency interests, security obligations, or government data — may not be arbitrable or may require government consent to resolve through private arbitration. Second, the AAA Commercial Rules do not include provisions for handling classified or controlled information, which creates a procedural gap if a dispute involves sensitive program details. Recommend adding a carve-out that preserves the right to seek injunctive or equitable relief in court for claims involving IP or confidentiality, and confirming with Client's federal contracting counsel that arbitration is permissible under the relevant prime contract terms.

---

**Bottom Line**

This agreement is not approvable in its current form. The liability cap is a structural feature that provides no protection in the disputes that matter. The indemnification obligations are unlimited. The confidentiality and IP carve-outs are written broadly enough to swallow the cap entirely in any serious dispute. These are not minor drafting issues — they are the architecture of the agreement's risk allocation, and they are wrong. Return to Vendor with the changes above before execution.
