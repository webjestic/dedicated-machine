**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally present but functionally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that realistically arises in a software professional services engagement — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap to govern only a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive material exposure in practice. Before execution, the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1 requires specific attention: as drafted, it covers *all* third-party claims arising out of Vendor's performance of the Services and all breaches of the Agreement, is entirely uncapped, and in a federal contractor context could expose Client to indemnification demands that dwarf the contract value. The agreement should not be executed in its current form without negotiating either a monetary ceiling on indemnification obligations or a narrowed indemnification scope, and counsel should assess whether the arbitration venue and governing law provisions are compatible with the client's federal contracting obligations before signature.

---

### Detailed Analysis

#### 1. The Liability Cap Is Not Functioning as a Cap

Section 8.1 establishes two protections: a consequential damages waiver and a 12-month fee cap on total cumulative liability. Both are carved out entirely by Section 8.2. Work through the carve-outs:

| Carve-Out | What It Actually Covers in Practice |
|---|---|
| **(a) Indemnification (§9)** | All third-party IP claims, all third-party claims from Services performance, all breach-based third-party claims |
| **(b) Confidentiality (§5)** | Any disclosure of non-public information — a primary risk in federal contractor engagements |
| **(c) IP infringement/misappropriation** | Core deliverable disputes; the most common high-value software services claim |
| **(d) Gross negligence / willful misconduct** | Any serious performance failure will be pleaded this way by opposing counsel |
| **(e) Unpaid fees (§3)** | Vendor's primary direct claim against Client |

What remains *inside* the cap after these carve-outs: ordinary contract breach claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or negligence. In a software professional services agreement, that residual category is nearly empty as a matter of commercial reality. The cap provides no protection in the disputes that actually occur.

This is not an unusual structure — it appears in vendor paper routinely — but it should not be accepted as-is, and it should not be described to the client as providing meaningful liability protection.

---

#### 2. The Indemnification Obligation Is the Primary Exposure

Section 9.1 is the clause that warrants the most immediate attention. As drafted:

- **Scope is extremely broad.** Vendor's indemnification obligation covers third-party claims arising out of *(a)* performance of the Services, *(b)* IP infringement by Deliverables, *(c)* any breach of the Agreement, and *(d)* gross negligence or willful misconduct. Categories (a) and (c) are particularly expansive — any third-party claim touching Vendor's work or any contractual breach triggers the obligation.

- **There is no monetary ceiling.** Because indemnification is carved out of Section 8.1 entirely, there is no cap on what either party owes under Section 9. This is the structural consequence of carve-out (a): the parties have agreed that indemnification exposure is unlimited in both directions.

- **Client's reciprocal obligation under 9.2 is also uncapped.** Client indemnifies Vendor for unauthorized use of Deliverables, Client's breach, and Client's gross negligence. In a federal contractor context, "unauthorized use" and "breach" are categories that can be contested aggressively by a vendor seeking to shift liability.

- **The "sole control" provision in 9.3 requires scrutiny.** Granting Vendor sole control of defense and settlement of third-party claims that implicate Client's federal contractor relationships is a significant concession. If a third-party claim involves a government counterparty or implicates federal compliance obligations, Client needs retained approval rights over settlement terms, not merely a consent right limited to settlements that "impose liability or obligation" on Client.

**Recommended fix:** Negotiate a cap on indemnification obligations — either a fixed dollar ceiling (e.g., 2x or 3x contract value) or a tie-back to insurance coverage limits. Alternatively, narrow the indemnification trigger in 9.1(a) and 9.1(c) to remove the open-ended "performance of Services" and "any breach" language, replacing it with specific, defined triggering events.

---

#### 3. IP Assignment Has a Structural Gap That Will Surface Later

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client, but excludes Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into them. Section 4.3 provides a license to that Pre-Existing IP — non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free, but limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."

The problem: **"Vendor Pre-Existing IP" is not defined with any specificity.** The agreement defines Pre-Existing IP as materials developed "prior to or independently of this Agreement," but there is no schedule, no disclosure obligation, and no mechanism for Vendor to identify what Pre-Existing IP it is incorporating at the time of delivery. In practice, this means:

- Client receives an assignment of Deliverables that may be substantially hollow if Vendor later claims significant pre-existing components.
- The license in 4.3 is use-restricted in a way that may not accommodate Client's operational needs, particularly if Deliverables are integrated into broader federal systems.
- There is no representation that Vendor will disclose Pre-Existing IP incorporation at the time of SOW execution or delivery.

**Recommended fix:** Require Vendor to identify and disclose any Pre-Existing IP intended for incorporation in each SOW prior to execution. Expand the license in 4.3 to cover modifications, derivatives, and integration into Client's broader systems, not merely use "as incorporated."

---

#### 4. Federal Contractor Context Creates Additional Concerns

The agreement is presented as suitable for a federal contractor engagement. Several provisions require assessment against that context before execution:

**Governing law and arbitration (§10.1, §10.2).** Delaware law and AAA arbitration in Wilmington are standard commercial choices, but federal contractor agreements may be subject to mandatory dispute resolution procedures, agency-specific clauses, or federal common law requirements depending on the nature of the engagement. If this agreement governs work performed under a federal prime contract or subcontract, the governing law and dispute resolution provisions need to be reviewed against the prime contract's flow-down requirements.

**Confidentiality (§5).** The five-year survival period is reasonable commercially, but if the Services involve access to Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), federal information systems, or data subject to FISMA, DFARS, or similar frameworks, Section 5 as drafted is almost certainly insufficient. The confidentiality obligation needs to be supplemented with specific data handling, security, and breach notification requirements appropriate to the federal context.

**Subcontractors (§2.3).** Vendor retains the right to use subcontractors with no prior approval requirement from Client. In a federal contractor context, subcontractor use may require Client consent, security clearance verification, or compliance with specific small business subcontracting requirements. This provision should require prior written approval for subcontractors who will access Client systems or data.

---

#### 5. What Is Acceptable as Drafted

The following provisions are commercially standard and do not require changes:

- SOW structure (§2.1–2.2) is appropriate.
- Payment terms and late fee rate (§3.1–3.2) are market standard.
- Mutual authority representations (§6.1) are standard.
- Termination for convenience with 60-day notice (§7.2) is reasonable.
- Cure period for material breach (§7.3) is standard.
- Survival clause (§7.4) correctly identifies the provisions that should survive.
- General provisions (§10.3–10.8) are boilerplate and unproblematic.

#### Summary of Required Changes Before Execution

| Priority | Issue | Required Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | Indemnification is uncapped and overbroad | Negotiate monetary ceiling; narrow triggering scope in 9.1(a) and 9.1(c) |
| **Critical** | Liability cap is functionally illusory | Restructure carve-outs or negotiate cap that applies to indemnification |
| **High** | Pre-Existing IP not disclosed or defined | Add disclosure obligation and schedule to each SOW |
| **High** | IP license scope too narrow for federal use | Expand 4.3 to cover integration and derivative use |
| **High** | Settlement control in 9.3 | Retain Client approval rights over any settlement affecting federal relationships |
| **Moderate** | Subcontractor approval | Require prior written consent for subcontractors accessing Client systems or data |
| **Moderate** | Federal compliance gap in confidentiality | Supplement §5 with CUI/data security requirements if applicable |
| **Moderate** | Governing law / arbitration compatibility | Verify against prime contract flow-down requirements before execution |
