# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal Review Team*

---

## DECISION: ⚠️ REQUEST CHANGES

**Summary:** This agreement contains several provisions that create asymmetric and unacceptable legal exposure for Meridian Federal Partners in a federal contracting context. While the document presents as a balanced commercial MSA, closer examination reveals structural vulnerabilities in the IP assignment mechanics, a confidentiality framework inadequate for national security-adjacent work, and an arbitration clause that may conflict with federal dispute resolution obligations. These issues are not cosmetic — they go to the core of how risk is allocated and how Meridian would be positioned in the event of a dispute or government audit.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**FINDING 1 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment is Contingent and Potentially Illusory**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables, excluding any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein."*

**The Problem:**
This is a conditional present-tense assignment that does not vest until full payment is made. In a federal contracting environment, Meridian may be delivering work product to a government end-user before invoices are fully settled. If any payment dispute arises — even a minor one — Vendor could argue that assignment never occurred, leaving Meridian holding deliverables it does not legally own at the moment the government needs them.

Additionally, the carve-out for "Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein" is undefined in scope and unbounded in application. There is no obligation on Vendor to *disclose* what Pre-Existing IP has been incorporated, no schedule or exhibit identifying such IP, and no mechanism for Meridian to audit or challenge that designation. A sophisticated vendor could embed proprietary components into every deliverable, then retroactively assert broad Pre-Existing IP claims to limit what Meridian actually received.

**The Risk:**
Meridian could find itself in a position where it has paid in full, delivered to a government client, and still does not hold clean title to the work product. In a national security context, this is a potential FAR compliance issue and a contractual liability to the government end-user.

**Recommended Fix:**
- Change to an unconditional present-tense assignment effective upon creation, with a license-back to Vendor if needed pending payment.
- Require Vendor to identify and schedule all Pre-Existing IP prior to or at SOW execution.
- Add a representation that no unlisted Pre-Existing IP will be incorporated without prior written consent.

---

**FINDING 2 — Section 4.3: The Pre-Existing IP License is Dangerously Narrow**

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**The Problem:**
The license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP is restricted to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This means Meridian cannot modify, update, maintain, or integrate those components into successor systems without potentially exceeding the license scope. In a federal IT services context, where systems evolve, are patched, and are integrated into broader infrastructure, this restriction is operationally unworkable.

**The Risk:**
Any modification of a deliverable that touches Vendor Pre-Existing IP — including routine maintenance or security patching — could constitute a license violation. Vendor could use this as leverage in a future dispute or as a basis for injunctive relief at a critical operational moment.

**Recommended Fix:**
Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, maintain, integrate, and create derivative works of Vendor Pre-Existing IP as reasonably necessary to exercise full benefit of the Deliverables.

---

**FINDING 3 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival is Inadequate for Federal Work**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**
This is the most structurally dangerous provision in the agreement for a firm operating in the national security and federal services space. Five years is a commercial standard drawn from the private sector. It is wholly insufficient here.

Federal contractors routinely handle information subject to:
- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) frameworks under 32 CFR Part 2002
- NIST SP 800-171 / CMMC requirements
- Export control obligations under ITAR/EAR
- Agency-specific non-disclosure requirements that may impose indefinite or perpetual obligations

A five-year sunset on confidentiality means that sensitive government program information, technical data, or personally identifiable information (PII) could be freely disclosed by either party five years post-termination. This is not just a contractual risk — it is a potential regulatory and criminal exposure issue.

Furthermore, the confidentiality definition in Section 5.1, while broad, contains no explicit carve-in for government-mandated protections. There is no acknowledgment that certain information may carry obligations *beyond* what this contract provides, and no clause preserving those obligations independent of the contractual term.

**The Risk:**
Meridian could face regulatory enforcement action, loss of clearances, or government contract termination for cause based on a confidentiality framework it believed was adequate but was not.

**Recommended Fix:**
- Extend survival to perpetual, or at minimum co-terminus with any applicable government-imposed obligation, whichever is longer.
- Add a carve-in clause: *"Notwithstanding the foregoing, obligations with respect to Confidential Information subject to applicable law, regulation, or government contract requirements shall survive for the duration required by such law, regulation, or requirement."*
- Consider adding explicit CUI/ITAR/EAR acknowledgment language if the engagement involves controlled technical data.

### 🟠 SIGNIFICANT — Strongly Recommended Changes

---

**FINDING 4 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause May Conflict with Federal Obligations**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Problem:**
The arbitration clause is drafted without any carve-out for disputes involving federal law, regulatory compliance, or government contract flow-down obligations. In a federal contracting context, certain disputes — particularly those touching on False Claims Act exposure, security incidents, or agency-directed actions — may require or permit litigation in federal court, agency-level resolution, or government intervention. A blanket mandatory arbitration clause could be used to argue that even those disputes must go to a private AAA arbitrator in Wilmington, Delaware.

Additionally, if Meridian's prime contract with a government agency contains a disputes clause (as virtually all FAR-based contracts do under FAR 52.233-1), a downstream vendor agreement that routes all disputes to private arbitration creates a structural conflict in the dispute resolution chain.

**The Risk:**
Meridian could be contractually obligated to arbitrate a dispute that its government client requires be handled through federal channels. This creates a lose-lose scenario where Meridian is in breach of either its vendor agreement or its prime contract.

**Recommended Fix:**
Add explicit carve-outs for:
- Disputes arising under or related to any applicable government contract or federal law
- Claims subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction (e.g., FCA, FOIA, security incident reporting)
- Government-directed actions or investigations

---

**FINDING 5 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization is Unconditional**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

**The Problem:**
Vendor has blanket authority to subcontract any portion of the Services without Client's prior approval. In a national security context, this is a significant security and compliance gap. Subcontractors may need to access classified systems, CUI, or sensitive government networks. Meridian has no visibility into who those subcontractors are, whether they hold required clearances, whether they are on any debarment lists, or whether they are foreign nationals or entities subject to FARA or OFAC restrictions.

The "remains responsible" language provides contractual recourse after the fact but does nothing to prevent a security incident or compliance violation from occurring in the first place.

**The Risk:**
An unauthorized subcontractor with access to sensitive systems could trigger a reportable security incident, a FAR/DFARS violation, or a clearance review — all before Meridian even knew the subcontractor existed.

**Recommended Fix:**
- Require prior written consent for any subcontracting.
- Require Vendor to provide subcontractor identity, clearance status, and compliance certifications before engagement.
- Add a flow-down clause requiring all subcontractors to be bound by the same security and compliance obligations as Vendor.

---

**FINDING 6 — Section 8.2(c): IP Carve-Out from Liability Cap is Asymmetric in Practice**

> *"(c) infringement, misappropriation, or violation of either party's Intellectual Property Rights;"*

**The Problem:**
On its face, this appears balanced — both parties get uncapped exposure for IP violations. In practice, however, this provision disproportionately benefits Vendor. Vendor holds the Pre-Existing IP. Vendor controls what gets incorporated into deliverables. Vendor could assert an IP infringement claim against Meridian for using deliverables in ways Vendor later characterizes as outside the license scope (see Finding 2), and that claim would carry *uncapped* liability exposure for Meridian.

Meanwhile, Meridian's own IP portfolio in this engagement is likely limited. The asymmetry in who actually holds IP leverage means this "mutual" carve-out functions as a one-sided trap.

**The Risk:**
Meridian could face uncapped liability for IP claims arising from Vendor's own failure to clearly define and disclose Pre-Existing IP at the outset — a risk Meridian cannot fully control.

**Recommended Fix:**
Tie the IP carve-out to claims arising from *third-party* IP infringement only, or add a mutual representation that no party will assert IP claims arising from ambiguities in the Pre-Existing IP definition that were within that party's control to clarify.

### 🟡 NOTABLE — Clarification Recommended

---

**FINDING 7 — Section 6.3: "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts with Section 6.2 Warranty**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**The Problem:**
Section 6.2 provides an express warranty that Deliverables "will not knowingly infringe the Intellectual Property Rights of any third party." Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties and states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is." The word "knowingly" in Section 6.2 already significantly weakens the warranty — but the "as is" language in 6.3 creates an internal conflict that a court or arbitrator would need to resolve. Vendor could argue the "as is" disclaimer swallows the Section 6.2 warranty entirely.

**Recommended Fix:**
Clarify that the Section 6.3 disclaimer applies *except as expressly stated in Section 6.2*, and remove the "as is" characterization as applied to Deliverables for which specific warranties have been made.

---

**FINDING 8 — Section 7.4: Termination for Convenience Payment Obligation Has No Cap**

> *"Client shall pay Vendor for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination."*

**The Problem:**
"Expenses incurred" is undefined and unbounded. Vendor could argue this includes costs incurred in anticipation of future work, wind-down costs, or subcontractor termination fees — none of which are capped or defined. In a federal contracting context where Meridian may need to terminate for convenience in response to a government direction, this open-ended obligation creates unpredictable financial exposure.

**Recommended Fix:**
Define "expenses" to mean only direct, documented, pre-approved out-of-pocket costs incurred prior to receipt of the termination notice, and exclude any anticipatory or consequential costs.

## BOTTOM LINE

This agreement is **not ready for execution**. It presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains provisions that systematically undermine Meridian's IP ownership, security posture, and regulatory compliance in ways that are not apparent on a surface read. The confidentiality and IP provisions alone are disqualifying in a national security context. Return to Vendor with redlines on all Critical and Significant findings before any further engagement.
