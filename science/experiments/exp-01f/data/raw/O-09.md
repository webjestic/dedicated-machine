**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 is structurally present but functionally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively remove from the cap's protection every category of dispute that is likely to generate material exposure in this engagement — indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees — leaving the cap operative only over a residual category of ordinary breach claims that rarely drive significant litigation value. For a federal contractor engagement where IP ownership, confidentiality of government-related data, and indemnification for third-party IP claims are the primary risk vectors, the cap provides no meaningful ceiling on the disputes that actually matter. Three additional issues require attention before execution: the IP assignment in Section 4.2 is conditioned on full payment, which creates a title cloud on Deliverables during any fee dispute; the indemnification scope in Section 9.1(a) — covering all third-party claims arising from Vendor's *performance of the Services* — is overbroad and could sweep in claims with no nexus to Vendor fault; and the mandatory arbitration venue in Wilmington, Delaware warrants scrutiny if your client's federal contracting obligations impose forum or dispute resolution requirements that would conflict.

---

**Detailed Findings**

---

**Finding 1 — The Liability Cap Is Functionally Illusory**

Section 8.1 establishes a cap equal to fees paid in the prior twelve months. In a professional services engagement, that figure is typically modest relative to the consequences of a serious breach. That is a known and accepted commercial tradeoff. The problem is not the cap amount. The problem is what Section 8.2 removes from it.

Work through the carve-outs against the actual risk profile of this engagement:

- **8.2(a) — Indemnification obligations.** Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party IP claims, for any third-party claim arising from Vendor's performance, and for Vendor's breach. These are not edge cases. A third-party patent or copyright claim against a software deliverable is one of the most common and expensive disputes in this category of engagement. That exposure is now uncapped — in both directions, because the carve-out is mutual.

- **8.2(b) — Confidentiality breach.** In a federal contractor context, the data handled under this agreement may include controlled unclassified information, proprietary government data, or information subject to regulatory handling requirements. A confidentiality breach in that environment does not produce modest damages. It produces regulatory exposure, contract termination risk, and reputational harm that dwarfs twelve months of fees. That exposure is now uncapped.

- **8.2(c) — IP infringement or misappropriation.** This carve-out operates independently of the indemnification carve-out. A direct IP claim between the parties — not just a third-party claim routed through indemnification — is also uncapped. Given that this agreement involves software development and IP assignment, IP disputes are not hypothetical.

- **8.2(d) — Gross negligence or willful misconduct.** This carve-out is standard and generally appropriate. It does, however, interact with the indemnification carve-out in a way that expands uncapped exposure further: a claim characterized as arising from gross negligence that also triggers indemnification obligations is doubly outside the cap.

- **8.2(e) — Fees, expenses, and taxes.** This carve-out benefits Vendor exclusively. Client's payment obligations are uncapped. Vendor's service delivery obligations are capped (to the extent the other carve-outs do not apply). That asymmetry should be named and evaluated, not accepted silently.

What remains inside the cap after these carve-outs are applied: ordinary breach of contract claims that do not involve IP, confidentiality, indemnification, gross negligence, or payment. In practice, that is a narrow and low-value category. The cap is not protecting your client where protection is needed.

**Recommended changes:**
- Negotiate a meaningful aggregate cap that applies to indemnification obligations, or establish a separate, higher sub-cap for indemnification rather than removing it from the cap entirely.
- If the confidentiality carve-out cannot be eliminated, consider whether it can be limited to breaches involving gross negligence or willful misconduct, consistent with 8.2(d), rather than any breach.
- The IP carve-out in 8.2(c) should be examined for scope. If it is intended to address willful misappropriation, say so. As drafted, it captures inadvertent infringement with no cap.
- Remove or narrow 8.2(e) to apply only to undisputed fees, or make the carve-out mutual in a way that provides Client a corresponding benefit.

---

**Finding 2 — IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment**

Section 4.2 states that Vendor assigns IP in the Deliverables to Client "*subject to full payment of all fees.*"

This is a payment-contingent assignment. The practical consequence: if a fee dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy, scope creep, or deliverable quality — title to the Deliverables remains with Vendor until the dispute is resolved. Client may be using, deploying, or delivering to a government customer work product that it does not legally own while the dispute is pending.

In a federal contracting context, this creates a specific problem. If Client has represented to its government customer that it owns or has rights to the Deliverables, a title cloud created by a fee dispute could constitute a representation problem with the government, independent of the commercial dispute with Vendor.

**Recommended changes:**
- Revise to provide that assignment is effective upon delivery and acceptance of the Deliverables, with payment obligations governed separately as a contractual covenant.
- Alternatively, provide that Vendor's remedy for non-payment is suspension of the license or a breach claim, not retention of title in delivered work product.
- At minimum, ensure the agreement addresses what happens to title during a good-faith disputed invoice — the current language does not.

---

**Finding 3 — Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's *performance of the Services.*"

"Arising out of or related to performance" is among the broadest causal formulations in commercial drafting. It does not require Vendor fault. A third party injured by something that happened in the vicinity of Vendor's performance could assert a claim that meets this standard. Vendor's subcontractors are covered under Section 2.3, which means Vendor is indemnifying for subcontractor conduct as well — again, without a fault requirement in 9.1(a).

This matters to your client in a specific way: broad indemnification obligations running to Client are only valuable if Vendor can actually satisfy them. If Vendor is a mid-size professional services firm, uncapped indemnification for all third-party claims arising from performance is an obligation they may not be able to fund in a serious loss scenario. The clause looks favorable to Client on its face but may not be collectible in practice, while simultaneously creating reciprocal exposure under 9.2 that is equally broad.

**Recommended changes:**
- Narrow 9.1(a) to claims arising from Vendor's *negligent or wrongful* performance, or from Vendor's *breach* of this Agreement, rather than performance generally.
- Confirm that Vendor carries adequate professional liability and general liability insurance, and require certificates of insurance as a condition of execution. The indemnification obligation is only as good as the balance sheet behind it.

---

**Finding 4 — Arbitration Venue and Federal Contracting Compatibility**

Section 10.2 requires binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules.

This is a standard commercial arbitration clause and is not facially problematic. However, for a federal contractor engagement, two questions should be confirmed before execution:

First, does Client's prime contract or any applicable federal regulation impose dispute resolution requirements — including requirements to litigate rather than arbitrate certain categories of dispute — that would conflict with this clause? Certain government contract flow-down provisions and regulatory frameworks restrict or prohibit mandatory arbitration for specific claim types.

Second, Wilmington, Delaware as a venue may be inconvenient or inconsistent with Client's operational footprint. If the engagement is performed elsewhere and Client's personnel are located elsewhere, arbitration venue should reflect that reality or include a remote hearing option.

**Recommended action:** Confirm with Client's government contracts counsel that mandatory arbitration is permissible under the applicable prime contract and regulatory framework before executing.
