# Contract Review — Vendor Professional Services Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and contains several well-drafted provisions, but it presents four issues that require resolution before execution is appropriate for a federal contractor engagement. None of the deficiencies are fatal on their face, but two carry meaningful exposure in the national security context and must be corrected rather than accepted as-is. The agreement should not be executed in its current form.

---

## Detailed Findings

### ✅ Provisions That Are Acceptable

**Section 1 – Definitions**
Clean and functional. "Losses" is defined to include attorneys' fees and court costs, which is appropriate. "Deliverables" is scoped to work product created *specifically for Client under a SOW*, which correctly excludes vendor background IP from the assignment obligation. No issues.

**Section 2 – Services and Deliverables**
The professional and workmanlike standard in §2.1 is standard and acceptable. The subcontractor provision in §2.3 is adequate — Vendor retains responsibility for subcontractor performance. However, see the flag below regarding subcontractor security obligations.

**Section 3 – Fees and Payment**
Payment terms are standard. The 1.5%/month late fee is commercially typical. Tax allocation in §3.3 is appropriately structured. No issues.

**Section 4 – Intellectual Property**
The assignment structure in §4.2 is acceptable: assignment is conditioned on full payment, and Vendor Pre-Existing IP is carved out with a perpetual, royalty-free license back to Client under §4.3. This is a standard and defensible construct. The license scope — "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables" — is appropriately narrow. No issues.

**Section 6.1 – Authority Representation**
Standard mutual authority warranty. Acceptable.

**Section 7 – Term and Termination**
The 60-day convenience termination notice period is reasonable. The 30-day cure period for material breach is standard. The survival clause in §7.4 correctly identifies Sections 4, 5, 8, 9, and 10. No issues.

**Section 8 – Limitation of Liability**
The liability cap structure — fees paid in the preceding 12 months — is commercially standard. The carve-outs in §8.2 are well-constructed: they correctly exclude indemnification obligations, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees from the cap. This is a materially better exception structure than most vendor-form agreements present. Acceptable as drafted.

**Section 9.3 – Indemnification Procedure**
The notice, control, and cooperation mechanics are standard. The prohibition on settlements that impose obligations on the indemnified party without consent is appropriate and protective. No issues.

**Sections 10.3–10.8 – Boilerplate**
Standard and acceptable. No issues.

### ⚠️ Issues Requiring Resolution

---

#### Issue 1 — CRITICAL: No Federal Compliance or Security Obligations on Vendor

**Affected Sections:** §2.3, §5, §6 (gap)
**Risk Level:** High

This agreement contains no representations, warranties, or affirmative obligations requiring Vendor to comply with federal regulatory requirements applicable to the engagement. For a government contracting firm operating in the national security space, this is a significant omission.

Depending on the nature of the work, the following may apply and are entirely unaddressed:

- **NIST SP 800-171 / CMMC** — if Vendor will access Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) or operate on systems subject to DFARS 252.204-7012, Vendor must represent compliance and flow down those obligations contractually
- **FAR/DFARS flow-down** — if this engagement supports a federal prime contract, applicable FAR clauses must flow to the vendor; this agreement contains no flow-down mechanism whatsoever
- **Personnel security** — no provision addresses background check requirements, security clearance obligations, or restrictions on foreign nationals accessing sensitive systems or data
- **Incident reporting** — no obligation on Vendor to report security incidents, breaches, or unauthorized access within any defined timeframe

The confidentiality clause in §5, while adequate for a commercial engagement, does not substitute for these obligations. A breach of CUI handling requirements under DFARS 252.204-7012 carries consequences — including mandatory reporting to DoD within 72 hours — that §5 does not contemplate and does not trigger.

**Required Action:** Add a federal compliance section requiring Vendor to (a) identify and comply with all applicable federal regulations governing the engagement, (b) flow down required FAR/DFARS clauses, (c) meet applicable cybersecurity standards for any system or data in scope, (d) impose equivalent obligations on subcontractors, and (e) report security incidents within a defined contractual window.

---

#### Issue 2 — SIGNIFICANT: Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for This Context

**Affected Section:** §5.3
**Risk Level:** Medium-High

The five-year confidentiality survival period is standard in commercial software agreements. It is not adequate here. In a national security or federal services context, sensitive information disclosed under this agreement — including technical architectures, system vulnerabilities, personnel data, or program-related information — may retain sensitivity well beyond five years.

More critically, if the engagement involves CUI, the confidentiality obligations are not merely contractual — they are regulatory. A contractual sunset does not extinguish regulatory obligations, but it creates a gap between what the contract requires and what the law requires, which is a compliance and enforcement problem.

**Required Action:** Extend survival to the longer of (a) ten years or (b) the period required by applicable law or regulation. For any information that constitutes CUI or is subject to classification review, survival should be indefinite or tied to formal declassification or destruction confirmation.

---

#### Issue 3 — MODERATE: Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Asymmetric

**Affected Section:** §9.1(a)
**Risk Level:** Medium

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for third-party claims arising out of or related to "Vendor's performance of the Services" without qualification. Read literally, this sweeps in claims that arise from Client's own direction, specifications, or interference. If Client directs Vendor to implement a particular design, use a specific third-party component, or follow a process that later gives rise to a claim, Vendor could be exposed to indemnification liability for a harm it did not independently cause.

This provision is also asymmetric: Client's indemnification obligation in §9.2 is limited to unauthorized use, breach, and gross negligence/willful misconduct — all of which require some degree of Client fault. Vendor's obligation under §9.1(a) contains no comparable fault limitation.

**Required Action:** Qualify §9.1(a) to exclude claims arising from Client's specifications, directions, or materials, or from Client's modification of Deliverables after delivery. A standard carve-out would read: *"...except to the extent such claims arise from Client's specifications, instructions, or materials provided to Vendor, or Client's modification of the Deliverables."*

---

#### Issue 4 — MODERATE: Arbitration Clause May Conflict with Federal Dispute Resolution Requirements

**Affected Section:** §10.2
**Risk Level:** Medium

The mandatory binding arbitration clause in §10.2 requires all disputes to be resolved through AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware. This is unexceptional in a purely commercial context. In a government contracting context, it is potentially problematic for two reasons.

First, if this agreement supports a federal prime contract, certain disputes — particularly those involving contract interpretation, performance standards, or government data rights — may be subject to mandatory federal forum requirements or the Contract Disputes Act. A blanket arbitration clause that purports to be "final and binding" could conflict with those requirements or create ambiguity about which forum governs.

Second, the clause contains no carve-out for injunctive or emergency relief. In a national security engagement, a breach of confidentiality or IP misappropriation may require immediate injunctive relief from a court of competent jurisdiction. Requiring arbitration as the exclusive remedy in that scenario introduces dangerous delay.

**Required Action:** Add (a) a carve-out preserving either party's right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute, and (b) a provision clarifying that the arbitration clause does not supersede any mandatory federal dispute resolution mechanism applicable to the underlying government contract.

---

**Bottom Line:** This agreement reads as a competently drafted commercial MSA. It is not drafted for a federal contractor engagement. The absence of any federal compliance infrastructure — no flow-down, no CUI handling, no incident reporting, no cybersecurity standards — is the dominant concern and must be corrected before execution. The remaining issues are addressable through targeted redlines. Do not execute in current form.
