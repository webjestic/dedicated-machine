# Contract Review — Software Professional Services MSA

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review — the liability cap exceptions, mutual indemnification, and IP assignment mechanics are all coherent and internally consistent. However, several provisions create unacceptable exposure for a federal contractor operating in the national security space, and two clauses contain material gaps that would not survive a contested claim. The issues identified below are not negotiating preferences; they are conditions precedent to execution.

---

## Issues Requiring Resolution Before Execution

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Be Resolved

---

**1. Arbitration Clause Conflicts with Federal Contractor Obligations (§ 10.2)**

The mandatory AAA arbitration provision is facially incompatible with the regulatory environment in which obotix.one operates. Federal contracts governed by the FAR, DFARS, or agency-specific supplements frequently require disputes to be resolved through the Boards of Contract Appeals, the Court of Federal Claims, or other federal adjudicatory mechanisms. A blanket commercial arbitration clause binding the prime contractor to private dispute resolution creates a structural conflict: if a dispute with this vendor is intertwined with a government claim or flows up to a federal forum, the arbitration clause cannot follow it there — but it may have already consumed the evidentiary record, waived rights, or produced an award that complicates the federal proceeding.

Additionally, mandatory arbitration forecloses injunctive relief in federal court for IP misappropriation or confidentiality breaches — precisely the scenarios where speed and enforceability of a court order matter most. The clause as written does not carve out emergency equitable relief.

**Required change:** Add an explicit carve-out for (a) emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction, and (b) disputes arising from or connected to federal prime contracts, which shall be resolved in accordance with applicable federal law and contract dispute mechanisms. Consider whether arbitration should be replaced entirely with federal court jurisdiction given the operating context.

---

**2. Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for the Engagement Profile (§ 5.3)**

A five-year survival period is standard in commercial software agreements. It is not adequate here. obotix.one operates in the national security and federal services space. Information disclosed under this agreement may include system architectures, security configurations, personnel data, government client information, or other materials whose sensitivity does not expire on a commercial timeline. Five years from termination of a contract that may itself run for years means the confidentiality obligation could lapse while the underlying information remains operationally sensitive or classified-adjacent.

**Required change:** Extend survival to the longer of (a) ten years, or (b) for any information that constitutes government-furnished information, controlled unclassified information (CUI), or information subject to a federal non-disclosure requirement, for so long as such obligation exists under applicable law or the governing federal contract. If the vendor will have access to CUI, a separate CUI handling addendum should be executed in parallel.

---

**3. Subcontractor Flow-Down Is Insufficient for a Cleared or Sensitive Environment (§ 2.3)**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no restrictions on who those subcontractors are, no requirement for prior written approval, no flow-down of security or compliance obligations, and no requirement that subcontractors be subject to background checks, facility clearances, or any federal compliance framework. In a national security context, this is not a gap — it is an open door. A vendor could route sensitive work through an unapproved, unvetted, or foreign-affiliated subcontractor with no contractual basis for objection.

**Required change:** Amend to require (a) prior written consent of Client for any subcontractor engagement, (b) mandatory flow-down of all confidentiality, IP, security, and compliance obligations, (c) Client's right to reject any subcontractor for any reason related to security or federal compliance, and (d) explicit prohibition on subcontractors who are foreign nationals or foreign-owned entities without separate written authorization. If the engagement involves classified work, this section must also reference applicable NISPOM or agency security requirements.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Require Negotiation

---

**4. IP Assignment Is Contingent on Full Payment and Contains an Undefined Carve-Out (§ 4.2)**

The assignment of Deliverables to Client is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This creates a scenario where a payment dispute — including a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — suspends IP ownership and leaves Client operating under no clear license while the dispute is pending. In a federal engagement, Client may have already delivered the Deliverable to the government under a prime contract. If ownership has not transferred due to a payment contingency, Client is potentially in breach of its government IP obligations.

Additionally, the carve-out for "Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein" is undefined in scope. There is no mechanism requiring Vendor to identify Pre-Existing IP at the time of delivery, no schedule or exhibit listing such IP, and no limitation on how broadly Vendor may later claim Pre-Existing IP to claw back assignment. The license granted in § 4.3 is non-exclusive and use-restricted, which may be insufficient if the government requires broader rights under FAR 52.227 or DFARS 252.227.

**Required change:** (a) Remove or modify the payment contingency so that IP assignment is not suspended during a good-faith payment dispute; (b) require Vendor to identify and schedule all Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables at the time of SOW execution and delivery; (c) confirm that the § 4.3 license is sufficient to satisfy any government data rights obligations, or expand it accordingly.

---

**5. Vendor's IP Infringement Warranty Is Qualified to the Point of Unreliability (§ 6.2)**

The representation that Deliverables "will not knowingly infringe" third-party IP rights is not a warranty — it is a knowledge qualifier that effectively limits Vendor's exposure to intentional infringement. Unknowing infringement is the more common scenario in software development (incorporated open-source components with incompatible licenses, third-party libraries, prior art). The "knowingly" qualifier means Vendor can deliver infringing code, disclaim knowledge, and escape both the warranty and the indemnification trigger in § 9.1(b), which is keyed to the same representation.

**Required change:** Remove "knowingly" from § 6.2. The warranty should be an objective representation that Deliverables do not infringe, with Vendor bearing the obligation to conduct reasonable diligence. If Vendor insists on a knowledge qualifier, require a corresponding obligation to conduct and document a pre-delivery IP clearance review.

---

**6. No Federal Regulatory Compliance Representation**

This agreement contains no representation or warranty that Vendor will comply with federal laws and regulations applicable to the engagement. For a vendor supporting a federal contractor, this includes at minimum: export control laws (EAR/ITAR), cybersecurity requirements (NIST SP 800-171, CMMC if applicable), anti-kickback statutes, and applicable FAR/DFARS clauses that flow down to subcontractors. The absence of these representations means there is no contractual basis to terminate for cause or seek indemnification if Vendor's non-compliance triggers a government audit finding, contract default, or debarment proceeding against obotix.one.

**Required change:** Add a section requiring Vendor to represent ongoing compliance with all applicable federal laws and regulations, including export controls, cybersecurity frameworks, and any specific FAR/DFARS clauses identified in the applicable SOW. Include a right to audit and a termination-for-cause trigger tied to material regulatory non-compliance.

---

### 🔵 ADVISORY — Flag for Client Awareness

---

**7. Termination for Convenience Leaves No Transition Obligation**

Section 7.2 permits either party to terminate for convenience on 60 days' notice, but the agreement contains no transition assistance obligation. If Vendor terminates for convenience mid-engagement, Client has no contractual right to require Vendor to assist with knowledge transfer, documentation, or handoff to a successor vendor. In a federal engagement with deliverable milestones or system dependencies, this creates operational risk that is not addressed by the payment provision in § 7.4.

**Advisory:** Consider adding a transition assistance clause requiring Vendor to provide up to 90 days of transition support at standard rates upon any termination, regardless of which party initiates.

---

**8. Governing Law Is Delaware — Confirm Alignment with Prime Contract**

Delaware governing law is standard for commercial agreements and is not objectionable on its face. However, if the underlying prime contract specifies a different governing law or federal jurisdiction, this MSA's choice-of-law provision may create an inconsistency. This should be confirmed against the applicable prime contract before execution.

This agreement should not be executed in its current form. The three critical issues — arbitration structure, confidentiality duration, and subcontractor controls — each independently create exposure that is inconsistent with obotix.one's obligations as a federal contractor. A redlined draft addressing these points should be returned to Vendor's counsel before any SOW is issued under this MSA.
