# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal Review Team*

---

## DECISION: ⚠️ REQUEST CHANGES

**Summary:** This agreement presents itself as a balanced commercial MSA, but contains several provisions that create asymmetric and unacceptable legal risk for Meridian Federal Partners as a federal contractor operating in the national security space. Most critically, the IP assignment structure in Section 4 is conditionally defective, the confidentiality framework is materially inadequate for a federal contracting environment, and the arbitration clause in Section 10.2 may directly conflict with federal regulatory obligations and dispute resolution requirements that cannot be contractually waived. These issues are not cosmetic — they go to the structural integrity of the agreement and must be resolved before execution.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**FINDING 1 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment is Contingent and Potentially Illusory**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables, excluding any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein."*

**The Problem:**
The assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on "full payment of all fees" — with no definition of what constitutes full payment, no mechanism for partial delivery scenarios, and no cure or dispute carve-out. In a multi-SOW engagement, a disputed invoice on *any* SOW could theoretically suspend or void IP assignment across *all* Deliverables, including those already accepted and in operational use.

More critically, the carve-out for "Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein" is undefined in scope. There is no obligation on Vendor to disclose *what* Pre-Existing IP is incorporated, *when*, or to what extent. Meridian could accept a Deliverable, pay in full, and discover post-delivery that the core functional components remain Vendor-owned — with only a narrow use license attached. In a national security context, this is not a theoretical risk. It is a structural vulnerability.

**The license in Section 4.3 does not cure this.** A non-exclusive license to use Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables" does not convey modification rights, does not survive Vendor insolvency in an unambiguous way, and does not address government flowdown requirements under FAR 52.227 series clauses, which may require broader rights to technical data and software.

**Required Fix:**
- Vendor must be obligated to identify and disclose all Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables at the time of delivery (or in the SOW prior to commencement).
- The license in 4.3 must be expanded to include the right to modify, sublicense to the government client, and survive Vendor insolvency.
- The payment condition must include a dispute carve-out — assignment should not be suspended where fees are subject to a bona fide dispute.
- Confirm FAR/DFARS technical data rights flowdown compliance if any Deliverable touches a government prime contract.

---

**FINDING 2 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause May Be Unenforceable and Operationally Dangerous**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Problem:**
The arbitration clause is written with zero carve-outs. In a federal contracting environment, this is a serious structural defect for three independent reasons:

1. **Government Contract Disputes Act (CDA) Conflicts.** If Meridian is a prime or subcontractor on a federal contract, certain disputes — particularly those involving the government's interests — may be subject to mandatory CDA jurisdiction before the Armed Services Board of Contract Appeals (ASBCA), Civilian Board (CBCA), or the Court of Federal Claims. A blanket arbitration clause does not and cannot override federal statutory jurisdiction. Attempting to arbitrate a dispute that is legally required to go through CDA channels creates procedural chaos and potential waiver issues.

2. **No Carve-Out for Injunctive Relief.** There is no emergency injunctive relief carve-out. In the event of a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation — both of which are expressly excluded from the liability cap under Section 8.2 — Meridian would be forced to seek relief through AAA arbitration rather than pursuing immediate injunctive relief in federal court. The time delay alone could be catastrophic in a national security context.

3. **Venue is Vendor-Favorable.** Wilmington, Delaware is DataStream's home jurisdiction. There is no reciprocal or neutral venue analysis here. For a federal contractor whose work may be performed in Washington D.C., Virginia, or Maryland — the primary federal contracting corridor — this is a meaningful operational and cost disadvantage.

**Required Fix:**
- Insert explicit carve-out for disputes subject to federal statutory jurisdiction, including CDA-covered matters.
- Insert explicit carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights for underlying merits.
- Negotiate venue to a neutral location or mutual agreement, or at minimum Washington D.C./Northern Virginia.

---

**FINDING 3 — Section 5.3: Confidentiality Survival Period is Inadequate for Federal Contractor Context**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**
A five-year confidentiality tail is commercially standard. It is *not* adequate for a federal contractor operating in the national security space. Consider what Meridian may be sharing with DataStream: system architecture for federal IT infrastructure, personnel security information, program names, agency relationships, contract structures, and potentially CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information) or information touching classified programs.

The five-year sunset means that competitively sensitive federal program information disclosed in Year 1 of this agreement becomes freely disclosable in Year 6. In long-duration federal programs — many of which run 10, 15, or 20 years — this is not a theoretical gap. It is an active exposure window.

Furthermore, there is **no mention of CUI handling obligations**, no reference to NIST SP 800-171 compliance, no DFARS 252.204-7012 (Safeguarding Covered Defense Information) flowdown, and no acknowledgment of the regulatory confidentiality framework that governs Meridian's actual operating environment. The agreement treats this as a standard commercial NDA. It is not.

**Required Fix:**
- Extend confidentiality survival to the longer of (a) ten (10) years or (b) the duration of any related government contract plus five years, with trade secrets protected indefinitely.
- Add explicit CUI handling provisions or incorporate by reference applicable federal information handling requirements.
- Add a provision requiring Vendor to notify Meridian immediately upon any unauthorized disclosure or suspected breach — the current agreement has no breach notification obligation.
- Confirm whether DFARS 252.204-7012 flowdown is required and incorporate if applicable.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — High Priority Negotiation Points

---

**FINDING 4 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization is Unconditional**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

**The Problem:**
Vendor has blanket authority to subcontract any portion of the Services without Client consent, notice, or approval. In a federal contracting environment, this is a significant gap. Subcontractors may require security clearances, may be subject to foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) restrictions, or may be on excluded parties lists (SAM.gov). Vendor's contractual responsibility for subcontractor *performance* does not cure Meridian's regulatory exposure if a non-compliant subcontractor is introduced into a federal program.

**Required Fix:**
- Require prior written consent for subcontracting, or at minimum advance written notice with a right to object.
- Require Vendor to represent that all subcontractors are SAM.gov eligible, not subject to disqualifying FOCI, and cleared to the extent required by the applicable SOW.
- Require Vendor to flow down all material obligations of this Agreement to subcontractors, including confidentiality, IP assignment, and security requirements.

---

**FINDING 5 — Section 6.3: "As Is" Disclaimer Directly Contradicts Section 6.2 and Creates Interpretive Conflict**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**The Problem:**
Section 6.2 provides an express warranty that Services will be performed professionally and Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties and states Services and Deliverables are provided "as is." These provisions are in direct tension.

The phrase "except as expressly set forth in Section 6.2" attempts to reconcile them, but the "as is" language at the end of 6.3 is a broad disclaimer that a counterparty — or an arbitrator — could read to swallow the Section 6.2 warranties entirely. This is a drafting trap. The "as is" language should not appear in the same clause that purports to preserve express warranties. It creates ambiguity that will be exploited in a dispute.

**Required Fix:**
- Remove the "as is" language from Section 6.3 entirely, or restructure so that the disclaimer applies only to implied warranties not otherwise addressed, with unambiguous preservation of the Section 6.2 express warranties.
- Consider adding a fitness-for-purpose warranty tied to the SOW specifications, particularly for software deliverables with defined acceptance criteria.

---

**FINDING 6 — Section 7.2: Termination for Convenience is Symmetric, But the Payment Obligation in 7.4 is Not**

> *"Client shall pay Vendor for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination."*

**The Problem:**
On its face, this is reasonable. But there is no cap on "expenses incurred," no requirement that expenses be pre-approved or consistent with the SOW budget, and no obligation on Vendor to mitigate costs upon receiving a termination notice. In a 60-day notice window, a Vendor could front-load expenses, accelerate subcontractor engagements, or incur costs that would not otherwise have been authorized — and Client would be obligated to pay them all.

Additionally, there is no termination for convenience fee structure or wind-down cost limitation, which is standard in well-drafted MSAs for engagements of this type.

**Required Fix:**
- Cap termination expenses at amounts pre-approved in the applicable SOW or a reasonable wind-down budget to be agreed within 10 days of termination notice.
- Add a Vendor mitigation obligation upon receipt of termination notice.
- Clarify whether "expenses incurred" includes subcontractor termination fees and, if so, require advance disclosure of any such exposure.

---

**FINDING 7 — Section 9.1: Vendor Indemnification Scope Contains a Structural Overreach**

> *"...arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**The Problem:**
Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Client for *any* third-party claim arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of Services — with no negligence or fault qualifier. Read literally, this could require Vendor to indemnify Client even where Client's own instructions, specifications, or materials contributed to or caused the third-party claim. This will be contested in any real dispute, and the absence of a contributory fault carve-out creates an argument that the indemnity is overbroad and therefore unenforceable.

Paradoxically, this apparent Client-favorable provision could backfire: an arbitrator finding the indemnity unenforceable as written may decline to apply it at all, leaving Client with no indemnity coverage for scenarios where it was clearly warranted.

**Required Fix:**
- Add a contributory fault carve-out: Vendor's indemnity obligation under 9.1(a) should not apply to the extent a claim arises from Client's own negligence, willful misconduct, or failure to follow Vendor's reasonable instructions.
- This actually *strengthens* the provision by making it more likely to be enforced as written.

### 🔵 NOTABLE — Flag for Client Awareness

---

**FINDING 8 — Section 3.2: 1.5% Monthly Interest Rate**

At 1.5% per month, this is an 18% annualized rate. In many states this would approach or exceed usury thresholds for commercial transactions, though Delaware (governing law here) is generally permissive. However, in a federal contracting environment where payment disputes are common and invoice approval cycles can be lengthy, this rate creates meaningful financial exposure on contested invoices. Client should ensure its internal AP processes can reliably meet the 30-day payment window or negotiate a longer payment period (45–60 days) with a lower default rate.

---

**FINDING 9 — Section 10.7: Notice by Email**

> *"All notices under this Agreement shall be in writing and delivered by email with confirmation..."*

Email notice is permitted for all contractual notices, including termination. In a high-stakes federal engagement, relying on email delivery confirmation as the trigger for a 60-day termination clock or a 30-day cure period creates unnecessary risk. Email delivery failures, spam filtering, and personnel transitions can all create genuine ambiguity about when notice was received. Certified mail or overnight courier should be the primary notice mechanism, with email as a supplemental option only.

---

**FINDING 10 — No Force Majeure Provision**

This agreement contains no force majeure clause. In a federal services context — where performance obligations may be tied to government-imposed deadlines, security events, or emergency declarations — the absence of a force majeure provision means neither party has a contractual basis to excuse performance delays caused by circumstances beyond their control. This is a gap that should be filled, particularly given the post-pandemic enforcement landscape around force majeure in government-adjacent contracts.
