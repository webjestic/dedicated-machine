**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs we see at this stage, but it contains several provisions that are unacceptable or require negotiation before execution in a federal contractor context. The issues identified below range from material risk exposure to provisions that are simply incompatible with the regulatory environment in which obotix.one operates. None are fatal on their own, but collectively they prevent approval as written.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### SECTION 2 — SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES

**Issue: Subcontracting (§2.3) — No Prior Approval Requirement**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

**Risk Level: High**

In a national security or federal services engagement, this provision is operationally dangerous. Vendor retaining "responsibility" for subcontractor performance is not a substitute for Client having visibility into and approval over who those subcontractors are. In classified or controlled environments, subcontractors may require security clearances, may be subject to foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) review, or may be restricted under FAR/DFARS flow-down requirements. An unvetted subcontractor introduced mid-performance could trigger compliance violations that Client cannot remediate after the fact.

**Required Change:** Add a provision requiring Client's prior written approval before Vendor engages any subcontractor. At minimum, Vendor should be required to disclose subcontractor identities, confirm they hold any required clearances, and certify compliance with applicable federal regulations. Consider adding a representation that no subcontractor is subject to FOCI concerns or is otherwise disqualified under applicable law.

### SECTION 4 — INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY

**Issue: Payment Condition on IP Assignment (§4.2)**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**Risk Level: Medium-High**

The assignment of IP is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." This creates a scenario where a payment dispute — even a good-faith one over invoice accuracy or scope — could be used by Vendor to argue that IP has not transferred. In a federal engagement, Client may have already delivered the Deliverables to an agency customer under the assumption that it holds the underlying IP. If that assumption is later contested, Client faces a breach of its prime contract obligations. The condition also creates leverage for Vendor in any dispute that is entirely disproportionate to the underlying payment amount.

**Required Change:** Remove the payment condition from the assignment clause, or restructure it so that IP vests upon delivery and Vendor's remedy for non-payment is a breach of contract claim, not a cloud on title. If Vendor insists on retaining some security interest, consider an escrow mechanism rather than a conditional assignment.

**Issue: Vendor Pre-Existing IP License Scope (§4.3)**

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**Risk Level: Medium**

The license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP is limited to use "as incorporated in the Deliverables." This language could be interpreted to prohibit Client from modifying, updating, or maintaining the Deliverables if doing so requires interacting with the underlying Vendor IP in ways that go beyond its current form. In a software context, this is a real operational constraint — patches, integrations, and agency-mandated modifications could all implicate this restriction.

**Required Change:** Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, maintain, and create derivative works of the Deliverables, including any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein, for Client's internal business purposes and in connection with its performance of government contracts. If Vendor objects, negotiate a broader license scope in exchange for a field-of-use restriction (e.g., limited to Client's federal contracting activities).

### SECTION 5 — CONFIDENTIALITY

**Issue: Five-Year Survival Cap (§5.3)**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**Risk Level: High**

A five-year confidentiality tail is standard in commercial agreements. It is inadequate in a national security context. Depending on the nature of the engagement, Confidential Information exchanged under this Agreement may include information that is sensitive indefinitely — program architectures, vulnerability assessments, system configurations, personnel data, or information that is controlled under CUI, ITAR, or EAR frameworks. A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means that a vendor who received sensitive technical information in Year 1 is legally free to disclose it in Year 6.

**Required Change:** For Confidential Information that constitutes government-sensitive, classified, export-controlled, or CUI-designated information, confidentiality obligations should survive indefinitely or for the maximum period permitted by law. At minimum, carve out a category of "Highly Sensitive Confidential Information" with no expiration. The five-year term may remain for ordinary commercial confidential information.

**Issue: No Regulatory Compliance Carve-Out**

The confidentiality section contains no acknowledgment of mandatory disclosure obligations under federal law — for example, mandatory reporting under FAR 52.203-13 (contractor code of business ethics), cybersecurity incident reporting under DFARS 252.204-7012, or whistleblower protections. The current "required by law" exception is implied but not explicit, and in a litigation context, ambiguity here could be used against Client.

**Required Change:** Add an explicit carve-out confirming that nothing in Section 5 prohibits either party from making disclosures required by applicable federal law, regulation, or government contract obligation, provided that the disclosing party provides prompt notice to the other party to the extent permitted by law.

### SECTION 6 — REPRESENTATIONS AND WARRANTIES

**Issue: "As Is" Disclaimer (§6.3) in Tension with Federal Contract Obligations**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**Risk Level: Medium**

The "as is" disclaimer in §6.3 is in direct tension with the warranty in §6.2 that Services will be performed professionally and Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. More importantly, in a federal contracting context, Client may be required under its prime contract to provide certain warranties to the government regarding the software or services it delivers. If Client's upstream obligations include fitness-for-purpose or security warranties, and Client's vendor agreement strips those warranties out, Client is holding a gap it cannot fill.

**Required Change:** Negotiate a minimum warranty period (e.g., 90 days post-delivery) during which Vendor warrants that Deliverables will perform materially in accordance with the applicable SOW specifications. This is a standard ask and most vendors will accept it. Additionally, confirm that the "as is" disclaimer does not apply to the express warranties in §6.2 — the current drafting creates ambiguity about whether §6.3 partially overrides §6.2.

**Issue: No Cybersecurity or Compliance Representation**

**Risk Level: High**

For a federal contractor engagement, the absence of any cybersecurity representation is a significant gap. There is no representation that Vendor complies with NIST SP 800-171, has a System Security Plan (SSP), has undergone any CMMC assessment, or that its software development practices comply with applicable secure development frameworks. If Client is subject to CMMC Level 2 or higher requirements, it cannot flow those requirements down through this agreement as currently drafted.

**Required Change:** Add a representation and warranty from Vendor that it maintains cybersecurity practices consistent with applicable federal requirements (NIST 800-171 at minimum), that it will promptly notify Client of any cybersecurity incident affecting systems used to perform the Services, and that it will comply with all applicable DFARS cybersecurity clauses. If a CMMC certification is required, add a condition precedent or covenant to that effect.

### SECTION 7 — TERM AND TERMINATION

**Issue: Termination for Convenience (§7.2) — No Limitation on Client Obligation**

> *"Upon termination, Client shall pay Vendor for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination."*

**Risk Level: Medium**

This is facially reasonable, but "expenses incurred" is undefined and could be read to include costs Vendor committed to after receiving notice of termination. In a government contracting context, termination for convenience is a standard right, but it comes with defined cost recovery rules under FAR Part 49. This agreement does not incorporate those rules and does not cap or define what "expenses" are recoverable.

**Required Change:** Define "expenses" as costs actually incurred and not cancellable prior to the termination effective date, consistent with commercially reasonable mitigation obligations. Add a duty to mitigate on Vendor's part upon receipt of termination notice. If this agreement supports a federal prime contract, consider incorporating FAR 52.249-1 or equivalent termination for convenience cost principles by reference.

### SECTION 8 — LIMITATION OF LIABILITY

**Issue: Cap Based on Trailing Twelve Months (§8.1)**

> *"...shall not exceed the total fees paid or payable by Client to Vendor in the twelve (12) months immediately preceding the event giving rise to the claim."*

**Risk Level: Medium**

The trailing twelve-month fee cap is a common structure, but it creates a perverse incentive problem in the early stages of an engagement. If a material breach or data incident occurs in Month 2 of a long-term contract, the cap may be based on a very small fee base — potentially far less than the actual harm caused. Conversely, if the engagement is large and the cap is high, it may be appropriate. The issue is that the cap is not calibrated to the actual risk profile of the engagement.

**Required Change:** Negotiate a floor on the liability cap — for example, the greater of (i) fees paid in the trailing twelve months or (ii) a fixed minimum amount agreed in the SOW. Alternatively, negotiate a cap equal to total fees paid under the applicable SOW, which better aligns Vendor's liability exposure with the scope of work that caused the harm.
