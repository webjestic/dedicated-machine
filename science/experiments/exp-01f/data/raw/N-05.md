# Contract Review — Software Professional Services Vendor Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review — the liability cap exceptions, mutual indemnification, and IP assignment mechanics are all coherent and internally consistent. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable or unresolved risk for a federal contractor operating in the national security space, and at least two clauses require substantive revision before execution. The issues identified below range from critical (must fix before signing) to significant (should fix) to advisory (flag for client awareness).

---

## CRITICAL ISSUES — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

### 1. Binding Arbitration Clause Conflicts With Federal Contractor Obligations (§ 10.2)

**The Problem**

Section 10.2 mandates binding arbitration for *any* dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement, administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware. For a federal contractor, this is not a neutral procedural choice — it is a structural conflict.

Federal contracts frequently incorporate dispute resolution mechanisms governed by the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), the FAR, or agency-specific regulations. Where the government is a party or a real party in interest, mandatory private arbitration may be legally impermissible or unenforceable. Even where the government is not a direct party, disputes touching on contract performance, IP ownership, or security incidents may implicate federal jurisdiction that cannot be contractually waived into a private forum.

Additionally, binding arbitration eliminates the right to seek emergency injunctive relief in court — a remedy that may be essential in the event of a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation involving classified or controlled unclassified information (CUI).

**Required Change**

Replace mandatory arbitration with a tiered dispute resolution clause: (1) good-faith negotiation between senior representatives for 30 days; (2) optional mediation; (3) litigation in federal or state courts of competent jurisdiction. If the client insists on retaining arbitration as an option, carve out: (a) disputes involving federal contract performance or government interests; (b) claims for emergency injunctive or equitable relief; and (c) any matter subject to mandatory federal forum selection.

---

### 2. Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for National Security Engagements (§ 5.3)

**The Problem**

Section 5.3 limits confidentiality obligations to five (5) years post-termination. In a standard commercial context, five years is defensible. In a federal contractor engagement involving national security, this is a material underprotection.

Information disclosed under this agreement may include CUI, export-controlled technical data (EAR/ITAR), personally identifiable information (PII) subject to federal privacy statutes, or proprietary government program information. The sensitivity of such information does not expire on a five-year commercial schedule. A vendor who receives details of a classified system architecture, a government customer's operational requirements, or security vulnerability assessments is not absolved of disclosure risk simply because five years have passed.

Furthermore, trade secret protection under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) and applicable state law is not time-limited — a contractual five-year cap may actually *shorten* protections that would otherwise exist at law.

**Required Change**

For information constituting trade secrets, classified information, or information subject to federal regulatory protection (CUI, ITAR, EAR, HIPAA, etc.), confidentiality obligations must survive indefinitely or for so long as the information retains its protected character. The five-year cap should apply only to general business confidential information that does not fall within those categories. Revise § 5.3 accordingly with tiered survival language.

## SIGNIFICANT ISSUES — Should Resolve Before Execution

---

### 3. IP Assignment Is Contingent on Full Payment — Creates Ownership Gap During Performance (§ 4.2)

**The Problem**

Section 4.2 conditions IP assignment on "full payment of all fees." This means that during the entire performance period — and potentially for weeks or months after delivery — Client does not own the Deliverables. Vendor retains title until the last invoice clears.

In a federal contractor context, this creates several compounding risks:

- If Client is required to deliver work product to the government under a prime contract, it may be representing ownership it does not yet legally hold.
- If a payment dispute arises mid-performance, Vendor could assert ownership over partially completed Deliverables that Client has already integrated into a government system or deliverable.
- If Vendor becomes insolvent before full payment is made, the Deliverables may become assets of the bankruptcy estate, subject to trustee claims — even if Client has paid substantially all fees.

**Required Change**

IP assignment should vest upon delivery and acceptance of each Deliverable (or upon substantial completion of the applicable SOW), with Vendor retaining a security interest in assigned IP solely as collateral for unpaid fees — not as a condition precedent to assignment itself. Alternatively, structure as a present assignment with a license-back to Vendor limited to the period of any payment dispute. The current contingent assignment structure is not appropriate for government-facing work product.

---

### 4. Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad as Drafted — Creates Asymmetric Exposure (§ 9.1)

**The Problem**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for any third-party claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" — without any carve-out for claims caused or contributed to by Client's own acts, omissions, or specifications. The "arising out of or related to" standard is among the broadest causal formulations in contract drafting.

Under this language, if a third party sues Client because Client directed Vendor to implement a specific design that infringed a patent, or because Client provided defective specifications that caused a system failure, Vendor could still be obligated to defend and indemnify Client. That result is inequitable and, more practically, may be uninsurable — Vendor's professional liability or E&O carrier may decline coverage for indemnification obligations that extend to Client-caused losses.

An uninsurable indemnification obligation is not a real protection. If Vendor cannot fund the obligation, the clause fails at the moment Client needs it most.

**Required Change**

Add a standard comparative fault carve-out to § 9.1: Vendor's indemnification obligation shall not apply to the extent that the underlying claim arises from or is attributable to (i) Client's negligence, willful misconduct, or breach of this Agreement; (ii) Client's specifications, directions, or materials provided to Vendor; or (iii) Client's modification of the Deliverables after delivery. This is a standard commercial carve-out and should not be controversial.

---

### 5. No Federal Regulatory Compliance Obligations on Vendor (Missing Provision)

**The Problem**

This agreement contains no representations, warranties, or affirmative obligations requiring Vendor to comply with federal regulatory frameworks applicable to the engagement. For a national security or federal services context, this is a significant structural gap. Depending on the nature of the Services, the following may apply:

- **CMMC / DFARS 252.204-7012** — cybersecurity and CUI handling requirements for defense contractors
- **FAR 52.204-21** — basic safeguarding of covered contractor information systems
- **ITAR / EAR** — export control restrictions on technical data and software
- **FedRAMP** — if Vendor is providing cloud-based services or software
- **FISMA** — if Vendor systems process federal information

None of these are addressed. Section 6.2's warranty that Services will be performed "consistent with applicable industry standards" is not a substitute for specific regulatory compliance representations. "Industry standards" in the commercial software market are not the same as federal security requirements, and a court or arbitrator is unlikely to read CMMC compliance into a generic workmanlike performance standard.

**Required Change**

Add a dedicated compliance provision requiring Vendor to: (a) identify and comply with all applicable federal regulatory requirements relevant to the Services; (b) represent that its systems and personnel meet applicable security requirements (CMMC level as appropriate, NIST 800-171, etc.); (c) notify Client promptly of any compliance failure or security incident affecting Client data or Deliverables; and (d) flow down applicable compliance obligations to subcontractors (§ 2.3 currently requires only general compliance, which is insufficient).

## ADVISORY ITEMS — Flag for Client Awareness

---

### 6. Subcontractor Disclosure and Approval (§ 2.3)

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors without Client pre-approval, requiring only that Vendor "remains responsible" for subcontractor performance. In a national security engagement, Client should have the right to approve or reject subcontractors — particularly foreign nationals or entities with foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) — before they are engaged. Recommend adding a prior written approval requirement for subcontractors who will have access to Client systems, data, or Deliverables.

---

### 7. Termination for Convenience — No Fee Cap on Termination Liability (§ 7.2 / § 7.4)

Section 7.4 requires Client to pay for "all Services performed and expenses incurred" through the termination date, but there is no cap on termination liability, no requirement that Vendor mitigate costs upon receiving notice, and no provision addressing pre-purchased materials or committed subcontractor costs. In a long-duration engagement, a 60-day notice period with uncapped expense reimbursement could generate significant unbudgeted liability. Recommend adding a mitigation obligation and a cap on reimbursable termination costs.

---

### 8. "As Is" Disclaimer Partially Contradicts § 6.2 Warranty (§ 6.3)

The disclaimer in § 6.3 states that Services and Deliverables are provided "as is" — but § 6.2 includes an express warranty of professional performance and non-infringement. These provisions are not irreconcilable (the "as is" language is qualified by "except as expressly set forth in Section 6.2"), but the drafting creates interpretive ambiguity that a vendor's counsel could exploit in a dispute. Recommend revising § 6.3 to more precisely identify what is and is not disclaimed, rather than relying on the exception clause to do that work.

---
