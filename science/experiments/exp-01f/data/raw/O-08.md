**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a textbook example of a cap that does not function as one. The three carve-outs in Section 8.2 — indemnification, confidentiality, and IP — are precisely the categories where material exposure concentrates in a software professional services engagement of this type, and all three are removed from the cap entirely, with no alternative ceiling applied. What remains inside the cap is a residual category of ordinary breach and performance disputes that rarely generate the kind of damages that make liability caps commercially meaningful in the first place. The clause as drafted tells the client they have a cap; what they actually have is uncapped exposure in the disputes most likely to arise and a cap that governs the disputes least likely to matter.

---

**Detailed Analysis**

**Section 8.2 Carve-Outs — The Core Problem**

Section 8.2 removes the following from both the consequential damages exclusion and the 12-month fee cap:

- **(a) Indemnification obligations (Section 9)** — Vendor's indemnification scope in Section 9.1 covers performance of Services, IP infringement by Deliverables, breach of Agreement, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. That is not a narrow carve-out. That is the entire operational surface of the engagement. A third-party IP infringement claim against the client arising from Vendor's Deliverables — a realistic scenario in any software development engagement — runs through Section 9.1(b), exits the cap via Section 8.2(a), and carries no ceiling. The same is true for a third-party claim arising from a subcontractor's conduct under Section 9.1(d). The indemnification carve-out does not preserve the cap for indemnification disputes; it eliminates it entirely for the category of claims most likely to involve significant third-party exposure.

- **(b) Confidentiality breaches (Section 5)** — In a federal contractor engagement, Confidential Information will include government data, controlled unclassified information, and client operational data. A confidentiality breach in this context is not a hypothetical tail risk. It is a foreseeable event with regulatory, contractual, and reputational consequences that can dwarf the contract value. Removing confidentiality from the cap without substituting an alternative ceiling — a multiple of fees, a fixed floor, anything — means the client faces unlimited exposure for a vendor data handling failure on a single engagement.

- **(c) IP infringement and misappropriation** — Section 8.2(c) carves out "infringement, misappropriation, or violation of either party's Intellectual Property Rights." Read alongside Section 4's Pre-Existing IP retention and the license-back structure in Section 4.3, this creates a scenario where a dispute over whether Vendor's Pre-Existing IP was properly licensed, or whether the client's use exceeded the scope of the Section 4.3 license, is entirely uncapped. IP boundary disputes are a known failure mode in software services agreements precisely because the line between Pre-Existing IP and Deliverables is rarely clean in practice.

- **(e) Fees, expenses, and taxes (Section 3)** — This carve-out is less alarming in isolation but worth noting: it means the client cannot invoke the cap as a defense against a fee recovery claim, including accrued interest at 1.5% per month. In a disputed engagement where the client withholds payment pending resolution of a performance dispute, this asymmetry matters.

---

**What the Cap Actually Covers**

After applying the Section 8.2 carve-outs, the 12-month fee cap and consequential damages exclusion govern disputes that are:

- Not related to indemnification
- Not related to confidentiality
- Not related to IP
- Not involving gross negligence or willful misconduct
- Not involving unpaid fees

In a software professional services agreement, that residual category is narrow. It captures ordinary service delivery disputes — missed timelines, substandard workmanship, disagreements over SOW scope — where the damages are typically direct and bounded by the work itself. Those are the disputes where a cap is least necessary because the exposure is already naturally limited. The cap provides protection where the client needs it least and no protection where the client needs it most.

---

**Section 9.1 — Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad**

Vendor's indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(a) covers "Vendor's performance of the Services" without limiting to third-party claims arising from negligence or wrongful acts. Read literally, this could sweep in third-party claims arising from Vendor's ordinary performance — a subcontractor injury on client premises, a claim by a third party who interacted with Vendor personnel during the engagement. The standard formulation limits this to claims arising from Vendor's negligent or wrongful acts in performance. The current language is broader than necessary and, combined with the uncapped indemnification carve-out, creates compounding exposure.

---

**Section 6.3 — Warranty Disclaimer Tension**

Section 6.2 includes a representation that Deliverables "will not knowingly infringe" third-party IP rights. Section 6.3 then disclaims all implied warranties including non-infringement. The "knowingly" qualifier in 6.2 is doing significant work here: it limits the warranty to actual knowledge at time of delivery, which means a Vendor that did not conduct a freedom-to-operate review has no warranty exposure even if the Deliverables infringe. In a federal contractor context where the client may be downstream liable for IP issues in deployed software, this is a meaningful gap.

---

**Federal Contractor Context — Elevated Risk Profile**

The agreement is presented as suitable for a federal contractor engagement. That context amplifies several of the risks above:

1. Confidential Information in this engagement likely includes government data subject to federal handling requirements. A vendor confidentiality breach may trigger regulatory obligations and agency notification requirements that generate costs entirely disproportionate to the contract value. An uncapped confidentiality carve-out in this context is not a standard commercial risk allocation — it is an open-ended exposure to consequences the contract value cannot absorb.

2. IP ownership of software Deliverables in a federal contractor context may implicate government license rights under FAR/DFARS. The agreement does not address this. If the client is a prime contractor or subcontractor with flow-down obligations, the IP assignment structure in Section 4 may be insufficient or inconsistent with those obligations.

3. Arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules is standard and not objectionable on its face, but the client should confirm this is consistent with any dispute resolution requirements in the prime contract or agency agreement.

---

**Requested Changes**

1. **Section 8.2 — Apply alternative caps to carve-out categories.** The carve-outs for confidentiality and IP should not be entirely uncapped. Negotiate a separate, higher ceiling for these categories — a multiple of total fees paid under the agreement (e.g., 2x or 3x), or a fixed dollar floor that reflects the realistic exposure profile. Indemnification for third-party IP claims may warrant a separate cap tied to the value of the specific SOW giving rise to the claim.

2. **Section 9.1(a) — Narrow the indemnification trigger.** Limit Vendor's indemnification for Services performance to claims arising from Vendor's negligent or wrongful acts, errors, or omissions. Remove or qualify the current language that covers performance of Services without a fault nexus.

3. **Section 6.2 — Remove the "knowingly" qualifier or require a diligence standard.** Either remove the knowledge limitation on the IP non-infringement representation, or replace it with a representation that Vendor has conducted reasonable diligence and has no basis to believe the Deliverables infringe third-party rights.

4. **Section 5 / Federal Contractor Context — Add data handling and government compliance provisions.** If Confidential Information will include government data or controlled unclassified information, the agreement needs specific data handling obligations, breach notification timelines, and compliance with applicable federal data security requirements. The current confidentiality provision is a standard commercial formulation that is insufficient for this context.

5. **Section 4 — Address government license rights.** If the client has flow-down obligations under a prime contract or agency agreement, the IP provisions need to be reviewed against those requirements and amended as necessary.
