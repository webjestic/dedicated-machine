# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Contract Review Division*

---

## DECISION: ⚠️ REQUEST CHANGES

**Summary:** This agreement contains several provisions that create material, asymmetric risk for Meridian Federal Partners as a federal contractor operating in the national security space. While the agreement presents itself as a balanced commercial MSA, closer examination reveals structural vulnerabilities in the IP assignment mechanics, a confidentiality framework that is dangerously inadequate for classified or CUI-adjacent engagements, and an arbitration clause that may conflict with mandatory federal dispute resolution requirements. These issues are not cosmetic — they go to the core of how exposure is allocated and how federal obligations are preserved. The agreement cannot be executed as written.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL ISSUES — Must Be Resolved Before Execution

---

#### 1. IP Assignment Is Contingent on Full Payment — Creates a Weaponized Leverage Point (§ 4.2)

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**
This is a conditional assignment — not an unconditional one. Title to the Deliverables does not vest in Meridian until *all* fees are paid in full. In a federal contracting context, this is a serious structural defect. Consider the scenario: Meridian delivers a software system to a federal agency under a prime contract. A billing dispute arises — even a minor one. Vendor can now credibly argue that IP ownership never transferred, meaning Meridian may have delivered government work product it does not legally own. This creates downstream liability to the government client, potential False Claims Act exposure, and a breach of Meridian's prime contract obligations.

**What's Missing:**
There is no carve-out for government-mandated deliverables, no provision addressing what happens to IP ownership during a payment dispute, and no mechanism for Meridian to escrow disputed amounts while retaining IP rights.

**Required Fix:**
Assignment should be unconditional upon delivery and acceptance, with Vendor's remedy for non-payment limited to contract damages — not IP recapture. Alternatively, include a clear provision that IP assignment is not affected by good-faith payment disputes.

---

#### 2. Confidentiality Framework Is Wholly Inadequate for Federal/National Security Context (§ 5)

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem — Multiple Layers:**

**(a) Five-Year Survival Is Legally Insufficient:**
In the national security and federal services space, five years is not a standard — it is a floor that routinely falls short. Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), export-controlled data (ITAR/EAR), and information subject to agency-specific handling requirements (e.g., NIST SP 800-171, CMMC) carry protection obligations that are *indefinite* or tied to government classification schedules — not commercial timelines. A five-year sunset on confidentiality could expose Meridian to regulatory liability long after the contractual obligation has lapsed.

**(b) No Government Data Handling Provisions:**
There is zero reference to federal data handling frameworks — no CUI markings, no CMMC compliance obligations, no ITAR/EAR restrictions, no FedRAMP alignment. If Vendor is touching federal systems or data, this agreement provides no mechanism to enforce the regulatory baseline Meridian is almost certainly required to flow down from its prime contract.

**(c) Subcontractor Disclosure Gap (§ 2.3 + § 5.2):**
Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no prior approval requirement from Meridian. Section 5.2 permits disclosure to contractors "bound by confidentiality obligations at least as protective as those in this Agreement." The combination is dangerous: Vendor can unilaterally bring in a subcontractor, share sensitive federal data with them, and Meridian has no visibility, no approval right, and no contractual privity with that subcontractor. In a national security context, this is an unacceptable chain-of-custody gap.

**Required Fixes:**
- Extend confidentiality survival to indefinite duration for government data, CUI, and classified information, with a carve-out for standard commercial information at a longer fixed term (10 years minimum)
- Add explicit CUI/CMMC/ITAR/EAR compliance obligations as Vendor representations and ongoing covenants
- Require Meridian's prior written approval for any subcontractor who will access federal data or systems
- Require Vendor to flow down all applicable federal data handling obligations to approved subcontractors, with Meridian as a third-party beneficiary of those obligations

---

#### 3. Arbitration Clause May Be Unenforceable or Contractually Conflicting in Federal Context (§ 10.2)

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Problem:**
The mandatory arbitration clause is written with no exceptions. In a federal contracting environment, this creates at least two serious issues:

**(a) Conflicts with Government Contract Dispute Mechanisms:**
If this MSA supports performance under a federal prime contract, disputes touching on government work may be subject to the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), agency-specific dispute resolution procedures, or mandatory federal court jurisdiction. A blanket private arbitration clause does not — and cannot — override those federal mechanisms. The clause as written creates a direct conflict with Meridian's upstream obligations.

**(b) No Carve-Out for Injunctive Relief:**
There is no emergency injunctive relief carve-out. In the event of an IP breach, a confidentiality violation, or a data security incident, Meridian would be forced to seek relief through AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware — a process that takes months — rather than seeking immediate injunctive relief in court. This is particularly dangerous given the national security context where speed of response to a breach is critical.

**(c) Venue Is One-Sided:**
Wilmington, Delaware is Vendor's home jurisdiction. There is no analysis of whether this is a neutral forum for Meridian, and no consideration of whether federal courts in Meridian's operating jurisdiction would be more appropriate for disputes with federal nexus.

**Required Fixes:**
- Add explicit carve-out preserving the right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction
- Add a carve-out for disputes arising under or related to federal prime contracts, which shall be resolved in accordance with applicable federal law and contract dispute mechanisms
- Negotiate venue to a neutral jurisdiction or Meridian's principal place of business

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT ISSUES — Material Risk, Require Negotiation

---

#### 4. Vendor IP License Is Dangerously Narrow and Operationally Unworkable (§ 4.3)

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**The Problem:**
The license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP is restricted to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This means Meridian cannot modify the Deliverables, cannot extract components for use in related government work, and cannot adapt the system to meet evolving agency requirements — without potentially infringing Vendor's retained IP. In a federal services context where systems must be updated, patched, integrated, and modified over the life of a contract, this restriction is operationally unworkable and creates a perpetual dependency on Vendor.

**Additional Concern — No Sublicense Right:**
Meridian almost certainly has an obligation to grant the U.S. Government a license to use, modify, and reproduce deliverables under FAR 52.227 clauses. The current license structure does not permit Meridian to sublicense Vendor Pre-Existing IP to the government. This is a potential breach of Meridian's prime contract obligations.

**Required Fix:**
Expand the license to include the right to modify, adapt, integrate, and sublicense (to the U.S. Government and authorized government contractors) Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into the Deliverables, to the extent required by Meridian's federal contract obligations.

---

#### 5. Warranty Disclaimer Creates a "As-Is" Trap for Federal Deliverables (§ 6.3)

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**The Problem:**
The "as-is" disclaimer, read in conjunction with the narrow warranty in Section 6.2, means Vendor provides essentially no functional warranty on the Deliverables. The only warranty is that services will be performed in a "professional manner" and that Deliverables will "not *knowingly* infringe" third-party IP. The word "knowingly" is doing enormous work here — it effectively eliminates strict liability for IP infringement and creates a subjective standard that is nearly impossible to enforce.

More critically, there is no warranty that the Deliverables will:
- Function as specified in the SOW
- Be free of material defects
- Comply with applicable federal security standards (NIST, CMMC, FedRAMP)
- Operate in the intended environment

In a federal services context, Meridian is likely warranting to the government that delivered systems meet specific functional and security requirements. If Vendor delivers non-conforming work product under an "as-is" disclaimer, Meridian absorbs the entire gap.

**Required Fix:**
Add a conformance warranty — Deliverables will materially conform to the specifications in the applicable SOW for a defined warranty period (minimum 90 days post-acceptance). Remove or qualify the "knowingly" qualifier from the IP non-infringement warranty. Add an explicit warranty of compliance with applicable federal security and regulatory standards identified in the SOW.

---

#### 6. Termination for Convenience Creates Uncapped Payment Obligation (§ 7.2 + § 7.4)

> *"Upon termination, Client shall pay Vendor for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination."*

**The Problem:**
Section 7.4 requires Client to pay for all services performed and expenses incurred through termination — but there is no cap, no requirement for Vendor to mitigate costs, and no definition of what constitutes a reimbursable "expense." A Vendor who receives a 60-day termination notice could front-load expenses, accelerate work, and incur costs strategically during the notice period, with Meridian on the hook for all of it.

Additionally, there is no provision for Vendor's obligation to wind down in an orderly fashion, transition work product, or cooperate with a successor vendor — all of which are standard in federal services agreements.

**Required Fix:**
- Cap termination payment at fees for work actually performed and pre-approved expenses incurred prior to notice
- Add Vendor's obligation to mitigate costs upon receipt of termination notice
- Add a transition assistance provision requiring Vendor to cooperate with successor vendors for a defined period post-termination

---

#### 7. Indemnification Scope Is Asymmetric and Exposes Meridian to Uncapped Vendor Claims (§ 9.1)

> *"Vendor shall defend, indemnify, and hold harmless Client...arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**The Problem — Subtle but Important:**
Section 9.1(a) indemnifies Meridian for claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." This is broad on its face, but note what is missing: there is no indemnification for Vendor's *failure* to perform, or for Vendor's delivery of non-conforming work that causes Meridian to breach its prime contract. If Vendor delivers defective software that causes Meridian to miss a government milestone, the resulting government claim against Meridian may not be covered by this indemnification because it arises from Vendor's *non-performance*, not its performance.

**Required Fix:**
Expand Vendor indemnification to expressly cover claims arising from Vendor's failure to perform, delivery of non-conforming Deliverables, and any resulting breach of Meridian's obligations under its federal prime contract.

---

### 🔵 OBSERVATIONS — Lower Risk, Worth Noting

---

#### 8. No Data Security or Breach Notification Obligations
The agreement contains no data security requirements, no incident response obligations, and no breach notification timeline. For a vendor with access to Meridian's systems (§ 2.2), this is a notable gap, particularly given CMMC and state-level breach notification requirements.

#### 9. No Audit Rights
Meridian has no right to audit Vendor's performance, compliance with confidentiality obligations, or subcontractor arrangements. In a federal context, audit rights are standard and may be required by Meridian's prime contract.

#### 10. "Effective Date" Is Undefined
The agreement references an "Effective Date" in Section 7.1 but never defines it. This is a minor drafting deficiency but should be corrected to avoid disputes about when obligations commenced.

#### 11. No Force Majeure Provision
There is no force majeure clause. While this is not unusual in shorter commercial agreements, in a multi-year federal services context, the absence of a force majeure provision — particularly one that addresses government-directed work stoppages — leaves both parties exposed.

## RECOMMENDATION

**Do not execute this agreement in its current form.** Return to Vendor with a marked redline addressing all Critical and Significant issues above. The agreement as presented is not "balanced" as characterized — it systematically advantages Vendor on IP, confidentiality, and dispute resolution while leaving Meridian exposed on the provisions that matter most in a federal contracting context. The federal-nexus gaps in particular suggest this agreement was drafted for a standard commercial engagement and has not been adapted for the national security and government services environment in which Meridian operates.
