# CONTRACT REVIEW — MASTER SERVICES AGREEMENT
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal | Contract Risk Assessment*

---

## Decision: **Request Changes**

## Summary

This agreement presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains several structural traps that create disproportionate and unacceptable risk exposure for Meridian Federal Partners as the Client in a federal contracting environment. The IP assignment conditionality, the uncapped indemnification asymmetry, and the arbitration clause are the most critical issues requiring resolution before execution. Additional concerns around subcontractor vetting, confidentiality scope, and the "as is" warranty disclaimer compound the risk profile in ways that are inconsistent with the federal services context in which this agreement will operate.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on "Full Payment": A Ransom Clause**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

This is the single most dangerous provision in the agreement. The word **"full"** is doing enormous work here and it is not accidental. Under this construction:

- If there is **any disputed invoice** — even a single line item — Vendor can argue that "full payment" has not been made and that IP assignment has not triggered.
- In a federal contracting context, where Deliverables may be incorporated into government systems, program deliverables, or prime contract obligations, a failure of IP assignment could constitute a **breach of Client's obligations to the government** and expose Client to termination for default.
- There is no mechanism defined for what constitutes "full payment" in the event of a good-faith dispute. Vendor holds IP hostage while the dispute resolves — potentially through arbitration that takes months or years.

**Required Fix:** Assignment should be effective upon delivery and acceptance of Deliverables. Payment obligations should be separately enforceable as a financial remedy, not a condition precedent to IP vesting. At minimum, insert an escrow or conditional license bridge that preserves Client's operational rights during any payment dispute.

---

**2. Section 4.3 — Vendor Pre-Existing IP License: Scope Is Fatally Narrow**

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

The license to use Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is restricted to use "as incorporated in the Deliverables." This creates a serious operational trap:

- If Client needs to **modify, update, integrate, or build upon** the Deliverables — which is virtually certain in a software engagement — any such activity that touches the underlying Vendor IP arguably exceeds the license scope.
- There is **no right to sublicense** to the government customer, a prime contractor, or a teaming partner. In federal services, Client almost certainly has a downstream obligation to deliver or license work product to a government end user. This clause may make that legally impossible without Vendor consent.
- The license is **non-exclusive**, meaning Vendor can license the same Pre-Existing IP to a competitor working on the same program.

**Required Fix:** Expand license scope to include the right to use, modify, reproduce, and sublicense Vendor Pre-Existing IP as necessary to fulfill Client's obligations under applicable government contracts. Add a representation that Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated into Deliverables is not subject to third-party restrictions that would impair Client's use.

---

**3. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware: Jurisdictional Trap**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

The breadth of this clause combined with its mandatory, binding nature raises multiple red flags in a federal contracting context:

- **"Any dispute"** with no carve-outs means that even disputes involving **national security equities, classified information, or government-sensitive data** would be routed to a private AAA arbitrator in Delaware. This is legally and operationally untenable.
- Federal contractors frequently have **mandatory dispute resolution mechanisms** imposed by their prime contracts or FAR/DFARS clauses. A blanket arbitration clause may directly conflict with those obligations, creating a situation where Client is in breach of its government contract by complying with this MSA.
- There is **no emergency injunctive relief carve-out**. If Vendor misappropriates Client's IP or discloses classified information, Client cannot seek immediate injunctive relief in court without first arguing that the arbitration clause does not apply — a fight that takes time Client may not have.
- Wilmington, Delaware as the mandatory seat imposes geographic burden on a federal contractor whose operations and witnesses are likely concentrated in the DC/Virginia/Maryland corridor.

**Required Fix:** Carve out the right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction. Add explicit exclusion for disputes involving government-sensitive or classified information. Negotiate venue to Northern Virginia or DC, or at minimum make venue neutral/virtual. Consider whether arbitration is appropriate at all given the federal contracting context.

---

### 🟠 SIGNIFICANT — High Risk, Requires Negotiation

---

**4. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization: Blank Check**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance..."*

This provision grants Vendor **unilateral, unrestricted authority** to subcontract any portion of the Services without Client notice, approval, or vetting. In a national security and federal services context, this is not commercially standard — it is a security and compliance failure waiting to happen:

- There is **no requirement for Client consent** before a subcontractor is engaged.
- There is **no security clearance requirement** for subcontractors who may access Client systems, sensitive data, or government information.
- There is **no restriction on foreign nationals or foreign-owned entities** as subcontractors — a critical gap given FOCI (Foreign Ownership, Control, or Influence) concerns in the federal space.
- Vendor's "responsibility" for subcontractor performance is contractually hollow if the subcontractor causes a security incident that cannot be remediated through financial damages alone.

**Required Fix:** Require prior written Client approval for any subcontractor engagement. Mandate that subcontractors meet the same security, clearance, and compliance requirements as Vendor. Prohibit use of foreign-owned or FOCI-affected subcontractors without explicit Client consent. Require Vendor to flow down all material obligations of this Agreement to subcontractors.

---

**5. Section 8.2(a) — Indemnification Carved Out from Liability Cap: Asymmetric Exposure**

> *"The exclusions and limitations of liability in Section 8.1 shall not apply to: (a) either party's indemnification obligations under Section 9..."*

On its face, carving indemnification out from the liability cap appears protective and is common in commercial agreements. However, the asymmetry here is significant when you read it against the indemnification obligations in Section 9:

- **Vendor's indemnification obligation** under 9.1(a) covers "Vendor's performance of the Services" — this is extraordinarily broad. Every service delivery failure, every subcontractor misstep, every performance dispute could trigger an uncapped indemnification obligation running against Client.
- **Client's indemnification obligation** under 9.2(a) covers "use of the Deliverables in a manner not authorized by this Agreement." Given the narrow IP license in Section 4.3, the definition of "authorized" use is dangerously ambiguous. Client could inadvertently trigger its own uncapped indemnification exposure simply by using the Deliverables in ways that Vendor later characterizes as outside the license scope.
- There is **no proportional fault allocation** in the indemnification obligations. A scenario where both parties contribute to a loss defaults to full indemnification obligations on both sides with no mechanism for apportionment.

**Required Fix:** Narrow Vendor's indemnification trigger in 9.1(a) to claims arising from Vendor's negligence or breach, not merely "performance of the Services." Clarify "authorized use" in 9.2(a) by cross-referencing a defined and expanded license scope. Add a proportional fault/comparative negligence carve-out to both indemnification obligations.

---

**6. Section 5.3 — Five-Year Confidentiality Survival: Inadequate for Federal Context**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

Five years is a standard commercial confidentiality survival period. It is **not adequate** for a federal contracting engagement:

- Classified information, controlled unclassified information (CUI), and other government-sensitive data may carry **perpetual or indefinite protection obligations** under federal law, regulation, and the underlying government contract. A five-year contractual sunset does not override those obligations, but it creates a false impression of expiration that could lead to inadvertent disclosure.
- Trade secrets, by their nature, do not expire. A five-year cap on trade secret confidentiality obligations is inconsistent with the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) and could undermine Client's ability to enforce trade secret protections after year five.
- There is **no carve-out for information subject to longer protection obligations** by law or regulation.

**Required Fix:** Add a carve-out providing that information subject to longer protection obligations under applicable law, regulation, or government contract shall be protected for the duration of such obligation. Consider extending the survival period to the life of the Agreement plus ten years, or making it indefinite for trade secrets and government-sensitive information.

---

### 🟡 NOTABLE — Moderate Risk, Address in Negotiation

---

**7. Section 6.3 — "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts with Section 6.2**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

The "as is" disclaimer in 6.3 is in direct tension with the affirmative warranty in 6.2 that Services will be performed professionally and Deliverables will not knowingly infringe third-party IP. The disclaimer is written broadly enough that Vendor could argue it qualifies or limits the Section 6.2 warranty in ways not intended. The phrase "EXCEPT AS EXPRESSLY SET FORTH IN SECTION 6.2" provides some protection, but the interaction between these provisions is ambiguous and will be litigated if a dispute arises.

Additionally, the warranty in 6.2 is limited to **"knowingly"** infringe — a scienter requirement that dramatically narrows Client's protection. Vendor could deliver infringing Deliverables and escape warranty liability simply by claiming it did not know about the infringement.

**Required Fix:** Remove the scienter qualifier from the non-infringement warranty. Clarify that the "as is" disclaimer does not limit or qualify the affirmative warranties in 6.2. Consider adding a fitness-for-purpose warranty tied to the SOW specifications.

---

**8. Section 7.2 — Termination for Convenience: No Fee Cap on Wind-Down Costs**

> *"Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' written notice..."*

Read together with Section 7.4, which requires Client to pay for "all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination," the termination for convenience clause creates an open-ended financial obligation. There is no cap on expenses incurred, no requirement that expenses be pre-approved or reasonable, and no obligation on Vendor to mitigate costs upon receiving notice of termination.

In a federal contracting context, where termination for convenience is a routine occurrence driven by government direction rather than Client choice, this could result in Client absorbing Vendor wind-down costs that Client cannot recover from the government.

**Required Fix:** Add a reasonableness standard and pre-approval requirement for expenses incurred after notice of termination. Require Vendor to take reasonable steps to mitigate costs upon receipt of termination notice. Cap termination fees at a defined percentage of the remaining SOW value.

---

**9. Section 10.1 — Delaware Governing Law: Potential Conflict with Federal Law**

> *"This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of Delaware..."*

Delaware law is a commercially reasonable choice in many contexts. However, in a federal contracting engagement, there is no acknowledgment that federal law — including the FAR, DFARS, applicable agency supplements, and federal statutes — may preempt or supersede Delaware law on specific issues. The absence of a federal law supremacy clause is a gap that could create interpretive disputes.

**Required Fix:** Add a provision acknowledging that to the extent any provision of this Agreement conflicts with applicable federal law, regulation, or the terms of any applicable government contract, federal law and the government contract shall control.

## Recommendation

**Do not execute in current form.** Return to Vendor with a marked redline addressing all Critical and Significant items as non-negotiable. Notable items should be negotiated concurrently. The agreement as drafted is facially balanced but structurally favors Vendor on the issues that matter most in a federal contracting context — IP control, liability exposure, and dispute resolution. The checklist representation that this is a "balanced commercial agreement suitable for a federal contractor engagement" is not accurate and should not be relied upon.
