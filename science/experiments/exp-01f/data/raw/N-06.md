# Contract Review — Software Professional Services MSA

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review — the liability cap exceptions, mutual indemnification, and IP assignment mechanics are all coherent and internally consistent. However, several provisions create unacceptable or unquantified risk for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses require material revision before execution. The agreement as presented is not approvable in its current form.

---

## Issue Log

### 🔴 Critical — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**Issue 1 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization Is Unconditional**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**Problem:** In a federal contractor context, this is a significant compliance gap. Vendor retains the unilateral right to engage any subcontractor without Client pre-approval. In national security engagements, subcontractors may require security clearance vetting, facility clearance (FCL) verification, or compliance with CMMC, ITAR, EAR, or FAR/DFARS flow-down requirements. Vendor's retained "responsibility" for subcontractor performance is a contractual remedy after the fact — it does not prevent a clearance violation, a data spill, or a regulatory breach from occurring in the first place.

**Required Change:** Add a provision requiring Client's prior written approval before Vendor engages any subcontractor. Include an explicit obligation that Vendor shall flow down all applicable regulatory, security, and compliance requirements to subcontractors, and that subcontractors must meet any security or clearance requirements specified in the applicable SOW.

---

**Issue 2 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for This Engagement**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**Problem:** A five-year survival period is commercially standard but operationally insufficient for a federal contractor engagement involving national security information, controlled unclassified information (CUI), or any data subject to government-imposed handling requirements. Classified information and certain categories of CUI carry indefinite or programmatically defined protection obligations that are not extinguished by a commercial contract's sunset clause. If this agreement governs work touching those categories, a five-year window creates a gap between contractual obligation and regulatory reality.

**Required Change:** Either (a) extend the survival period to the longer of ten (10) years or the period required by applicable law or government regulation, or (b) carve out government-regulated information categories from the time limitation entirely, providing that confidentiality obligations with respect to such information survive indefinitely or as required by the applicable regulatory framework. The SOW template should also include a field to specify applicable information handling requirements by program.

---

**Issue 3 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause Creates Injunctive Relief Risk**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration... The arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding."*

**Problem:** The arbitration clause is written without exception. It does not preserve either party's right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction. In the event of an IP misappropriation, a confidentiality breach, or an unauthorized disclosure of sensitive government information, the inability to immediately seek a temporary restraining order or preliminary injunction from a federal or state court could cause irreparable harm that arbitration timelines cannot address. AAA Commercial Arbitration proceedings routinely take twelve to twenty-four months to reach a final award — that is not a viable remedy for an active data breach.

**Required Change:** Add an explicit carve-out preserving each party's right to seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute. This is standard in well-drafted arbitration clauses and should not be controversial.

### 🟡 Significant — Strongly Recommend Resolution

---

**Issue 4 — Section 4.2 and 4.3: Vendor Pre-Existing IP Incorporated Into Deliverables Is Not Scoped**

> *"Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables, excluding any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein."*
> *"Vendor grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license to use such Vendor Pre-Existing IP solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**Problem:** The agreement assigns Deliverables to Client but silently permits Vendor to embed Pre-Existing IP of undefined scope into those Deliverables, with Client receiving only a narrow use license. There is no mechanism requiring Vendor to disclose what Pre-Existing IP will be incorporated, no requirement to do so before or during SOW execution, and no restriction on Vendor licensing the same Pre-Existing IP to competitors or adverse parties. In a federal engagement, this creates two downstream risks: (1) Client may be unable to modify, maintain, or transfer the Deliverables without Vendor's continued involvement, creating vendor lock-in; and (2) if Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is later subject to an IP dispute, Client's use of the Deliverables may be enjoined.

**Required Change:** Add a disclosure obligation requiring Vendor to identify, in each SOW, any Pre-Existing IP that will be incorporated into Deliverables prior to commencement of work. Consider negotiating for a broader license — at minimum, the license should include the right to modify, sublicense to government end-users, and have the Deliverables maintained by third parties. Evaluate whether a right of first refusal or escrow arrangement for Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is warranted on high-dependency engagements.

---

**Issue 5 — Section 9.1: Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Broad But Lacks a Regulatory Compliance Trigger**

> *"...arising out of or related to: (a) Vendor's performance of the Services..."*

**Problem:** The indemnification trigger in 9.1(a) covers third-party claims arising from Vendor's performance, which is appropriate. However, there is no express indemnification obligation covering Vendor's failure to comply with applicable federal regulations — including but not limited to CMMC requirements, DFARS 252.204-7012 (safeguarding covered defense information), ITAR, or other program-specific compliance obligations. A general performance indemnity may not clearly reach regulatory violations that result in government-imposed penalties, debarment proceedings, or contract termination for cause against Client.

**Required Change:** Add a specific indemnification trigger for Vendor's failure to comply with applicable federal, state, and local laws and regulations, including any regulatory requirements specified in the applicable SOW. This is particularly important where Vendor's non-compliance could expose Client to government enforcement action or loss of contract vehicle.

---

### 🔵 Advisory — Flag for Client Awareness

---

**Issue 6 — Section 8.1: Liability Cap Is Fees-Paid-in-Prior-12-Months**

The liability cap is set at total fees paid or payable in the twelve months preceding the claim event. This is a standard commercial formulation, but it should be evaluated against actual contract economics. On a large, multi-year engagement where fees are front-loaded or where the most significant performance risk occurs late in the engagement cycle, the cap may be substantially lower than the actual exposure at the time of a breach. Clients should model the expected cap value at various points in the contract lifecycle and assess whether it is proportionate to the risk being transferred. If it is not, negotiate for a fixed floor — for example, the greater of fees paid in the prior twelve months or a specified minimum dollar amount.

---

**Issue 7 — Section 10.1: Delaware Governing Law**

Delaware governing law is standard and generally favorable for commercial disputes. No objection to this provision. However, note that in the event of a dispute with federal government nexus — particularly one implicating federal procurement law, the False Claims Act, or national security statutes — federal law will govern those claims regardless of the contractual choice of law. Clients should not assume that Delaware law governs the full range of potential disputes arising from this engagement.
