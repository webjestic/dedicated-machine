**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 is structurally present but functionally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively exempt indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees — which is to say, they exempt every category of dispute that is likely to generate material exposure in a professional services engagement of this type. What remains inside the cap is a narrow residual of ordinary breach claims that, in practice, rarely drive significant litigation; the cap provides meaningful protection only in the disputes least likely to occur. Before execution, the parties should negotiate either a mutual aggregate cap that applies to indemnification obligations (with a separate, higher sublimit rather than a full carve-out), a defined sublimit on confidentiality and IP claims rather than uncapped exposure, and clarification that the Section 3 fee carve-out in 8.2(e) is limited to undisputed amounts — otherwise a vendor can characterize any disputed invoice as outside the cap entirely.

**Extended Analysis**

I want to be precise about what this agreement actually does, because the checklist and the presentation obscure it.

---

**The Cap That Is Not a Cap**

Section 8.1 states a 12-month trailing fees cap on total cumulative liability. On its face, that is a standard commercial limitation. The problem is Section 8.2.

Work through the carve-outs one at a time and ask what disputes they cover in a software professional services engagement:

**8.2(a) — Indemnification (Section 9).** Vendor's indemnification obligation under 9.1 covers: performance of Services, IP infringement by Deliverables, any breach of the Agreement, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. Read that again. *Any breach of the Agreement* triggers indemnification under 9.1(c). That means a vendor breach claim — the most common dispute in a services engagement — can be routed through the indemnification obligation and immediately exits the cap. The cap does not govern it. Client's indemnification under 9.2 is similarly uncapped.

**8.2(b) — Confidentiality.** In a federal contractor engagement, confidentiality breaches are not theoretical. Government-sensitive data, CUI, contractor proprietary information — a confidentiality breach here is the scenario that generates the largest actual damages. Uncapped.

**8.2(c) — IP Rights.** The Deliverables involve software. IP disputes — ownership, infringement, misappropriation of pre-existing IP — are endemic to software services engagements. Uncapped.

**8.2(d) — Gross Negligence / Willful Misconduct.** This carve-out is common and often reasonable in isolation. In combination with the others, it is redundant coverage of the same high-severity scenarios.

**8.2(e) — Fees, Expenses, Taxes.** Client owes all amounts under Section 3 without the cap applying. This is a one-sided carve-out that benefits only Vendor. A vendor can invoice aggressively, dispute any offset or credit, and pursue full recovery outside the cap. The phrase "fees, expenses, or taxes" is not limited to undisputed amounts.

---

**What the Cap Actually Governs**

Strip out everything in 8.2 and ask what disputes remain inside the 12-month cap. The answer is: ordinary, non-breach-based, non-IP, non-confidentiality, non-indemnification disputes that do not involve gross negligence. In a software professional services context, that residual category is nearly empty. The cap is real protection only for disputes that are unlikely to generate material damages in the first place.

---

**Specific Changes to Request**

**1. Indemnification sublimit, not full carve-out.**
The indemnification carve-out in 8.2(a) should be replaced with a separate aggregate sublimit — typically 2x or 3x the contract value, negotiated based on risk profile. Full carve-out for indemnification, combined with the "any breach" trigger in 9.1(c), effectively eliminates the cap for all material breach claims. This is the single most important change.

**2. Confidentiality and IP sublimits.**
Rather than uncapped exposure, negotiate a defined sublimit for confidentiality and IP claims — a multiple of contract value or an agreed fixed amount. Uncapped confidentiality exposure is particularly dangerous in a federal contractor context where regulatory and contractual downstream obligations may compound damages significantly.

**3. Section 3 carve-out limited to undisputed amounts.**
Section 8.2(e) should be revised to apply only to undisputed fees, expenses, and taxes. As drafted, a vendor can characterize any disputed invoice as a Section 3 obligation and pursue it outside the cap. This is a one-sided provision that should not survive negotiation.

**4. Indemnification trigger in 9.1(c) — "breach of this Agreement" is overbroad.**
The combination of 9.1(c) (indemnification for any breach) and 8.2(a) (indemnification is uncapped) means that any breach claim by Client against Vendor is effectively uncapped. Either narrow 9.1(c) to third-party claims arising from breach — not first-party breach claims between the contracting parties — or ensure the indemnification sublimit in point 1 above applies.

**5. Arbitration venue.**
Wilmington, Delaware is Vendor's chosen forum. For a federal contractor engagement, confirm this is acceptable to your client's contracting and compliance teams. Some federal contractor engagements have forum or governing law constraints that may conflict.

---

**Bottom Line**

This agreement was presented as balanced. It is not balanced on liability. The cap in Section 8.1 is real text that provides the appearance of protection. The exceptions in Section 8.2 are the operative commercial reality. Do not execute this agreement in its current form on the assumption that your client's exposure is limited to 12 months of fees. In any dispute that actually matters — IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or fees — it is not.
