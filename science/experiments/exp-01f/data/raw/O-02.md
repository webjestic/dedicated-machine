**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The five carve-outs in Section 8.2 collectively exempt indemnification, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees — which is to say, every category of dispute that actually arises in a software professional services engagement of any consequence. What remains inside the cap is a narrow residual of ordinary contract disputes where neither party acted badly, no IP was touched, no data was exposed, and no fees are in question; that is not where real exposure lives, and the client should not treat the 12-month fee cap as meaningful protection when it will not apply to the claims most likely to be asserted. Two additional issues warrant attention before execution: the IP assignment in Section 4.2 is conditioned on full payment, which means a payment dispute can retroactively cloud title to deliverables already integrated into client systems or submitted under a federal contract — a serious problem in a government contracting context where the client may have flow-down obligations regarding IP ownership; and the indemnification in Section 9.1 is written broadly enough that Vendor's obligation to indemnify for "performance of the Services" and "breach of this Agreement" is itself uncapped under 8.2(a), meaning the client bears uncapped exposure on its own indemnification obligations to Vendor under 9.2 symmetrically, without any practical ceiling. Recommended changes: negotiate either a meaningful aggregate cap that applies to indemnification obligations or a separate, defined cap for indemnification claims; restructure the IP assignment to vest on delivery rather than on payment, with a security interest or payment remedy in lieu of title defeasance; and confirm whether the arbitration venue and Delaware governing law are compatible with any federal contracting requirements or agency flow-down clauses that may govern this engagement.
