# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Contract Review Division*

---

## DECISION: ⚠️ REQUEST CHANGES

**Summary:** This agreement presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains several structural vulnerabilities that create disproportionate exposure for Meridian Federal Partners in a federal contracting context. The most critical issues are a subcontractor engagement model with no security or compliance passthrough requirements, a confidentiality framework that is dangerously inadequate for national security-adjacent work, and an IP assignment conditioned on full payment that could leave the client without enforceable ownership rights mid-engagement. These are not cosmetic deficiencies — they are provisions that could compromise federal program integrity and expose the client to downstream liability with its government customers.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

#### 1. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Authorization: No Compliance Passthrough

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**The Problem:**
This clause is a trap dressed as a safeguard. "Responsible for compliance with this Agreement" is not the same as requiring subcontractors to be *bound by* equivalent obligations. There is no requirement that subcontractors:

- Execute written agreements with equivalent confidentiality terms
- Comply with federal security requirements (NIST 800-171, CMMC, FAR/DFARS clauses)
- Be pre-approved by Client
- Be U.S. persons or cleared personnel
- Be prohibited from further sub-tier delegation

In a federal services context, this is not a minor gap. If Vendor engages a foreign national subcontractor or a firm with no security posture, Meridian could face program-level consequences with its government customer — consequences that this agreement does nothing to prevent and that Vendor's indemnification obligation may not fully remedy after the fact.

**Required Fix:**
Subcontractors must be subject to prior written Client approval. Vendor must be required to flow down all material obligations — confidentiality, IP assignment, security compliance, and any applicable federal regulatory requirements — through written agreements with each subcontractor. Client must have audit rights over subcontractor compliance.

---

#### 2. Section 5 — Confidentiality: Structurally Inadequate for Federal Work

**5-Year Survival Cap (§5.3):**
> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

This is the single most dangerous provision in the agreement for a national security-adjacent engagement. Five years is a commercial standard. It is not a federal standard. Classified information, CUI (Controlled Unclassified Information), and sensitive program data do not become unprotected after five years by operation of law or regulation — but this contract would allow Vendor to treat them as such.

If Meridian is handling information that touches federal programs, a five-year sunset on confidentiality is potentially a federal compliance violation waiting to happen, and it is certainly a provision that Meridian's government customer would find unacceptable if they ever reviewed the supply chain agreement.

**Required Fix:** Confidentiality obligations must survive indefinitely for any information that constitutes CUI, classified information, or information subject to federal regulatory protection. At minimum, the survival period must be extended to the life of the relevant federal program plus a defined tail period, with a carve-out making clear that no sunset applies to federally regulated information categories.

**No Security Standards Reference:**
The confidentiality section contains zero reference to NIST 800-171, CMMC, FedRAMP, ITAR, EAR, or any other applicable federal information security framework. For a vendor touching federal contractor systems or data, this is a structural omission. The confidentiality obligations as written are purely contractual — they create no obligation to implement any technical or administrative safeguards.

**Required Fix:** Add an explicit obligation requiring Vendor to implement and maintain information security controls consistent with applicable federal standards, identified by reference to the specific regulatory frameworks governing the engagement.

**No Government Disclosure Carve-Out:**
Section 5.2 permits disclosure only to employees and contractors with need-to-know. There is no carve-out permitting Meridian to disclose Vendor information to its government customer as required by law, audit, or program oversight. This could put Meridian in breach of this MSA simply by complying with its federal obligations.

**Required Fix:** Add an explicit carve-out permitting disclosure to government customers, oversight bodies, and inspectors general as required by law or contract, with reasonable advance notice to Vendor where legally permissible.

---

#### 3. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**
This is a conditional present-tense assignment. Until every dollar is paid, the assignment has not legally occurred. In a multi-SOW engagement where a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith dispute over invoice accuracy — Vendor could argue that IP ownership has not transferred. This creates a scenario where:

- Client has integrated Deliverables into a federal program
- A billing dispute emerges on a later SOW
- Vendor asserts it retains ownership of previously delivered work product
- Client's government program is now operating on IP it does not legally own

This is not a theoretical risk. Courts have enforced conditional IP assignments against buyers in exactly this posture.

**Required Fix:** IP assignment should be unconditional upon delivery and acceptance. Payment disputes should be addressed through separate breach and remedy mechanisms, not through a reversionary IP interest. At minimum, add an escrow or license-back provision ensuring Client has uninterrupted rights to use Deliverables during any payment dispute.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Requires Negotiation

---

#### 4. Section 4.3 — Vendor Pre-Existing IP License: Scope Is Too Narrow

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**The Problem:**
The license to use Vendor Pre-Existing IP is limited to use "as incorporated in the Deliverables." This means Client cannot extract, adapt, or build upon the underlying Vendor IP even where doing so is necessary to maintain, modify, or extend the Deliverables for its federal program. In practice, this could make the Deliverables functionally unusable for any purpose beyond their original form.

Consider a scenario where a federal program requires modification of a Deliverable to meet a new security requirement. If that modification requires touching Vendor Pre-Existing IP, Client may be in breach of the license.

**Required Fix:** Expand the license scope to include the right to use, modify, maintain, and create derivative works of Vendor Pre-Existing IP to the extent necessary to exercise full rights in the Deliverables. Add a government purpose license if federal flow-down obligations require it.

---

#### 5. Section 8.2(e) — Asymmetric Carve-Out Favoring Vendor

> *"(e) amounts owed by Client for fees, expenses, or taxes under Section 3 of this Agreement."*

**The Problem:**
The liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are presented as applying to "either party," but subsection (e) is unilaterally Vendor-favorable. It removes the liability cap from Client's payment obligations to Vendor, meaning Vendor can recover unlimited fees from Client. There is no reciprocal carve-out protecting Client's financial exposure in the other direction — for example, for Vendor's failure to deliver or for overbilling.

This asymmetry is subtle but deliberate. It ensures that Vendor's revenue stream is fully protected while Client's damages for Vendor non-performance remain capped.

**Required Fix:** Either remove subsection (e) or add a reciprocal carve-out for Client's damages arising from Vendor's material failure to deliver Services or Deliverables as specified in an SOW.

---

#### 6. Section 7.2 — Termination for Convenience: No Fee Cap on Wind-Down Costs

> *"Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' written notice..."*

**The Problem:**
Section 7.4 requires Client to pay for "all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination." In a convenience termination scenario, this is reasonable — but there is no cap on "expenses incurred," no requirement that expenses be pre-approved or reasonable, and no obligation on Vendor to mitigate costs during the notice period.

A Vendor could front-load expenses during the 60-day notice period — purchasing equipment, engaging subcontractors, prepaying licenses — and present Client with a substantial bill that Client has no contractual basis to dispute.

**Required Fix:** Add a requirement that Vendor mitigate costs upon receipt of a termination notice and that only pre-approved or reasonably necessary expenses incurred prior to notice are recoverable. Define "expenses" with specificity or cap wind-down costs.

---

#### 7. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Arbitration: No Carve-Out for Injunctive Relief

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Problem:**
The arbitration clause is absolute. There is no carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in court. In a federal contracting context, where a Vendor breach — particularly a confidentiality breach or IP misappropriation — could have immediate, irreversible consequences for a government program, the inability to seek a temporary restraining order or preliminary injunction from a court of competent jurisdiction is a significant operational risk.

Arbitration timelines, even expedited, are measured in weeks to months. Program damage can occur in hours.

**Required Fix:** Add a standard carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate the underlying dispute.

---

### 🔵 ADVISORY — Flag for Client Awareness

---

#### 8. Section 6.2 — Warranty Qualified by "Knowingly": Enforcement Gap

> *"...the Deliverables, as delivered, will not knowingly infringe the Intellectual Property Rights of any third party."*

The word "knowingly" significantly weakens this warranty. It converts an objective IP non-infringement commitment into a subjective knowledge standard. Vendor could deliver infringing work product, disclaim knowledge of the infringement, and avoid warranty liability entirely. In a federal program context where IP chain-of-title matters, this is a meaningful limitation.

**Recommendation:** Remove "knowingly" or supplement with an obligation requiring Vendor to conduct reasonable IP clearance diligence on Deliverables prior to delivery.

---

#### 9. No Federal Flow-Down Provisions

This agreement contains zero reference to FAR, DFARS, or any federal regulatory flow-down requirements. For a federal contractor engagement, this is a structural omission. Depending on the nature of the prime contract, Meridian may be obligated to flow down specific clauses to its vendors — including data rights clauses, security requirements, audit rights, and small business subcontracting obligations.

**Recommendation:** Add a federal compliance addendum or flow-down clause identifying the specific FAR/DFARS provisions applicable to this engagement and requiring Vendor to comply with and flow down such provisions to its subcontractors.

---

#### 10. Section 5.1 — No Definition of CUI or Classified Information

The confidentiality definition relies on a "reasonably should be understood to be confidential" standard. This is adequate for commercial agreements but is insufficient for federal work where specific information categories — CUI, FOUO, SBU, classified — carry defined handling requirements that exist independently of whether a party "designates" them as confidential.

**Recommendation:** Add definitions for federally regulated information categories and specify that such information is automatically treated as Confidential Information subject to the highest applicable handling standard, regardless of marking or designation.
