# Contract Review — Vendor Professional Services Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

---

## Executive Summary

This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review. The liability framework, indemnification carve-outs, and IP assignment mechanics are coherent and internally consistent. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk for a federal contractor operating in the national security space, and two clauses contain structural defects that would undermine their stated protections at the moment of enforcement. The agreement cannot be executed as written.

---

## Issue Log

### 🔴 Critical — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**Issue 1 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Authorization Is Unconditional**

*Risk: Regulatory / Security Clearance Exposure*

The agreement permits Vendor to engage subcontractors without Client pre-approval. In a national security or federal contracting context, this is not a commercial inconvenience — it is a potential violation of FAR/DFARS requirements, facility clearance obligations, and agency-specific access controls. A subcontractor who has not been vetted, cleared, or approved by the relevant agency could access controlled unclassified information (CUI), classified systems, or sensitive program data before Client is even aware of their involvement.

The current language — "provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance" — addresses financial accountability but does nothing to address security exposure. Vendor's indemnification obligation does not remediate a security incident after the fact.

**Required Change:** Subcontractor engagement must require prior written approval from Client. At minimum, the clause must prohibit subcontractor access to any Client systems, data, or facilities without explicit written authorization, and must require subcontractors to execute agreements containing security and confidentiality obligations no less protective than those in this MSA. Consider adding a flow-down clause requiring compliance with applicable federal security requirements.

---

**Issue 2 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival Is Inadequate for This Engagement**

*Risk: Exposure of Sensitive Federal Information Post-Termination*

The five-year survival period on confidentiality obligations is standard in commercial agreements. It is not appropriate here. Federal contractor engagements routinely involve CUI, sensitive government information, proprietary program data, and information subject to agency-specific handling requirements that carry indefinite or statutorily defined protection periods. A five-year clock that begins running on the termination date means Vendor's confidentiality obligations could expire while Client remains bound by federal law to protect the same information indefinitely.

This creates an asymmetric exposure: Client continues to bear legal obligations; Vendor does not.

**Required Change:** Confidentiality survival should be extended to the longer of: (i) ten years, or (ii) the period required by applicable law, regulation, or government contract obligation. For any information that constitutes a trade secret under applicable law, obligations should survive indefinitely. Consider adding explicit language addressing CUI handling requirements if applicable to the engagement.

---

**Issue 3 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Clause Creates Structural Conflict with Federal Dispute Resolution Obligations**

*Risk: Jurisdictional / Regulatory Conflict*

Binding arbitration administered by the AAA in Wilmington, Delaware is standard in commercial agreements. It is problematic here. Federal contracts and subcontracts are frequently subject to mandatory dispute resolution mechanisms under the Contract Disputes Act (CDA), agency-specific protest procedures, or other federal statutory frameworks that cannot be contractually displaced. If this MSA governs work performed under or in support of a prime contract with a federal agency, a mandatory arbitration clause may be unenforceable as applied to disputes touching those obligations — or worse, may create a procedural conflict that delays resolution and increases cost.

Additionally, binding arbitration eliminates the right to seek emergency injunctive relief through a court of competent jurisdiction. Given the IP assignment provisions and the sensitivity of the information involved, the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief in a federal or state court is a meaningful loss of protection.

**Required Change:** Carve out from mandatory arbitration: (i) any dispute subject to federal statutory dispute resolution requirements; and (ii) claims seeking emergency injunctive or equitable relief to protect IP or confidential information. Either party should retain the right to seek provisional remedies in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights for the underlying dispute.

### 🟡 Significant — Strongly Recommended Before Execution

---

**Issue 4 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment Is Conditioned on Full Payment; No Escrow or Partial Assignment Mechanism**

*Risk: Operational Continuity / Program Delivery*

The assignment of Deliverables is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." Until that condition is satisfied, title remains with Vendor. In a multi-SOW engagement with rolling invoices, disputed charges, or a payment dispute that arises mid-program, Client could find itself operating under a license it does not actually hold — or no license at all — for work product already integrated into a federal deliverable.

This is not a theoretical risk. Payment disputes happen. The condition-on-full-payment structure means a billing disagreement could retroactively affect IP ownership at the worst possible moment.

**Required Change:** IP assignment should vest on delivery and acceptance of each Deliverable, with a security interest or license-back mechanism protecting Vendor's economic interest pending payment. Alternatively, assignment should be unconditional with a separately enforceable payment obligation. At minimum, add a provision ensuring Client retains a license to use and operate Deliverables during any bona fide payment dispute.

---

**Issue 5 — Section 4.3: Vendor Pre-Existing IP License Is Narrowly Scoped and May Be Inadequate**

*Risk: Operational Lock-In / Downstream Deliverability*

The license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This restriction may prevent Client from maintaining, modifying, updating, or integrating the Deliverables into broader systems — all of which are routine activities in a federal IT or software services engagement. If Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is embedded in a core deliverable and Client needs to patch, update, or extend that deliverable, the current license language may not authorize it.

The agreement also contains no representation that Vendor has the right to grant this license — meaning if Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is itself subject to a third-party license with restrictions, Client's license could be invalid.

**Required Change:** Expand the license scope to include the right to use, modify, maintain, and create derivative works of the Deliverables incorporating Vendor Pre-Existing IP. Add a representation that Vendor has full authority to grant the license in Section 4.3 and that doing so does not violate any third-party agreement.

---

**Issue 6 — Section 9.1: Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Broad but Contains a Structural Gap**

*Risk: Indemnification May Not Attach to Subcontractor-Caused Incidents*

Section 9.1(d) limits Vendor's indemnification for subcontractor conduct to claims arising from "gross negligence or willful misconduct." Section 9.1(a) covers claims arising from "Vendor's performance of the Services" — which may or may not be interpreted to encompass subcontractor acts depending on the jurisdiction and arbitrator.

If a subcontractor causes a data breach, security incident, or regulatory violation through ordinary negligence — not gross negligence — Client may find itself without an indemnification remedy against Vendor for the resulting third-party claims. Given the subcontractor authorization issue identified above, this gap is compounded.

**Required Change:** Clarify that Vendor's indemnification obligations in Section 9.1 expressly extend to acts and omissions of Vendor's subcontractors to the same extent as Vendor's own acts and omissions. Remove the gross negligence/willful misconduct qualifier as the threshold for subcontractor indemnification, or expand the trigger to include ordinary negligence.

---

**Issue 7 — Section 7.2: Sixty-Day Convenience Termination Is Symmetrical but Asymmetrically Harmful**

*Risk: Program Continuity*

A sixty-day convenience termination right is standard. In a federal contractor context, it warrants scrutiny. Vendor's ability to terminate for convenience on sixty days' notice could leave Client without a critical service provider mid-performance on a government contract, potentially triggering default exposure under the prime contract. Client's termination-for-convenience right is less operationally significant — Client controls whether to continue the engagement.

**Recommended Change:** Consider restricting Vendor's termination-for-convenience right during active SOW performance periods, or requiring Vendor to complete all in-progress SOW work before termination becomes effective. At minimum, add a transition assistance obligation requiring Vendor to cooperate with knowledge transfer and successor vendor onboarding for a defined period following notice of termination.

---

### 🔵 Clarification Required

---

**Issue 8 — Governing Law (Section 10.1) vs. Federal Contract Obligations**

Delaware law governs this agreement. That is commercially standard. However, the agreement contains no acknowledgment of, or subordination to, applicable federal law, FAR/DFARS requirements, or agency-specific regulations that may govern the underlying work. If this MSA is used as the vehicle for work performed under a federal prime contract or subcontract, the absence of a federal law supremacy clause or flow-down provision creates ambiguity about which framework controls in a conflict.

**Clarification Needed:** Confirm whether this MSA will govern work performed under or in support of a federal prime contract. If so, add a clause establishing that applicable federal law, regulations, and prime contract flow-down requirements take precedence over this Agreement to the extent of any conflict.

---

**Issue 9 — No Data Security or Incident Response Obligations**

The agreement addresses confidentiality but contains no data security standards, breach notification obligations, or incident response requirements. For a vendor with access to Client systems and data in a national security context, this is a material omission. There is no obligation to maintain any particular security posture, no requirement to notify Client of a breach within any defined timeframe, and no obligation to cooperate with incident response.

**Clarification Needed:** Determine whether a Data Security Addendum or similar exhibit is intended to accompany this MSA. If not, data security and breach notification obligations must be incorporated into the agreement before execution.
