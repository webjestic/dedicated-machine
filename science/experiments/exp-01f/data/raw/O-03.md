**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 presents a 12-month fee cap as meaningful bilateral protection, but Section 8.2 carves out indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and all unpaid fees — which together constitute the primary categories of dispute that arise in software professional services engagements. What remains inside the cap is the residual: routine service delivery disputes where damages are typically modest anyway. For the client, this structure is particularly asymmetric in practice: Vendor's indemnification obligation under 9.1 is uncapped and broad (covering all third-party claims arising from Services performance, IP infringement, and any breach), while the fee payment obligation under Section 3 — which runs only one direction — is also fully carved out of the cap under 8.2(e), meaning Client faces unlimited exposure on payment while Vendor's core performance risk flows through the uncapped indemnification channel. Before execution, the following should be negotiated or clarified:

---

**1. The cap is structurally hollow for this client.**

The 12-month fee cap sounds protective. In a federal contractor engagement, 12 months of professional services fees may be a meaningful number. But trace what it actually governs: direct damages from ordinary breach of the services delivery standard in Section 6.2, where the warranty is already narrowed to "professional manner consistent with industry standards" and expressly excludes implied warranties including fitness for purpose. The disputes that generate real exposure — IP infringement allegations (8.2(c)), confidentiality breaches involving federal data (8.2(b)), indemnification obligations triggered by third-party claims (8.2(a)), and gross negligence in systems work (8.2(d)) — are all outside the cap. The cap governs the category of dispute least likely to produce catastrophic loss.

**Requested change:** Negotiate a separate aggregate cap on indemnification obligations — particularly Vendor's indemnification under 9.1 — expressed as a multiple of contract value (e.g., 2x or 3x total fees paid). Uncapped indemnification in a federal contractor context, where third-party claims can involve government agencies, is not a standard commercial risk allocation.

---

**2. Section 8.2(e) is a one-way carve-out.**

The exception for "amounts owed by Client for fees, expenses, or taxes under Section 3" removes the cap entirely from Client's payment obligations. This means if Client disputes a fee and withholds payment, Vendor can pursue the full amount plus 1.5% monthly interest with no liability ceiling. That is appropriate for undisputed invoices. It is not appropriate as written, because there is no carve-out limiting this exception to undisputed amounts. A good-faith fee dispute should not expose Client to uncapped liability simply because the payment obligation is categorically excluded from the cap.

**Requested change:** Amend 8.2(e) to apply only to undisputed fees, or add a dispute resolution mechanism for invoiced amounts that suspends the interest accrual and cap exclusion during a bona fide dispute period.

---

**3. The IP assignment in Section 4.2 contains a condition that creates execution risk.**

Assignment of Deliverables is conditioned on "full payment of all fees." In a multi-SOW engagement, this means IP ownership is contingent and potentially revocable if any payment dispute arises — including a dispute over a single invoice. For a federal contractor client who may be incorporating Deliverables into government-facing systems, operating without clear IP title during a payment dispute is a compliance and contracting risk, not just a commercial one.

**Requested change:** Either remove the payment condition from the assignment and address non-payment through other remedies (suspension of services, termination), or add a provision clarifying that IP assignment is not retroactively voided by subsequent payment disputes on later invoices.

---

**4. Subcontractor provision in Section 2.3 is unacceptable for a federal contractor engagement.**

Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors with no restrictions on who, what access they receive, or whether they hold required clearances or comply with federal contractor obligations. Vendor "remains responsible" for performance, but that is a remedy after the fact — it does not prevent a subcontractor without appropriate clearance from accessing controlled data, which in a federal context can trigger regulatory consequences that no indemnification clause can fully remediate.

**Requested change:** Add a provision requiring Client's prior written approval for any subcontractor who will have access to Client systems, data, or facilities; requiring subcontractors to be bound by confidentiality obligations at least as protective as Section 5; and, if applicable, requiring compliance with relevant federal contractor security requirements (e.g., NIST 800-171, CMMC, or applicable FAR/DFARS clauses depending on the engagement).

---

**5. Confidentiality survival is five years, but the risk profile may require longer.**

Section 5.3 sets a five-year post-termination survival period. For a federal contractor engagement involving proprietary systems architecture, personnel data, or information that could qualify as controlled unclassified information (CUI), five years may be insufficient. Trade secret protection under the Defend Trade Secrets Act does not have a fixed expiration, but a contractual five-year window can create ambiguity about obligations after that period lapses.

**Requested change:** Either extend the survival period to the life of the confidential information's sensitivity (indefinite for trade secrets and CUI), or add a carve-out providing that obligations with respect to trade secrets and any information subject to federal data handling requirements survive indefinitely.

---

**Bottom line:** This agreement is not balanced as presented. The liability cap is real on paper and largely illusory in practice. The asymmetries in the carve-outs, the IP assignment condition, and the absence of subcontractor controls are each individually addressable — but together, in a federal contractor context, they represent a risk profile that does not match the "standard balanced commercial agreement" characterization in the submission. Do not execute without the changes above.
