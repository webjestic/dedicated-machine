# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Contract Review Division*

---

## DECISION: ⚠️ REQUEST CHANGES

**Summary:** This agreement presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains several provisions that create asymmetric and unacceptable risk exposure for Meridian Federal Partners as a federal contractor operating in the national security space. The IP assignment structure is conditionally defective, the confidentiality term is dangerously inadequate for classified or sensitive government work, and the mandatory arbitration clause in Delaware creates a jurisdictional conflict that could impair Meridian's ability to fulfill federal obligations. These are not cosmetic issues — they go to the structural integrity of the agreement and must be resolved before execution.

---

## DETAILED FINDINGS

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**FINDING 1 — Section 4.2: IP Assignment is Contingent and Defeasible**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables..."*

**The Problem:**
This is a conditional present-tense assignment. The word "subject to" creates a condition precedent — meaning IP title does not fully vest in Meridian until *all* fees are paid in full. In a federal contracting context, this is a structural trap. If a payment dispute arises — even a good-faith one — Vendor could argue that IP ownership never transferred, leaving Meridian potentially delivering work product to a federal agency under a contract where it does not actually hold title to the underlying deliverables.

This creates downstream liability exposure under FAR clauses governing IP, data rights, and government use licenses. If Meridian has represented to its federal customer that it owns or controls the deliverables, a disputed payment could retroactively invalidate that representation.

**What's Missing:**
There is no carve-out protecting previously-transferred deliverables from clawback. There is no mechanism for Meridian to cure a disputed payment while preserving IP rights. There is no escrow or interim license bridge.

**Required Fix:**
Assignment should be unconditional upon delivery, with payment obligations addressed as a separate breach remedy. Alternatively, add explicit language that partial non-payment does not operate to rescind or void assignment of previously accepted deliverables.

---

**FINDING 2 — Section 4.3: "Vendor Pre-Existing IP" is Undefined and Unbounded**

> *"...Vendor grants Client a non-exclusive, perpetual, royalty-free license to use such Vendor Pre-Existing IP solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**The Problem:**
The definition of "Pre-Existing IP" in Section 4.1 is dangerously broad — it includes anything developed *"prior to or independently of"* this Agreement. The phrase "independently of" is the trap. A sophisticated vendor could argue that any tool, framework, methodology, or module developed on a parallel track — even during the engagement — qualifies as Pre-Existing IP, thereby carving it out of the assignment in Section 4.2.

The license granted in 4.3 is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." This means Meridian cannot extract, modify, or reuse the underlying Vendor IP components even if the deliverable becomes mission-critical infrastructure. In a federal services context where systems must be maintained, modified, or transitioned to successor vendors, this restriction could be operationally catastrophic.

**What's Missing:**
- No definition of what constitutes "independently developed" with a temporal or subject-matter boundary
- No right to sublicense to the federal government client (required under many FAR/DFARS data rights clauses)
- No modification rights within the licensed scope
- No transition assistance or source code escrow provision

**Required Fix:**
Define Pre-Existing IP with specificity. Expand the license in 4.3 to include the right to modify, maintain, and sublicense to the government customer to the extent required by applicable federal contract obligations. Add a source code escrow or transition license provision.

---

**FINDING 3 — Section 5.3: Five-Year Confidentiality Survival is Inadequate for Federal Work**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Problem:**
Five years is a standard commercial confidentiality term. It is not adequate for a federal contractor operating in the national security space. Federal contracts routinely involve Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), export-controlled technical data under ITAR/EAR, and information subject to agency-specific handling requirements that impose indefinite or substantially longer protection obligations.

A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means that sensitive government program information, proprietary system architectures, or personnel data could be legally disclosed by the Vendor five years post-termination — potentially while the underlying federal program is still active.

**What's Missing:**
- No carve-out for information subject to federal law, regulation, or agency directive (CUI, ITAR, classified handling)
- No indefinite survival for trade secrets (which is the commercial standard)
- No acknowledgment of or deference to applicable federal information handling requirements

**Required Fix:**
Add a carve-out providing that the five-year term does not limit obligations imposed by applicable federal law or regulation. Add indefinite survival for trade secrets consistent with the Defend Trade Secrets Act. Consider extending the base term to ten years minimum given the engagement context.

---

**FINDING 4 — Section 10.2: Mandatory Arbitration Creates Federal Jurisdiction Conflict**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration... in Wilmington, Delaware."*

**The Problem:**
This is a mandatory, binding arbitration clause with no carve-out for disputes involving federal law, federal program obligations, or government-directed actions. For a federal contractor, this creates two serious problems:

First, disputes that implicate the False Claims Act, federal procurement regulations, or agency-directed contract modifications may not be arbitrable as a matter of federal public policy. A blanket arbitration clause that purports to cover "any dispute" could be challenged — or worse, could result in an arbitral award that conflicts with a federal agency's rights or determinations.

Second, the federal government is not a party to this agreement. If a dispute arises from a government-directed change, a contracting officer's decision, or a federal audit finding, Meridian could find itself in arbitration over a dispute it cannot fully resolve without the government's participation — and the government cannot be compelled into AAA arbitration.

**What's Missing:**
- No carve-out for disputes arising from or relating to federal contract obligations or government-directed actions
- No carve-out for injunctive or emergency relief (standard in commercial agreements)
- No acknowledgment that federal law governs where it conflicts with Delaware law

**Required Fix:**
Add explicit carve-outs for: (1) disputes arising from federal contract obligations or government-directed actions; (2) IP and confidentiality claims where injunctive relief may be sought in court; and (3) any matter where federal law preempts or conflicts with the arbitration obligation. Consider replacing mandatory arbitration with a tiered dispute resolution clause (negotiation → mediation → litigation) given the federal contracting context.

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Should Be Addressed

---

**FINDING 5 — Section 2.3: Subcontractor Flow-Down is Insufficient**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**The Problem:**
In federal contracting, subcontractor use is not a simple pass-through responsibility matter. FAR 52.244 and related clauses impose specific consent, disclosure, and flow-down requirements. This provision gives Vendor unilateral authority to subcontract any portion of the Services without Client consent or notification. For national security engagements, this is unacceptable — Meridian may have security clearance requirements, background check obligations, or agency-mandated vetting processes that apply to all personnel with access to program information.

**Required Fix:**
Add a requirement for prior written Client approval of subcontractors. Add mandatory flow-down of security, confidentiality, and compliance obligations. Add a representation that subcontractors will meet all applicable federal personnel security requirements.

---

**FINDING 6 — Section 6.3: "As Is" Disclaimer Conflicts with Section 9.1 Indemnification**

> *"THE SERVICES AND DELIVERABLES ARE PROVIDED 'AS IS.'"*

**The Problem:**
Section 6.3 disclaims all warranties and provides deliverables "as is." However, Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for losses arising out of "Vendor's performance of the Services" — which is a broad performance-based obligation. These provisions are in tension. Vendor will argue the "as is" disclaimer limits the scope of any performance-based indemnity claim. Meridian will argue the indemnity is independent of the warranty disclaimer.

This ambiguity will be exploited in any dispute. Courts and arbitrators have split on how to resolve this conflict, and the outcome is unpredictable.

**Required Fix:**
Clarify that the warranty disclaimer in 6.3 does not limit or modify Vendor's indemnification obligations under Section 9. Add a minimum fitness warranty for deliverables that are incorporated into federal systems.

---

**FINDING 7 — Section 7.2: Termination for Convenience Leaves Meridian Exposed on Active SOWs**

> *"Either party may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon sixty (60) days' written notice..."*

**The Problem:**
The termination for convenience provision applies to the Agreement but does not address the status of active SOWs at the time of termination. Section 7.4 requires Client to pay for services performed through termination, but there is no provision addressing: (1) whether Vendor must complete in-progress deliverables; (2) whether partial deliverables are accepted and assigned; (3) what happens to work product in progress that has not been formally delivered.

In a federal contracting context, an incomplete deliverable mid-engagement could leave Meridian in breach of its prime contract obligations with no recourse against Vendor.

**Required Fix:**
Add SOW-level termination provisions specifying Vendor's obligations to complete, wind down, or transition in-progress work. Add a provision requiring delivery of all work product in progress upon termination, regardless of completion status, with IP assignment applying to all such materials.

---

**FINDING 8 — Section 8.2(e): Fees Exception to Liability Cap is Asymmetric**

> *"(e) amounts owed by Client for fees, expenses, or taxes under Section 3 of this Agreement."*

**The Problem:**
The liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are presented as applying to "either party," but exception (e) applies exclusively to Client's payment obligations. There is no corresponding exception for Vendor's obligation to refund fees in the event of non-performance, material breach, or termination. This means Vendor's exposure for over-billing, wrongful invoicing, or failure to deliver is capped at 12 months of fees — while Client's payment obligation is entirely uncapped.

This is a one-sided carve-out dressed up as a mutual provision.

**Required Fix:**
Add a corresponding exception for Client's right to recover fees paid for undelivered or defective services, or remove exception (e) entirely and address payment obligations through the breach and indemnification framework.

---

### 🔵 ADVISORY — Flag for Client Awareness

---

**FINDING 9 — No Data Security or Breach Notification Provision**

For an engagement involving federal systems, there is no data security obligation, no breach notification requirement, and no reference to NIST 800-171, CMMC, or any applicable federal cybersecurity framework. This is a significant gap for any vendor with access to federal systems or CUI. This should be addressed either in the MSA or mandated in every SOW.

---

**FINDING 10 — No Non-Solicitation Provision**

There is no restriction on Vendor soliciting or hiring Meridian's employees or federal agency contacts during or after the engagement. In a federal services context where personnel relationships are core to contract performance, this is an exploitable gap.

---

**FINDING 11 — Governing Law / Federal Law Supremacy**

Section 10.1 designates Delaware law as governing without any acknowledgment that federal law governs where applicable. For a federal contractor, this omission could create interpretive conflicts when federal statutes, regulations, or agency directives bear on the agreement's subject matter.
