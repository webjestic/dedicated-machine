**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct from the cap entirely — which means the cap governs only the narrow residual category of ordinary breach claims that don't implicate any of those theories, and in a federal contractor engagement, that residual category is not where the real exposure lives. Section 8.2(e) compounds this by carving out all fees, expenses, and taxes owed under Section 3, which means the cap doesn't even protect Vendor from uncapped collection exposure on payment disputes. What remains inside the cap after all five carve-outs is applied is thin enough that the clause provides Meridian with materially less protection than its plain language suggests.

---

**Detailed Analysis**

**The cap is real on its face and fictional in practice.**

Section 8.1 presents as a standard bilateral cap: no consequential damages, aggregate liability capped at twelve months of fees. That structure is commercially familiar and would be acceptable if it held. It does not hold.

Section 8.2 removes from the cap:

- All indemnification obligations (8.2(a))
- All confidentiality breaches (8.2(b))
- All IP infringement and misappropriation claims (8.2(c))
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct (8.2(d))
- All fees, expenses, and taxes owed under Section 3 (8.2(e))

In a software professional services engagement for a federal contractor, those five categories are not edge cases. They are the primary vectors of serious commercial dispute. A vendor who mishandles classified or controlled unclassified information triggers 8.2(b). A deliverable that incorporates third-party code without a clean license triggers 8.2(c) and 8.2(a) simultaneously, through both the IP carve-out and the indemnification carve-out in Section 9.1(b). A subcontractor who causes a data incident through gross negligence triggers 8.2(d). In each of those scenarios — the scenarios most likely to produce catastrophic exposure in a national security adjacent engagement — the cap does not apply.

The twelve-month fee cap that Section 8.1 promises is effectively reserved for the least dangerous category of dispute: an ordinary breach claim that cannot be recharacterized as implicating IP, confidentiality, indemnification, or misconduct. That category is narrow, and opposing counsel will not struggle to plead around it.

**The indemnification carve-out deserves specific attention.**

Section 9.1 requires Vendor to indemnify Meridian for third-party claims arising out of Vendor's performance of the Services, IP infringement by the Deliverables, Vendor's breach of the Agreement, and Vendor's gross negligence or willful misconduct. Section 8.2(a) then removes all indemnification obligations from the liability cap entirely.

The practical consequence: if a third party brings a claim against Meridian arising from Vendor's performance — which in a federal services context could mean a government counterparty, a subcontractor, or a downstream agency — Vendor's indemnification exposure is uncapped and includes attorneys' fees, court costs, and all resulting Losses as defined in Section 1. There is no ceiling. The twelve-month cap that Vendor negotiated provides zero protection against the indemnification scenario, which is also the scenario most likely to involve government parties and the largest potential damages.

**The IP structure creates a compounding exposure problem.**

Section 4.2 assigns Deliverables to Client subject to full payment, but Section 4.3 grants only a non-exclusive license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP embedded in those Deliverables. The scope of what constitutes Vendor Pre-Existing IP is undefined beyond the general definition in Section 4.1, and there is no representation that Vendor has identified or disclosed what Pre-Existing IP will be incorporated into any given Deliverable before work begins.

This matters because Section 6.2 warrants only that Deliverables will not *knowingly* infringe third-party IP — a qualified warranty that limits Vendor's exposure to actual knowledge. If Vendor incorporates third-party components it did not know were infringing, the warranty does not apply. But the indemnification obligation in Section 9.1(b) covers any allegation of IP infringement without a knowledge qualifier. The interaction between the qualified warranty and the unqualified indemnification creates an asymmetry that Meridian should understand before signing.

**The confidentiality survival period is insufficient for this engagement.**

Five years under Section 5.3 is a standard commercial term. It is not adequate for a federal contractor engagement where the Confidential Information may include government-sensitive data, program information, or materials subject to regulatory retention requirements that extend beyond five years. If Meridian is handling information that carries longer protection obligations under federal law or contract, a five-year contractual survival period creates a gap between the contractual obligation and the actual compliance requirement.

**The arbitration clause creates a practical problem in federal disputes.**

Section 10.2 requires binding AAA arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware. If a dispute under this agreement implicates a government contract, a federal agency interest, or a matter touching on national security, mandatory private arbitration may conflict with applicable federal dispute resolution requirements or agency oversight obligations. This clause should be reviewed against Meridian's prime contract terms and any applicable FAR provisions before execution.

---

**Changes Required Before Execution**

1. **Restructure Section 8.2 to narrow the carve-outs.** Indemnification should not be a blanket carve-out. At minimum, the indemnification carve-out should be limited to third-party claims and should not permit first-party damages between the parties to escape the cap. Consider capping indemnification obligations at a separate, higher multiplier of fees rather than leaving them entirely uncapped.

2. **Add a mutual aggregate cap on indemnification exposure**, or alternatively negotiate a separate indemnification cap at a defined multiple of the contract value, so that Vendor's exposure has a ceiling even in indemnification scenarios.

3. **Require pre-SOW disclosure of Vendor Pre-Existing IP** that will be incorporated into Deliverables, so Meridian can assess the license scope and any third-party IP risk before work begins rather than after delivery.

4. **Extend confidentiality survival** to match the longer of ten years or the duration of any applicable federal retention or protection requirement, with a carve-out for information that remains subject to ongoing regulatory obligation.

5. **Add a government contract compliance carve-out to Section 10.2** that permits either party to pursue claims through required federal channels if the dispute implicates a government contract, prime contract obligation, or agency requirement that mandates a different dispute resolution process.

6. **Clarify Section 8.2(e).** The carve-out for fees, expenses, and taxes owed under Section 3 should be limited to undisputed amounts. As drafted, it creates uncapped exposure for any payment dispute, including disputed invoices, which removes the cap from a category of claims that should be within it.
