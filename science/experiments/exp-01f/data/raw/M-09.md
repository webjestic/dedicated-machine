**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally present but operationally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct from the cap entirely — which means the cap governs only a narrow residual category of ordinary breach claims, while every dispute category that actually materializes in federal contractor engagements runs unlimited. For a national security-adjacent engagement where confidentiality breaches, IP disputes, and indemnification triggers are not edge cases but foreseeable operational risks, this is not a balanced limitation of liability clause — it is a cap that does not protect where protection is needed. Additional concerns include the absence of any federal contracting compliance obligations (FAR, DFARS, data handling requirements), a mandatory arbitration clause that may conflict with federal dispute resolution frameworks applicable to government-adjacent work, and an IP assignment conditioned on full payment that creates a gap period where Client may be operating on deliverables it does not yet own.

---

**Full Analysis**

### Section 8 — Limitation of Liability: The Cap Is Not a Cap

Start with the math. Section 8.1 caps total cumulative liability at fees paid in the trailing twelve months. For a professional services engagement, that number is knowable and finite — it is the number the CFO looks at when assessing risk tolerance.

Now read Section 8.2.

The exceptions remove from that cap:

- **All indemnification obligations** (Section 9) — which themselves cover third-party claims arising from Vendor's performance of Services, IP infringement allegations, any breach of the Agreement, and gross negligence or willful misconduct. That is not a narrow carve-out. That is the indemnification section covering nearly every significant claim category, now running unlimited.
- **All confidentiality breaches** (Section 5) — in a national security-adjacent engagement, a confidentiality breach is not a hypothetical. It is the scenario the agreement exists to address. Unlimited exposure here is not a theoretical concern.
- **All IP claims** — infringement, misappropriation, violation. Given that Section 4 involves assignment of deliverables and a license back to Vendor Pre-Existing IP, IP disputes are structurally embedded in the agreement's core mechanics.
- **Gross negligence and willful misconduct** — categories that, in practice, opposing counsel will plead in virtually any serious dispute because they are the path around the cap.
- **All fees, expenses, and taxes owed** — Client's payment obligations run unlimited regardless of any counterclaim or offset theory.

What remains inside the cap after these exceptions? Ordinary breach claims that do not touch IP, confidentiality, indemnification, gross negligence, or willful misconduct. In practice, that is a narrow residual category. The cap provides protection in the disputes least likely to occur and no protection in the disputes most likely to occur.

This is the pattern I have seen used against clients. The cap is real in the document. It is not real in the dispute.

---

### Section 9 — Indemnification: Scope Is Broad and Now Uncapped

Vendor's indemnification obligation in Section 9.1 covers: (a) Vendor's performance of Services, (b) IP infringement allegations, (c) Vendor's breach of this Agreement, and (d) gross negligence or willful misconduct. Because Section 8.2(a) removes indemnification from the cap entirely, Vendor's exposure on any of these triggers is unlimited.

That cuts both ways. Client's indemnification obligation under Section 9.2 — covering unauthorized use of deliverables, Client's breach, and Client's gross negligence or willful misconduct — is also uncapped. In a government contracting context where Client's "use" of deliverables may be directed by agency requirements rather than purely by Client's election, the "unauthorized use" trigger in Section 9.2(a) needs to be defined with precision. As written, it is not.

The indemnification procedure in Section 9.3 grants the indemnifying party **sole control of defense and settlement**. In a federal contractor engagement, that is a structural problem. Client may have regulatory obligations, agency relationships, and security clearance considerations that make ceding settlement control to a vendor legally untenable. The consent carve-out — requiring written consent before a settlement that imposes liability or obligation on the indemnified party — is present but insufficient. Vendor could settle a third-party IP claim in a manner that affects Client's program deliverables or agency relationships without that settlement technically imposing a direct financial liability on Client.

---

### Section 4 — IP Assignment: Payment Condition Creates an Ownership Gap

Section 4.2 conditions assignment of Deliverables on **full payment of all fees**. Until that condition is satisfied, Client does not own the work product it has received and may be operating on. In a government contracting context, where deliverables may be submitted to an agency before final invoices are reconciled, this creates a gap period during which Client is using materials it does not own. If a payment dispute arises — even a legitimate one — Vendor has a colorable argument that assignment never occurred.

The license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP in Section 4.3 is limited to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." If Client needs to modify, extend, or integrate those deliverables in ways that require access to the underlying Vendor IP beyond the incorporated form, the license does not cover it. For software deliverables in particular, that limitation may be operationally unworkable.

---

### Section 10.2 — Arbitration: Potential Conflict with Federal Contracting Context

Mandatory binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules may conflict with dispute resolution requirements applicable to government-adjacent work. Depending on the nature of the engagement and whether federal funds flow through Client, certain disputes may be subject to federal jurisdiction, agency oversight, or statutory dispute resolution frameworks that arbitration clauses cannot displace. This needs to be assessed against the specific SOWs and the nature of Client's federal engagement before execution.

---

### What Is Missing Entirely

This agreement contains no provisions addressing:

- **Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) or DFARS compliance** — standard in any agreement where the vendor is supporting a federal contractor's performance obligations
- **Data handling and security requirements** — no reference to NIST 800-171, CMMC, FedRAMP, or any applicable federal data security framework, despite the national security context
- **Government audit rights or flow-down clauses** — if Client's prime contract requires these, they need to be present in subcontractor and vendor agreements
- **Export control compliance** — ITAR/EAR obligations are not addressed; in a national security engagement, this is not optional
- **Security clearance obligations** — if Vendor personnel require clearances to perform Services, the agreement is silent on who bears responsibility for obtaining and maintaining them

---

### Required Changes Before Execution

1. **Section 8.2 — Restructure exceptions.** At minimum, cap indemnification obligations at a negotiated multiple of the fee cap (e.g., 2–3x trailing twelve months). Confidentiality and IP carve-outs may be appropriate to retain as uncapped, but that decision should be made deliberately with client sign-off, not by default.

2. **Section 9.1 and 9.2 — Narrow indemnification triggers.** "Vendor's performance of Services" and "Client's breach of this Agreement" as standalone indemnification triggers are overbroad. Indemnification should be limited to third-party claims arising from specific, defined conduct — not any performance or any breach.

3. **Section 9.3 — Modify sole control of defense.** Client must retain the right to participate in defense and must have meaningful consent rights over any settlement that affects Client's agency relationships, program obligations, or regulatory standing — not just settlements that impose direct financial liability.

4. **Section 4.2 — Remove or modify payment condition on assignment.** Assignment should occur upon delivery and acceptance, with Vendor retaining a security interest in fees owed rather than a reversionary IP interest. Alternatively, the agreement should specify that Client receives a broad license upon delivery, with assignment completing upon payment.

5. **Section 10.2 — Evaluate arbitration clause.** Confirm compatibility with Client's prime contract dispute resolution requirements and applicable federal frameworks before accepting mandatory arbitration.

6. **Add federal contracting compliance provisions.** FAR/DFARS flow-down, data security framework compliance, export control obligations, and audit rights are not optional additions — they are baseline requirements for this engagement context.
