# Contract Review — Vendor Professional Services Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

---

## Summary

This agreement is structurally sound and better drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review in this space. The liability cap exceptions in Section 8.2 are appropriately broad, the indemnification obligations are reasonably balanced, and the IP assignment framework is workable. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable risk or require clarification before execution in a federal contractor context, and they are significant enough that I cannot approve this agreement as written.

---

## Detailed Findings

### 🔴 Critical Issues — Must Be Resolved

---

**1. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Use: No Federal Compliance Requirement**

The agreement permits Vendor to engage subcontractors with no restriction beyond general performance responsibility. In a federal contracting environment, this is inadequate.

*Problem:* Subcontractors may have access to controlled unclassified information (CUI), federal systems, or personnel requiring security clearances. There is no requirement that subcontractors comply with applicable federal regulations — NIST SP 800-171, CMMC, FAR/DFARS flow-downs, or equivalent — nor any requirement for Client pre-approval of subcontractors.

*Required Change:* Add a provision requiring (a) Client's prior written approval for any subcontractor who will access Client systems, data, or facilities; (b) mandatory flow-down of all applicable federal compliance obligations; and (c) Vendor's express representation that subcontractors meet any security or clearance requirements specified in the applicable SOW.

---

**2. Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival: Five-Year Cap Is Inadequate for This Context**

*Problem:* A five-year survival period is standard in commercial agreements. It is not standard — and is potentially dangerous — when the Confidential Information includes national security-related data, federal program information, CUI, or proprietary technical data subject to export controls. Five years provides no meaningful protection for information that may remain sensitive for decades or that is subject to indefinite protection obligations under federal law or regulation.

*Required Change:* Either (a) extend the survival period to the life of the information's sensitivity or indefinitely for information that constitutes CUI, classified information, or trade secrets; or (b) carve out a separate, indefinite survival obligation for any information subject to federal regulatory protection requirements. At minimum, the agreement should not permit the five-year clock to override any longer obligation imposed by applicable law.

---

**3. Section 8.1 — Liability Cap: "Fees Paid in Preceding 12 Months" Creates Near-Zero Exposure at Engagement Outset**

*Problem:* The cap is calculated against fees paid or payable in the twelve months preceding the triggering event. In the early months of a new engagement — when integration risk is highest, system access is being established, and errors are most likely — this cap may be negligible or zero. A vendor who causes significant harm in month two of a contract is effectively judgment-proof under this structure.

*Required Change:* Establish a minimum floor on the liability cap — either a fixed dollar amount or the greater of (a) fees paid in the preceding twelve months or (b) total fees committed under the applicable SOW. This is a standard negotiating position and should not be controversial with a reasonable counterparty.

---

**4. Section 10.2 — Mandatory Binding Arbitration: Potential Conflict with Federal Dispute Resolution Requirements**

*Problem:* Depending on the nature of the underlying federal contract, mandatory binding arbitration may conflict with dispute resolution mechanisms required or preferred under FAR, agency-specific regulations, or the Contract Disputes Act. Additionally, arbitration clauses can limit discovery in ways that are particularly damaging in disputes involving technical performance failures or data security incidents — exactly the disputes most likely to arise in this context.

*Required Change:* At minimum, add a carve-out preserving the right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving the right to arbitrate. Additionally, confirm with the client whether any applicable federal contract or agency requirement restricts or governs dispute resolution for work performed under this MSA. If so, this clause requires revision to conform.

---

### 🟡 Significant Issues — Should Be Negotiated

---

**5. Section 4.2 — IP Assignment Conditioned on Full Payment**

*Problem:* The assignment of Deliverables to Client is expressly conditioned on "full payment of all fees." Until that condition is satisfied, Vendor retains ownership of all work product. In a government contracting context, this creates a scenario where partially completed Deliverables — potentially including sensitive technical work integrated into federal systems — remain Vendor property during any payment dispute.

*Recommended Change:* Negotiate either (a) a present assignment with a license-back to Vendor pending payment, which is a more protective structure for Client; or (b) a provision clarifying that Client retains a license to use Deliverables in progress during any good-faith payment dispute, with ownership reverting only upon final adjudication of non-payment.

---

**6. Section 6.3 — Broad "As Is" Disclaimer Partially Undermines Section 6.2 Warranties**

*Problem:* Section 6.2 provides a warranty of professional performance and non-knowing infringement. Section 6.3 then disclaims all warranties not expressly set forth in 6.2, including fitness for a particular purpose. In a federal services context where the Deliverables must meet specific functional, security, or compliance requirements, this disclaimer may be used to resist warranty claims tied to those requirements unless they are expressly documented in the SOW.

*Recommended Change:* Ensure that all material functional, security, and compliance requirements are explicitly captured in each SOW and that the Section 6.2 warranty is understood to encompass conformance to SOW specifications. Consider adding an express warranty of conformance to SOW requirements as a standalone provision.

---

**7. Section 9.1 — Vendor Indemnification Scope: "Performance of Services" Is Overbroad in One Direction, Underprotective in Another**

*Problem:* The indemnification trigger in 9.1(a) — "Vendor's performance of the Services" — is broad enough that Vendor could argue it covers only acts in performance, not omissions or failures to perform. Separately, there is no indemnification obligation covering data breaches, security incidents, or regulatory violations caused by Vendor's handling of Client data or federal information. In this operating environment, that is a material gap.

*Recommended Change:* Add an express indemnification obligation covering third-party claims and regulatory penalties arising from Vendor's unauthorized disclosure, loss, or compromise of Client data or federal information, including CUI. This is not covered by the IP infringement indemnity and is not clearly captured by "breach of this Agreement" unless the agreement contains explicit data security obligations — which it currently does not.

---

**8. No Data Security or Incident Response Obligations**

*Problem:* The agreement contains no data security requirements, no obligation to maintain any particular security posture, and no incident notification obligation. For a vendor accessing federal systems or handling government-related data, this is a significant structural gap. The absence of these provisions is not cured by the confidentiality clause, which addresses disclosure obligations but not security controls or breach response.

*Recommended Change:* Add a data security addendum or standalone section requiring Vendor to (a) maintain a security program appropriate to the sensitivity of the data handled; (b) comply with applicable federal data security requirements as specified in each SOW; and (c) notify Client within a defined period (typically 72 hours or as required by applicable regulation) of any actual or suspected security incident involving Client data.

---

### 🟢 Provisions That Are Acceptable As Drafted

- **Section 3** — Payment terms, interest accrual, and tax allocation are standard and acceptable.
- **Section 4.1 and 4.3** — Pre-existing IP retention and license-back structure are reasonable and clearly drafted.
- **Section 7** — Termination for convenience and for cause are appropriately structured. The 30-day cure period is standard.
- **Section 8.2** — The carve-outs from the liability cap are well-constructed. Excluding indemnification, confidentiality breaches, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees from the cap is the correct approach and reflects a balanced negotiating posture.
- **Section 9.3** — Indemnification procedure is standard and acceptable, including the settlement consent requirement.
- **Section 10.1** — Delaware governing law is acceptable.
- **Sections 10.3–10.8** — Boilerplate provisions are standard and unproblematic.

---

## Recommended Path Forward

Return the agreement to Vendor's counsel with redlines addressing the eight items above, prioritizing the four critical issues. The data security gap and the subcontractor compliance gap are the most operationally dangerous provisions in this agreement for a federal contractor engagement. The liability cap floor and the arbitration carve-out are the most important structural protections to secure before execution.

This agreement should not be executed in its current form.
