# Contract Review — Vendor Professional Services Agreement

**Decision: Request Changes**

**Summary:** This agreement is structurally sound and better-drafted than most commercial MSAs submitted for review — the liability cap exceptions, bilateral indemnification, and IP assignment mechanics are all coherent and internally consistent. However, several provisions introduce unacceptable or unresolved risk for a federal contractor engagement specifically, and two clauses require substantive revision before execution. The agreement as presented is not suitable for execution in its current form.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### ✅ Provisions That Pass Review

**Section 2 — Services and Deliverables**
The SOW-based structure is appropriate. Section 2.3's subcontractor provision is acceptable because it preserves Vendor's primary responsibility for subcontractor performance and compliance — this is the correct formulation. No changes required.

**Section 3 — Fees and Payment**
Standard and enforceable. The 1.5%/month interest rate is commercially reasonable and the lower-of-legal-maximum qualifier is appropriate. Tax allocation in 3.3 is correctly structured.

**Section 4 — Intellectual Property**
The assignment mechanics in 4.2 are sound. The payment condition on assignment is standard and acceptable. The carve-out for Pre-Existing IP with a perpetual, royalty-free license in 4.3 is a reasonable and well-structured compromise. One note: the license in 4.3 is scoped to use "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables" — this is appropriately narrow and protects Vendor's IP estate without impairing Client's operational use of the finished work product.

**Section 6.1 — Authority Representations**
Standard and adequate.

**Section 8 — Limitation of Liability**
The cap structure is defensible. The exceptions in 8.2 are the critical mechanism here, and they are correctly drafted: confidentiality breaches, IP violations, indemnification obligations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and fee obligations all sit outside the cap. This is the correct architecture for a high-stakes engagement. The twelve-month lookback for the fee-based cap is standard and acceptable.

**Section 9.3 — Indemnification Procedure**
The procedure clause is well-drafted. The requirement that the indemnifying party obtain written consent before settling in a manner that imposes obligation on the indemnified party is an important protection and is correctly included.

**Section 10.4–10.8 — Boilerplate**
Standard and enforceable. No issues.

### ⚠️ Provisions Requiring Changes

---

#### 1. Section 5.3 — Confidentiality Survival Period (MATERIAL)

**The Problem:**
The five-year survival period on confidentiality obligations is commercially standard for a typical software services agreement. It is **not** adequate for a federal contractor engagement in the national security space.

In this context, Client will almost certainly be handling Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI), potentially Covered Defense Information (CDI), and possibly information subject to ITAR, EAR, or agency-specific handling requirements. A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations creates a defined window after which a vendor — or its personnel — faces no contractual exposure for disclosing information that may remain operationally sensitive, classified at a derivative level, or subject to federal regulatory protection indefinitely.

Federal regulatory frameworks do not sunset on commercial timelines. If a breach occurs in year six, Client has no contractual remedy regardless of the sensitivity of the disclosed information or the damage caused.

**Required Change:**
The survival period must be extended. For information that is subject to federal regulatory requirements (CUI, CDI, ITAR-controlled technical data, etc.), confidentiality obligations must survive either indefinitely or for the duration of the applicable regulatory requirement, whichever is longer. At minimum, the clause should be bifurcated: standard commercial confidential information may carry a five-year tail, but a defined category of "Sensitive Government Information" or "Regulated Information" should carry an indefinite or regulatory-duration survival obligation.

Proposed language:

> *Notwithstanding Section 5.3, confidentiality obligations with respect to any information subject to federal law or regulation, including but not limited to Controlled Unclassified Information, export-controlled technical data, or information designated by a government agency as requiring protection, shall survive termination of this Agreement indefinitely or for such longer period as required by applicable law or regulation.*

---

#### 2. Section 10.2 — Binding Arbitration / Dispute Resolution (MATERIAL)

**The Problem:**
The mandatory binding arbitration clause with venue in Wilmington, Delaware creates two distinct problems for a federal contractor engagement.

**First**, certain disputes arising from federal contracts — including those involving government data rights, CUI handling obligations, export control compliance, or performance under a prime contract — may be subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction or administrative processes (e.g., the Contract Disputes Act, agency inspector general referrals, or federal court jurisdiction over qui tam actions). A blanket arbitration clause that purports to be the exclusive dispute resolution mechanism for "any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement" may conflict with those mandatory federal processes or create ambiguity about whether a party can pursue them.

**Second**, arbitration clauses in government-adjacent contracts can create complications with flow-down obligations. If Client's prime contract requires disputes to be resolved in a specific forum or under specific procedures, this clause conflicts with that requirement and the entire agreement clause in 10.3 does not resolve that conflict — it makes it worse by superseding all prior understandings without carving out prime contract obligations.

**Required Change:**
The arbitration clause must include an explicit carve-out for disputes that are subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction, administrative processes, or prime contract dispute resolution requirements. Additionally, Client should negotiate the right to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in a court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights — this is standard in well-drafted arbitration clauses and its absence here is a gap.

Proposed additions:

> *Notwithstanding the foregoing, either party may seek emergency injunctive or other equitable relief from a court of competent jurisdiction to prevent irreparable harm pending resolution of a dispute by arbitration. Further, nothing in this Section shall limit either party's ability to comply with mandatory federal dispute resolution processes, administrative procedures, or obligations arising under applicable federal law or regulation.*

---

#### 3. Section 6.3 — Disclaimer of Warranties / "As Is" Provision (MODERATE)

**The Problem:**
The "as is" disclaimer in 6.3 is standard boilerplate, but its interaction with the federal contractor context creates a meaningful gap. Section 6.2 limits Vendor's warranty to professional performance and non-knowing infringement. There is no warranty of regulatory compliance — specifically, no representation that the Services or Deliverables will comply with applicable federal security requirements (e.g., NIST SP 800-171, CMMC, FedRAMP, FIPS 140-2, or Section 508 accessibility standards).

In a commercial engagement, this gap is acceptable — the client assumes the risk of regulatory fit. In a federal contractor engagement, the Deliverables may be deployed in environments where non-compliance with federal security standards is not a business risk but a legal and contractual violation with consequences that flow up to the prime contract and potentially to the government. The vendor's "as is" disclaimer does not insulate Client from those downstream consequences.

**Required Change:**
Add a representation in Section 6.2 that Vendor will perform Services and deliver Deliverables in compliance with applicable federal security and regulatory requirements as specified in the relevant SOW. The SOW template should also include a field for specifying applicable compliance frameworks. This does not need to be a blanket warranty — it can be scoped to requirements identified in writing at the SOW level — but the current agreement contains no mechanism for capturing this obligation at all.

---

#### 4. Section 2.3 — Subcontractor Use (LOW, FLAG FOR NATIONAL SECURITY CONTEXT)

**The Problem:**
Section 2.3 permits Vendor to use subcontractors without Client's prior approval, subject only to Vendor remaining responsible for their performance. In a standard commercial engagement, this is acceptable. In a national security or federal contractor context, it is not.

Subcontractors who access Client systems, handle CUI, or perform work on federal programs may be subject to personnel security requirements, facility clearance requirements, or foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI) screening obligations. A vendor's contractual promise to remain "responsible" for subcontractor performance does not satisfy those requirements and does not give Client visibility into who is actually performing work on sensitive engagements.

**Required Change:**
Add a requirement for prior written Client approval before Vendor engages any subcontractor who will have access to Client systems, facilities, data, or Confidential Information. The approval right should be non-waivable for subcontractors performing work on classified or CUI-designated programs.

### Summary of Required Changes

| Section | Issue | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| 5.3 | Confidentiality survival period inadequate for federal/regulated information | Material |
| 10.2 | Arbitration clause conflicts with potential federal jurisdiction and prime contract obligations; no injunctive relief carve-out | Material |
| 6.3 / 6.2 | No warranty of compliance with applicable federal security standards | Moderate |
| 2.3 | Subcontractor use without prior approval is unacceptable in national security context | Moderate |

---

**Bottom Line:** Do not execute in current form. The two material issues — confidentiality survival and arbitration scope — each independently create conditions under which Client's exposure is either unquantifiable or unenforceable at the moment it matters most. Return to Vendor with redlines on all four flagged provisions before proceeding.
