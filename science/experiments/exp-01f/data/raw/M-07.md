**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The liability cap in Section 8.1 is structurally hollow. The carve-outs in Section 8.2 remove indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence, and willful misconduct from the cap entirely — which means the cap governs only a residual category of garden-variety breach claims that rarely drive material exposure in federal services engagements. In practice, the disputes that actually matter in this context (IP infringement allegations, confidentiality breaches involving government or classified-adjacent data, indemnification triggers from third-party claims) are all uncapped and unlimited on both sides. Three additional provisions compound this problem and require negotiation before execution.

---

## Detailed Analysis

### 1. The Liability Cap Does Not Protect Against the Disputes That Matter

**Section 8.1** establishes a cap at twelve months of fees paid — a reasonable-looking number. **Section 8.2** then removes from that cap:

- All indemnification obligations (8.2(a))
- All confidentiality breaches (8.2(b))
- All IP infringement and misappropriation claims (8.2(c))
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct (8.2(d))
- All unpaid fees (8.2(e))

In a federal contractor engagement, these are not edge cases. These are the dispute categories. A confidentiality breach involving government program data, a subcontractor IP dispute, or an indemnification trigger from a third-party claim — all of it sits outside the cap. What remains inside the cap is ordinary breach of contract liability that does not implicate any of those categories. That is a narrow and largely theoretical residual.

This is not a bilateral protection. It is a bilateral exposure agreement with a cap attached to the wrong category of risk.

**Required change:** Negotiate a mutual aggregate liability ceiling that applies to indemnification and confidentiality obligations, with a separate, higher sublimit — not unlimited exposure — for IP claims. Gross negligence and willful misconduct carve-outs are standard and acceptable. The fee carve-out (8.2(e)) is also acceptable. Everything else needs a ceiling.

---

### 2. Indemnification Scope Is Asymmetric and Overbroad Against Client

**Section 9.1** requires Vendor to indemnify Client for claims arising out of Vendor's *performance of the Services* generally — not just third-party IP claims or gross negligence. That is a broad trigger.

**Section 9.2** requires Client to indemnify Vendor for claims arising from Client's use of Deliverables *in a manner not authorized by this Agreement*. The authorization scope is defined by the SOW and Section 4. If Client uses a Deliverable in a way Vendor later characterizes as outside the SOW, Client bears indemnification exposure.

In a government contracting environment, Client's use of Deliverables is often directed by agency requirements that post-date the SOW. Client could find itself using a Deliverable in a manner technically outside the original SOW authorization — not because of misconduct, but because the engagement evolved — and face an indemnification obligation to Vendor as a result.

**Required change:** Section 9.2(a) should be limited to unauthorized use that is *material* and *not required by applicable law, regulation, or government directive*. Client should not bear indemnification exposure for use modifications driven by agency requirements.

---

### 3. Subcontractor Exposure Is Unmitigated

**Section 2.3** permits Vendor to use subcontractors and states Vendor "remains responsible" for their performance. In federal services, this is insufficient. Vendor's subcontractors may have access to government systems, sensitive data, or classified-adjacent information. The agreement contains no requirement that subcontractors be disclosed to Client, no requirement that subcontractors be subject to background check or clearance requirements, and no restriction on subcontractor use in sensitive SOW contexts.

Vendor's responsibility for subcontractor performance is a contractual promise. It does not prevent the harm. It only determines who pays afterward — and given the uncapped indemnification structure, "who pays" is a question that may never resolve cleanly.

**Required change:** Add a provision requiring Vendor to (a) disclose subcontractor identity upon Client request, (b) ensure subcontractors are bound by confidentiality obligations at least as protective as Section 5, (c) obtain Client's prior written approval before using subcontractors in connection with any SOW involving government systems or sensitive data, and (d) represent that subcontractors meet any applicable security or clearance requirements specified in the SOW.

---

### 4. Confidentiality Survival Period Is Inadequate for This Engagement Type

**Section 5.3** limits confidentiality obligations to five years post-termination. In a national security or federal services context, five years is a commercial standard that does not fit the sensitivity profile of the information involved. Government program data, acquisition strategies, system architecture details, and personnel information may remain sensitive well beyond that window.

**Required change:** Confidentiality obligations should survive indefinitely for information that constitutes government-furnished information, information subject to federal classification or controlled unclassified information (CUI) handling requirements, or information that Client designates in writing as requiring extended protection. The five-year term can remain for ordinary commercial confidential information.

---

### 5. Arbitration Venue Creates Practical Friction

**Section 10.2** requires binding arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules. For a federal contractor, mandatory arbitration is not inherently problematic, but the venue and rules selection warrants scrutiny. If Client is a federal contractor operating under agency-specific dispute resolution requirements or if any SOW involves a federal prime contract with mandatory dispute clauses, this arbitration provision may conflict with those upstream obligations.

**Required change:** Add a carve-out preserving Client's ability to comply with any dispute resolution requirements imposed by applicable federal prime contracts or agency regulations. Confirm that the arbitration clause does not conflict with any existing government contract flow-down obligations before execution.

---

## Summary of Required Changes

| Issue | Section | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Uncapped indemnification and confidentiality exposure | 8.2(a), 8.2(b), 8.2(c) | Critical |
| Client indemnification overbroad for government-directed use | 9.2(a) | High |
| Subcontractor disclosure and security requirements absent | 2.3 | High |
| Confidentiality survival inadequate for federal data | 5.3 | High |
| Arbitration clause may conflict with prime contract obligations | 10.2 | Moderate |

Do not execute this agreement in its current form. The liability structure as written provides the appearance of mutual protection while leaving both parties — and Client in particular — with unlimited exposure in the categories of risk that define this engagement type.
