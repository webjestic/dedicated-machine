**Decision:** Request Changes

**Summary:** The limitation of liability clause in Section 8.1 is structurally present but operationally hollow. The Section 8.2 carve-outs remove indemnification, confidentiality, IP, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees from the cap entirely — which is to say, the cap governs the residual category of ordinary breach claims while leaving uncapped every dispute category that actually generates material exposure in a federal services engagement. For a government contracting firm operating in the national security space, where confidentiality breaches and IP disputes are not edge cases but foreseeable operational risks, executing this agreement means accepting unlimited liability in the areas that matter most, behind the cosmetic comfort of a twelve-month fee cap that will not apply when it is needed. Additionally, Section 9.1's vendor indemnification scope — covering Vendor's "performance of the Services" and "breach of this Agreement" broadly — combined with its exemption from the liability cap creates an asymmetric exposure profile that warrants renegotiation before signature.

---

**Detailed Findings**

**1. The Liability Cap Is Not a Cap**

Section 8.1 establishes a twelve-month fee cap. Section 8.2 then removes from that cap:

- All indemnification obligations (8.2(a))
- All confidentiality breaches (8.2(b))
- All IP infringement or misappropriation claims (8.2(c))
- Gross negligence and willful misconduct (8.2(d))
- All unpaid fees (8.2(e))

What remains inside the cap is ordinary breach of contract that does not involve confidentiality, IP, indemnification, negligence, or payment. In a professional services agreement for a national security contractor, that residual category is nearly empty in practice. The disputes you will actually face — a confidentiality breach involving federal program data, an IP ownership dispute over a deliverable, a vendor indemnification claim following a third-party suit — all fall outside the cap. The twelve-month fee ceiling provides no protection in those scenarios. This is not a balanced commercial agreement. It is a document that presents the appearance of mutual risk allocation while preserving unlimited exposure on both sides for every category of dispute with real stakes.

**2. Vendor Indemnification Scope Is Overbroad and Uncapped**

Section 9.1(a) requires Vendor to indemnify Client for claims "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services." That language, read broadly, could encompass nearly any third-party claim connected to how Vendor does its work — including claims that Client contributed to or that arise from Client-directed decisions embedded in the SOW. Combined with Section 8.2(a)'s exemption of all indemnification from the cap, this creates an uncapped obligation on Vendor's side that opposing counsel will argue against aggressively the moment a real claim arises. The indemnification trigger should be tightened to require that the claim arise from Vendor's acts or omissions, not merely from Vendor's performance generally.

**3. Client Indemnification Is Asymmetrically Narrow**

Section 9.2 limits Client's indemnification obligation to unauthorized use of Deliverables, Client's breach, and Client's gross negligence or willful misconduct. Vendor's obligation under 9.1 is substantially broader — covering all Services performance and all breach, not just gross negligence. This asymmetry may be acceptable depending on negotiating posture, but it should be a conscious decision, not an artifact of unreviewed boilerplate.

**4. IP License Is Inadequately Scoped for Federal Contracting**

Section 4.3 grants Client a license to Vendor Pre-Existing IP "solely as incorporated in the Deliverables." In a federal contracting context, Deliverables frequently need to be modified, integrated with government systems, transferred to agency partners, or reproduced for compliance purposes. A license restricted to use "solely as incorporated" may not cover those downstream uses. If Client modifies a Deliverable, sublicenses it to a federal agency, or integrates it into a broader program, Vendor could argue the license does not extend to those activities. The license grant needs to be broader: modification, integration, reproduction, and sublicense to government customers should be expressly included.

**5. Confidentiality Survival Is Insufficient for National Security Context**

Section 5.3 provides a five-year survival period for confidentiality obligations. In a national security and federal services engagement, five years is inadequate for information that may remain sensitive indefinitely — classified program structures, agency relationships, security architectures, personnel information. The survival period should either be extended substantially or, for specifically identified categories of information, made perpetual. This is a standard ask in government contracting and should not be a point of significant vendor resistance.

**6. Arbitration Venue Creates Practical Friction**

Section 10.2 requires arbitration in Wilmington, Delaware under AAA Commercial Rules. For a federal contractor, mandatory arbitration in a fixed venue creates logistical burden and, more importantly, may create complications if disputes involve classified or controlled unclassified information that cannot be freely presented in a commercial arbitration proceeding. The dispute resolution clause should include a carve-out for injunctive relief in federal court, and the parties should consider whether AAA Commercial Rules are appropriate for disputes that may implicate federal program information.

---

**Requested Changes Before Execution**

1. **Section 8.2 — Carve-out restructuring:** Either (a) eliminate or narrow the indemnification and confidentiality carve-outs so the cap applies to a meaningful category of disputes, or (b) negotiate mutual sublimits for carved-out categories rather than leaving them fully uncapped. A cap of three to five times annual contract value on indemnification and confidentiality claims is a defensible middle position.

2. **Section 9.1 — Vendor indemnification trigger:** Revise "arising out of or related to Vendor's performance of the Services" to "arising out of or related to Vendor's negligent or wrongful acts or omissions in performing the Services." The current language is too broad to be enforceable as written and will be contested.

3. **Section 4.3 — License scope:** Expand the Pre-Existing IP license to include modification, integration, reproduction, and sublicense to federal government customers, consistent with Client's anticipated use in federal program delivery.

4. **Section 5.3 — Confidentiality survival:** Extend survival to ten years at minimum, with perpetual survival for information designated as relating to national security programs, classified activities, or federal agency relationships.

5. **Section 10.2 — Dispute resolution:** Add an express carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in federal court without waiving arbitration rights. Consider whether AAA Commercial Rules are appropriate given the nature of the engagement.
