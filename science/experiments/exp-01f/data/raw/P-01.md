# Contract Review — Master Services Agreement
**DataStream Solutions, Inc. / Meridian Federal Partners, LLC**
*Reviewed by: obotix.one Legal — Contract Review Division*

---

## Decision: **Request Changes**

## Summary

This agreement presents as a balanced commercial MSA but contains several structural vulnerabilities that create disproportionate risk exposure for Meridian Federal Partners as the Client in a federal contracting context. The most critical issues involve an unqualified IP assignment trigger tied solely to payment, a confidentiality survival period that is dangerously inadequate for national security-adjacent work, and an arbitration clause that may conflict with federal dispute resolution obligations and create jurisdictional complications. These are not cosmetic deficiencies — they are provisions that could materially impair Client's legal position, operational continuity, and compliance posture if left unaddressed.

---

## Detailed Findings

---

### 🔴 CRITICAL — Must Resolve Before Execution

---

**1. IP Assignment Conditioned Solely on Full Payment (§4.2)**

> *"Subject to full payment of all fees, Vendor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to the Deliverables, excluding any Vendor Pre-Existing IP incorporated therein."*

**The Trap:** This is a conditional assignment — ownership of Deliverables does not vest in Client until *all* fees are paid in full. In a multi-SOW engagement with disputed invoices, rolling deliverables, or any payment dispute, Vendor retains legal ownership of work product that may already be embedded in Client's federal systems or submitted to a government end-user. This creates a scenario where:

- Client could be using Deliverables it does not legally own;
- Vendor could assert ownership rights over work product integrated into a federal program;
- A payment dispute — even a minor one — could retroactively cloud title to previously delivered work.

In a federal contracting environment, this is particularly dangerous. If the government asserts rights in the Deliverables under FAR clauses (e.g., FAR 52.227-14, Rights in Data), Client needs clean, unconditional title or a clear license chain. A conditional assignment breaks that chain.

**Required Fix:** Assignment should be unconditional upon delivery and acceptance, with Vendor retaining a security interest or license-back as a payment remedy — not ownership. Alternatively, assignment should vest incrementally upon delivery of each discrete Deliverable, with payment disputes addressed through separate contractual remedies rather than title suspension.

---

**2. Vendor Pre-Existing IP License Is Dangerously Narrow (§4.3)**

> *"...solely as incorporated in the Deliverables."*

**The Trap:** The license to use Vendor's Pre-Existing IP is restricted to use *as incorporated in the Deliverables* — meaning Client cannot extract, modify, update, or independently use any Vendor IP component even if operationally necessary. More critically:

- If a Deliverable becomes obsolete and Client needs to rebuild or migrate the system, the underlying Vendor IP components cannot be reused outside the original Deliverable context;
- There is no right to sublicense to the government end-user or prime contractor, which is almost certainly required under Client's upstream federal obligations;
- There is no right to use the Vendor IP for maintenance, modification, or derivative works — meaning Client could be locked out of updating its own system without Vendor's consent.

In federal services, the government typically requires that contractors flow down broad data rights. A license this narrow may put Client in breach of its prime contract or agency agreement.

**Required Fix:** Expand the license to include the right to use, modify, maintain, and create derivative works of Vendor Pre-Existing IP as necessary to use, support, and maintain the Deliverables. Add an explicit sublicense right to Client's government customers to the extent required by applicable federal agreements.

---

**3. Confidentiality Survival Period Insufficient for Federal Context (§5.3)**

> *"Confidentiality obligations survive termination of this Agreement for a period of five (5) years."*

**The Trap:** Five years is a standard commercial survival period — it is not appropriate here. Meridian Federal Partners operates in the national security and federal services space. The information exchanged under this agreement may include:

- Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) subject to 32 CFR Part 2002;
- Information subject to NIST SP 800-171 or CMMC obligations;
- Sensitive program data with classification or sensitivity designations that carry indefinite protection requirements;
- Trade secrets with no natural expiration.

A five-year sunset on confidentiality obligations means that in year six, Vendor is legally free to disclose everything it learned about Client's federal programs, system architectures, personnel, and operational methods. This is not a theoretical risk — it is a structural vulnerability.

**Required Fix:** Confidentiality obligations for Confidential Information that constitutes trade secrets should survive indefinitely (or for the duration of trade secret status). For all other Confidential Information in a national security context, survival should be extended to a minimum of ten (10) years, with a carve-out for information subject to government-mandated protection requirements that survives for as long as required by applicable law or regulation.

---

**4. Arbitration Clause Creates Federal Jurisdiction Conflict (§10.2)**

> *"Any dispute arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be resolved by binding arbitration..."*

**The Trap:** This is a mandatory, binding, all-disputes arbitration clause with no carve-outs. In a federal contracting context, this creates multiple serious problems:

- **Government Contract Disputes Act (CDA) Conflicts:** If the underlying work is performed under a federal prime contract, certain disputes may be subject to mandatory CDA jurisdiction — the Boards of Contract Appeals or the Court of Federal Claims. A blanket arbitration clause does not override federal statutory jurisdiction and could create parallel proceedings or jurisdictional confusion;
- **No Injunctive Relief Carve-Out:** There is no provision allowing either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in court. In an IP misappropriation or confidentiality breach scenario — both of which are explicitly carved out of the liability cap — the inability to seek immediate injunctive relief in court could be catastrophic. Arbitration timelines are incompatible with emergency IP protection;
- **Wilmington, Delaware Venue:** While defensible for a Delaware-governed agreement, this imposes a fixed venue on Client regardless of where operations, personnel, or the relevant federal program are located.

**Required Fix:** Add an explicit carve-out permitting either party to seek emergency injunctive or equitable relief in any court of competent jurisdiction without waiving arbitration rights for the underlying merits. Add a carve-out for disputes that are subject to mandatory federal jurisdiction. Consider adding a venue flexibility provision or mutual consent requirement for venue.

---

### 🟡 SIGNIFICANT — Strongly Recommended Changes

---

**5. Subcontractor Rights Are Unconstrained (§2.3)**

> *"Vendor may use subcontractors to perform portions of the Services, provided that Vendor remains responsible for subcontractor performance and compliance with this Agreement."*

**The Trap:** Vendor has unilateral, unlimited right to subcontract any portion of the Services to any third party. In a federal services engagement, this is a significant compliance gap:

- No requirement for Client consent before subcontracting sensitive work;
- No restriction on subcontractors who may be foreign nationals, foreign-owned entities, or debarred parties — all of which could create ITAR, EAR, FOCI, or FAR compliance violations for Client;
- No requirement that subcontractors hold required security clearances;
- No flow-down requirement for specific contractual protections (e.g., CMMC, CUI handling, background check requirements).

Vendor's "responsibility" for subcontractor compliance is a contractual remedy after the fact — it does not prevent the compliance violation from occurring in the first place.

**Required Fix:** Require Client's prior written consent for subcontracting of any material portion of the Services. Require that all subcontractors be subject to background checks, security clearance requirements, and all applicable federal compliance obligations as a condition of engagement. Prohibit use of subcontractors who are debarred, suspended, or subject to FOCI concerns.

---

**6. "Knowingly Infringe" Qualifier Guts the IP Warranty (§6.2)**

> *"...the Deliverables, as delivered, will not **knowingly** infringe the Intellectual Property Rights of any third party."*

**The Trap:** The word "knowingly" transforms this from a warranty into a near-nullity. Vendor only warrants against infringement it is *aware of* at the time of delivery. This means:

- If Vendor's developers incorporated infringing open-source code without realizing it, there is no warranty breach;
- If a patent troll asserts rights against Client based on Vendor's Deliverables, Vendor can disclaim warranty liability by claiming it was unaware of the patent;
- Client bears the litigation risk for infringement that Vendor's own work product caused.

This is particularly problematic because the IP indemnification in §9.1(b) covers third-party IP claims against the Deliverables — but if the warranty is limited to knowing infringement, there may be an argument that Vendor's indemnification obligation is similarly constrained by the warranty scope.

**Required Fix:** Remove "knowingly." The warranty should be an unqualified representation that the Deliverables do not infringe third-party IP rights, consistent with the indemnification obligation in §9.1(b). Vendor can manage this risk through its own IP clearance process — that risk should not be transferred to Client.

---

**7. Termination for Convenience Leaves Client Exposed on Sunk Costs (§7.2 / §7.4)**

> *"Upon termination, Client shall pay Vendor for all Services performed and expenses incurred through the effective date of termination."*

**The Trap:** The termination-for-convenience payment obligation is open-ended. "Expenses incurred" is undefined and uncapped. In a time-and-materials or cost-plus engagement, Vendor could front-load expenses, commit to long-term subcontractor agreements, or incur significant costs in the final weeks before a termination notice takes effect — all of which Client would be obligated to pay. There is no:

- Cap on reimbursable expenses;
- Requirement that expenses be pre-approved or consistent with SOW budgets;
- Obligation on Vendor to mitigate costs upon receiving a termination notice;
- Definition of what constitutes a reimbursable "expense."

**Required Fix:** Define "expenses" by reference to SOW-approved budget categories. Require Vendor to take reasonable steps to mitigate costs upon receipt of a termination notice. Cap reimbursable expenses at amounts consistent with the applicable SOW budget. Require expense documentation and Client approval for expenses above a defined threshold.

---

**8. No Data Security or Breach Notification Obligations**

**The Trap:** This agreement contains zero provisions addressing:

- Data security standards (NIST 800-171, CMMC, FedRAMP, ISO 27001, or any other framework);
- Handling of government data, CUI, or personally identifiable information (PII);
- Vendor's obligation to notify Client of a security incident or data breach;
- Breach notification timelines (federal contracts typically require 1-hour or 72-hour notification windows);
- Data destruction or return obligations upon termination.

For a federal contractor operating in the national security space, the absence of these provisions is not a minor gap — it is a compliance failure waiting to happen. A Vendor breach with no contractual notification obligation leaves Client unable to fulfill its own upstream reporting requirements.

**Required Fix:** Add a dedicated Data Security and Incident Response section requiring Vendor to maintain appropriate security controls, notify Client of any security incident within a defined timeframe (recommend 24 hours for suspected incidents, consistent with CISA guidance), cooperate in breach response, and certify compliance with applicable federal data handling requirements.

---

### 🟢 OBSERVATIONS — Low Risk / Noted for Record

---

**9. Governing Law — Delaware (§10.1)**

Delaware is a commercially neutral and well-developed jurisdiction for contract law. No objection from a legal standpoint, but Client should confirm this is consistent with any forum selection requirements in its prime contract or agency agreement.

---

**10. Liability Cap Mechanics Are Structurally Sound (§8.1 / §8.2)**

The 12-month trailing fee cap with carve-outs for indemnification, confidentiality breach, IP violations, gross negligence/willful misconduct, and unpaid fees is a well-structured liability framework. The exceptions are appropriately broad and the carve-outs are clearly enumerated. No changes recommended to the cap structure itself, though the adequacy of the cap should be evaluated against the value and sensitivity of each individual SOW engagement.

---

**11. Notice Provision Permits Email (§10.7)**

Email with confirmation is permitted for formal notices. In high-stakes termination or indemnification scenarios, this creates ambiguity around delivery confirmation and timing. Recommend requiring that notices for termination, indemnification claims, and breach cure periods be delivered by overnight courier or certified mail only, with email permitted as a secondary/courtesy copy.
