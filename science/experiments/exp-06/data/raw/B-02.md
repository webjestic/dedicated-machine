# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component prompt engineering framework organized into a "World Layer" (Persona, Context, Stakes, Tone) and "Task Layer" (Instructions, Examples, Format, Request), with the central claim that the procedural sub-component of Persona (P_p) is the primary determinant of reasoning quality by determining the model's "consideration set." The authors validate this framework across eleven experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, reporting behavioral evidence for a P_p/P_d distinction, a two-vector Stakes taxonomy with measured token-delta coefficients, a converging ~11% Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient, and a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR prompt. The paper is ambitious in scope and unusually candid about the distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic speculation.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine epistemic discipline on mechanistic claims.** The explicit separation of "behavioral claims" (Appendix B) from "mechanistic hypotheses" (§6.1) is commendable and uncommon in the prompt engineering literature. The authors consistently flag where transformer-internal explanations are conjectural rather than confirmed, which is the correct scientific posture given current interpretability limitations.

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is a substantive conceptual contribution.** The observation that procedural identity language ("after you understand how the lock is acquired, you ask what happens to the write if the lock has already expired") produces qualitatively different outputs than dispositional identity language ("I cannot help but analyze every aspect deeply") is non-obvious and, if replicable, practically important. The Variant J result (0/10 with rich Context and weak Persona vs. 10/10 with strong P_p) is a striking data point.

- **The self-prediction gap observation is novel and interesting.** The finding that the authoring model rated Variant L at 6/10 while it scored 10/10 experimentally—explained via a descriptive/enactive register distinction—is a genuinely interesting phenomenon that has implications for prompt evaluation methodology beyond this framework.

- **The CO-STAR comparison uses a reasonable isolation methodology.** Generating the CO-STAR prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of PCSIEFTR is a meaningful attempt to control for author confounds. The 10/10 vs. 10/10 split on premise rejection vs. compliance validation is the paper's most compelling result.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is a concrete, falsifiable quantitative claim.** Three independent run sets converging on ~11% (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) is the kind of replicable quantitative regularity that distinguishes empirical work from anecdote. The scope re-framing mechanism is a specific, testable prediction.

- **The Confident Error characterization (exp-04c)** is a practically important failure mode—a model producing well-elaborated, confidently wrong output under Task Stakes with weak Persona—that has direct production safety relevance and is not well-characterized in prior work.

- **Cross-model replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro** is a meaningful positive result, particularly the finding that J on Gemini used 3,906 thinking tokens and still scored 0/10, which directly addresses the "just add more compute" objection.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The evaluation methodology has a critical single-evaluator confound that is never addressed.** The paper does not state who scored the outputs or how inter-rater reliability was established. With binary scoring on "detection of the primary structural failure mode, with named fix," the ground truth criterion is defined by the authors, and it is unclear whether the scoring was performed blind to condition. If the same researchers who designed the prompts also scored the outputs, the 10/10 vs. 0/10 results are uninterpretable regardless of how well-designed the stimuli are. This is not a minor methodological concern—it is the central validity threat to every behavioral claim in the paper.

- **The experimental stimuli are not released or fully described, making replication impossible.** The paper references `data/exp-*/raw/` but this data is not accessible in the submission. The actual prompt text for Variants A through P, the globalAuditProvider scenario, the Redis lock code, and the MSA contract are not reproduced. Without these, no reader can verify whether the P_p/P_d distinction is in the prompt text or in the scorer's interpretation of it. This is a fatal reproducibility problem for a top-tier venue.

- **The primary independent variable (P_p vs. P_d) is not operationalized with sufficient precision for replication.** The paper characterizes P_p as encoding "a search algorithm" and P_d as encoding "engagement energy," but the boundary between these categories is defined post-hoc by the authors. There is no operationalization that would allow an independent researcher to classify a novel Persona as P_p or P_d before running experiments. The framework risks being unfalsifiable: any Persona that produces good results can be retroactively labeled P_p; any that fails can be labeled P_d.

- **The sample sizes and statistical analysis are inadequate for the claims being made.** n=10 per variant is the standard cell size throughout. With binary outcomes, a 10/10 vs. 0/10 split has p < 0.001 under a binomial test, which is reassuring for the primary finding. However, many secondary claims rest on token count comparisons (e.g., the 710-token gap between variants A and B in exp-02, the D < A < B < C ordering) with no variance reported, no confidence intervals, and no statistical tests. Token counts at temperature 0.5 have substantial run-to-run variance; the paper reports means without standard deviations for any comparison. The two-vector formula coefficients ($\alpha_{amp}$, $\beta_{brake}$) are presented as "measured" but are derived from mean token deltas without uncertainty quantification.

- **The two-vector Stakes formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is not actually estimated.** The paper presents this as having "empirically grounded coefficients" and "measured token-delta coefficients," but the actual values of $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ are never reported. The token deltas from exp-04b (+236, +578, +67) are used to argue for an ordering of magnitudes, but these are not coefficients in any standard sense—they are raw mean differences between conditions that conflate multiple variables. The formula is presented with the notation of a fitted model but without the estimation procedure that would make it one.

- **The CO-STAR comparison confounds framework with prompt content.** The CO-STAR prompt's Context section contains the sentence "Audit logging is a critical control to ensure every user action is traceable and to prevent ghost transactions," which the paper correctly identifies as installing a compliance prior. But this is a content choice by the CO-STAR prompt author, not a structural property of CO-STAR as a framework. CO-STAR does not require the Context section to validate the premise being tested. A CO-STAR prompt with a skeptical Context section—"The PR description frames blocking await as a compliance requirement; evaluate whether this framing is correct"—might well produce premise rejection. The paper's claim that "this is the Context component working exactly as designed" is not demonstrated; it is asserted. The comparison is between one specific CO-STAR instantiation and one specific PCSIEFTR instantiation, not between frameworks.

- **The consideration-set mechanism is described in transformer-internal terms that are not supported by the evidence.** The claim that P_p performs "K/V space pre-filtering" is presented as a "mechanistic hypothesis," which is appropriate. But the paper repeatedly uses this hypothesis as an explanatory resource in ways that go beyond what the behavioral evidence supports. Statements like "P_p pre-weights the search space toward skeptical interrogation of requirements" are offered as explanations of the CO-STAR result, but the behavioral evidence is equally consistent with much simpler accounts (e.g., the P_p prompt contains explicit instructions to question requirements, which is a direct prompt effect, not a K/V filtering effect). The mechanistic framing adds no predictive value beyond the behavioral description and risks misleading readers about what is known.

- **The scope of generalization is overstated relative to the evidence.** The paper claims "all principal behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families" based on a single replication of two variants (I and J) on Gemini 2.5 Pro. This is two data points on one model family, one task domain, and one experiment design. The legal domain generalization (exp-01f) uses the same model (Claude Sonnet 4.6) and a task designed by the same authors. "Cross-domain and cross-model generalization" as a section heading substantially overstates what two replications establish.

- **The paper conflates "the model did not find the bug" with "the bug was not in the consideration set."** The consideration-set framing is a strong theoretical claim—that certain reasoning paths are structurally unreachable given a particular Persona. But the behavioral evidence (0/10 detection) is equally consistent with a weaker claim: that weak Persona makes the correct reasoning path less probable, not unreachable. The distinction matters because the stronger claim implies that no amount of additional prompting within the weak-Persona frame could produce the correct answer, while the weaker claim implies only that it is less likely. The paper does not test the stronger claim (e.g., by providing explicit hints to the weak-Persona condition and showing it still fails).

- **The experimental design does not adequately control for prompt length and specificity.** The paper's primary comparison is between prompts that differ in Persona type, but the P_p prompts are also substantially longer and more specific than the weak-Persona prompts. Variant J ("senior software engineer") vs. Variant I (rich procedural specification) differ in both Persona type and total prompt length/specificity. The paper argues that Context richness is held constant, but the Persona component itself varies in length and detail. It is not clear that the P_p/P_d distinction is doing the work rather than simply "more words in the prompt."

- **The paper does not engage seriously with alternative explanations.** Chain-of-thought prompting research (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) has established that prompting models to reason step-by-step substantially improves performance on reasoning tasks. The P_p Personas in this paper are, in substantial part, chain-of-thought prompts embedded in a Persona frame. The paper does not test whether a standard CoT prompt without the Persona framing would produce comparable results, which is the most important alternative explanation for the primary finding.

- **The masking test failure is presented as a limitation but is actually a validity threat.** The paper acknowledges that five experiments (exp-03 through exp-03d) designed to demonstrate Persona-overrides-Instructions all "failed on the masking dimension." The explanation offered—category overlap—is plausible, but the fact that the authors could not construct a clean test of their central Instructions mechanism after five attempts suggests either that the mechanism is weaker than claimed or that the task design is systematically confounded. The CO-STAR comparison is offered as the "structural equivalent," but it tests a different mechanism (premise rejection vs. Instructions override).

- **The ~$4.80 total experimental cost is presented as a feature but is a validity concern.** At $4.80 for 640 runs, the average cost per run is approximately $0.0075. This implies very short prompts and/or very short outputs, which is inconsistent with the reported token counts (many runs at the 2,500-token ceiling). This figure should be verified and explained; if accurate, it raises questions about whether the reported token counts are correct.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On scoring methodology:** Who scored the outputs, and was scoring performed blind to condition (Persona type, Stakes type, etc.)? What was the inter-rater agreement on the binary detection criterion? If a single rater scored all outputs, how do you address the experimenter-expectancy threat to validity?

- **On operationalization of P_p vs. P_d:** Can you provide a pre-registered operationalization of the P_p/P_d distinction that would allow an independent researcher to classify a novel Persona as one or the other before running experiments? Without this, the distinction is unfalsifiable. Specifically: what is the minimum unit of procedural language that qualifies as P_p, and how would you classify a Persona that contains one procedural sentence and three dispositional sentences?

- **On the CoT alternative explanation:** Have you tested whether a standard chain-of-thought prompt ("Think step by step, and specifically consider what happens to the write operation if the lock has already expired before the write executes") without the Persona framing produces comparable detection rates to your P_p variants? This is the most important alternative explanation for the primary finding, and its absence from the paper is a significant gap.

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Did you test a CO-STAR variant with a skeptical or neutral Context section (e.g., one that does not pre-validate the compliance framing)? If so, what were the results? If not, why not? The current comparison is between one CO-STAR instantiation and one PCSIEFTR instantiation, not between frameworks.

- **On the formula coefficients:** What are the actual numerical values of $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$? How were they estimated? What is the uncertainty on these estimates? The paper presents a formula with named coefficients but never reports the coefficients themselves.

- **On statistical analysis:** Can you provide standard deviations for all token count comparisons, and report the results of appropriate statistical tests (e.g., Mann-Whitney U for token count distributions, binomial tests for detection rates)? The D < A < B < C ordering claim in particular needs variance information to be interpretable.

- **On the consideration-set mechanism:** Have you tested whether providing explicit hints to the weak-Persona condition (e.g., "Consider what happens to the write operation if the lock expires during a GC pause") closes the detection gap? If weak Persona makes the correct reasoning path unreachable (strong claim) rather than merely less probable (weak claim), hints should not help. If hints do help, the consideration-set framing is an overstatement.

- **On the $4.80 cost figure:** Can you reconcile the reported total experimental cost of ~$4.80 with the reported token counts (many runs at the 2,500-token ceiling, 640 total runs)? At current Claude Sonnet pricing, 640 runs averaging even 1,000 output tokens would cost substantially more than $4.80. Is this figure accurate?

- **On the self-prediction gap:** The authoring model predicted Variant L at 6/10 and it scored 10/10. Was this a single prediction or an average across multiple predictions? What was the prediction for Variant J (which scored 0/10)? The gap is interesting, but a single data point in one direction is not sufficient to establish the descriptive/enactive register distinction as a systematic phenomenon.

- **On data availability:** Will the full prompt texts for all variants, the task stimuli (Redis lock code, MSA contract, globalAuditProvider scenario), and the raw outputs be released? Without these, the paper cannot be replicated, and the behavioral claims in Appendix B cannot be verified.

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper contains a genuinely interesting central observation—that procedural identity language in prompts produces qualitatively different reasoning behavior than dispositional identity language—and several specific findings (the ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient, the Confident Error characterization, the self-prediction gap) that would be publishable contributions if properly validated. However, the current submission has fatal methodological problems that preclude acceptance at a top-tier venue: the scoring methodology does not establish blind evaluation or inter-rater reliability; the primary stimuli are not released and cannot be replicated; the primary independent variable (P_p vs. P_d) is not operationalized for independent use; the most important alternative explanation (chain-of-thought prompting) is not tested; the formula coefficients are named but not reported; and the scope of generalization claims substantially exceeds what two cross-model replications support. These are not issues that can be addressed through minor revision—they require redesigning the core experiments with proper controls, blind scoring, and pre-registered operationalizations. The authors should also engage seriously with the interpretability literature (e.g., Anthropic's mechanistic interpretability work, probing studies on role-playing and persona adoption) before making claims about K/V space pre-filtering, even as hypotheses.
