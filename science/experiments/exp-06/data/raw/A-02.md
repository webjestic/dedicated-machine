# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes an eight-component, two-layer prompt engineering framework and argues that the Persona component — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality, with other components operating on what Persona has already installed. The central experimental apparatus consists of eleven experiment series (640 total runs) on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, testing whether procedural vs. dispositional Persona framing determines whether models detect structurally hidden failure modes. The paper additionally claims a converging ~11% Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient, a two-vector Stakes taxonomy with measured token-delta coefficients, and a 10/10 vs. 10/10 split against a CO-STAR comparison prompt.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is a genuine and testable contribution.** The paper does not merely assert that Persona matters; it operationalizes a specific structural difference (search algorithm vs. engagement energy) and tests it with a binary outcome criterion defined before each run. The J=0/10 result against I=10/10 with identical Context is the kind of clean ablation that prompt engineering papers rarely produce.

- **Epistemic discipline on mechanistic claims is consistently maintained.** The paper explicitly classifies behavioral claims separately from mechanistic hypotheses throughout, and the mechanistic framework section (§6) correctly frames the transformer mappings as "conceptual mappings, not literal implementation claims." This discipline is stated in the abstract and held through the body — a structural commitment that is more than cosmetic.

- **The self-prediction gap observation (§3.1.1, §7.2) is a novel and practically useful finding.** The gap between the authoring model's 6/10 prediction and the 10/10 execution result is not merely anecdotal; the paper provides a structural explanation (descriptive vs. enactive register) and a design implication (treat P_p evaluation as empirical, not authorial). This is the kind of finding that changes how practitioners build prompts.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is sound in one important respect.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of the framework is the correct approach for preventing author-advantage confounding. The paper names this methodology explicitly and explains why it matters.

- **The paper acknowledges its own masking-test failure honestly.** §7.1 names the category overlap problem directly, explains why five experiments failed to produce the intended clean split, and identifies what a valid masking test would require. This is the correct response to a negative result — not omission, not reframing as a positive, but structural diagnosis.

- **The Appendix B behavioral claim register is a genuine contribution to reproducibility.** Mapping each claim to its experimental basis in a structured table allows a reader to audit the claim-evidence chain without reconstructing it from the body text. This is not standard practice in prompt engineering papers and should be.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

### 1. Single-operator confound is uncontrolled and unacknowledged — the most serious structural flaw

All 640 runs were designed, scored, and interpreted by the same research team that developed the framework. The paper contains no inter-rater reliability measurement for the binary scoring criterion, no blind scoring procedure, no independent replication by a team without knowledge of the hypotheses, and no discussion of this as a limitation. For a paper making claims of the form "10/10 vs. 0/10," the scoring procedure is load-bearing. A binary criterion defined before each run is described but not operationalized in a way that would allow a third party to reproduce the scoring without judgment calls. The paper does not report whether any runs produced ambiguous outputs that required adjudication, nor how such cases were resolved. At a top-tier venue, this is a rejection-level gap for the primary claims.

### 2. N=10 per variant is insufficient for the precision of the quantitative claims

The paper presents token-delta coefficients ($\alpha_{amp}$, $\beta_{brake}$), a "converging" ~11% elaboration ceiling, and ordered magnitude claims ("Stakes type effect is 3–4× the Persona-strength effect"). None of these are accompanied by confidence intervals, standard deviations, or significance tests. The token counts reported (e.g., "A mean: 2,210 tokens; B mean: 1,500 tokens") are presented as measurements, not as estimates with uncertainty. At n=10, the variance in token output from a language model at temperature 0.5 is substantial enough that the reported differences could be within noise. The paper uses the word "converging" to describe three measurements of the same coefficient — but three measurements is not convergence in any statistical sense. The claim that the coefficient is "stable" requires a stability analysis, not three data points.

### 3. The two-task domain scope does not support the generalization claims

The paper claims cross-domain generalization on the basis of two domains: code review and legal contract review. Both are structured document analysis tasks with a single hidden structural failure mode as the ground truth. The claim that "the procedural search algorithm is domain-agnostic" is not supported by this evidence base. The paper does not test domains where the "hidden structural failure mode" framing does not apply — open-ended generation, multi-step planning, dialogue, mathematical reasoning, or any task where the ground truth is not a binary detect/miss criterion. The generalization claim in the abstract ("all principal behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families") is supported by one model (Gemini 2.5 Pro) on one experiment (exp-01g, two variants). This is not a cross-model generalization result; it is a single replication.

### 4. The CO-STAR comparison is confounded by task selection and cannot support the framework-level claim

The globalAuditProvider scenario was designed as a premise-rejection task — a task structure where P_p's specific encoded procedure ("I reject the implementation-as-requirement framing on sight") is directly applicable. The CO-STAR prompt was not designed for this task structure; it was designed by an operator given only the scenario. The comparison therefore conflates two variables: framework design and task-prompt fit. The paper acknowledges that CO-STAR's Context section "installed a prior" — but this is not a failure of CO-STAR as a framework; it is a failure of the specific CO-STAR prompt on a task specifically designed to expose dispositional framing. A valid framework comparison requires either (a) multiple tasks spanning the full range of task types both frameworks are designed for, or (b) a comparison where both prompts are optimized for the specific task by practitioners familiar with each framework. The current design tests whether a P_p-optimized PCSIEFTR prompt outperforms a CO-STAR prompt on a P_p-favoring task. The answer is yes, but this does not establish framework superiority.

### 5. The mechanistic pseudo-formula (§6.2) is not a formula

$$Response = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{Stakes \cdot (Request \cdot Persona^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$$

This expression mixes scalar quantities (Stakes), vector dot products ($Request \cdot Persona^T$), matrix operations (Softmax), and logical negation ($\neg Instructions$) without defining the type or dimensionality of any term. $\neg Instructions$ is not a defined mathematical operation on a token sequence. The expression cannot be evaluated, cannot be falsified, and does not generate predictions beyond those already stated in prose. The paper correctly labels it a "conceptual mapping," but its presence in a section titled "The Mechanistic Framework" and its formal notation give it an authority it does not earn. A conceptual mapping should be presented as a conceptual mapping — in prose, with explicit acknowledgment that the notation is metaphorical. The current presentation will mislead readers who take mathematical notation at face value.

### 6. The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is not a coefficient

The paper presents 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% across three run sets and calls this a "converging coefficient." These three measurements share the same task domain (code review), the same model (Claude Sonnet 4.6), the same prohibition type ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), and the same strong P_p Persona. The paper does not test whether the coefficient holds under a different prohibition type, a different task domain, a different Persona strength, or a different model. A coefficient that has been measured three times under identical conditions is a repeated measurement, not a generalization. The claim that this is a stable property of the Instructions component requires testing across the dimensions that were held constant.

### 7. The "consideration set" construct is behaviorally operationalized but theoretically underdefined

The paper's central theoretical contribution is that Persona determines the "consideration set" — which classes of reasoning paths exist in the model's reachable space. This is presented as a mechanistic claim distinct from "depth within a fixed space." But the paper does not operationalize the distinction between "not in the consideration set" and "in the consideration set but not reached at this temperature/token budget." J=0/10 is consistent with both interpretations: J may lack the zombie-write failure mode in its reachable space, or J may reach it with probability below the sampling threshold at n=10. The paper does not test this — for example, by running J at n=100, or by providing J with a hint and observing whether it can then reach the finding. The distinction between "unreachable" and "low-probability" is the entire theoretical claim, and the experimental design does not separate them.

### 8. Component asymmetry across the eight-component framework

The paper's experimental treatment is heavily asymmetric. Persona receives extensive experimental development across six experiment series. Stakes receives three series. Instructions receives five series (with acknowledged calibration failures). Context, Tone, Examples, Format, and Request receive minimal or no dedicated experimental treatment — they are described with behavioral claims that are either asserted without experimental basis or supported by secondary observations from experiments designed for other components. The paper claims to present an eight-component framework; it presents a three-component framework with five stubs. This is a component symmetry failure that the framework claim cannot absorb.

### 9. Temperature 0.5 is not justified and interacts with the primary claims

All Claude runs were conducted at temperature 0.5. The paper does not report whether the primary results hold at temperature 0 (where outputs are more deterministic) or at higher temperatures. This matters because the paper's central claim — that P_p installs a consideration set that determines reachability — is a claim about the model's search space, not about sampling variance. If the 10/10 vs. 0/10 results are temperature-sensitive, the consideration-set interpretation is weakened. If they are not, that is evidence worth reporting. The paper does not address this.

### 10. The "total cost: ~$4.80" disclosure undermines rather than supports the experimental claims

The paper reports total API cost as approximately $4.80 across 640 runs. This figure implies an average of less than one cent per run, which is inconsistent with the token counts reported (runs regularly reaching the 2,500-token ceiling, with thinking tokens on Gemini). More importantly, presenting cost as a positive signal — implicitly arguing that the experimental program is efficient — obscures the fact that low cost is a consequence of small n and limited model diversity, both of which are limitations. This disclosure should be removed or replaced with a transparent statement of the experimental scale's constraints.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On scoring reliability:** What was the exact operationalization of the binary scoring criterion for each experiment? Were any runs ambiguous under the criterion, and if so, how were they resolved? What is the inter-rater agreement if a second scorer, blind to the hypotheses, applies the criterion to the raw outputs?

- **On the consideration-set vs. low-probability distinction:** Have you tested whether Variant J can reach the zombie-write finding when given a hint, or when run at n=100? If J can reach the finding with a hint, the consideration-set interpretation (unreachable) is not supported over the low-probability interpretation (reachable but unlikely). What experiment would distinguish these?

- **On the ~11% coefficient:** Does the elaboration-ceiling coefficient hold when the prohibition type changes (e.g., "do not discuss performance implications"), when the Persona strength varies, or when the task domain is not code review? Three measurements under identical conditions are not a coefficient; what is the evidence that this generalizes?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Was the CO-STAR prompt author given any guidance on what a "strong" CO-STAR prompt looks like, or were they a naive user? If the CO-STAR prompt was generated by an AI system rather than a practitioner expert in CO-STAR, the comparison is between PCSIEFTR-optimized and CO-STAR-naive, not between frameworks. Can you provide the CO-STAR prompt and the generation methodology in full?

- **On temperature sensitivity:** Do the primary 10/10 vs. 0/10 results hold at temperature 0? At temperature 1.0? The consideration-set claim is about reachability, not sampling variance — if the results are temperature-sensitive, what does that imply for the interpretation?

- **On the formula in §6.2:** Can you specify the type and dimensionality of each term in the pseudo-formula? If $\neg Instructions$ is not a defined mathematical operation, why is it presented in formal notation rather than prose? What prediction does the formula generate that is not already stated in the prose description?

- **On component symmetry:** Tone, Examples, Format, and Request are described with behavioral claims but receive no dedicated experimental treatment. Are there experiments supporting the claims made about these components, or are these components included in the framework on theoretical grounds? If the latter, the paper should state this explicitly.

- **On cross-domain generalization:** The two tested domains (code review, legal contract review) share the structure of "find a single hidden structural failure mode in a document." What is the basis for the claim that the framework generalizes beyond this task structure? Have any experiments been run on tasks where the ground truth is not a binary detect/miss criterion?

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper contains a genuine empirical finding — that procedural Persona framing outperforms dispositional Persona framing on structurally hidden failure mode detection — but the experimental design does not support the precision, generality, or mechanistic framing of the claims as presented: the single-operator confound is uncontrolled, n=10 is insufficient for the quantitative claims made, the two-domain scope cannot support domain-agnostic generalization, the CO-STAR comparison is confounded by task selection, the central theoretical distinction (consideration set vs. low-probability reachability) is not experimentally separated, and five of eight framework components receive no dedicated experimental treatment. A substantially redesigned experimental program with blind scoring, adequate n, statistical testing, broader domain coverage, and symmetric component treatment would be required before the central claims can be evaluated at the standard this venue requires.
