# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component prompt engineering framework organized into a World Layer and Task Layer, and argues that Persona — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality by determining the "consideration set" of reachable reasoning paths. The authors validate this claim across eleven experiment series totaling 640 runs on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, and include a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR prompt. The paper is unusually self-aware about the distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic hypotheses, and the writing is precise enough that its structural problems are identifiable with corresponding precision.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is a genuine contribution.** The behavioral evidence that "senior software engineer" (P_d) and a procedurally-specified identity produce 0/10 vs. 10/10 on the same task with identical Context is a clean, interpretable finding. The authors do not overstate it — they note it is a behavioral observation, not a mechanistic proof.

- **The self-prediction gap observation is novel and practically useful.** The finding that the authoring model rated Variant L at 6/10 and it scored 10/10 is a concrete, actionable result that practitioners would not derive from first principles. The descriptive/enactive register distinction is a coherent explanation.

- **The paper maintains its stated epistemic distinctions more consistently than most work in this space.** The behavioral claim register in Appendix B is a genuine attempt at claim-evidence mapping. The mechanistic hypothesis table in §6.1 explicitly flags which claims are unverified at the activation level. This is not cosmetic — the distinction is maintained throughout the body text with only a few lapses (noted below).

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is sound in one important respect.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of PCSIEFTR is the correct design. The authors correctly identify that without this isolation, the comparison tests prompt authors, not frameworks.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is the paper's most replicable quantitative finding.** Three independent run sets converging at 11.0%, 11.3%, and 12.8% is a stable measurement. The authors correctly characterize it as a measurement of expression compression rather than cognitive suppression.

- **The Gemini zero-shot replication is meaningful.** Running Variants I and J without modification on a different model family and obtaining I=10/10, J=0/10 substantially strengthens the claim that P_p encodes a model-agnostic reasoning algorithm rather than a Claude-specific activation pattern.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

### 1. The experimental design cannot distinguish Persona effects from task-design effects — this is the paper's load-bearing flaw

The central claim is that P_p determines the consideration set. The primary evidence is that Variant J (weak Persona, rich Context) scores 0/10 on the zombie-write scenario while Variant I (strong P_p, rich Context) scores 10/10. But the paper does not establish that the zombie-write scenario is solvable by any model without a procedural prompt. If the task is calibrated to be solvable only by a model that has been explicitly told to simulate concurrent thread state across the full lock lifecycle, then the result is tautological: the prompt that contains the simulation instruction produces the simulation; the prompt that does not contain it does not. This is not a finding about Persona as a framework component — it is a finding about whether the prompt contains the relevant instruction.

The paper acknowledges a version of this problem in §7.1 ("calibration failures") but frames it as a limitation of the Instructions experiments, not of the core consideration-set claim. The problem applies equally to exp-01e. The authors need to demonstrate that P_p's procedural content is doing something beyond being an implicit instruction — that the *identity framing* of the procedure, rather than the procedure's presence anywhere in the prompt, is what matters. No experiment isolates this. A direct test would be: Instructions-only prompt containing the identical procedural steps as P_p (no identity framing), same task, same n. If Instructions-only matches P_p, the consideration-set mechanism is an Instructions effect, not a Persona effect.

This is not a minor gap. It is the experiment the paper's central claim requires.

### 2. N=10 per variant is insufficient for the strength of the claims being made, and the paper's statistical treatment is absent

The paper makes claims of the form "10/10 vs. 0/10" and treats these as definitive. A 10/10 result on n=10 has a 95% confidence interval for the true detection rate of approximately [0.69, 1.00]. A 0/10 result has a CI of [0.00, 0.31]. These intervals overlap substantially with a true rate of 0.5. The paper does not report a single confidence interval, p-value, or effect size anywhere. For a top-tier ML venue, this is disqualifying.

The problem is compounded by the paper's own observation that "P-01 (the single passing run) found the structural argument by stochastic sampling, not by design." If stochastic sampling can produce a correct result from a weak Persona at temperature 0.5, then the 0/10 results are not evidence of impossibility — they are evidence of low probability. The paper's language ("which classes of reasoning paths exist in the model's reachable space") implies impossibility. The evidence supports only improbability. These are structurally different claims, and the paper conflates them throughout.

The 640-run total is distributed across 11 experiment series with multiple variants each. Many individual comparisons rest on n=10 per cell. The ~11% coefficient is derived from three run sets of n=10. The two-vector formula coefficients (α_amp, β_brake) are derived from token means across n=10 runs. None of this is reported with uncertainty bounds.

### 3. The mechanistic framework is presented with more authority than the behavioral evidence warrants, despite the paper's stated disclaimers

The paper correctly states in §1.2 and §6.1 that mechanistic claims are hypotheses. But the pseudo-formula in §6.2 — $Response = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{Stakes \cdot (Request \cdot Persona^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$ — is presented in a format that implies quantitative precision it does not have. The terms $Request \cdot Persona^T$ and $Context$ are not vectors in any operationalizable sense. The formula cannot be computed, cannot be falsified in the form presented, and cannot generate predictions beyond those already generated by the verbal description. Its presence in the paper lends false formalism to what are verbal hypotheses.

Similarly, the two-vector formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is described as having "measured coefficients" and "concrete ordering of relative magnitudes." The measured quantities are token deltas from n=10 runs at temperature 0.5. Token delta is not a coefficient in a formula — it is an output measurement. The paper has not established that the relationship between Stakes type and token output is multiplicative, linear, or stable across tasks. The formula is a post-hoc description of a pattern in a small sample, not a validated model.

These are not stylistic complaints. The paper's stated commitment to distinguishing behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses is a strength. The formulas undermine that commitment.

### 4. The CO-STAR comparison confounds framework with prompt quality on a single task

The head-to-head comparison (exp-05) is the paper's most visible claim and its most methodologically fragile. The comparison is between one PCSIEFTR prompt and one CO-STAR prompt on one task. The paper correctly notes that the CO-STAR prompt was generated without knowledge of PCSIEFTR, which controls for author bias. But it does not control for the possibility that a CO-STAR prompt written with knowledge of the specific failure mode (premise rejection) would also reject the premise.

More critically: the paper's explanation for B's failure is that "CO-STAR's Context section installed a prior" that framed blocking audit persistence as correct. This is a claim about CO-STAR as a framework. But the prior was installed by the specific content the prompt author chose to put in the Context section — content that accepted the compliance framing. A CO-STAR prompt whose Context section said "audit logging requirements specify outcome, not implementation mechanism" would not have installed that prior. The failure is attributable to the prompt author's content choice, not to the CO-STAR framework's structure.

The paper is aware of this at one level — it notes "this is not a failure of the Context component — it is the Context component working exactly as designed." But this concession undermines the comparison's claim to be a framework-level result. If CO-STAR's Context component working as designed produces the failure, and the failure is in the content chosen for that component, then the comparison demonstrates that PCSIEFTR's P_p component is harder to misconfigure than CO-STAR's Context component — not that PCSIEFTR produces better outcomes in general.

A framework comparison requires multiple prompts per framework, written by multiple authors, across multiple tasks. One prompt pair on one task is a case study, not a comparison.

### 5. The paper's scope claims are not matched by its experimental coverage

The abstract claims the framework is "validated across eleven controlled experiments." The paper's primary domain is code review (distributed lock, idempotency, global middleware). The legal generalization (exp-01f) uses one MSA scenario. Cross-model replication uses one experiment (exp-01g) with two variants. The paper makes claims about "production AI systems" and "structurally hidden failure modes" as a general class, but the experimental base is two domains, one model family (with a single two-variant replication on a second), and task scenarios designed by the authors.

The paper does not test: retrieval-augmented generation, multi-turn conversation, tasks where the ground truth is not binary, tasks where the "structural failure mode" is contested rather than stipulated, or any domain where the authors did not design the task. The claim that "all principal behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families" is supported by two variants of one experiment on one alternative model. This is not zero-shot transfer across model families — it is a single replication point.

### 6. The ground-truth criterion is author-defined and not independently validated

Every experiment uses a binary scoring criterion defined before each run by the authors. The paper states this as a methodological virtue ("ground-truth criterion defined before each run"), and pre-registration of criteria is indeed good practice. But the criteria themselves are not validated. In exp-05, the ground-truth is that the blocking `await` is the architectural failure and should be removed. This is a reasonable engineering judgment, but it is a judgment. A reviewer who believed that synchronous audit logging is a legitimate compliance requirement (the position B's prompt author took) would score B's output as correct and A's as incorrect.

The paper does not report inter-rater reliability, does not describe whether any scorer other than the authors applied the criteria, and does not acknowledge that the ground-truth criteria for "hidden structural failure modes" are themselves contestable engineering positions. For behavioral claims of the form "10/10 vs. 0/10," the validity of the scoring criterion is not a minor methodological detail.

### 7. The Instructions override mechanism is named but not cleanly demonstrated

The paper names three override mechanisms (scope re-framing, premise-undermining, explicit override) and presents them as a taxonomy. But §7.1 acknowledges that all five masking experiments failed — both strong and weak Persona detected in all cases. The paper's explanation (category overlap problem) is plausible, but it means the taxonomy is derived from a series of failed experiments, not from a series of experiments that successfully isolated the mechanisms.

Scope re-framing is the primary finding, but the paper cannot demonstrate that scope re-framing is a P_p-specific behavior rather than a general model behavior. If weak Persona also scope-re-frames under the same prohibition (which the calibration failures suggest it does), then scope re-framing is not evidence for the P_p mechanism — it is evidence that the model generally finds permitted pathways to prohibited findings, regardless of Persona type.

## Questions for the Authors

1. **The critical missing experiment:** What happens when the procedural content of P_p is placed in the Instructions section rather than the Persona section, with an otherwise identical prompt? If Instructions-containing-the-procedure matches P_p on the zombie-write task, the consideration-set mechanism is an Instructions effect. Have you run this? If not, why not, and what is your prediction?

2. **Statistical reporting:** Can you provide confidence intervals for the 10/10 and 0/10 results? At n=10 and temperature 0.5, what is your estimate of the true detection rate difference between strong P_p and weak Persona, and what sample size would be required to detect that difference at p < 0.05 with 80% power?

3. **CO-STAR comparison scope:** Have you tested PCSIEFTR against CO-STAR on tasks where the failure mode is not premise rejection? On tasks where the CO-STAR author is given the same information about the failure mode that the PCSIEFTR author had? On tasks where the failure mode is not designed by the PCSIEFTR authors?

4. **Ground-truth validity:** Who scored the outputs? Were scorers blind to condition? What is the inter-rater reliability on the binary criterion? For exp-05 specifically: did any scorer with domain expertise disagree with the stipulation that the blocking `await` is unambiguously the architectural failure?

5. **The formula's operationalizability:** The two-vector formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is described as having measured coefficients. What are the numerical values of α_amp and β_brake? How were they estimated from token deltas? What is the formula's out-of-sample prediction on a held-out experiment?

6. **Scope re-framing specificity:** In the masking experiments, did weak Persona variants also scope re-frame under the prohibition, or did they simply comply? If both Persona types scope re-frame, what is the evidence that scope re-framing is a P_p mechanism rather than a general model behavior?

7. **Task design independence:** The zombie-write, MSA carve-out, and globalAuditProvider scenarios were all designed by the authors. Were these scenarios designed before or after the Persona variants were written? Is there a risk that the tasks were calibrated to the P_p variants' capabilities?

8. **The self-prediction gap replication:** The Variant L prediction (6/10 predicted, 10/10 scored) is presented as a structural finding. Is this based on a single instance? Has the self-prediction gap been measured systematically across multiple Personas and multiple authoring models?

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper contains a genuine behavioral finding — that procedurally-specified Persona outperforms credential-specified Persona on structurally hidden failure modes — but the central mechanistic claim (consideration-set determination) cannot be distinguished from a simpler explanation (procedural instructions work better than credential labels regardless of where they appear in the prompt), the statistical treatment is absent at a level that is disqualifying for this venue, and the primary comparison (CO-STAR head-to-head) rests on a single task pair that cannot support framework-level conclusions.
