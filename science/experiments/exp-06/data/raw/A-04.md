# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component, two-layer prompt engineering framework, and argues that Persona — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality by determining a "consideration set" of reachable reasoning paths. The central empirical claim is demonstrated through a series of eleven experiment series (640 total runs) on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, culminating in a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR prompt in which P_p rejects a flawed compliance premise 10/10 times while CO-STAR validates it 10/10 times. The paper is unusually self-aware about its epistemic limits, explicitly classifying mechanistic claims as hypotheses, but this self-awareness is not uniformly applied and does not resolve the paper's structural problems.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is a genuine and non-obvious contribution.** The observation that procedural identity language ("after you understand how the lock is acquired, you ask what happens to the write if the lock has already expired") produces qualitatively different outputs from dispositional identity language ("I cannot help but analyze every aspect deeply") is not obvious, is operationalized clearly, and is tested with a design that isolates the variable. The Variant J result — 0/10 with rich Context and weak Persona, same task as I's 10/10 — is a clean data point.

- **The self-prediction gap observation is original and practically important.** The finding that the authoring model rated Variant L at 6/10 and it scored 10/10 identifies a real and underappreciated failure mode in prompt development practice. The mechanistic account (descriptive vs. enactive register) is labeled as hypothesis, which is appropriate. The practical implication — evaluate P_p empirically, not by inspection — is actionable.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is stronger than typical framework comparisons.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of PCSIEFTR or Variant A's design is a meaningful methodological choice that reduces the most obvious confound (prompt author advantage). The paper names this explicitly.

- **The three-run convergence of the ~11% Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient is the paper's most credible quantitative finding.** Three independent run sets producing 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% is genuine convergence, not a single measurement dressed as a pattern. The claim is appropriately scoped: the coefficient describes elaboration compression, not cognitive masking.

- **The explicit behavioral/mechanistic claim register (§6.1, Appendix B) is a structural virtue.** Most prompt engineering papers do not separate what was measured from what was inferred. This paper does, and the separation is maintained with reasonable consistency.

- **The "Confident Error" finding (exp-04c) is the most practically consequential result in the paper.** A weak Persona under Task Stakes producing 1,500–1,950 tokens of fully elaborated wrong-direction analysis — not uncertainty, not truncation, but confident structured wrongness — is a production safety finding that practitioners need. It is also the result most likely to be misread as a positive Stakes endorsement, which the paper correctly flags.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by structural severity)

- **The entire experimental record rests on a single task type with a single ground-truth structure, evaluated by a single scorer with no inter-rater reliability reported.** Every experiment uses either a distributed systems code review or a legal contract review, both designed by the paper's authors, both with a single "correct" finding pre-specified by those same authors. The binary scoring criterion ("detection of the primary structural failure mode, with named fix") is defined before each run, but there is no indication that any run was scored by an independent evaluator, that the ground-truth criterion was validated by domain experts, or that scorer agreement was measured. For a paper making claims about "reasoning quality" as a generalizable property, this is a load-bearing gap. The 10/10 and 0/10 results are only as reliable as the scoring procedure, and the scoring procedure is underdescribed and unvalidated. This is not a minor methodological note — it is the foundation on which every quantitative claim rests.

- **The claim of "cross-domain generalization" is supported by two domains, both designed by the authors, both involving hidden structural traps requiring temporal simulation.** The paper frames legal contract review as domain generalization from code review. It is not. Both tasks share the same cognitive structure: a surface-level reading misses a failure mode that is only visible when simulating system state across time or across boundaries. This is one task type in two surface domains. The claim that "the procedural search algorithm is domain-agnostic" (§5) is not supported by the evidence base. A domain-agnostic claim requires testing on tasks where the cognitive structure differs — factual retrieval, open-ended generation, classification, summarization. Track B (factual reasoning control in exp-02) is the only gesture toward this, and it is five questions per run with no description of question selection, difficulty calibration, or domain coverage.

- **The paper's central quantitative contribution — the two-vector Stakes formula with "measured coefficients" — is not a formula with measured coefficients.** The formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ contains four terms ($R$, $P$, $S_i$, $S_t$) none of which are defined on a numerical scale. The "coefficients" $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$ are never given numerical values. The table in §3.4 (exp-04b) reports token deltas — +236, +578, +67 — and labels them as "measured coefficients," but token delta is not a coefficient in any standard sense. A coefficient requires a defined unit for both the independent and dependent variable and a functional relationship between them. What the paper has measured is token count differences across conditions. Presenting this as a formula with coefficients is a category error that will not survive scrutiny at a venue where quantitative claims are read carefully. The abstract's phrase "two-vector Stakes formula with measured coefficients" is the most overclaimed sentence in the paper.

- **n=10 per variant is insufficient for the strength of the claims being made, and the paper does not acknowledge this as a limitation.** The paper presents 10/10 and 0/10 results as decisive. At n=10, a 10/10 result has a 95% confidence interval of approximately [0.69, 1.00] under a binomial model. A 0/10 result is symmetric. The difference between I (10/10) and J (0/10) is real and large, but the paper treats it as a precise measurement rather than as a strong signal requiring replication at larger n. More critically, several results that are presented as supporting distinct mechanisms — the +710 token gap between A and B in exp-02, the D<A<B<C ordering in exp-04b — are based on mean token counts from 10 runs with no variance reported, no confidence intervals, and no statistical tests. The paper reports means as if they were stable parameters. They are not.

- **The mechanistic framework (§6) violates the paper's own stated epistemic standard in ways that accumulate into a structural problem.** The paper correctly states in §1.2 that "mechanistic claims about transformer internals are hypotheses." But throughout §3, mechanistic language appears in claim-making positions rather than hypothesis positions. "P_p performs K/V space pre-filtering" (§3.1.1) is stated as a mechanism, not a hypothesis, in the section that introduces the concept. "Stakes functions as inverse temperature on the softmax distribution" (§3.1.3) is stated as a mechanism in the taxonomy section. The pseudo-formula in §6.2 uses actual attention notation ($QK^T$, $\sqrt{d_k}$, softmax) in a way that implies mechanistic correspondence. The paper does walk these back in §6.1 and §6.2, but the walk-back comes after the claims have been used to organize the entire framework. A reader who reads §3 before §6 — which is every reader — will have absorbed the mechanistic framing before encountering the qualification. The distinction is stated but not maintained in the structure of the paper.

- **The CO-STAR comparison tests one scenario against one CO-STAR prompt, and the result is attributed to framework-level differences.** The CO-STAR prompt was generated by "a separate AI system in an isolated incognito session." This controls for author knowledge but does not control for prompt quality within the CO-STAR framework. A single CO-STAR prompt is not a representative sample of CO-STAR's capability space. The paper's conclusion — "CO-STAR's structured Objective section was processed downstream of the already-installed compliance frame" — is a mechanistic claim about why CO-STAR failed, derived from one prompt instance. The paper cannot distinguish between "CO-STAR as a framework installs wrong priors" and "this particular CO-STAR prompt installed a wrong prior." The comparison is suggestive and well-designed for a pilot study. It is not sufficient to support the framework-level claim made in the abstract and conclusion.

- **The Instructions experiments (exp-03 series) failed to demonstrate their primary hypothesis and the paper does not adequately account for this.** Five experiments were designed to show that strong P_p overrides Instructions while weak Persona complies. All five produced calibration failures — both variants detected. The paper reframes this as revealing the "category overlap problem" and presents the exp-05 premise-rejection result as the structural equivalent. This reframing is intellectually honest but it means the paper's stated contribution — "three override mechanisms named and ordered by visibility" — rests on a series of experiments that did not produce the intended result. The override mechanisms are inferred from the calibration failures, not demonstrated through a clean split. The paper should state this directly rather than presenting the exp-05 result as completing the arc the exp-03 series began.

- **The paper makes no attempt to characterize variance, distribution shape, or run-to-run consistency for any continuous measurement.** Token counts are reported as means. The single exception — noting that Variant H has a "floor at 620 tokens" — is not systematic. For a paper that uses token counts as its primary continuous measurement and derives ordering claims, coefficient estimates, and termination mechanism inferences from those counts, the absence of any distributional characterization is a methodological gap. The reader cannot assess whether the D<A<B<C ordering is stable or whether it reflects the means of overlapping distributions.

- **The component symmetry problem: the eight-component framework receives asymmetric treatment.** Persona receives approximately 40% of the experimental and theoretical treatment. Context, Stakes, and Instructions receive meaningful treatment. Tone, Examples, Format, and Request receive one to three paragraphs each with no dedicated experiments. The paper's claim to be an "eight-component framework" is not supported by symmetric treatment of those eight components. This is not a fatal flaw — Persona may genuinely dominate — but the framework claim is broader than the evidence base, and the paper does not acknowledge this asymmetry as a limitation.

- **The limitations section is absent as a named section.** §7 is labeled "Discussion" and contains two subsections on specific gaps (masking test, self-prediction gap) plus practical design rules. There is no systematic treatment of what the paper cannot claim, what domains it has not tested, what model families it has not tested, what task types are out of scope, or what would falsify the central claims. The two gaps that are named are both gaps the authors discovered during the research — they are not a comprehensive accounting of what the paper does not establish.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On scoring validity:** Who scored the binary detection outcomes, and what was the inter-rater reliability? If the primary author scored all runs, what procedure was used to prevent motivated scoring? The entire quantitative record depends on this answer.

- **On the formula:** What are the numerical values of $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$? If they cannot be given numerical values with units, what is the paper's claim for the formula beyond a qualitative ordering of effects? The abstract's phrase "measured coefficients" requires a specific answer.

- **On domain generalization:** Can the authors identify a task domain where the cognitive structure differs from "hidden structural trap requiring temporal simulation" and test whether the P_p/P_d distinction holds? Track B is the only candidate, and it is underdescribed. What would a result look like that would falsify the claim of domain-agnosticism?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** How many CO-STAR prompts were generated and tested before the one reported? If only one was generated and tested, the comparison is a single data point. If multiple were generated, what was the selection criterion?

- **On statistical inference:** For the token count ordering claims (D<A<B<C), what are the standard deviations? Do the distributions overlap? A mean ordering from n=10 samples with unknown variance does not establish a reliable ordering.

- **On the mechanistic framework:** The paper claims the mechanistic hypotheses "generated correct behavioral predictions." Which predictions were generated before the experiments and which were generated after? Predictions generated post-hoc from behavioral observations are not evidence for a mechanism. A clear pre-registration or prediction log would change the assessment of §6 substantially.

- **On falsifiability:** What result would falsify the consideration-set mechanism? If strong P_p on a task with a hidden structural trap produced 0/10, would that falsify the claim, or would it be attributed to poor P_p construction? The paper needs a criterion that distinguishes "P_p was weak" from "the mechanism failed."

- **On the exp-03 reframing:** The paper states that exp-05 provides "the structural equivalent" of the masking test the exp-03 series failed to produce. But exp-05 tests premise rejection, not Instructions override. These are different phenomena. Can the authors demonstrate a clean Instructions override split — strong P_p detects despite prohibition, weak Persona misses because of compliance — without relying on the category overlap problem as an explanation for why the split did not appear?

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper identifies a genuine and practically important phenomenon — that procedural identity specification produces qualitatively different reasoning outputs than dispositional identity specification — and presents it with more epistemic care than is typical in the prompt engineering literature, but the experimental infrastructure is insufficient to support the strength of the claims made: single-author binary scoring with no inter-rater reliability, n=10 per variant with no variance reporting, a quantitative formula whose terms have no numerical definitions, domain generalization claimed from two structurally identical task types, and a framework-level CO-STAR comparison derived from a single comparison prompt instance. The paper should be substantially revised to either scale down its claims to match the evidence base or scale up its experimental infrastructure to match its claims; in its current form, the gap between the two is too large for acceptance at this venue.
