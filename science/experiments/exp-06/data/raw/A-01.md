# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component, two-layer prompt engineering framework, and argues that Persona — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality by determining a model's "consideration set." The authors support this with eleven experiment series (640 total runs) on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, including a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR prompt, and present additional claims about a P_p/P_d distinction, a two-vector Stakes taxonomy with measured coefficients, and a converging ~11% Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d behavioral distinction is the paper's most credible finding.** The contrast between Variant I (10/10) and Variant J (0/10) on identical context is a clean, interpretable result. The legal domain replication (exp-01f) and cross-model replication (exp-01g) strengthen it. This is the kind of finding that merits publication if properly scoped.

- **The paper explicitly distinguishes behavioral claims from mechanistic hypotheses.** Section 6.1 and the claim register in Appendix B are genuine epistemic discipline. The authors do not claim to have opened the model; they claim to have made predictions from a hypothesis and confirmed them behaviorally. This is the correct framing for this class of work, and it is maintained more consistently than in most prompt engineering papers.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is sound in one important respect.** Generating the CO-STAR prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of the framework is a legitimate attempt to prevent author-confounded comparison. The 10/10 vs. 10/10 split on a binary premise-rejection criterion is the sharpest result in the paper.

- **The self-prediction gap observation (§3.1.1, §7.2) is a genuinely novel and useful finding.** The gap between the authoring model's predicted score (6/10) and the execution score (10/10) for Variant L is interesting and has practical implications. It is appropriately hedged.

- **The paper names its own failure modes.** The masking test gap (§7.1) and the category overlap problem are acknowledged, not buried. The authors correctly identify that five experiments failed to produce the clean demonstration they were designed for, and they explain why. This is structurally honest.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The central causal claim is not supported by the experimental design.** The paper claims that Persona "determines the consideration set — which classes of reasoning paths exist in the model's reachable space." This is a strong causal and mechanistic claim. What the experiments demonstrate is a correlation: prompts with procedural Persona language produce better outcomes on specific hidden-failure-mode tasks than prompts with thin Persona language. The confound is uncontrolled prompt quality. Variant I and Variant J differ not only in Persona type but in total prompt quality, specificity, and the cognitive work encoded in the prompt text. The paper has not isolated Persona as the causal variable because it cannot hold "everything else equal" when the thing being varied is the substantive content of the prompt. A prompt that says "after you understand how the lock is acquired and renewed, you ask what happens to the critical write if the lock has already expired" is not just a different Persona — it is a partial answer to the task. The consideration-set mechanism may be real, but the experiments do not distinguish it from "prompts that encode more task-relevant reasoning produce better task-relevant outputs." This is the load-bearing problem. Every downstream claim — the two-vector formula, the Stakes taxonomy, the Instructions ceiling — rests on a causal architecture that has not been established.

- **The ~11% Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is presented as a converging empirical constant, but the evidence base does not support this characterization.** Three measurements (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) from the same model, same task domain (code review), same prohibition type ("CI Verification only"), and same strong P_p Persona are not a converging coefficient — they are three measurements of the same phenomenon under nearly identical conditions. The paper's own language ("converging at approximately 11%") implies cross-condition stability. What has been measured is within-condition consistency. Whether this ratio holds for different prohibition types, different task domains, different Persona strengths, or different models is entirely unknown. The claim as stated in the abstract and repeated throughout the paper is overclaimed relative to the evidence.

- **The two-vector Stakes formula presents token-delta measurements as "empirically grounded coefficients" for a formula, but the formula is not fitted to data.** The formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is presented with a table of token deltas (exp-04b: +236, +578, +67). These are not coefficient estimates — they are raw differences between four variants in a single experiment. The paper does not fit $\alpha_{amp}$ or $\beta_{brake}$ to data, does not report units or normalization, does not test the formula's predictions against held-out runs, and does not establish that the multiplicative structure of the formula is correct rather than, say, additive. Calling these "measured coefficients" is a category error. They are token-count differences. The formula is a conceptual model dressed in quantitative notation.

- **n=10 per variant is insufficient for the strength of the claims being made, particularly for binary outcomes.** A 10/10 result on a binary detection task has a 95% confidence interval of approximately [0.69, 1.00] under a binomial model. A 0/10 result has a CI of [0.00, 0.31]. The paper treats 10/10 vs. 0/10 as establishing a categorical distinction, but these intervals overlap at the tails and — more importantly — the paper makes no attempt to characterize sampling variability. The Stakes taxonomy results (exp-02: ceiling rate 9/10 vs. 4/10) are particularly fragile at this sample size. The paper's language consistently implies categorical effects where the evidence supports probabilistic tendencies.

- **The CO-STAR comparison is confounded by task design.** The globalAuditProvider scenario was apparently designed or selected by the PCSIEFTR authors. The CO-STAR prompt was generated by a separate AI system given only the scenario. This means the PCSIEFTR prompt was designed with knowledge of the specific failure mode (blocking await as architectural error) while the CO-STAR prompt was not. The 10/10 vs. 10/10 split may reflect task-design advantage, not framework superiority. A valid comparison requires either (a) both prompts designed without knowledge of the specific ground-truth finding, or (b) the CO-STAR prompt designed by a human expert in CO-STAR methodology with full knowledge of the task. The current design does not control for this.

- **The mechanistic pseudo-formula (§6.2) should not appear in a paper submitted to a top-tier ML venue without explicit disavowal of its mathematical content.** The expression $Response = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{Stakes \cdot (Request \cdot Persona^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$ uses transformer notation in a way that implies mathematical precision while being dimensionally incoherent. $Persona^T$ is not a matrix; $\neg Instructions$ is not a defined operator on a vector space; $\times Context$ is not a defined operation. The paper calls this a "conceptual mapping, not a literal implementation claim," but presenting it in mathematical notation at a venue where reviewers will read it as mathematics is misleading. The conceptual content could be conveyed in prose without the false precision.

- **The cross-model replication (exp-01g) is limited to two variants (I and J) from one experiment series.** The paper claims "all principal behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families." What has been tested is whether the P_p/P_d distinction on one task replicates on one other model. The Stakes taxonomy, the Instructions ceiling coefficient, the two-vector formula, and the CO-STAR comparison have not been tested on Gemini 2.5 Pro. The generalization claim is substantially broader than the evidence.

- **The paper does not address the strongest available counter-argument: that P_p prompts work because they provide task-relevant information, not because they install a "search algorithm."** A hostile reviewer will immediately note that "after you understand how the lock is acquired and renewed, you ask what happens to the critical write if the lock has already expired" is not just a Persona specification — it is a near-complete description of the reasoning path required to find the zombie-write failure mode. The paper's framing attributes the effect to identity installation. An equally valid interpretation is that the prompt is doing few-shot reasoning specification inside the Persona slot. The paper does not test this interpretation, does not acknowledge it as a competing hypothesis, and does not design experiments to distinguish between them. This is the strongest objection to the central claim, and it is absent from the paper.

- **The Limitations section does not exist as a named section.** The paper distributes limitation acknowledgment across §7.1 (masking test gap) and §7.2 (self-prediction gap). Missing from any discussion: the single-evaluator scoring problem (all binary ground-truth criteria were defined by the authors), the possibility that results are specific to the task types chosen (hidden failure modes in code and legal review), the absence of inter-rater reliability for scoring, the lack of statistical testing of any kind, and the possibility that Claude Sonnet 4.6's training data includes prompt engineering literature that makes it specifically responsive to procedural Persona language in ways that would not generalize.

- **Component symmetry is violated.** The framework has eight components across two layers. Persona receives approximately 60% of the experimental and theoretical treatment. Tone receives one paragraph. Examples receives one paragraph. Format receives one paragraph. The paper's framework claim is that all eight components constitute a coherent two-layer architecture. The experimental record covers three components in depth (Persona, Stakes, Instructions) and names the remaining five as stubs. The framework claim is broader than the evidence base.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On the core causal claim:** Can you design an experiment that holds the total reasoning content of the prompt constant while varying only whether that content is framed as Persona identity versus as explicit Instructions? Specifically: does a prompt that places "ask what happens to the write if the lock has already expired" in the Instructions slot rather than the Persona slot produce different results? If not, the consideration-set mechanism may be a framing effect, not an architectural one.

- **On the 11% coefficient:** What is the ratio when the prohibition type changes (e.g., "do not discuss performance implications" rather than "CI Verification only")? What is the ratio on a non-code-review task? What is the ratio with a weak Persona rather than strong P_p? If the coefficient is task-type-specific or Persona-strength-dependent, the "converging constant" framing must be revised.

- **On the formula coefficients:** What are the units of $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$? How were they estimated? What is the prediction of the formula for a held-out variant not used in its construction, and how close is the prediction to the observed token count?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Was the globalAuditProvider scenario designed before or after the PCSIEFTR framework was developed? Did the authors know, when designing the scenario, that the ground-truth finding was premise rejection rather than implementation correction? If yes, how do you rule out task-design advantage as the explanation for the 10/10 split?

- **On the few-shot interpretation:** The P_p Persona for the zombie-write task encodes the specific reasoning step required to find the failure mode. How do you distinguish "P_p installs a search algorithm via identity" from "P_p provides task-relevant few-shot reasoning that any prompt slot would deliver equivalently"? What experiment would falsify the identity-installation interpretation?

- **On cross-model generalization:** Which of the following claims have been tested on Gemini 2.5 Pro: (a) the ~11% Instructions ceiling, (b) the Stakes taxonomy, (c) the CO-STAR comparison, (d) the two-vector formula coefficients? If the answer is none of them, the cross-model generalization claim must be scoped to the P_p/P_d distinction on one task type.

- **On scoring validity:** Who scored the outputs, and what was the inter-rater reliability? Were the ground-truth criteria for binary scoring defined before or after the outputs were observed? Is there any run where the scoring decision was ambiguous, and if so, how was it resolved?

- **On the self-prediction gap:** The authoring model predicted 6/10 for Variant L; it scored 10/10. Is this a single data point or a pattern across multiple Personas? If it is a single data point, it cannot support the general claim that "P_p Personas should be evaluated empirically, not rated by inspection."

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper contains a genuine behavioral finding — procedural Persona language produces better outcomes than thin Persona language on hidden-failure-mode tasks — but presents it inside a causal and quantitative framework that the experimental evidence does not support, fails to address the strongest competing interpretation of its own results, and makes cross-condition generalization claims (the 11% coefficient, the Stakes formula coefficients, cross-model transfer of all principal claims) that exceed what eleven experiment series at n=10 can establish.
