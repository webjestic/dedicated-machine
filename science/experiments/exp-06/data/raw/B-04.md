# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component prompt engineering framework organized into a two-layer architecture (World Layer: Persona, Context, Stakes, Tone; Task Layer: Instructions, Examples, Format, Request). The central empirical claim is that Persona — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality on structurally hidden failure modes, while other components (Context, Stakes, Instructions) operate on what Persona has already installed. The authors validate this framework across eleven experiment series on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, totaling 640 runs, and include a direct comparison against a CO-STAR-prompted baseline.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is a genuine conceptual contribution.** The observation that procedural identity language ("after you understand how the lock is acquired, you ask what happens to the write if the lock has already expired") produces qualitatively different outputs than dispositional identity language ("I cannot help but analyze every aspect deeply") is non-obvious and practically useful. The authors correctly identify this as a difference in kind, not degree. This distinction is underspecified in prior frameworks including CO-STAR and RISEN.

- **The consideration-set framing is a useful reframing of an important phenomenon.** The claim that Persona determines *which reasoning classes are reachable*, not merely how deeply the model reasons within a fixed space, is a meaningful theoretical contribution if supported. The J=0/10 / I=10/10 split with identical Context is the paper's strongest single data point and is genuinely striking.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is the paper's most methodologically credible finding.** The convergence of the A/C ratio across three independent run sets (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) is a clean quantitative result with a clear operational definition. This is the kind of stable, replicable coefficient that prompt engineering research has largely lacked.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is thoughtful.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of PCSIEFTR is a reasonable attempt to control for author-level confounds. The behavioral split (premise rejection vs. compliance validation, 10/10 vs. 10/10) is a concrete, interpretable outcome rather than a vague quality rating.

- **The paper maintains an explicit distinction between behavioral claims and mechanistic hypotheses.** The claim classification table in §6.1 and the repeated hedging on K/V filtering as a "conceptual mapping, not a literal implementation claim" reflect genuine methodological discipline. This is not universal in the prompt engineering literature.

- **The self-prediction gap observation (§3.1.1, §7.2) is interesting and underexplored.** The finding that the authoring model predicted 6/10 for a Persona that scored 10/10 suggests a systematic calibration failure in LLM self-assessment that has implications beyond this paper.

- **Cross-model replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro is a meaningful check.** Zero-shot transfer of the I=10/10 / J=0/10 split to a different model family is the minimum necessary condition for the claims to be non-trivially model-specific.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The evaluation methodology is fatally underspecified for the claims being made.** The paper's central claims rest on binary scoring against "a ground-truth criterion defined before each run," but the scoring rubric is never provided. For the zombie-write scenario (exp-01e), what exactly constitutes detection? Does the model need to name the GC pause specifically? Name the zombie write? Identify that the lock can expire during the write window? Propose a correct fix? The difference between these criteria could easily produce 10/10 vs. 0/10 splits on the same outputs. Without the rubric, the primary quantitative results are unverifiable. This is not a minor methodological gap — it is the difference between a scientific claim and an anecdote.

- **All scoring appears to have been performed by the authors, with no inter-rater reliability reported.** For a paper making strong quantitative claims (10/10 vs. 0/10, 1/10 vs. 10/10), the absence of any inter-rater agreement measure or blind scoring protocol is a serious validity threat. The binary outcomes are suspiciously clean. Real behavioral experiments on LLMs at n=10 rarely produce 0/10 or 10/10 results; when they do, it is either because the task is trivial or the scoring criterion is being applied non-uniformly. The paper provides no evidence that it is the former.

- **The experimental design does not adequately control for prompt length, lexical content, or structural confounds.** The comparison between P_p and P_d variants conflates multiple variables: procedural vs. dispositional framing, specific vocabulary choices (e.g., "Kleppmann/Jepsen biography"), prompt length, and structural organization. The paper does not report prompt lengths for compared variants, does not ablate individual P_p components, and does not test whether the specific vocabulary of the P_p prompts (rather than their procedural structure) drives the effect. A "senior software engineer" baseline is not a controlled P_d condition — it is a minimal condition. A P_d variant that is length-matched and vocabulary-matched to the P_p variant is required to isolate the procedural/dispositional distinction.

- **The mechanistic framework (§2, §6) is presented with more confidence than the evidence warrants, despite the authors' stated hedging.** The pseudo-formula in §6.2 uses standard transformer notation ($Q$, $K$, $V$, $d_k$, softmax) in a way that implies formal grounding while the authors simultaneously disclaim literal accuracy. The formula $Response = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{Stakes \cdot (Request \cdot Persona^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$ is dimensionally incoherent — Stakes, Persona, Context, and Instructions are not vectors in the same space, and the operations between them are undefined. This is not a "conceptual mapping"; it is notation that will mislead readers who take it seriously. The paper would be stronger if it dropped the pseudo-formula entirely and stated the mechanistic hypotheses in plain language.

- **The two-vector Stakes formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ presents token-count differences as "measured coefficients" without justification for the functional form.** The paper reports token deltas (e.g., +710 tokens for Identity Stakes on P_p, +578 for no Stakes vs. Task Stakes) and labels these as $\alpha_{amp}$ and $\beta_{brake}$, but the multiplicative form of the formula is not derived or justified. Why is the relationship multiplicative rather than additive? Why are $S_i$ and $S_t$ treated as scalars? The formula is presented as if it were a regression result when it is actually a post-hoc labeling of observed differences. The "empirically grounded coefficients" claim in the abstract is not supported by the experimental record as described.

- **The CO-STAR comparison (exp-05) is a single-scenario test against a single comparison prompt, and the scenario was designed by the PCSIEFTR authors.** The globalAuditProvider scenario was constructed to have a premise-rejection structure — a task type that the paper has already established is where P_p excels. This is not a neutral benchmark. A fair comparison would require: (a) multiple scenarios spanning different task types, including tasks where CO-STAR is expected to perform comparably; (b) the CO-STAR prompt being evaluated on its own terms (does it produce better output on tasks it was designed for?); (c) multiple CO-STAR prompt variants, not a single generated instance. The current comparison demonstrates that P_p outperforms a specific CO-STAR prompt on a scenario designed to favor P_p. This is not a general claim about framework superiority.

- **n=10 per variant is insufficient for the statistical claims being made.** The paper reports 10/10 and 0/10 outcomes as if they are decisive, but at n=10, a true detection probability of 0.5 produces 0/10 outcomes with probability ~0.001 and 10/10 outcomes with the same probability. These are not rare events. More importantly, the paper makes no use of confidence intervals, effect sizes, or statistical tests anywhere in the 640-run dataset. Token count comparisons (e.g., 1,772 vs. 1,078 avg tokens) are reported without standard deviations or significance tests. For a paper claiming to provide "measured coefficients," the absence of any inferential statistics is a significant methodological gap.

- **The paper tests exactly two models, both frontier commercial systems, with no access to weights, activations, or internal representations.** The mechanistic claims about K/V filtering, inverse temperature, and attention masking cannot be evaluated from behavioral data alone. The paper acknowledges this but then proceeds to use the mechanistic language extensively in ways that shape how the behavioral findings are interpreted. The framing "P_p performs K/V space pre-filtering" is not a neutral description of a behavioral observation — it is a mechanistic claim that implies a specific computational process. Readers without deep transformer expertise will not reliably distinguish the behavioral claims from the mechanistic hypotheses.

- **The "self-prediction gap" finding (Variant L predicted 6/10, scored 10/10) is a single data point presented as a structural property.** The paper develops a theoretical account of why P_p is systematically underestimated (descriptive vs. enactive register), but this account rests on one observation. The claim that "the self-prediction gap cuts both ways" — that overconfident P_p framing also underperforms — is asserted without evidence.

- **The scope re-framing finding (§3.2.1, §4.5) undermines the Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling claim rather than complementing it.** If strong P_p consistently re-categorizes prohibited findings through permitted channels, then the 11% coefficient is not measuring "elaboration compression without affecting detection" — it is measuring the output length of a re-framed finding relative to an unconstrained one. The detection rate is preserved not because the finding forms and is then suppressed, but because the finding is expressed differently. These are different mechanisms with different implications. The paper presents both claims simultaneously without resolving the tension.

- **The paper does not engage seriously with alternative explanations.** The most obvious alternative to the consideration-set hypothesis is that P_p prompts are simply longer, more specific, or contain domain-relevant keywords (e.g., "zombie write," "GC pause," "lock expiry") that activate relevant reasoning chains. The paper dismisses "senior software engineer" as a thin baseline but does not test whether adding domain-specific vocabulary to a dispositional Persona recovers P_p performance. The Kleppmann/Jepsen biography variant (L) is particularly concerning here — it is plausible that naming specific distributed systems researchers activates relevant knowledge, independent of the procedural framing.

## Questions for the Authors

- **Scoring rubric:** Please provide the exact binary scoring criterion used for each experiment series. For exp-01e specifically: what is the minimum output content required to score 1 (detection)? Does the model need to identify the GC pause, the lock expiry window, the zombie write, or all three? Were any runs scored as borderline cases, and if so, how were they resolved?

- **Inter-rater reliability:** Was any scoring performed by raters blind to condition? If not, what safeguards were in place against confirmation bias in scoring? For the CO-STAR comparison (exp-05), who scored B's outputs as "compliance validation," and what would a B output need to contain to score as premise rejection?

- **Prompt text:** The paper describes Persona variants in summary but does not provide the full text of any prompt used in the experiments. Can the authors provide the complete prompts for at least the key variants (I, J, L, P, A-exp05, B-exp05) in an appendix or supplementary material? Without these, the P_p/P_d distinction cannot be evaluated by readers.

- **Controlled ablation:** Is there a variant that takes the P_p prompt text and replaces only the procedural steps with dispositional equivalents while preserving length, vocabulary, and structure? If not, how do the authors rule out the explanation that prompt length or domain vocabulary (rather than procedural framing) drives the consideration-set effect?

- **The Kleppmann/Jepsen variant:** Variant L includes a biography referencing specific distributed systems researchers. Is there evidence that this biographical content — rather than the procedural step — drives L's 10/10 performance? Has a variant been tested with the biography but without the procedural step?

- **Statistical analysis:** Can the authors provide confidence intervals for the key detection rate comparisons and significance tests for the token count differences? At n=10, what is the authors' interpretation of the probability that the J=0/10 result is consistent with a true detection probability of, say, 0.3?

- **The pseudo-formula:** The formula in §6.2 uses transformer notation but the authors acknowledge it is not a literal implementation claim. Would the authors consider replacing it with a plain-language statement of the hypothesized relationships? If retained, can the authors clarify the dimensionality of each term and what mathematical operation "$\times Context$" denotes when Context is a natural language string?

- **CO-STAR scenario selection:** Was the globalAuditProvider scenario designed before or after the authors identified premise-rejection as the task type where P_p most clearly outperforms P_d? If after, how do the authors address the concern that the comparison scenario was selected to favor the proposed framework?

- **The 11% coefficient and scope re-framing:** If strong P_p under a prohibition re-categorizes the finding through a permitted channel (scope re-framing), then the constrained output is not a compressed version of the unconstrained output — it is a differently-framed version. Does the 11% coefficient measure elaboration suppression or re-framing compression? Are the constrained and unconstrained outputs making the same claim through different channels, or are they making different claims?

- **Replication materials:** The paper states all raw outputs are in `data/exp-*/raw/`. Will these be made available at submission, and do they include the full prompt text for each variant?

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper contains a genuinely interesting set of observations and a conceptual framework with real practical utility, but the core empirical claims rest on an evaluation methodology that is too underspecified to be verifiable — specifically, the absence of published scoring rubrics, the lack of blind scoring or inter-rater reliability measures, the failure to control for prompt length and vocabulary confounds, and the absence of any inferential statistics across 640 runs. These are not presentation issues; they are validity threats that prevent the reader from assessing whether the reported 10/10 vs. 0/10 splits reflect the proposed mechanism or scoring artifacts. The mechanistic pseudo-formula should be removed or substantially revised. The CO-STAR comparison, while methodologically more careful than most framework comparisons in the literature, tests a single scenario designed by the framework's proponents and cannot support general claims about framework superiority. The authors should be encouraged to resubmit with full prompt disclosure, pre-registered scoring criteria, controlled ablations isolating the procedural/dispositional distinction, and appropriate statistical analysis; the underlying phenomena appear real and worth investigating rigorously.
