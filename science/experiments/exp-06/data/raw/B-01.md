# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component, two-layer prompt engineering framework organized around the claim that Persona — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality in large language models, with other components (Context, Stakes, Instructions) operating on what Persona has already installed. The authors validate this framework across eleven experiment series totaling approximately 640 runs on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, reporting binary detection scores and token counts as primary metrics. The paper further proposes mechanistic hypotheses mapping framework components onto transformer attention operations (K/V pre-filtering, inverse temperature, attention masking), while explicitly disclaiming that these hypotheses are confirmed at the activation level.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine empirical commitment to falsifiability.** The paper does not merely assert that Persona matters; it constructs adversarial variants (Variant J: same Context as I, weak Persona, 0/10; Variant P: strong P_d + high Identity Stakes, 1/10) designed to isolate the variable of interest. The 10/10 vs. 0/10 splits are sharp and, if reproducible, constitute real evidence.

- **Appropriate epistemic humility about mechanistic claims.** The explicit separation of "behavioral claims" (Appendix B) from "mechanistic hypotheses" (§6.1) is methodologically sound and unusual in the prompt engineering literature, where mechanistic speculation is routinely presented as established fact. The authors consistently flag the K/V filtering and inverse temperature hypotheses as explanatory models, not confirmed mechanisms.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling finding is the paper's most credible quantitative result.** Three independent run sets producing 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% ratios is a genuine convergence result. The interpretation — that prohibition compresses expression without affecting the underlying finding — is behaviorally testable and the convergence is striking enough to warrant follow-up.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is defensible.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of the PCSIEFTR framework is a reasonable attempt to control for author confound. The behavioral split (premise rejection vs. premise validation, 10/10 vs. 10/10) is a concrete, interpretable outcome.

- **The self-prediction gap observation (§3.1.1, §7.2) is novel and practically useful.** The finding that the authoring model rated Variant L at 6/10 while it scored 10/10 — and the explanation in terms of descriptive vs. enactive registers — is the kind of observation that practitioners can act on immediately and that researchers can design experiments around.

- **Cross-model replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro is a meaningful validity check.** Zero-shot transfer of the I=10/10, J=0/10 split to a different model family substantially strengthens the claim that the effect is not Claude-specific.

- **The "Confident Error" characterization (exp-04c) is a useful failure taxonomy.** The observation that Task Stakes terminates a wrong-direction search at confident completion — producing structured, elaborated, wrong output rather than uncertain or terse output — is a practically important failure mode that is undercharacterized in the existing literature.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The single-model, single-temperature experimental design is the paper's most serious methodological limitation.** Ten of eleven experiment series run exclusively on Claude Sonnet 4.6 at temperature 0.5. This is not a controlled experiment in any statistically meaningful sense — it is a series of behavioral observations on one model configuration. The paper cannot distinguish between (a) a general principle about transformer reasoning and (b) a characterization of how Claude Sonnet 4.6 at temperature 0.5 responds to specific prompt patterns. The cross-model replication covers only two variants (I and J) on one additional model. The stakes taxonomy, the ~11% coefficient, the CO-STAR comparison, and the P_p/P_d distinction are all single-model results. This is a fundamental validity threat, not a minor limitation.

- **The binary scoring metric is underspecified and potentially circular.** The paper defines success as "detection of the primary structural failure mode, with named fix," scored against "a ground-truth criterion defined before each run." But the ground-truth criterion is defined by the authors, who also designed the P_p Persona to find that specific failure mode. There is no independent adjudication, no inter-rater reliability reported, no blinding of scorers to condition. The scoring procedure is described in one sentence (§4.1). For a paper making strong claims about detection rates (10/10 vs. 0/10), the measurement instrument needs substantially more validation. The risk of motivated scoring — even unconscious — is real when the authors score their own experiments with criteria they defined.

- **n=10 per variant is statistically inadequate for the claims being made.** The paper reports 10/10 and 0/10 splits as if these are stable estimates of underlying detection probabilities. At n=10, a 10/10 result is consistent with a true detection probability anywhere from approximately 0.74 to 1.0 (95% CI). A 0/10 result is consistent with a true probability from 0 to approximately 0.26. The paper treats these as categorical (detection vs. non-detection), but the underlying process is stochastic (temperature 0.5, not 0). The convergence of the ~11% coefficient across three run sets is the one result where the paper's quantitative claims are somewhat supported by replication, but even there the run sets are not independent in the relevant sense (same model, same task structure, same author).

- **The task scenarios are not publicly available, and the paper provides insufficient detail for replication.** The Redis distributed lock scenario, the MSA carve-out trap, and the globalAuditProvider scenario are described narratively but not reproduced. The prompts themselves are not included (raw outputs are referenced as `data/exp-*/raw/` but this is not a public repository). A reader cannot verify whether the "strong P_p" Persona actually encodes the specific procedural steps the paper claims, or whether the "weak Persona" is genuinely weak or merely a straw man. The paper's central empirical claim — that procedural Persona language is the operative variable — cannot be evaluated without seeing the actual Persona text.

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is defined by the authors and operationalized by the authors, creating a construct validity problem.** The paper claims P_p (procedural) and P_d (dispositional) are structurally distinct sub-components with different behavioral effects. But the classification of a given Persona as P_p or P_d is made by the authors, not by an independent measure. The paper does not provide a coding scheme, inter-rater reliability, or any operationalization that would allow a third party to classify a novel Persona as P_p or P_d. This means the P_p/P_d distinction is currently unfalsifiable by external researchers — a significant problem for a paper claiming it as a primary contribution.

- **The mechanistic framework (§6) makes claims that are not supported by the experimental evidence and that misrepresent transformer operations.** The pseudo-formula in §6.2 is presented as a "conceptual mapping," but several elements are not conceptually coherent. The $\times \neg Instructions$ term implies that Instructions function as a logical negation operator on the output space — this is not what attention masking does, and the paper's own evidence (scope re-framing, premise-undermining) shows that the relationship is far more complex than exclusion. The claim that Stakes functions as "inverse temperature on the softmax distribution" via "residual connection persistence" is not a coherent mechanistic hypothesis — residual connections preserve signal across layers; they do not modulate temperature. These are not minor imprecisions; they are conceptual errors that undermine the paper's framing of its behavioral findings in mechanistic terms.

- **The CO-STAR comparison confounds framework with prompt quality.** The paper acknowledges that the CO-STAR prompt was generated by "a separate AI system" given only the scenario. But the comparison is between (a) a PCSIEFTR prompt designed by researchers who have run eleven experiment series specifically on this task type, and (b) a CO-STAR prompt generated by an AI with no domain-specific knowledge. This is not a framework comparison — it is a comparison between an expert-optimized prompt and a naively generated one. The fact that the CO-STAR prompt's Context section installed a wrong prior is a finding about that specific prompt, not about the CO-STAR framework. A skilled CO-STAR practitioner who understood the globalAuditProvider scenario could write a Context section that does not install the compliance prior. The paper does not test this.

- **The token count as a proxy metric is overinterpreted throughout.** The paper uses token counts as evidence for theoretical constructs (Termination Inhibitor, Entropy Brake, ceiling pressure). But token count is a surface measure that reflects many factors: task complexity, model verbosity at a given temperature, format instructions, and the specific content of the output. The 710-token difference between Identity Stakes and no-Stakes conditions (exp-02) is interpreted as evidence for a "Termination Inhibitor" mechanism, but it could equally reflect that Identity Stakes prompts produce more elaborated outputs because the prompt itself is longer and more elaborate. The paper does not control for prompt length.

- **The "consideration set" construct is not operationalized independently of the experimental outcome.** The paper's central theoretical claim is that Persona determines the "consideration set" — which reasoning paths are reachable. But the consideration set is inferred entirely from whether the model detects the target failure mode. There is no independent measure of the consideration set. This means the claim "Persona determines the consideration set" is currently equivalent to "Persona determines detection rate," which is a much weaker claim. The mechanistic framing adds theoretical vocabulary without adding explanatory content that is independently testable.

- **The paper does not engage with the most relevant prior literature.** The prompt engineering literature includes work on role prompting (Salewski et al., 2023), chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022), least-to-most prompting (Zhou et al., 2022), and structured prompting frameworks. The paper cites CO-STAR and RISEN but does not engage with the empirical literature on role prompting, which directly bears on the P_p/P_d distinction. The claim that procedural identity language outperforms descriptive identity language may be novel, but the paper cannot establish novelty without engaging with the existing evidence on role specification in prompting.

- **The $4.80 total cost figure is presented as if it is a strength, but it signals a limitation.** 640 API calls at $4.80 implies an average cost of approximately $0.0075 per call, which is consistent with short prompts and short outputs. The paper claims outputs regularly reach 2,500 tokens; at Claude Sonnet pricing, 640 calls with 2,500-token outputs would cost substantially more than $4.80. Either the outputs are shorter than described, the token counts include thinking tokens that are not billed, or the cost figure is inaccurate. This inconsistency should be resolved.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On scoring validity:** Who scored the experimental outputs, and were scorers blinded to condition? What was the inter-rater reliability for the binary detection criterion? Can you provide the full ground-truth criteria for at least the three primary scenarios (Redis lock, MSA carve-out, globalAuditProvider)?

- **On prompt transparency:** Can you provide the full text of at least Variants I, J, A (exp-05), and B (exp-05)? The central claim of the paper — that procedural Persona language is the operative variable — cannot be evaluated without seeing the actual Persona text. If the prompts cannot be published for confidentiality reasons, can you provide a sufficiently detailed description that a third party could construct equivalent variants?

- **On the P_p/P_d operationalization:** What is the coding scheme for classifying a Persona as P_p vs. P_d? If a third-party researcher wrote a novel Persona and asked you to classify it, what criteria would you apply? Is there a linguistic feature (imperative vs. declarative syntax, presence of conditional clauses, etc.) that operationalizes the distinction, or is it a judgment call?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Have you tested whether a CO-STAR prompt written by a domain expert — one who understood the globalAuditProvider scenario and deliberately avoided installing the compliance prior in the Context section — would produce different results? If not, how do you distinguish between "CO-STAR installs wrong priors" and "this particular CO-STAR prompt installed a wrong prior"?

- **On the mechanistic claims:** The paper claims Stakes functions as "inverse temperature via residual connection persistence." Can you clarify what mechanism you are proposing? Residual connections sum the layer input with the layer output; they do not modulate the softmax temperature parameter. If this is a metaphor rather than a mechanistic claim, the paper should be clearer about that distinction.

- **On statistical adequacy:** At n=10 and temperature 0.5, the 10/10 and 0/10 results are consistent with a wide range of underlying detection probabilities. Have you considered running larger n experiments on the primary comparisons (I vs. J, A vs. B in exp-05) to establish tighter confidence intervals? What would your interpretation be if, at n=100, Variant J detected the failure mode 15% of the time?

- **On the ~11% coefficient:** The three run sets producing 12.8%, 11.3%, and 11.0% are described as "converging." But all three use the same task structure (code review with CI prohibition), the same model, and the same prohibition framing. Is this convergence evidence of a general mechanism, or evidence that this specific task structure produces a consistent ratio? Have you tested the coefficient on a different task type (e.g., the legal scenario from exp-01f)?

- **On the cost figure:** The paper reports $4.80 for 640 runs. At Claude Sonnet 4.6 pricing, this implies very short average outputs or a different billing structure than standard API pricing. Can you clarify how this figure was calculated, and whether it is consistent with the token counts reported in the experiments?

---

## Recommendation

**Reject.**

The paper addresses a genuine and important question — whether prompt structure determines which reasoning classes are reachable, not merely how deeply a model reasons within a fixed space — and the behavioral observations are interesting enough to warrant serious follow-up work. However, the paper cannot be accepted in its current form for a top-tier venue. The experimental design is insufficient to support the claims: single-model, single-temperature, n=10, author-scored with undefined inter-rater reliability, on non-public scenarios with non-public prompts. The mechanistic framework contains conceptual errors. The primary theoretical construct (consideration set) is not operationalized independently of the outcome it is invoked to explain. The CO-STAR comparison confounds framework with prompt quality. These are not issues that can be resolved through minor revision — they require a substantially redesigned experimental program with independent scoring, larger n, multiple temperatures, broader model coverage, and public release of prompts and scenarios. The authors have identified a potentially important phenomenon; they have not yet produced the evidence needed to establish it.
