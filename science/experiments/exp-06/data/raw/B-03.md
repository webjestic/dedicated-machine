# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component prompt engineering framework organized into a World Layer (Persona, Context, Stakes, Tone) and a Task Layer (Instructions, Examples, Format, Request). The central empirical claim is that a procedural sub-component of Persona (P_p) is the primary determinant of whether a model can detect structurally hidden failure modes, and that other components (Context, Stakes, Instructions) operate on what Persona has already installed rather than substituting for it. The paper validates these claims across eleven experiment series totaling 640 runs on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, and includes a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR-structured prompt on a premise-rejection task.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine empirical contribution on the P_p/P_d distinction.** The behavioral split between procedural and dispositional Persona is the paper's most interesting finding. The J=0/10 result (Variant J: weak Persona + rich Context, same task as I=10/10) is a clean, reproducible demonstration that Context richness does not substitute for Persona type. This is a non-obvious result that challenges the dominant "specification completeness" theory of prompt quality.

- **The self-prediction gap observation is novel and practically important.** The finding that the authoring model rated Variant L at 6/10 while it scored 10/10 in execution — and the explanation in terms of descriptive vs. enactive registers — is a genuinely interesting observation about prompt evaluation methodology. It has direct practical implications (evaluate empirically, not by inspection) and opens a tractable research question.

- **Commendable epistemic discipline in separating behavioral claims from mechanistic hypotheses.** Section 6.1 explicitly classifies claims into confirmed behavioral findings and unverified mechanistic conjectures. The pseudo-formula in §6.2 is labeled a "conceptual mapping, not a literal implementation claim." This is the correct scientific posture and is more careful than much of the prompt engineering literature.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is a concrete, replicable measurement.** Three independent run sets converging on ~11% (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%) is a specific, falsifiable quantitative claim. Whether it generalizes is an open question, but the measurement itself is well-documented and the convergence across run sets is meaningful.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is sound in one important respect.** Using a separate AI system in an isolated session to generate the comparison prompt — with no knowledge of PCSIEFTR or Variant A's design — is a reasonable attempt to control for prompt author bias. The authors correctly identify this as a framework comparison rather than an author comparison.

- **The "Confident Error" finding (exp-04c) is practically significant.** The observation that Task Stakes terminates a wrong-direction search at confident completion — producing structured, plausible, wrong output — is an important failure mode for production deployment. The characterization is precise and the experimental evidence supports it.

- **Cross-model replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro is included.** Zero-shot transfer of the primary I/J split to a different model family strengthens the generalizability claim, even if the replication is limited in scope.

---

## Weaknesses

*Ordered by severity.*

- **[Critical] The evaluation methodology is not independently reproducible.** All 640 runs were conducted by the paper's authors on proprietary API endpoints (Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 2.5 Pro) with no public release of raw outputs, prompts, or scoring rubrics. The claim that "All raw outputs are in `data/exp-*/raw/`" refers to a repository that is not linked, versioned, or submitted with the paper. Binary scoring on "ground-truth criterion defined before each run" is described but the criteria themselves are not provided for independent verification. A reviewer cannot confirm that the zombie-write failure mode, the MSA carve-out trap, or the globalAuditProvider premise rejection were scored consistently across runs. This is a fundamental reproducibility failure for a paper making quantitative claims at a top-tier venue.

- **[Critical] The experimental design confounds Persona quality with task difficulty in the primary comparison.** The paper's central claim rests on I=10/10 vs. J=0/10, but Variants I and J differ not only in Persona type but in Persona *length*, *specificity*, and *domain knowledge*. Variant I encodes Kleppmann/Jepsen-style reasoning about distributed systems; Variant J is "senior software engineer." These differ on at least three dimensions simultaneously: procedural vs. dispositional framing, domain expertise encoding, and prompt length. The paper does not include an ablation that holds domain expertise constant while varying procedural vs. dispositional framing, which is what the P_p/P_d distinction requires. A P_d Persona with equivalent distributed systems expertise (e.g., "I have deep expertise in Redis distributed locking and have debugged zombie-write failures caused by GC pauses") has not been tested. Without this control, the paper cannot distinguish "procedural framing matters" from "domain expertise encoding matters."

- **[Critical] The CO-STAR comparison is not a fair framework evaluation.** The paper compares a PCSIEFTR prompt designed by the paper's authors (who know the target finding) against a CO-STAR prompt generated by "a separate AI system" given only the scenario. This is not a comparison of frameworks applied with equal knowledge of the target — it is a comparison of an expert-designed prompt against a naive-generated one. The CO-STAR prompt's failure to reject the compliance premise may reflect the quality of the CO-STAR prompt generation process, not a structural limitation of CO-STAR as a framework. A CO-STAR prompt authored by a human expert who knows the zombie-write failure mode and the compliance-as-implementation-error distinction has not been tested. The paper's claim that "the comparison is between frameworks, not between prompt authors" is not supported by the methodology.

- **[Major] The sample sizes are insufficient for the quantitative claims being made.** The two-vector Stakes formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is presented with "measured coefficients" derived from token-delta comparisons (e.g., +236, +578, +67 tokens). These are point estimates from n=10 runs with no confidence intervals, no standard deviations reported for the token counts, and no statistical tests. The paper reports "mean tokens" throughout but does not report variance. The token distributions are likely non-normal (bounded at 2,500, with ceiling effects acknowledged). The claim that "Stakes type effect is 3–4× the Persona-strength effect on elaboration length" is presented as a measured coefficient, but it is a ratio of two point estimates from small samples. This does not meet the evidentiary standard for a quantitative formula.

- **[Major] The mechanistic framework is not falsifiable as stated.** The K/V space pre-filtering hypothesis, the inverse temperature analogy for Stakes, and the attention masking hypothesis for Instructions are presented as explanatory models that "generated correct predictions." But the predictions they generated (P_p outperforms P_d, Stakes amplifies existing signal, Instructions compress output) are also predicted by much simpler accounts (e.g., more specific prompts produce more specific outputs; prohibitions reduce output length). The paper does not identify a prediction that the mechanistic hypothesis makes that the simpler account does not. Without such a differentiating prediction, the mechanistic framework is unfalsifiable decoration on top of behavioral findings that do not require it.

- **[Major] The ~11% elaboration ceiling coefficient is likely task-specific and the paper does not establish its generality.** All three run sets producing the ~11% figure use the same task type (code review), the same prohibition type ("CI Verification only; do not flag security concerns"), and the same model (Claude Sonnet 4.6). The convergence across three runs of the same experimental setup is not evidence that the coefficient generalizes across prohibition types, task types, or models. The paper presents this as a "converging coefficient" without acknowledging that the convergence is within a single experimental cell.

- **[Major] The paper does not engage seriously with alternative explanations for its primary findings.** The most obvious alternative to the consideration-set mechanism is that P_p prompts simply provide more task-relevant information to the model (e.g., "ask what happens to the write if the lock has already expired" literally tells the model what to look for). The paper does not test whether a Task Layer instruction containing the same procedural content as the P_p Persona produces equivalent results. If it does, the World Layer / Task Layer distinction is not load-bearing. If it does not, the paper needs to explain why the same content in Persona vs. Instructions produces different outcomes — which would be a much stronger and more interesting finding.

- **[Moderate] The paper makes strong claims about transformer internals that are not supported by the evidence presented.** Despite the disclaimer in §6.1, the paper repeatedly uses mechanistic language as if it were explanatory rather than metaphorical: "K/V space pre-filtering," "sharpens the softmax distribution," "inverse temperature on the softmax distribution," "attention masking." These phrases appear in the abstract, introduction, and throughout the experimental sections, not only in the designated mechanistic hypothesis sections. A reader who does not carefully track the §6.1 disclaimer will come away with the impression that these mechanisms have been established. The paper should either test these mechanistic claims (via activation analysis, attention visualization, or probing) or remove the mechanistic language from the empirical sections entirely.

- **[Moderate] The experimental scope is narrow despite the breadth of claims.** The paper tests two task domains (code review, legal contract review) and two models. The code review tasks all involve the same class of failure mode (hidden structural vulnerabilities requiring cross-component simulation). The legal task is a single MSA scenario. The claim that "all primary behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families" is supported by 20 runs (I and J on Gemini). The claim that the framework generalizes to "structurally hidden failure modes" in general is not established by two task domains with the same underlying structure.

- **[Moderate] The paper does not adequately characterize the failure modes of its own framework.** When does PCSIEFTR fail? The paper acknowledges calibration failures in exp-03 through exp-03d but frames them as revealing the category overlap problem rather than as failures of the framework. The paper does not report cases where P_p produced false positives (rejected correct requirements), over-specified findings, or failed to converge. A framework paper at a top-tier venue should include a systematic characterization of failure conditions.

- **[Minor] The PCSIEFTR pseudo-formula (§6.2) is mathematically inconsistent.** The formula $\text{Softmax}(\cdot) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$ applies softmax to a scalar-like expression and then multiplies by Context (a vector?) and $\neg Instructions$ (a negation of what?). The notation mixes scalar operations, vector operations, and logical negation in a way that cannot be interpreted consistently. If this is a conceptual mapping, it should be presented as prose or a diagram, not as a formula with mathematical notation that implies precision it does not have.

- **[Minor] The paper's framing of "prompt architecture vs. prompt engineering" is not adequately distinguished from prior work.** The claim that PCSIEFTR is an "architectural" rather than "engineering" framework is asserted but not grounded in the existing literature on prompt design. The paper cites CO-STAR and RISEN but does not engage with the broader literature on structured prompting, role prompting, or chain-of-thought prompting in sufficient depth to establish what is genuinely novel about the World Layer / Task Layer distinction.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On experimental reproducibility:** Will the authors release the complete prompt text for all variants (I, J, K, L, M, N, O, P, A, B, C, D, E, F and all exp-03 through exp-05 variants), the scoring rubrics used for binary classification, and the raw outputs? Without these, the primary claims cannot be evaluated. If there is a reason these cannot be released (e.g., API terms of service), what alternative verification mechanism do the authors propose?

- **On the P_p/P_d confound:** Has the authors tested a P_d Persona with equivalent distributed systems domain expertise to Variant I — specifically, one that encodes knowledge of GC-pause zombie-write failure modes in dispositional rather than procedural framing? If so, what was the result? If not, how do the authors distinguish "procedural framing" from "domain expertise encoding" as the causal variable?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** What would the result be if a human expert who knows the target finding (the blocking await is architecturally wrong) were asked to write the strongest possible CO-STAR prompt for the globalAuditProvider scenario? The paper's methodology uses an AI system with no knowledge of the target finding to generate the CO-STAR prompt. Is the authors' claim that this controls for "prompt author bias" — or does it instead introduce a systematic disadvantage for CO-STAR?

- **On the differentiating prediction:** What prediction does the K/V space pre-filtering hypothesis make that is not also made by the simpler account that "more specific prompts produce more specific outputs"? Specifically: if the procedural content of Variant I's Persona were moved verbatim into the Instructions component (Task Layer), would the result be I=10/10 or J=0/10? This experiment would directly test whether the World Layer / Task Layer distinction is load-bearing.

- **On the ~11% coefficient:** The three run sets producing the ~11% figure all use the same task type, prohibition type, and model. What is the authors' prediction for this coefficient on a different task type (e.g., the legal contract review from exp-01f), a different prohibition type (e.g., "do not discuss remediation"), or on Gemini 2.5 Pro? Has this been tested?

- **On statistical reporting:** Can the authors provide standard deviations (or interquartile ranges) for all reported token means, and report the results of appropriate statistical tests for the key comparisons (e.g., A vs. B in exp-02, A vs. C in exp-03b)? Given the ceiling effects and non-normal distributions, what distributional assumptions are the authors making?

- **On false positives:** In how many runs did P_p Personas reject a *correct* architectural requirement (false positive premise rejection)? The paper reports only true positive rates. Without a false positive rate, the precision of the P_p mechanism cannot be assessed.

- **On the self-prediction gap:** The authoring model predicted Variant L at 6/10 and it scored 10/10. Was this a single prediction or an average across multiple elicitations? What was the variance in the prediction? And critically: is the "authoring model" the same model family (Claude) as the executing model? If so, the self-prediction gap may reflect a specific property of Claude's introspective access rather than a general property of P_p language.

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper contains a genuinely interesting empirical observation — the P_p/P_d behavioral split — and several secondary findings (the self-prediction gap, the Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient, the Confident Error failure mode) that are worth publishing. However, the paper cannot be accepted in its current form due to: (1) the absence of reproducible experimental materials, (2) the uncontrolled confound between procedural framing and domain expertise encoding in the primary comparison, (3) the methodologically compromised CO-STAR comparison, (4) the presentation of point estimates from small samples as "measured coefficients" in a quantitative formula, and (5) the pervasive use of mechanistic language that the paper's own §6.1 acknowledges is unverified. A major revision that releases experimental materials, adds the critical ablation (procedural content in Instructions vs. Persona), corrects the statistical presentation, and either tests or removes the mechanistic framework would produce a publishable contribution.
