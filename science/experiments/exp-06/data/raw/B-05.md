# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

This paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component, two-layer prompt engineering framework, and argues that the Persona component — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality in LLMs by determining the "consideration set" of reachable reasoning paths. The authors validate their framework across eleven experiment series (640 total runs) on Claude Sonnet 4.6 and Gemini 2.5 Pro, reporting a P_p/P_d (procedural vs. dispositional) distinction, a two-vector Stakes taxonomy with measured token-delta coefficients, a converging ~11% Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient, and a head-to-head comparison against a CO-STAR prompt. The paper is ambitious in scope, internally consistent, and unusually self-aware about the distinction between behavioral findings and mechanistic speculation.

---

## Strengths

- **Genuine methodological self-discipline on mechanism vs. behavior.** The explicit Claim Classification section (§6.1) and the consistent hedging of mechanistic hypotheses as "explanatory models that generated correct predictions" is a standard rarely met in the prompt engineering literature. The authors do not overclaim transformer internals; they present behavioral evidence and name the mechanistic story separately. This is the right epistemic posture for this type of work.

- **The consideration-set finding is sharp and the decisive data point (Variant J) is well-chosen.** J uses identical Context, identical task, identical instructions as I, differs only in Persona, and scores 0/10 vs. 10/10. The J-05 Gemini run — 3,906 thinking tokens, still 0/10 — is a particularly effective demonstration that the failure is not compute-limited. This is the kind of controlled contrast that makes a behavioral claim credible.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is sound in one important respect.** Generating the CO-STAR prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of the framework under test is the correct approach to avoid author-confounded comparisons. The behavioral split (10/10 premise rejection vs. 10/10 compliance validation) is clean and the mechanism is clearly articulated.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is a concrete, replicable quantitative finding.** Three independent run sets converging at 12.8% → 11.3% → 11.0% is the kind of stable empirical regularity that earns credibility. The claim is narrow, measurable, and falsifiable.

- **Honest reporting of experimental failures.** The authors report that five experiments (exp-03 through exp-03d) failed to produce the clean masking demonstration they were designed for, explain why (category overlap problem), and redirect to exp-05 for the structural equivalent. This is good scientific practice and increases confidence in the positive results.

- **The "Confident Error" finding (exp-04c) is substantively important.** The observation that Task Stakes terminates a wrong-direction search at confident completion — producing fully elaborated, wrong, non-rambling output — has genuine production safety implications and is not obvious from prior work.

- **Cross-model replication on Gemini 2.5 Pro is meaningful.** Zero-shot transfer of I=10/10, J=0/10 on a different model family without modification strengthens the claim that P_p encodes a model-agnostic reasoning algorithm rather than a Claude-specific activation pattern.

---

## Weaknesses (ordered by severity)

- **The experimental design has a single-author confound that is never adequately addressed.** All prompts across all variants — including the "weak" Persona conditions — were designed by the same research team that developed the framework. The weak Persona conditions (e.g., "senior software engineer") may be intentionally thin rather than representative of how practitioners actually write weak prompts. There is no evidence that the authors attempted to construct the strongest possible weak-Persona prompt, or that they recruited blind evaluators to write comparison prompts. The CO-STAR comparison partially addresses this for one experiment, but the core ablation series (exp-01e, exp-01f, exp-02) does not. A framework that beats straw-man baselines is not validated.

- **The primary claim — "Persona is the primary determinant of reasoning quality" — is operationalized on a highly specific and arguably unrepresentative task class.** The paper explicitly restricts its scope to "hidden structural failure modes" that require cross-service or cross-temporal simulation. This is a legitimate and important task class, but the paper's framing ("the failure modes we study are not edge cases") is not supported. For the vast majority of production LLM use cases — summarization, question answering, code generation for well-specified tasks, classification — the claim that Persona dominates is neither tested nor plausible. The Track B factual reasoning control (§4.4) shows Stakes has no effect on simple factual questions, but this does not establish that Persona is the primary determinant there either. The scope restriction should be foregrounded in the abstract and title, not buried in §1.2.

- **The mechanistic framework (§6) is presented with more formalism than its epistemic status warrants.** The pseudo-formula in §6.2 uses standard attention notation ($Q$, $K$, $V$, $\sqrt{d_k}$, softmax) in a way that implies quantitative grounding it does not have. The $\neg Instructions$ term is particularly problematic: negation of a set of tokens is not a defined operation in transformer arithmetic, and the behavioral finding (Instructions compress output, not cognition) does not map onto logical negation. Similarly, the two-vector Stakes formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ presents token-delta measurements as if they were fitted coefficients of a validated model, when they are differences between two conditions in a single experiment. The paper would be stronger — and more honest — if these were presented as conceptual diagrams rather than quasi-mathematical formalisms.

- **The "consideration set" construct is never independently operationalized.** The paper's central theoretical claim is that Persona determines which reasoning paths are in the model's "reachable space." But consideration set membership is inferred entirely from output — if the model finds the bug, the bug was in the consideration set; if it doesn't, it wasn't. This is circular. There is no independent measure of consideration set size or membership that would allow the claim to be falsified by a method other than the one used to establish it. An alternative explanation — that P_p prompts simply provide more relevant in-context information that triggers the correct reasoning chain — is never ruled out.

- **n=10 per variant is insufficient for the statistical claims being made.** The paper reports 10/10 and 0/10 results as if they are decisive. Under a binomial model, 10/10 gives a 95% confidence interval for the true success rate of [0.69, 1.00] — consistent with a 70% success rate. 0/10 gives [0.00, 0.31] — consistent with a 30% success rate. The overlap between these intervals is zero, which is meaningful, but the paper's language ("entirely determined by frame installation," "the split is 10/10 vs. 10/10") implies a deterministic mechanism that 10-run experiments cannot establish. The ~11% elaboration coefficient is more credible because it is a continuous measurement replicated three times, but the binary detection claims need larger n or confidence intervals.

- **The CO-STAR comparison conflates framework quality with prompt quality for a single task.** CO-STAR is a general-purpose framework validated on generation tasks; the comparison task (architecturally flawed compliance requirement) is specifically chosen to favor the P_p mechanism. This is not a fair framework comparison — it is a demonstration that P_p outperforms P_d on the task class where P_p is theoretically predicted to win. A fair comparison would include tasks where CO-STAR's structured specification is predicted to have advantages, or would show that PCSIEFTR subsumes CO-STAR's performance on CO-STAR's home turf. The current comparison is a cherry-picked win.

- **The self-prediction gap (§3.1.1, §7.2) is presented as a validated finding but is a single data point.** Variant L predicted at 6/10, scored 10/10 — this is one observation. The "descriptive/enactive register distinction" is a post-hoc theoretical explanation for one data point. The claim that "P_p Personas should be evaluated empirically, not rated by inspection" may be correct, but it is not established by a single prediction miss. A systematic study of prediction vs. outcome across multiple Persona variants would be needed to validate this as a general principle.

- **The paper's writing style oscillates between scientific register and advocacy.** Phrases like "J is the archetype: a prompt that looks thorough, feels complete, and scores 0/10 on the task it was built for," "The amplifier does not know it is wrong," and "The frame never installed in A" are rhetorically effective but inappropriate for a scientific paper. This is a consistent pattern throughout, not an isolated instance, and it undermines the credibility of the empirical claims by making the paper read as a sales document for the framework.

- **Reproducibility is asserted but not demonstrated.** The paper states "All raw outputs are in `data/exp-*/raw/`" but provides no accessible link, DOI, or repository URL. The prompts themselves — the primary experimental materials — are not reproduced in the paper or appendix. Without the actual prompt text for each variant, the experimental claims cannot be replicated. This is a fatal reproducibility gap for a paper whose central claims depend entirely on prompt design.

- **The "total cost: ~$4.80" disclosure is a double-edged sword.** This implies approximately 640 API calls at roughly $0.0075 per call, which is consistent with short prompts and responses. But the paper reports mean token counts of 1,500–2,500 tokens per run. At current Claude Sonnet pricing, 640 runs averaging 2,000 output tokens would cost substantially more than $4.80. Either the token counts are inconsistent with the cost, or the cost figure is inaccurate. This discrepancy should be resolved, as it bears on the credibility of the quantitative reporting.

## Questions for the Authors

- **On the single-author confound:** Were the weak Persona conditions (e.g., Variant J's "senior software engineer") designed to be representative of how practitioners actually write prompts, or were they designed to be weak? Can you provide evidence — e.g., a sample of prompts from practitioners asked to write code review prompts without framework guidance — that your weak baselines are ecologically valid?

- **On the consideration-set operationalization:** How would you distinguish empirically between (a) P_p installs a qualitatively different consideration set, and (b) P_p provides more relevant in-context information that triggers the correct reasoning chain through standard in-context learning? What experiment would falsify the consideration-set interpretation in favor of the in-context-learning interpretation?

- **On the mechanistic formalism:** The $\neg Instructions$ term in the pseudo-formula implies logical negation of a token set. Can you specify what mathematical operation you intend, and why that operation is a better description of the behavioral finding than "Instructions reduce output length by approximately 89%"? More broadly, what would it take to falsify the K/V pre-filtering hypothesis at the activation level?

- **On statistical power:** Given that 10/10 is consistent with a true success rate as low as 69%, how do you rule out the possibility that your "0/10 vs. 10/10" results reflect a 30% vs. 70% difference rather than a 0% vs. 100% difference? Have you run any variants at n=50 or n=100 to check whether the 10/10 results are stable?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Can you show that PCSIEFTR performs comparably to CO-STAR on tasks where CO-STAR was designed to excel — e.g., creative writing, structured generation, multi-turn dialogue? Without this, the comparison demonstrates only that P_p outperforms P_d on the task class P_p was built for.

- **On reproducibility:** Where are the actual prompt texts for each variant? The paper's claims are entirely dependent on the specific language used in the Persona components, and without those texts, nothing can be replicated. Will you commit to releasing a complete prompt library as a condition of publication?

- **On the cost discrepancy:** Can you reconcile the "$4.80 total cost" figure with the reported token counts? Specifically, what were the mean input and output token counts per run, and what API pricing tier was used?

- **On scope:** The paper claims the consideration-set mechanism explains "the failure modes that matter most in production use." Can you provide evidence for this claim? What fraction of production LLM deployments involve the class of hidden structural failure modes your experiments target?

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision**

The paper contains a genuine empirical contribution — the consideration-set behavioral finding, the Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient, and the Confident Error observation are real results worth publishing — but the work as submitted has critical gaps in reproducibility (prompt texts not provided), statistical rigor (n=10 per variant for binary claims), scope validity (claims generalize beyond what is tested), and methodological controls (single-author confound, cherry-picked CO-STAR comparison) that must be addressed before the paper is suitable for a top-tier venue.
