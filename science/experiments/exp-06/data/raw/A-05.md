# Peer Review: PCSIEFTR: A Two-Layer Prompt Engineering Framework for Structured Reasoning in Large Language Models

---

## Summary

The paper proposes PCSIEFTR, an eight-component prompt engineering framework organized into a World Layer and Task Layer, and argues that the Persona component — specifically its procedural sub-component (P_p) — is the primary determinant of reasoning quality by determining the model's "consideration set." The central empirical claim is that P_p produces 10/10 detection of hidden structural failure modes while dispositional Persona (P_d) produces 0/10 or near-0/10 on the same tasks, a result that transfers across domains (code review, legal contract review) and model families (Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 2.5 Pro). Additional claims include a two-vector Stakes taxonomy with measured token-delta coefficients, an Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling mechanism with a converging ~11% coefficient, and a 10/10 vs. 10/10 head-to-head against a CO-STAR prompt on a premise-rejection task.

---

## Strengths

- **The P_p/P_d distinction is the paper's most credible contribution.** The behavioral split — P_p 10/10, P_d 0–1/10 on the same task with the same Context — is sharp, reproducible across domains, and not obviously confounded by the specific task instantiation. The Variant J result (0/10 with rich Context, 0/10 on Gemini with 3,906 thinking tokens) is a genuine empirical finding that resists the obvious alternative explanation (insufficient compute).

- **The paper is epistemically self-aware in its stated framing.** The claim classification in §6.1 explicitly separates behavioral findings from mechanistic hypotheses, and the abstract correctly flags the mechanistic claims as "explanatory models that generated correct predictions, not confirmed facts." This is the right epistemic posture for this class of work, and it is stated clearly.

- **The Instructions-as-elaboration-ceiling coefficient is the most methodologically disciplined measurement in the paper.** Three independent run sets, same methodology, converging values (12.8%, 11.3%, 11.0%). This is not a strong causal claim, but it is a stable behavioral measurement. The convergence across run sets is meaningful.

- **The CO-STAR comparison methodology is better than most head-to-head prompt comparisons in the literature.** Generating the comparison prompt in an isolated session with no knowledge of the framework under test is the correct approach. The behavioral split (premise rejection vs. premise validation, 10/10 each) is concrete and the difference in output is qualitatively described with sufficient specificity to be verifiable.

- **The paper names its own failure modes.** The masking test gap (§7.1), the self-prediction gap (§7.2), the category overlap problem, and the calibration failures in exp-03 through exp-03d are all acknowledged explicitly. This is not common in prompt engineering work and it raises the credibility of the claims that are made.

- **The Appendix B behavioral claim register is a genuine structural contribution.** Mapping every claim to its experimental basis in a single table makes the claim-evidence structure auditable. This is what the field needs more of.

---

## Weaknesses

**Ordered by structural severity.**

---

**1. The entire experimental record rests on a single scorer with no inter-rater reliability, no blind scoring, and no stated scoring protocol beyond "binary on a ground-truth criterion defined before each run."**

This is the load-bearing flaw. Every detection rate in the paper — 10/10, 0/10, 1/10 — is a human judgment call about whether the model output constitutes "detection of the primary structural failure mode, with named fix." The paper does not state who scored the outputs, whether the scorer was blind to condition, what the exact scoring rubric was, or whether any outputs were scored by more than one rater. At n=10 per variant, a single ambiguous call changes a 10/10 to 9/10 or a 0/10 to 1/10. More importantly, the P_p/P_d distinction is defined partly in terms of what counts as finding "the structural failure mode" versus finding "real bugs that appear on a careful first pass." That distinction is doing significant work in the scoring, and it is not operationalized. The paper cannot be replicated without this information.

This is not a methodological quibble. The central claim — that P_p produces qualitatively different outputs than P_d, not just quantitatively different ones — depends entirely on the validity of the binary scoring. If the scoring criterion is "did the model name the specific failure mode the authors designated as primary," then the result is partially a claim about the authors' choice of what counts as the finding, not purely about the model's reasoning. The paper does not address this.

---

**2. The paper makes a causal claim — "Persona determines the consideration set" — on evidence that is entirely correlational and confounded by prompt length and content.**

The P_p prompts are longer, more specific, and contain more domain-relevant procedural content than the P_d prompts. The paper does not control for prompt length, token count, or information content. "You are a senior software engineer" versus a multi-sentence procedural specification that names specific failure modes (zombie writes, lock expiry, GC pauses) is not a clean P_p/P_d manipulation — it is simultaneously a manipulation of role label, procedural specificity, domain knowledge encoded in the prompt, and prompt length. The paper acknowledges this nowhere.

The consideration-set mechanism hypothesis — that P_p performs K/V space pre-filtering — is stated as the explanation for the behavioral results. But the behavioral results are equally consistent with a simpler explanation: the P_p prompts contain more task-relevant information and implicitly specify what to look for, while the P_d prompts do not. The Variant L result (Kleppmann/Jepsen biography + procedural step, 10/10) is particularly important here: the Persona explicitly references distributed systems failure mode literature. This is not a pure procedural manipulation — it is also an information manipulation. The paper does not attempt to disentangle these.

A controlled experiment would hold prompt length and domain-information content constant while varying only the procedural vs. dispositional framing. No such experiment exists in this paper.

---

**3. The two-vector Stakes formula presents measured token-delta coefficients as if they are stable parameters of a generalizable model. They are not.**

The formula $R = P \times (1 + \alpha_{amp} S_i - \beta_{brake} S_t)$ is presented with "empirically grounded coefficients" derived from a single experiment (exp-04b) on a single task type (distributed lock code review) at a single temperature (0.5) on a single model (Claude Sonnet 4.6). The token deltas (+236, +578, +67) are measurements, not coefficients. A coefficient is a parameter that generalizes across conditions. These numbers will not replicate on a different task, a different model, a different temperature, or a different prompt. The paper does not claim they will, but it presents them in a formula that implies they are structural properties of the framework rather than measurements of a specific experimental configuration. The formula is a conceptual summary dressed as a quantitative model.

Additionally, the formula mixes behavioral measurements (token counts) with mechanistic hypotheses (inverse temperature, residual connection persistence) in a single expression without clearly marking which terms are measured and which are hypothesized. This is a precision problem that will mislead practitioners who attempt to use the formula.

---

**4. The CO-STAR comparison is a comparison of two specific prompts, not two frameworks, and the confounds are not controlled.**

The paper claims the CO-STAR head-to-head demonstrates a framework-level difference. But the CO-STAR prompt was generated by "a separate AI system" given only the scenario and asked to produce "the strongest possible code review prompt." This means the CO-STAR prompt was not written by a skilled human practitioner applying CO-STAR deliberately — it was generated by an AI system that may or may not have correctly instantiated CO-STAR's design principles. The paper does not show the CO-STAR prompt in full, does not evaluate whether it is a strong representative of CO-STAR, and does not consider whether a human expert applying CO-STAR would produce the same result.

The specific confound the paper itself identifies — that the CO-STAR Context section installed a compliance prior — is a prompt-authoring choice, not a structural property of CO-STAR. A CO-STAR prompt whose Context section did not validate the compliance framing would presumably behave differently. The paper is comparing a specific PCSIEFTR instantiation against a specific AI-generated CO-STAR instantiation, not the frameworks themselves. The 10/10 vs. 10/10 result is real; the framework-level interpretation is not supported.

---

**5. The mechanistic framework in §6 and the pseudo-formula in §6.2 are presented with more structural weight than the paper's own epistemic standards permit.**

The paper correctly states in §6.1 that mechanistic claims are hypotheses, not confirmed facts. But §6.2 presents a formula that uses standard transformer notation ($Q$, $K$, $V$, $d_k$, softmax) to represent the mechanistic hypotheses as if they are extensions of the attention equation. The formula $Response = \text{Softmax}\left(\frac{Stakes \cdot (Request \cdot Persona^T)}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \times Context \times \neg Instructions$ is not a description of what the transformer is doing. It is a metaphor. The $\neg Instructions$ term is particularly problematic: it implies that Instructions function as a literal negation operator on the attention output, which is not what the behavioral evidence shows (the evidence shows compression and re-routing, not negation). Presenting this as a formula in mathematical notation, in a paper submitted to a venue that reviews mechanistic claims carefully, will be read as a stronger claim than the text intends.

The self-prediction gap (§3.1.1, §7.2) is similarly framed with more mechanistic precision than the evidence supports. The "descriptive register" vs. "enactive register" distinction is an interesting observation about one data point (Variant L predicted 6/10, scored 10/10). One data point is not a structural property of instinct-language identity. The paper treats it as one.

---

**6. The experimental design is not pre-registered, and the relationship between the eleven experiments is not clearly stated.**

The paper presents eleven experiment series as if they are a coherent validation program, but the internal logic of the series — which experiments were designed before results were known, which were designed in response to prior results, which were calibration runs that failed and were replaced — is not transparent. The acknowledgment of "calibration failures" in exp-03 through exp-03d is honest, but the paper does not state how many experimental designs were attempted before the reported ones, whether the ground-truth criteria were set before or after running pilot variants, or whether any reported results were selected from a larger set of runs. At $4.80 total cost, the barrier to running many variants and reporting the ones that support the hypothesis is essentially zero. The paper does not address this.

---

**7. The n=10 per variant is insufficient for the precision of the claims being made, and the paper does not report confidence intervals or significance tests.**

10/10 and 0/10 are striking results, but they are not statistically characterized. The paper does not report confidence intervals, does not test whether the P_p/P_d difference is statistically significant under any reasonable model of the data-generating process, and does not report variance in token counts beyond mean values. The token-delta measurements (+236, +578, +67) are presented as if they are precise, but without standard deviations or confidence intervals they cannot be interpreted as measurements. The paper acknowledges n=10 as a design choice but does not justify it statistically.

---

**8. The "cross-model replication" claim is overstated.**

Exp-01g runs two variants (I and J) on Gemini 2.5 Pro and reports I=10/10, J=0/10. This is described as "zero-shot transfer" and "all primary behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families." Two variants on one additional model is not cross-model replication of all primary behavioral claims. The Stakes taxonomy, the Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient, the P_d/P_p distinction in the legal domain, and the CO-STAR comparison were not tested on Gemini. The paper's cross-model claim is supported only by the consideration-set mechanism on one task type. This should be stated precisely.

---

**9. The "dedicated machine" observation and related theoretical claims in §3.1.3 are not grounded in any experiment.**

The claim that "a transformer in generation mode is already fully committed to the task" and that "engagement intensity is not the scarce variable" is presented as a theoretical insight that motivates the Stakes taxonomy. It is not derived from any experiment in the paper. It is an assertion about transformer behavior that may or may not be correct and that is doing explanatory work the experimental record does not support.

---

## Questions for the Authors

- **On scoring validity:** What was the exact scoring rubric for "detection of the primary structural failure mode, with named fix"? Who scored the outputs? Were scorers blind to condition? What was inter-rater reliability on a sample of outputs? If a single scorer made all judgments, what is the basis for confidence that the P_p/P_d distinction in the outputs is not partly a distinction in what the scorer counted as detection?

- **On confound control:** The P_p Personas in the paper are longer, more specific, and contain more domain-relevant procedural content than the P_d Personas. Can you show a P_p Persona that is matched to a P_d Persona in length and domain-information content, varying only the procedural vs. dispositional framing, that still produces the 10/10 vs. 0/10 split? Without this, the consideration-set mechanism cannot be distinguished from "P_p prompts contain more task-relevant information."

- **On the Variant L result:** Variant L's Persona references Kleppmann and Jepsen — authors whose work specifically addresses distributed systems failure modes including lock expiry and zombie writes. Is it possible that the 10/10 result for Variant L is driven by the domain-specific knowledge encoded in the author references rather than by the procedural framing? How would you test this?

- **On the CO-STAR comparison:** Can you show the full CO-STAR prompt used in exp-05? Can you show that a human expert applying CO-STAR with a Context section that did not validate the compliance framing would produce the same 0/10 premise-rejection result? Without this, the comparison is between two specific prompts, not two frameworks.

- **On the formula coefficients:** The token deltas in exp-04b (+236, +578, +67) are presented as "measured coefficients" in the two-vector formula. Do these values replicate on a different task type, a different model, or a different temperature setting? If not, in what sense are they coefficients of the framework rather than measurements of a specific experimental configuration?

- **On experimental design transparency:** How many experimental designs were attempted before the eleven reported series? Were the ground-truth criteria for each experiment set before or after running any variants? Were any variants run and not reported? The $4.80 total cost means the selection pressure on reported results is essentially unconstrained by cost.

- **On the self-prediction gap:** The descriptive/enactive register distinction is supported by one data point (Variant L predicted 6/10, scored 10/10). How many Persona variants were rated by the authoring model and then scored empirically? Is the systematic underestimation claim supported by more than one instance?

- **On cross-model scope:** Which of the paper's primary behavioral claims — P_p/P_d distinction, Stakes taxonomy, Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient, CO-STAR comparison — have been tested on Gemini 2.5 Pro or any model other than Claude Sonnet 4.6? The abstract states "all principal behavioral claims transfer zero-shot across model families." This is not supported by exp-01g alone.

---

## Recommendation

**Major Revision.**

The paper contains a genuine empirical finding — the P_p/P_d behavioral split — that is sharp, reproducible across domains, and not obviously explained by the most naive alternative hypotheses. The Instructions-as-ceiling coefficient is a disciplined measurement. The CO-STAR comparison methodology is better than the field norm. These are real contributions.

But the paper cannot be accepted in its current form because the load-bearing empirical claims rest on uncharacterized scoring, the primary manipulation (P_p vs. P_d) is confounded with prompt length and information content in ways the paper does not address, the quantitative formula presents measurements as generalizable parameters, the cross-model replication claim is substantially overstated, and the mechanistic framework uses transformer notation in ways that will be read as stronger claims than the text intends. These are not presentation problems — they are structural problems that require new experiments or substantially revised claims. The paper has the right instincts about what matters; it does not yet have the experimental architecture to carry the weight of its conclusions.
